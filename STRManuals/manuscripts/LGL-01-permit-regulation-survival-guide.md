## Disclaimer

This manual is plain-English education on how to research short-term rental regulations in U.S. cities. **It is not legal advice.** Local regulations change constantly, and the consequences of getting them wrong include fines, property liens, criminal charges in extreme cases, and being shut down. Before operating an STR in any jurisdiction, consult a local attorney — ideally one with documented STR clients in that market.

This manual is U.S.-focused. Some principles transfer abroad; the manual does not claim coverage for international markets.

The framework described — categories, research approach, six-question call — is intended as a starting point for your own research, not a substitute for it.

# Chapter 1 — Why this manual exists

Every city does short-term rental regulation differently. New Orleans's rules look nothing like Bend, Oregon's, which look nothing like Park City, Utah's. The fines for violations range from $50 to $25,000 per occurrence. Some cities require a four-step permit process before you can list. Others have no STR rules at all yet but are debating them in next month's city council meeting.

The default approach most operators take is hopeful research: Google search, scan a couple of articles, ask in a Facebook group, list the property. This works until it doesn't. Until a neighbor reports the property. Until the city sends a cease-and-desist. Until your property tax assessment changes. Until you realize you've been collecting lodging tax all year and never remitted it because you didn't know it existed.

This manual isn't a comprehensive list of regulations for every U.S. city — that's impossible to maintain and would be wrong by the time it printed. It's a research framework that works for any U.S. market, plus a set of decision rules for when to act on what you find.

## The framework, briefly

Every U.S. jurisdiction with STR regulation has rules in some combination of four categories:

1. **Zoning** — what types of properties are allowed to operate as STRs, and where in the city.
2. **Permits and registration** — administrative requirements before you can operate.
3. **Lodging tax** — what taxes you collect from guests and remit to the city/state.
4. **Private restrictions** — HOA covenants, condo bylaws, deed restrictions, lease terms.

Plus a fifth category that's not a regulation per se but is a real risk:

5. **Insurance and liability** — what coverage you need, and what gaps standard policies leave.

The rest of this manual is about finding what your specific jurisdiction says about each category, in the right order, with the right authoritative sources.

## How to use this manual

Read chapters 1–3 straight through. They give you the framework and the research approach.

Chapters 4–8 are reference chapters — read them in order on the first pass, then come back to specific ones as questions arise.

Chapter 9 — the six-question call — is the single most useful page of the manual. Most STR research can be resolved by one well-prepared 30-minute phone call to the right city office. The chapter is the script.

The companion Permit Research Worksheet is filled in as you read. By the end, you should have a complete regulatory profile for your specific property.

## A note on jurisdictions in flux

Many U.S. cities are actively rewriting their STR rules. What was legal in 2024 might require a permit in 2026. What needed a permit in 2024 might be banned in 2026.

The manual is good practice for the moment. The framework is durable. Specific rules require ongoing monitoring — typically one check per quarter, with deeper review when the city signals a regulatory effort underway.

This manual covers how to do that monitoring. The work itself is yours.

# Chapter 2 — The four regulation categories

Before you research, know what you're looking for. The categories below are universal — every U.S. jurisdiction's STR rules will fit into them somehow.

## Category 1: Zoning

Zoning is the most fundamental regulatory question. It answers: "Is short-term rental even allowed in my property's location?"

Cities divide their territory into zoning districts (residential R-1, R-2, R-3; commercial; mixed-use; agricultural; etc.) and assign permitted uses to each. STRs are sometimes treated as a permitted use in residential zones, sometimes as a conditional use requiring a special permit, sometimes prohibited entirely.

A property's zoning is determined by location, not by the building. You can have two identical houses on the same street where one is in an STR-allowed zone and one isn't. Always check the specific parcel.

**What to find:**

- Your property's zoning district designation.
- Whether STRs are permitted, conditional, or prohibited in that district.
- Any subdistricts or overlay zones that modify standard rules.
- Caps or limits (e.g., "STRs cannot exceed 5% of housing units in the district").
- Owner-occupancy requirements (some cities only allow STRs in primary residences).

**Where to find it:** the city's zoning code (also called the municipal code or zoning ordinance), typically published online. Chapter 4 covers how to read these codes.

## Category 2: Permits and registration

Even if zoning allows STRs, most cities require some administrative step before you can operate. This is the permit/registration category.

Common requirements:

- **STR registration.** A simple registration with the city, typically requiring proof of insurance and an annual fee ($50–$500).
- **Operating permit / business license.** A more involved approval, sometimes requiring inspection, neighbor notification, or public hearing.
- **Conditional use permit (CUP).** Required in some districts for STR operation; involves a planning commission review and potentially neighbor input.
- **Inspection.** Some cities require building, fire, or health inspection before issuing the permit.

**What to find:**

- The specific permit/registration type required for your property.
- The application process and timeline.
- Required documentation.
- Fees (initial and renewal).
- Renewal frequency.
- Whether the permit is property-specific or owner-specific (matters when you sell).

**Where to find it:** the city's planning department, building department, or finance department, depending on the permit type. The official city website usually has a "Short-term rental" page that consolidates the relevant departments and forms.

## Category 3: Lodging tax

Lodging tax (also called transient occupancy tax / TOT, hotel tax, or accommodations tax) is a percentage charge on the rental amount that you collect from guests and remit to the city or state.

Rates vary widely. Common ranges:

- City lodging tax: 3–14%.
- County lodging tax: 0–5%.
- State lodging tax: 0–8%.

A property in some markets faces 15–20% combined lodging tax. The total is paid by the guest (added to the rental rate) but the host is legally responsible for collection and remittance.

Airbnb and Vrbo collect and remit lodging tax automatically in many U.S. jurisdictions — but not all, and not for all tax types. The host is responsible for any tax not collected by the platform.

**What to find:**

- All applicable lodging taxes (city, county, state).
- Rates for each.
- Whether the platform collects and remits each.
- For taxes the platform doesn't handle: registration with the tax authority, filing frequency, payment method.
- Penalties for late or missed filings.

**Where to find it:** the city or county finance department, the state department of revenue, and the platform's tax-handling page (which lists which taxes they collect and which they don't).

## Category 4: Private restrictions

Private restrictions are rules imposed by entities other than the government — homeowners' associations (HOAs), condo associations (COAs), deed restrictions, or lease agreements.

These are not visible in city code. They're contractual. And they routinely override what city zoning allows.

**Common private restrictions:**

- HOA covenants prohibiting STRs entirely.
- Condo bylaws restricting rentals to minimum 30 days.
- Deed restrictions limiting rental terms (specific to the property's title).
- Lease terms in your own lease (if you're a tenant trying to STR a unit you don't own).

**What to find:**

- Any HOA, COA, or master association the property belongs to.
- The current rules and covenants (sometimes called CC&Rs — Conditions, Covenants & Restrictions).
- Any pending changes the association is debating.
- Enforcement history (have they sued or fined STR operators recently?).

**Where to find it:** the HOA management company (if applicable); the property's title documents; the property's CC&Rs (typically delivered at closing); your lease (if relevant).

## Category 5: Insurance and liability

Not technically a regulation, but a parallel risk that operators frequently ignore.

A standard homeowner's insurance policy excludes commercial use of the property. An STR is commercial use. If a guest is injured and your only coverage is a homeowner's policy, the claim may be denied entirely.

Airbnb's AirCover provides limited host damage protection but does not provide liability coverage suitable for a serious injury claim.

**What to find:**

- A short-term rental-specific liability policy (typically $1M–$2M coverage).
- A property damage policy that covers STR use.
- Optional: umbrella liability for additional coverage.

**Where to find it:** specialized insurers like Proper Insurance, Slice, or Steadily. Standard insurers (State Farm, Allstate, etc.) sometimes offer STR riders but coverage varies.

This is covered in Chapter 8.

# Chapter 3 — Where to actually look

The internet is full of STR regulation information. Most of it is wrong, outdated, or generic enough to be useless for your specific property.

This chapter ranks sources by reliability. Use this ranking to filter what you find.

## Tier 1: Official government sources

The only authoritative sources for what regulations actually say.

**City zoning code.** Always available online for U.S. cities, typically on the city's website under "city code," "municipal code," or "ordinances." Sometimes hosted on third-party platforms (Municode, Code Publishing).

**City STR-specific pages.** Most cities with STR regulation have a dedicated page explaining the requirements. Search "[city name] short-term rental" and look for a .gov result.

**Application forms and instructions.** The forms themselves often clarify ambiguities the rules don't. Read every line of the application.

**City Council meeting minutes.** When STR rules are being debated or recently passed, the minutes show the legislative intent and any compromises made. These help interpret ambiguous rules.

**State revenue department.** For state-level lodging tax, the state's department of revenue is authoritative.

## Tier 2: Local professional sources

These are useful but less authoritative than direct government sources.

**Local STR attorney.** A local attorney with documented STR clients can interpret unclear rules in your specific context. Worth $300–$500 for a 1-hour consultation if you're unsure.

**Local CPA with STR clients.** For tax questions specifically, a local CPA familiar with STR work knows the practical handling of lodging taxes in your jurisdiction.

**Real estate attorney from the property purchase.** If you bought the property with an attorney, they likely have insight into title-level restrictions (deed restrictions, HOA implications).

## Tier 3: STR community knowledge

Useful for orientation but never authoritative.

**Local STR Facebook group or meetup.** Other operators in your market often know which rules are enforced and which are mostly paper. Take with a grain of salt — Facebook commenters are often confidently wrong.

**BiggerPockets and similar forums.** Posts about specific cities can be helpful, but rules change. Verify against current city sources.

**STR-focused podcasts and YouTube.** Best for general framework knowledge. Specific city rules from these sources are usually outdated by the time you watch.

## Tier 4: Generic content (ignore)

Avoid:

**Generic "how to comply with STR regulations" articles.** Most of these are SEO content from property management companies trying to drive leads. Information is generic and often wrong for your market.

**Auto-generated city profiles.** Some sites publish "STR rules in [city]" pages built by scraping city websites. They're often months or years out of date.

**AI summaries of regulations.** Tempting, but the regulations are too specific for AI to handle reliably. Use AI to help you read official sources, not to summarize them.

## A research workflow that works

The fastest path to a reliable regulatory profile:

1. **30 minutes on Tier 1 sources.** Read the city's STR page. Pull the zoning code. Identify the parcel zoning. Find the permit application.
2. **15 minutes on Tier 2 sources.** Confirm any ambiguities with a local CPA's blog post or attorney's website.
3. **The six-question call.** The script in Chapter 9. 30 minutes to the city's planning department or STR officer. Resolves remaining ambiguity.
4. **Tier 3 sources for context.** Browse a local Facebook group post about recent enforcement actions. Helps set expectation about what the city actually does, not just what they say.

Total time: about 90 minutes to build a complete regulatory profile for most properties.

# Chapter 4 — Reading a zoning code without a law degree

Zoning codes look intimidating. They're written in dense legal language with cross-references to other code sections, definitions buried in different chapters, and exceptions that modify exceptions.

The good news: you don't need to read the entire code. You need to find about ten specific provisions. This chapter shows you how.

## How a zoning code is structured

Most U.S. zoning codes follow a similar structure:

**Chapter on definitions.** Defines terms used throughout the code: "dwelling unit," "short-term rental," "transient occupancy," "principal use," etc. Critical for interpretation.

**Chapter on districts.** Lists each zoning district (R-1, R-2, R-3, C-1, etc.) and the permitted uses in each.

**Chapter on use regulations.** Defines what each permitted use means and any specific restrictions on it.

**Chapter on conditional uses.** Covers uses that require additional approval (CUPs).

**Chapter on overlay zones.** Special zones layered on top of the base zoning that modify rules.

**Chapter on enforcement.** Penalties, processes for challenging violations, etc.

## Finding STR provisions

Most cities now address STRs explicitly in the code. Look for:

- A definition of "short-term rental" or "transient lodging" in the definitions chapter.
- A reference to short-term rentals in the use regulations chapter.
- A specific section devoted to STR rules (often in a chapter titled "specific uses" or "supplementary regulations").

If the code doesn't address STRs by name, look for definitions of "transient occupancy," "lodging," "tourist home," or "vacation rental." Different cities use different terms; some use multiple.

## Reading a zoning district provision

Once you've identified your property's zoning district, find the section in the code that lists permitted uses in that district. A typical provision looks like:

```
Section 17.32.030 — R-2 Two-Family Residential District

Permitted uses:
A. Single-family detached dwellings
B. Two-family dwellings
C. Public and private parks and playgrounds
D. Schools, public and private (subject to Section 17.40.020)

Conditional uses (subject to Chapter 17.60):
A. Churches and similar places of worship
B. Bed-and-breakfast establishments
C. Short-term rentals

Prohibited uses:
A. Multi-family dwellings
B. Commercial uses except as specifically permitted
```

In this example, STRs are conditional uses — meaning they're allowed but require a Conditional Use Permit (CUP). A regular property in this district can't just be turned into an STR; the owner needs to go through the CUP process.

The key is identifying which list (permitted, conditional, prohibited) STRs fall into for your district.

## Reading a definition

Zoning codes often define terms more specifically than colloquial usage. The definition is what controls. A typical STR definition:

```
Section 17.04.520 — Short-term rental.

A "short-term rental" means the rental of all or part of a residential
dwelling unit to any person or entity for a period of less than thirty
(30) consecutive days. A short-term rental does not include:

A. Bed-and-breakfast establishments operating under a permit issued
under Chapter 17.62.

B. Hotels and motels operating under a commercial lodging license.

C. Rentals of less than thirty (30) days where the property is the
permanent residence of the rentee.
```

Notice the specifics: 30 days, residential dwelling unit, exclusions for B&Bs and hotels. A property renting for stays of 25 days would be an STR under this code; a 35-day stay wouldn't.

This level of precision matters. A code that defines STR as "less than 30 consecutive days" gives you a different operational option than one that defines it as "any rental of a transient nature."

## Reading exceptions and conditional language

Zoning codes often include exceptions that look minor but matter:

```
Section 17.32.040 — STR-specific provisions.

Notwithstanding Section 17.32.030, short-term rentals may be conducted
in the R-2 district as a permitted use without a Conditional Use Permit
provided that:

A. The dwelling is the operator's primary residence.

B. The operator is present during all rental periods.

C. The rental period does not exceed ninety (90) days per calendar year.
```

This exception fundamentally changes the rule. STRs are normally conditional in R-2 — but if you live there and host while present and limit it to 90 days/year, no CUP needed.

This is the kind of provision that's easy to miss on a casual read but completely changes the operational picture. Read carefully.

## What to look for, specifically

Make a list as you read. For each property, you should identify:

1. **Zoning district** of the parcel.
2. **STR classification** (permitted, conditional, prohibited).
3. **Definition of STR** in your code (the day threshold, owner-occupancy requirement, etc.).
4. **Specific STR provisions** (caps on number of rentals, days per year limits, distance requirements between STRs, etc.).
5. **Permit type required** (registration, business license, CUP).
6. **Inspection requirements.**
7. **Penalties** for unpermitted operation.
8. **Notice and appeal rights.**

## When the code is silent

Some cities haven't addressed STRs specifically. Their code uses general definitions of "rental" or "transient occupancy" but doesn't have STR-specific provisions.

In these cases, you're operating in a gray area. The conservative interpretation is that you should treat STRs the same as the most-similar named use in the code (typically "transient lodging" or "tourist home").

A safer approach: call the city. Tell them you're considering an STR and want to confirm the regulatory situation. The city's response is often "we don't have specific rules yet, but we're working on it" — useful information for planning.

## When you can't read the code

Some codes are genuinely too dense for non-attorneys to navigate confidently. If after 30 minutes of effort you can't determine your property's regulatory status, hire a local attorney for a 1-hour consultation. The cost ($300–$500) is small compared to the cost of operating against rules you didn't see.

# Chapter 5 — Permits and registrations

Once zoning permits STRs in your district, the next question is: what administrative steps do you need to take before you can operate?

This chapter covers the difference between registration, permits, and licenses, and how to identify which apply to your property.

## Registration vs. permit vs. license

Different cities use these words differently. The distinctions matter:

**Registration.** Usually a simple administrative process. You submit a form with property info, pay a fee, and get a registration number. Often required just to be in the city's database for compliance and tax purposes.

**Permit.** A more involved process that explicitly authorizes a specific activity at a specific property. May require inspection, public notice, or planning commission review.

**License.** Typically a business operation authorization. Often issued by the city's finance or business department.

**Conditional Use Permit (CUP).** A special category of permit required for uses not "by right" in a district. Involves planning department review, often a public hearing, and sometimes neighbor notification.

A given city might require any combination: registration only, permit only, license only, or multiple of these.

## How to identify what's required

Three steps:

**Step 1:** Look at your city's STR-specific page (usually at [cityname].gov/short-term-rentals or similar). This typically lists every requirement with links to forms.

**Step 2:** Cross-reference with the zoning code. The code's STR section may reference different administrative requirements than the city's promotional page captures.

**Step 3:** Call the city's planning department to confirm. Some requirements aren't well-documented online.

## Common requirement patterns

A few common patterns by city type:

**Patterns A — Light-touch markets** (e.g., parts of the southwest, smaller cities just starting to regulate):

- Simple STR registration: $50–$150 annual fee.
- Proof of liability insurance: $1M minimum coverage.
- Local contact information (someone available 24/7 to respond to issues).

That's it. Total time: 1–2 hours.

**Pattern B — Moderate-regulation markets** (most mid-sized U.S. cities):

- STR registration with the city.
- Business license.
- Proof of insurance.
- Property inspection (sometimes building, sometimes fire safety, sometimes both).
- Lodging tax registration with the finance department.

Total time: 2–6 weeks for full approval. Often involves coordinating between 2–3 city departments.

**Pattern C — Heavy-regulation markets** (NYC, San Francisco, New Orleans, Honolulu, parts of Aspen and Park City):

- Multiple permits including CUPs.
- Owner-occupancy requirements.
- Caps on operating days per year.
- Public hearing or neighbor notification.
- Strict inspection regimes.
- Heavy fines for non-compliance ($1,000–$25,000 per occurrence).

Total time: 1–6 months for approval. May not be possible at all in some markets.

## What the application actually requires

A typical STR permit application asks for:

- Owner identification and contact information.
- Property address and parcel identification number (APN).
- Number of bedrooms and maximum guest occupancy.
- Local responsible person (you or your property manager) who can respond to neighbor complaints within a specified window (typically 60 minutes).
- Proof of liability insurance.
- Floor plan of the rental space (sometimes).
- Site plan showing parking (sometimes).
- Application fee ($50–$5,000 depending on the city).

For cities requiring inspection, the application process often involves:

- Initial application submission and fee.
- Scheduling of one or more inspections.
- Potential corrective work based on inspection findings.
- Re-inspection after corrections.
- Final permit issuance after all inspections pass.

## Renewal

Most STR permits and registrations are annual. Renewal involves:

- Confirming property and operational details haven't materially changed.
- Paying the renewal fee.
- Re-confirming insurance.
- Sometimes a renewed inspection.

Calendar this. Late renewal can result in lapsed permits, which means operating without authorization.

## Transferability when selling

A critical question often missed: is the permit attached to the property or to the owner?

- **Property-attached permits** transfer to the new owner with the property. Useful when selling — the buyer inherits the regulatory status.
- **Owner-attached permits** require the new owner to apply fresh. The new owner's application may not be approved, especially in tight markets where the city has imposed caps.

If you're buying an STR, confirm permit status before closing. A property advertised as "STR-permitted" might mean the previous owner had a permit that doesn't transfer.

## Special note: caps and lotteries

Some markets have hit STR saturation and imposed caps — a maximum number of STR permits issued at any time. New permits in these markets are typically issued by lottery when existing permits expire or are revoked.

If you're considering buying an STR in a capped market, the permit isn't optional. It's the entire deal. Buying a property without a permit in a capped market means you might never be able to operate it as an STR.

# Chapter 6 — Lodging taxes

Lodging tax is the regulatory category most operators get wrong. Not because the rules are obscure, but because the rules involve multiple jurisdictions, vary by platform, and require ongoing administrative work that many operators ignore.

The penalty for ignoring lodging tax: back taxes plus interest plus penalties, retroactive to when you started operating. Penalties of 25–100% of the unpaid tax are common.

## What lodging tax is

Lodging tax (also called transient occupancy tax / TOT, hotel tax, or accommodations tax) is a percentage charge on the rental amount applied to short-term stays.

It's collected from the guest (added to the rental price) and remitted by the host to the relevant tax authority. The host doesn't pay the tax out of pocket — but the host is legally responsible for collecting and remitting it.

## The layered tax structure

Most U.S. STR markets have multiple layers of lodging tax:

- **City tax.** The city's tax on lodging within its limits.
- **County tax.** The county's tax (sometimes only in unincorporated areas, sometimes county-wide).
- **State tax.** A state-level tax on lodging or sales tax that applies to short-term lodging.
- **Tourism district tax.** Some markets have additional tourism improvement district (TID) taxes.

A property in some markets might have 3–4 separate layers totaling 12–18%. A property in another might have only one layer at 3%.

## What platforms collect for you

Airbnb collects and remits lodging tax automatically in many U.S. jurisdictions — but coverage varies:

- **Cities Airbnb has agreements with** — Airbnb collects city, county, and sometimes state tax automatically. The host doesn't have to do anything.
- **States Airbnb has agreements with** — state-level tax is handled even if city-level isn't.
- **Cities/states without agreements** — the host is responsible for everything.

The same logic applies to Vrbo, with different specific coverage.

Critical: a platform might collect *some* of the lodging tax but not *all*. The host is responsible for whatever the platform doesn't collect.

## Finding what you actually owe

For each tax authority (city, county, state):

1. **Identify whether the tax exists.** Check the authority's website or call their finance department.
2. **Identify the rate.**
3. **Identify whether the platform collects it.** Check Airbnb's or Vrbo's tax page for your specific city. Pay attention to caveats.
4. **For taxes the platform doesn't collect:** register with the authority, set up filing, and remit on schedule.

The companion Permit Research Worksheet has a section for tracking each tax layer with these four data points.

## Direct bookings — the host owns it all

When you take direct bookings (REV-02), the platforms aren't collecting tax for you. You're 100% responsible for collecting and remitting all applicable lodging taxes.

Most direct-booking platforms (Lodgify, OwnerRez, Hostfully) include automatic tax collection in their booking flow. Configure the rates and the platform adds them to the guest's total. You then remit on schedule to each authority.

Tax compliance is one of the genuine headaches of direct bookings. It's manageable but can't be ignored.

## Filing schedules

Common filing patterns:

- **Monthly.** Most common for cities and counties. Filing typically due by the 15th or 20th of the following month.
- **Quarterly.** Used by some states and smaller jurisdictions.
- **Annually.** Rare; sometimes used as the only requirement in low-volume markets.

Even if you owe zero (no bookings that month), most authorities require zero filings — meaning you submit the form showing $0 owed. Skipping zero filings can result in penalties.

## The registration process

Before you can file, you need to register with the tax authority. This typically requires:

- Business identification (your name or LLC name, federal tax ID).
- Property location.
- Estimated annual revenue.
- Business start date (the date you started operating, not the date you registered).

Critical: the start date matters. If you've been operating for 6 months without registering, expect to be liable for tax from the start date — not from the registration date.

## What to do if you've been non-compliant

A common situation: you've been operating for 1–2 years and now realize you should have been collecting and remitting lodging tax.

The honest path forward:

1. **Calculate what you owe.** Total revenue × applicable tax rate × number of months. Add interest at the authority's stated rate (typically 6–12% annual).
2. **Self-disclose.** Most tax authorities have voluntary disclosure programs for taxpayers coming forward. These typically waive or reduce penalties (but not the underlying tax or interest) in exchange for upfront cooperation.
3. **Register going forward.** Get into compliance from this point on.
4. **Talk to a CPA.** Especially before self-disclosing — there may be tactical considerations (statute of limitations, payment arrangements) a CPA can advise on.

The cost of self-disclosing is real but is usually a fraction of the cost of being caught by audit. If you've been non-compliant for any meaningful period, address it proactively.

## Pass-through vs. absorbed

A subtle point: lodging tax should be a *pass-through*, not an expense absorbed by the host. If the rate is 12% and you charge $200/night, the guest sees $200/night × 12% = $24 tax, total $224.

If you're listing your nightly rate at $200 and not separately charging the 12% tax, you're absorbing the tax — meaning your effective rate is $179/night and your tax burden is $21. This is a common mistake among new operators.

Configure your platform settings (Airbnb, Vrbo, your direct site) to show the rental rate and tax separately. Guests are accustomed to this; transparent breakdown is industry standard.

# Chapter 7 — HOA covenants and what overrides what

Every property is subject to two layers of rules: public (zoning, permits, taxes) and private (HOAs, deed restrictions, lease terms). Most operators understand the public rules. Many ignore the private rules — and find out the hard way that private rules can shut down their operation regardless of what the city allows.

## The legal hierarchy

In most U.S. states, the legal hierarchy for property use rules is:

1. **State and federal law.** Wins over everything.
2. **Local zoning and ordinances.** Wins over private restrictions, with some exceptions.
3. **Private restrictions** (HOA covenants, deed restrictions, condo bylaws). Apply within the limits set by public law.

But there are significant exceptions to this hierarchy:

**Exception 1: Private restrictions can be more restrictive than public law.** Zoning permits STRs in your district. Your HOA bans them. The HOA wins for properties bound by it. Public law sets the floor; private restrictions can raise the ceiling.

**Exception 2: Some states have laws preempting HOA restrictions.** A handful of states have enacted laws that limit how much HOAs can restrict short-term rentals. These laws are evolving; check your state.

**Exception 3: Recent vs. retroactive restrictions.** Some HOA restrictions take effect immediately for new owners; others require unanimous consent for changes affecting existing owners. The timing matters.

## How to find your private restrictions

For an HOA-controlled property:

1. **Get the CC&Rs (Conditions, Covenants & Restrictions).** Typically delivered at closing. If you don't have them, request from the title company or the HOA management company. They're public records.

2. **Get the bylaws.** Different document from CC&Rs. Covers governance, voting, enforcement.

3. **Get the rules and regulations.** Sometimes called "house rules." Often the HOA can amend these by board action without a member vote.

4. **Get any recent meeting minutes.** Look for STR-related discussions. Boards sometimes signal incoming restrictions before formally adopting them.

For non-HOA properties:

1. **Check the deed.** Deed restrictions sometimes appear directly on the property's title.

2. **Pull the title commitment from your purchase.** This lists encumbrances on the property.

3. **Check for master-association membership.** Some properties not in an HOA are still subject to a master association covering a larger area.

## What HOA rules typically say about STRs

Common provisions:

- **Outright ban on rentals under X days.** "No rental of a unit for periods of less than 30 days." This is the most common STR restriction.

- **Minimum number of consecutive days.** "Rentals must be for periods of not less than 6 months." Effectively bans STR.

- **Owner-occupancy requirements.** "The unit must be occupied by an owner for at least 6 months of any 12-month period." Limits STR to part-time use.

- **Rental cap percentages.** "Not more than 30% of units may be rented in any form." Limits new STRs as the cap fills.

- **Approval requirements for rentals.** "Rentals subject to board approval." Effectively allows the board to decline at will.

- **Pet, noise, or guest-conduct rules.** Even when STR is allowed, these rules can make STR operation difficult — particularly if guests violate them and the host is fined.

## Enforcement reality

Some HOAs aggressively enforce STR restrictions. Others have rules on paper that are widely ignored. Knowing which is which matters.

Indicators of aggressive enforcement:

- Recent enforcement actions against STR operators in the community.
- Active board with strong neighborhood preferences against STRs.
- Recent rule updates specifically targeting STRs.
- Litigation history involving STR disputes.

Indicators of casual enforcement:

- Long-standing rules that haven't been updated.
- No documented enforcement actions in recent years.
- Board members who themselves operate STRs.
- Community demographics shifting toward investor ownership.

A casual-enforcement HOA can become aggressive overnight when neighbors complain or the board changes. Don't assume casual enforcement will continue indefinitely.

## What to do when private rules conflict with your plan

Three options:

**Option 1: Comply with the private restrictions.** If the HOA bans STR, don't operate one. Use the property differently or sell.

**Option 2: Try to change the rules.** Some HOAs have processes for changing covenants. Requires owner votes (typically supermajority). Slow, sometimes impossible, but viable for owners willing to organize.

**Option 3: Operate against the rules.** Hosts sometimes operate quietly against HOA rules and avoid detection. This is risky. If discovered, the HOA can fine, lien, sue, or in extreme cases force sale of the property. Doing this knowingly is a deliberate gamble; doing it unknowingly is a research failure.

The right answer depends on the property, the HOA, and your risk tolerance. The wrong answer is to ignore the question.

# Chapter 8 — Insurance — AirCover isn't enough

Insurance is the regulatory category most operators most-egregiously underestimate. The default assumption — "I have homeowner's insurance and Airbnb's AirCover, so I'm covered" — is wrong, and the wrongness becomes expensive when an actual claim happens.

## What standard homeowner's insurance covers

A standard homeowner's policy covers:

- Damage to the structure from typical perils (fire, theft, weather).
- Personal property inside the home.
- Liability for injuries to guests at the home — IF the home is being used as your primary residence.

The last clause is the problem. Most standard homeowner's policies explicitly exclude commercial use of the property. Renting the property to short-term guests is commercial use. A claim arising from STR operation may be denied entirely.

## What AirCover covers

Airbnb's AirCover provides:

- Up to $3 million in property damage protection (host damage protection).
- Up to $1 million in liability insurance for guest claims.

This sounds substantial. But:

- AirCover is structured as **secondary** insurance — it pays only after your primary insurance denies or pays out.
- AirCover has significant exclusions: pre-existing damage, normal wear, certain types of personal property, certain claim categories.
- AirCover only covers Airbnb-booked stays. Direct bookings, Vrbo bookings, and non-Airbnb activity are uncovered.

For most STR operators, AirCover is a useful supplement but not adequate primary coverage.

## What you actually need

Three coverage types:

**1. Property damage policy that covers commercial use.** Either a standard homeowner's policy with an STR rider, or a specialized STR policy. Covers physical damage to the property regardless of who caused it.

**2. Liability insurance for STR-specific risks.** $1M–$2M coverage typical. Pays out if a guest is injured or causes injury during their stay.

**3. Optional: umbrella policy.** Adds liability coverage above the underlying policy. Useful for high-value properties or operators who own multiple STRs.

## Specialized STR insurers

Several companies specialize in STR-specific coverage:

- **Proper Insurance.** Established STR insurer. Good for high-occupancy properties.
- **Slice.** Provides on-demand coverage; activates only on rental days.
- **Steadily.** Newer entrant; technology-forward.
- **Foremost.** Offers a "vacation home" product line that includes STR coverage.

Standard insurers (State Farm, Allstate, Liberty Mutual) sometimes offer STR riders or endorsements on existing homeowner's policies. Coverage and cost vary widely; quote multiple options.

## Cost expectations

For a typical $400,000 STR with $1M liability:

- Standard homeowner's policy with STR rider: $1,500–$3,000 per year.
- Specialized STR policy: $2,000–$5,000 per year.
- Umbrella policy adding $1M liability: $300–$600 per year.

The specialized STR policy is more expensive but typically provides more comprehensive coverage and easier claims handling. For a property generating $50,000+ in revenue, the additional cost is small relative to the protection.

## What to ask when shopping

Questions to ask any insurance broker quoting you:

1. Does this policy cover guests' bodily injury during their stay?
2. Does this policy cover damage caused by guests, including malicious damage?
3. Does this policy cover loss of rental income if the property becomes uninhabitable?
4. What's the deductible for STR-related claims vs. non-STR claims?
5. Are there any caps specific to STR claims that don't apply to non-STR claims?
6. Are guest pets covered if they cause damage?
7. What about claims related to amenities (hot tub, pool, fire pit)?
8. Is the coverage primary or secondary to platform-provided protection like AirCover?
9. What's the claim process? How long do claims typically take?
10. What's the renewal pattern? Can the rate go up significantly year-over-year?

A broker who can't answer these crisply isn't well-versed in STR insurance. Find one who is.

## Documentation for claims

When something happens that might trigger a claim:

1. **Photograph everything immediately** — damages, conditions, scene.
2. **Notify your insurer within 24 hours.** Even if you're not sure you'll file, the notification preserves your right to do so.
3. **Get written statements from any witnesses.**
4. **Collect contact information from the guest** before they leave.
5. **Document any medical attention** the guest received (for injury claims).
6. **Don't admit fault or make settlement offers** without consulting your insurer.

A claim handled correctly often resolves quickly. A claim handled poorly — late notification, missing documentation, ad hoc settlements — can result in denied coverage even when the incident itself was clearly covered.

# Chapter 9 — The six-question call to your city

The single most useful piece of regulatory research you can do is a phone call to your city's STR officer or planning department. This chapter is the script.

## Who to call

Most cities with STR regulation have a designated point of contact:

- A specific "STR program coordinator" or "vacation rental officer" — increasingly common in regulated markets.
- The planning department's general line, where you'll be routed to whoever handles STRs.
- The business licensing office or finance department for tax-related questions.

Search "[city name] short-term rental contact" on Google. The official contact is typically listed on the city's STR page.

## When to call

After you've done initial Tier 1 research (Chapter 3). The call resolves ambiguities you've identified, not basic questions you could have answered yourself.

Going in cold ("hi, I want to start an Airbnb, what do I need to do?") wastes the officer's time and yours. Coming in prepared ("I've reviewed Section 17.32, I see that STRs are conditional uses in R-2 with the owner-occupancy exception in 17.32.040; I'm trying to confirm that...") is what produces a useful conversation.

## The six questions

A 30-minute call typically covers all six:

**Question 1:** "I've identified that my property at [address] is in zoning district [X]. I see that short-term rentals are [permitted/conditional/prohibited] in this district per Section [Y]. Can you confirm this is the current rule?"

This confirms zoning. The officer either confirms or corrects. If they correct, ask which section governs.

**Question 2:** "What's the specific permit or registration I need to apply for? What's the application process and timeline?"

This identifies the permit pathway. Ask for the form, the fee, and a typical timeline. Get specific names of departments and contacts.

**Question 3:** "Are there inspection requirements? If so, what's covered and how do I schedule?"

Inspections are often the longest part of permit timeline. Get specifics.

**Question 4:** "What lodging taxes apply, and which does the platform collect for me?"

The officer may not know platform-specifics, but they should know what taxes apply at the city level. For state and county, they can usually direct you to the right contact.

**Question 5:** "Are there any operational rules I should know about — caps on guest occupancy, parking requirements, noise rules, neighbor notification?"

These are often in the code but easy to miss. The officer's quick summary saves you re-reading the code section by section.

**Question 6:** "Are there any pending changes to STR rules I should be aware of? Anything the council or commission is considering for the next 12 months?"

This is the most valuable question. Officers often know about pending changes well before they're public. Hearing "we're considering an owner-occupancy requirement" or "the council is reviewing a cap" lets you plan accordingly.

## What to bring to the call

- A list of your specific questions in writing.
- Your property's address and parcel ID.
- Notes from your Tier 1 research (zoning section numbers, permit references).
- A pen and paper (or notes app) — the call moves fast.
- Patience — officers handle hundreds of these calls; some are friendlier than others.

## After the call

Within 24 hours, write a summary of what you learned, including:

- The specific rules confirmed or corrected.
- The permit type and timeline.
- The lodging tax requirements.
- Any pending changes.
- The officer's name and contact info for follow-up.

This summary is part of your due-diligence file. If a question arises later, you have a written record of what you were told.

If something the officer said contradicts what your CPA, attorney, or research found, follow up. Sometimes officers are wrong; sometimes your earlier sources were wrong. Either way, the written summary lets you triangulate.

## When the call doesn't help

Some cities are uncooperative or under-resourced. The officer may not return calls, may give vague answers, or may not know basic facts.

In those cases:

- Try email instead. Some officers are better via email.
- Ask for a different officer or supervisor.
- Submit a public records request for relevant guidance documents.
- If the city is genuinely opaque, hire a local attorney for a 1-hour consultation.

A 1-hour attorney consultation costs $300–$500. It's expensive compared to a free phone call but cheap compared to operating without clear regulatory standing.

# Chapter 10 — When to hire a lawyer

Most STR regulatory research can be handled without an attorney. Some can't. This chapter covers when professional help is genuinely worth the cost.

## You probably don't need an attorney for

- Basic zoning research where the code is clear.
- Permit applications where the form is standard.
- Lodging tax registration and routine filings.
- Standard HOA covenant review.
- Standard insurance shopping.

The companion worksheet plus the framework in this manual handles all of these for most operators.

## You probably do need an attorney for

**1. Genuinely ambiguous zoning.** Where the code is unclear about whether STRs are permitted in your district, and the city won't give you a definitive answer in writing.

**2. Disputes with HOAs or COAs.** When a homeowners' association is moving against your operation, an attorney helps with response strategy, negotiation, or litigation.

**3. Buying STR-permitted properties in tight markets.** Where the permit's transferability and conditions are deal-critical, an attorney's review of the permit and sale documents is worth it.

**4. Writing or reviewing custom rental agreements.** For direct bookings, especially in jurisdictions with strict consumer-protection rules, a lawyer-reviewed rental agreement protects you.

**5. Responding to enforcement actions.** When the city sends a violation notice, cease-and-desist, or fine, do not respond without an attorney. Anything you say in response can affect your position.

**6. Operating across multiple jurisdictions.** When your portfolio spans states with different rules, an attorney with multi-jurisdictional experience helps build consistent operating practices.

**7. Setting up business entities for STR portfolios.** LLC formation, multi-member arrangements, S-corp elections — these benefit from attorney involvement.

**8. Sales and dispositions.** When selling a property, especially with operating STR business, legal review of the sale documents and operational handover protects you.

## How to find the right attorney

For STR-specific work, look for:

- **Local STR attorney specifically.** A real estate attorney without STR experience often misses the operational nuances.
- **Documented STR clients.** Ask for references or examples.
- **Familiarity with the specific city.** A San Francisco STR attorney isn't the same as a New Orleans STR attorney.
- **Reasonable hourly rates.** $300–$600/hour is typical for experienced STR attorneys; higher in major cities.

Avoid:

- Generic real estate attorneys without STR depth.
- Attorneys whose practice is primarily transactional (closings only).
- Out-of-state attorneys for local regulatory questions.

## Cost expectations

Typical engagement costs:

- 1-hour consultation: $300–$600.
- Permit application review and assistance: $500–$2,000.
- HOA dispute representation: $2,000–$10,000+ depending on complexity.
- Litigation defense: $5,000+ and rising fast.

For most operators, a 1-hour consultation plus occasional follow-up (totaling $500–$1,500 per year) is the right level of legal involvement. Major issues warrant more; routine compliance shouldn't require ongoing legal spending.

## When the attorney's advice is "you can't operate"

Sometimes the answer to legal research is no. The property is in a zone where STRs aren't allowed, or the HOA bans them, or the operational requirements would make the property unprofitable.

This answer is hard to hear after you've bought the property. But it's better to hear it from your attorney than from a city enforcement officer six months in. A no-go answer is information you can act on — sell the property, pivot to long-term rental, or wait for the rules to change.

The most expensive mistake is operating against unclear rules and getting caught. The next-most-expensive is hiring an attorney to interpret rules that are clearly negative and ignoring the answer.

## What's next

You've reached the end of the manual. The framework you have now:

- The four regulation categories (plus insurance) that exhaust most U.S. STR rules.
- A reliable source ranking for research.
- A reading-the-code methodology.
- A research workflow that takes about 90 minutes per property.
- The six-question call script.
- A decision rule for when to hire an attorney.

The companion Permit Research Worksheet is filled in property-by-property. Run through it for each STR you operate or are considering. Save the completed worksheet with your other property documents.

Regulatory research isn't a one-time exercise. Cities update rules. HOAs change boards. State legislatures pass STR preemption laws. A quarterly check on each property's regulatory status — 10 minutes per property — keeps you ahead of changes that might otherwise blindside you.

# Jurisdiction case study

A worked example of the framework applied to a hypothetical property in a moderately-regulated market:

**Property:** 3-bedroom single-family home, suburban Denver, Colorado.

**Zoning research:** Property is in R-2 (low-density residential). Denver Municipal Code Section 11.4 covers STRs. Section 11.4.020 defines short-term rental as "any rental of a residential dwelling for less than 30 consecutive days." Section 11.4.030(B) lists STRs as conditional uses in R-2, with an exception in 11.4.030(C) for primary-residence operators with annual operating-day caps.

**Outcome:** The property is the owner's primary residence. The exception applies. STRs allowed without CUP, subject to 90-day operating-day cap and registration.

**Permit research:** STR registration required (Section 11.4.040). Application is online via denvergov.org/str. Initial fee $100; annual renewal $50. Required documents: proof of insurance ($1M minimum), local responsible person, floor plan. Approval typically 7–14 days.

**Tax research:** City of Denver Lodgers Tax: 11.75% (per Denver Revised Municipal Code Chapter 53). Airbnb collects and remits this automatically per agreement. Colorado state tax (2.9% sales tax + state lodging surcharge): also collected by Airbnb. No additional county tax in Denver County. Direct bookings would require host registration with city.

**Private restrictions:** Property has no HOA. Title check shows no relevant deed restrictions. Subdivision homeowners' association exists for shared roads but does not restrict residential use.

**Insurance research:** Existing State Farm homeowner's policy explicitly excludes commercial use. Quoted Proper Insurance STR-specific policy at $2,400/year for $1M liability and full property damage coverage. Adding $1M umbrella from existing carrier at $400/year additional.

**Six-question call:** 25-minute conversation with Denver STR program coordinator. Confirmed all of the above. Mentioned that the city is not currently considering changes for 2026 but that an owner-occupancy verification process may be added in 2027.

**Decision:** Operate as STR. Apply for registration. Switch insurance. Track operating days against 90-day cap.

This profile takes about 2.5 hours of total work plus the 30-minute call. The insurance and registration applications add another 3–5 hours over a 2-week period.

# Glossary

**APN.** Assessor's Parcel Number. Unique identifier for a property in the county records.

**CC&Rs.** Conditions, Covenants & Restrictions. The legal document that establishes HOA rules.

**CUP.** Conditional Use Permit. Required for uses that aren't "by right" in a zoning district.

**HOA / COA.** Homeowners' Association / Condominium Owners' Association. Private associations enforcing covenants.

**Lodging tax / TOT.** Transient Occupancy Tax. Tax on short-term rental revenue.

**Master association.** A larger association sometimes overlaid on individual HOAs in a planned community.

**Overlay zone.** A zoning designation layered on top of base zoning, modifying rules.

**Owner-occupancy requirement.** A rule requiring the host to live at the property as a primary residence.

**Permit transfer.** Whether an STR permit moves to a new owner with the property.

**Preemption.** When a higher-level law (state) overrides a lower-level rule (HOA or city).
