# Chapter 1 — Why this manual exists

There's a specific failure mode that ruins more STR loophole claims than any other.

It isn't a misunderstanding of the 7-day rule. Most owners have heard about that and most know whether their property qualifies. It isn't filing on the wrong schedule. CPAs with even modest STR experience get that right.

It's material participation. Specifically: failing to prove it.

The IRS doesn't usually challenge whether a property's average stay was 7 days. They have the booking data. They can compute the average themselves. What they challenge is whether you actually spent the hours you claim, doing the work you claim, and whether anyone else was doing more.

When that challenge comes, the difference between winning and losing is the documentation in front of the auditor.

This is a manual about that documentation. About the seven tests, ranked by which actually matter for STR owners. About the kinds of activity that count and the kinds that don't. About how spouses, cohosts, cleaners, and contractors affect your test math. About the contemporaneous log format that survives a tax court challenge. And about what to do if the year is half-over and you haven't been keeping records.

## The stakes

Material participation is the gatekeeper between two outcomes:

- **You materially participate.** The carve-out from rental-activity classification holds. Your STR's losses are active. They flow through your tax return and reduce your W-2 income. A typical year-one combined loss (depreciation, cost seg, operating expenses) might be $40,000–$80,000 — savings of $13,000–$30,000 in real tax dollars at typical brackets.

- **You don't materially participate.** The 7-day rule still kept you out of "rental activity" status, but you've fallen into the broader passive activity rules under Section 469. Your losses suspend. They don't go away — they carry forward and get used in some future year — but they don't help your current tax bill at all.

That difference, in the year you're reading this, is the difference between a $30,000 tax refund and a tax bill you didn't expect.

## What the test is, in one sentence

The IRS regulation defining material participation — Treas. Reg. § 1.469-5T(a) — gives seven tests. You only need to pass one. They are not equally accessible. Most STR owners pass via Test 1, Test 2, or Test 3.

The seven, briefly:

1. **More than 500 hours** in the activity.
2. **Substantially all participation** — your hours = essentially all hours by anyone.
3. **More than 100 hours, not less than anyone else.**
4. **Significant participation activity** — niche, rarely matters for a single STR.
5. **Materially participated 5 of last 10 years** — useful when scaling back in later years.
6. **Personal service activity** — doesn't apply to STRs.
7. **Facts and circumstances**, with more than 100 hours — the catch-all, used cautiously.

Tests 1, 2, and 3 do the work for almost everyone. The next several chapters take each one apart.

## Why the IRS cares

The reason this is the most-challenged part of the loophole strategy comes down to incentive.

The 7-day rule is mathematical. The IRS computes your average from your booking data and either you're under 7 or you're over. There's no judgment involved.

Material participation, by contrast, depends on facts that only you have. Did you spend 130 hours on the property this year? Maybe. Did your cleaner spend 35 hours? Maybe. Did you keep records that prove it, or are you reconstructing from memory in February? The IRS has every reason to assume the worst on these questions, because they can't see what actually happened. Your records are their only window.

When the records are good, the IRS rarely challenges. When the records are weak — vague descriptions, suspicious round numbers, hours that look reconstructed after the fact — the audit-loss rate is striking. Tax court has decided dozens of these cases over the past five years and the pattern is consistent: weak documentation loses.

This manual is about being on the right side of that pattern.

## What you'll learn

By the time you finish:

- You'll know exactly which of the three viable tests is the easiest path for your situation.
- You'll know which activities count toward your hours and which the IRS will strike as "investor activity."
- You'll have a contemporaneous log format you can run all year in 30 seconds per entry.
- You'll have an audit-defense binder structure you can assemble in two hours when you need it.
- You'll know what to do if you're catching up on a year you didn't track.
- You'll know how spouse hours, cohost hours, and contractor hours interact with each test — and which combinations are dangerous.

The manual is short on theory and long on what to actually do. Open the companion hours-log spreadsheet alongside this — much of what's described here is built into the template.

Let's start with the seven tests.

# Chapter 2 — The seven tests, in depth

The Treasury regulations define material participation with seven tests, listed in Treas. Reg. § 1.469-5T(a). You only need to pass one to qualify. This chapter walks through all seven, ranked by relevance for STR owners, with the practical mechanics of each.

If you've read TAX-01, you've seen this list briefly. Here, we go further.

## Test 1 — More than 500 hours

The plain text of Test 1: "The individual participates in the activity for more than 500 hours during such year."

That's about 9.6 hours per week, every week. Or two solid days every month. Or any combination that adds up.

For a single self-managed STR, 500 hours is achievable but not casual. A typical week looks like:

- 3–4 hours guest communication (messaging across multiple bookings, walkthroughs, troubleshooting)
- 2 hours of cleaning if you do your own turnovers
- 2 hours of pricing review, calendar management, and listing optimization
- 2 hours on quarterly maintenance, supply runs, and seasonal projects (averaged across weeks)

That's 9–10 hours, hitting the 500 threshold over 52 weeks.

If you outsource cleaning, the math gets harder. A 2-hour cleaning load × 30 turnovers per year is 60 hours that disappear from your column and appear in someone else's. Depending on what else fills the gap, you might fall to 250–300 hours total. That's plenty for Test 3 if no one else exceeds you, but not enough for Test 1.

Test 1 is unambiguous when it applies. Either your hours total exceeds 500 or it doesn't. There's no relative comparison to anyone else's work, and no judgment about whether your participation was "substantial enough." 500.01 hours wins.

The downside: 500 hours is a real commitment. Most owners with full-time W-2 jobs and one STR don't naturally hit it. The next two tests are more accessible paths.

## Test 2 — Substantially all participation

The plain text: "The individual's participation in the activity for the taxable year constitutes substantially all of the participation in such activity of all individuals (including individuals who are not owners of interests in the activity) for such year."

In English: your hours represent essentially all of the hours that anyone — you, your spouse, your cleaner, your contractor, your bookkeeper, your cohost — spent on this activity over the year.

The regulation doesn't define "substantially all" with a specific percentage. Tax court has generally treated it as 95% or more.

For Test 2 to apply, the operation has to be very lean on outside help. Most owners who pass it do their own cleaning, do their own maintenance, and outsource almost nothing. A typical example:

- Owner: 280 hours (does all guest communication, all cleaning, all pricing, all maintenance, all bookkeeping)
- Cleaner: 0 hours (none used)
- Contractor: 8 hours (one HVAC service call)
- Bookkeeper: 0 hours (owner does the books)

Total non-owner hours: 8. Owner share: 280 / 288 = 97.2%. Substantially all.

Now imagine the same owner hires a cleaner for $80 per turnover and does 30 turnovers in the year:

- Owner: 220 hours (no longer doing cleaning)
- Cleaner: 60 hours (2 hours × 30 turnovers)
- Contractor: 8 hours

Owner share: 220 / 288 = 76.4%. Not substantially all. Test 2 fails. (Test 3 would still pass — covered next.)

The lesson: Test 2 is most useful for self-cleaning owners. The moment you outsource a significant repetitive task, the substantially-all percentage drops below 95%.

## Test 3 — More than 100 hours, not less than any other individual

The plain text: "The individual participates in the activity for more than 100 hours during such year, and such individual's participation in the activity for the taxable year is not less than the participation in the activity of any other individual (including individuals who are not owners of interests in the activity) for such year."

Two requirements: more than 100 hours, AND your hours are at least as many as anyone else's.

Notice: this isn't "more than the total of everyone else combined." It's a one-on-one comparison. If you're at 150 hours and your cleaner is at 60 and your bookkeeper is at 12 and your contractor is at 8, you pass — because your 150 is greater than the cleaner's 60 (the highest individual contributor).

Test 3 is the workhorse for partially-outsourced STR operations. You can hire help, just not more help from any one person than you yourself put in.

Where Test 3 breaks: hiring a property manager. PMs typically log 200–400 hours per year on a property they manage actively. If you're at 80 hours and your PM is at 280, Test 3 fails.

We'll cover Test 3 in detail in Chapter 3 — it's worth a chapter of its own.

## Test 4 — Significant participation activity

The plain text: "The activity is a significant participation activity for the taxable year, and the individual's aggregate participation in all significant participation activities during such year exceeds 500 hours."

A "significant participation activity" is one in which you participated more than 100 hours and met no other test. The regulation lets you aggregate across multiple such activities.

For most STR owners, Test 4 doesn't add anything Test 1 didn't already provide — if you're at 500+ hours total across activities, you'd be at 500+ hours on the property if it's your main one. The exception is if you operate multiple unrelated businesses (each over 100 hours but each under 500), and want to combine. That's outside what most readers face.

## Test 5 — Materially participated 5 of last 10 years

The plain text: "The individual materially participated in the activity for any 5 taxable years (whether or not consecutive) during the 10 taxable years that immediately precede the taxable year."

This is the "scale-back" test. Once you've materially participated for 5 of the prior 10 years, you're treated as materially participating in subsequent years even if your hours drop.

It's relevant when you're winding down operational involvement after years of running the property hands-on. Useful for retiring owners. Not relevant for new STR owners trying to qualify in their first or second year.

## Test 6 — Personal service activity

The plain text: "The activity is a personal service activity, and the individual materially participated in the activity for any 3 taxable years preceding the taxable year."

A personal service activity is one in which capital is not a material income-producing factor — law, accounting, medicine, consulting, performing arts. Real estate doesn't qualify. Test 6 doesn't apply to STR rentals.

## Test 7 — Facts and circumstances

The plain text: "Based on all of the facts and circumstances, the individual participates in the activity on a regular, continuous, and substantial basis during such year." With a constraint: this test only applies if you spent more than 100 hours and no other individual participated for more hours doing managerial-type work.

Test 7 is the catch-all. It exists for situations where a taxpayer's pattern of involvement is clearly material but doesn't fit neatly into Tests 1–6.

Tax court has been cautious about Test 7 — the regulation requires more than 100 hours plus genuinely substantial involvement, and a "manager-type" actor (paid or unpaid) can't have done more managerial work than you. In practice, if you can pass Test 7, you can usually pass Test 3 with cleaner documentation.

## Ranking for STR owners

The honest ranking, by usefulness for the typical STR loophole filer:

1. **Test 3 (100+ hours, not less than anyone)** — the most accessible test for owners with a property manager, cleaner, or any consistent outside help. Most readers' default.

2. **Test 2 (substantially all participation)** — the default for self-cleaning, hands-on owners. Cleaner audit story than Test 3 because the numbers are usually unambiguous.

3. **Test 1 (500+ hours)** — the default for owners running the property as a primary or near-primary occupation.

4. **Test 5 (5-of-10-year carryover)** — relevant only after you've already materially participated for 5+ prior years.

5. **Tests 4 / 7** — niche.

6. **Test 6** — never applies.

The next three chapters take Tests 3, 1, and 2 in detail.

# Chapter 3 — Test 3: The 100-hour-and-not-less test

Test 3 is the backbone of most STR material participation claims. It's the test that lets owners hire help — cleaners, bookkeepers, even part-time cohosts — without losing the loophole, as long as the owner remains the largest single contributor of hours.

## The two requirements

To pass Test 3, both must be true:

1. You spent more than 100 hours on the activity during the tax year.
2. No other individual spent more hours on the activity than you did.

Both requirements have to be true. 100 hours alone isn't enough. Beating everyone individually isn't enough if you're still under 100. You need both.

## The arithmetic

Pulled together as a yearly check: list every individual who did any work on the property (you, your spouse if she's helping with the property, your cleaner, your bookkeeper, your contractor, your cohost, your handyman). Tally each one's hours. Your number has to be (a) over 100 and (b) at least as high as the largest of theirs.

A worked example:

- You: 180 hours (guest comm 80h, maintenance 30h, supply runs 25h, pricing/marketing 25h, bookkeeping 20h)
- Spouse: 0 hours (not involved)
- Cleaner: 56 hours (2 hours × 28 turnovers)
- Bookkeeper: 12 hours (monthly check-ins)
- Plumber: 4 hours (one service call)

Your hours: 180. The next-highest individual: cleaner at 56. You pass — 180 > 56, and 180 > 100.

Now add a property manager:

- You: 80 hours (you handed most of it off)
- PM: 240 hours (handles guest comm, dispatches everyone)
- Cleaner: 56 hours
- Plumber: 4 hours

Your hours: 80. PM: 240. You fail Test 3 on both counts (under 100, and less than PM).

## "Not less than" is one-on-one

Test 3 doesn't compare your hours to the *total* of everyone else's. It compares your hours to *each* individual contributor.

If you have 200 hours, your spouse has 80, the cleaner has 75, and the bookkeeper has 40, you pass. Your 200 is greater than each of 80, 75, and 40 individually. Even though their combined hours are 195 — almost matching yours — Test 3 doesn't care about combined hours. It cares about whether anyone individually exceeds you.

This is what makes Test 3 reliable for owners with multiple contributors. The math doesn't care that you have many helpers — only that no single one is doing more work than you.

## "At least as much" — the tie case

Subtle point: the regulation says "not less than" the participation of any other individual. That means equal participation passes. If you're at 180 and your spouse is also at 180, Test 3 still works (you're not less than her, and she's not less than you — both pass).

This is rarely the relevant case in practice, but it's the answer to the question CPAs sometimes dodge. "What if my spouse and I are doing the same hours?" Both pass independently.

## Spouse hours combine for the threshold

A wrinkle: if you and your spouse file jointly, the rules let you combine your hours for material participation. So even if you individually have only 80 hours and your spouse has 90, your combined 170 is what matters for the 100-hour requirement.

The combined-hours treatment is in Treas. Reg. § 1.469-5T(f)(3). It applies regardless of which spouse holds title or which is on the LLC operating agreement. Filing jointly is the only requirement.

This is the rule that makes the loophole accessible to families where one spouse has a demanding W-2 job. The non-W-2 spouse takes the lead on the property; the combined hours qualify; the active loss treatment offsets the W-2 earner's wages on the joint return.

## What goes wrong

The most common Test 3 failure mode is the property manager problem. The owner hires a PM thinking they'll save time. The PM logs 250–400 hours per year. The owner ends up at 50–80 hours. Test 3 dies.

The second-most-common: cohost arrangements where the cohost is doing more than the owner. Cohosts on Airbnb's platform often handle messaging, dispatch, and many guest issues. If you've handed those tasks to a cohost while doing the bookkeeping yourself, the cohost likely has more hours than you.

Third: cleaner-driven failures. If you have a property with frequent turnovers (60+ per year) and you outsource all cleaning, the cleaner's hours can grow large. A 2-hour-per-turnover × 70 turnovers = 140 cleaner hours. If your operational hours are only 120, the cleaner exceeds you.

## Fixing a Test 3 problem

If you're realizing mid-year that your hours are below your PM's or your cleaner's, you have several options.

**Option A: take the cleaning back.** Self-cleaning is the single biggest hours-shifter in an STR operation. Reclaiming 100+ hours by doing your own turnovers makes Test 3 pass on its own for many operators.

**Option B: take messaging back.** Guest communication is high-volume and time-intensive. If your PM or cohost has been handling it, taking it back yourself shifts hours dramatically.

**Option C: scope back the help.** If your PM contract is full-service and that's the problem, renegotiate to a co-host model where they handle defined tasks (only the messaging, only the dispatch) and you handle the rest.

**Option D: live without the loophole this year.** If the structural fixes won't work for your situation, accept the result. Your loss suspends; it doesn't disappear. You use it next year when your structure has changed.

The wrong move: continuing as you are while logging hours that don't match reality. The audit risk and penalty risk on inflated logs are far worse than just missing a year of the loophole.

# Chapter 4 — Test 1: The 500-hour test

The 500-hour test is conceptually the simplest of the seven. You spent more than 500 hours running the property. You materially participate. Done.

In practice, this test is harder than it sounds because 500 hours is a meaningful commitment. Most W-2 earners with a single STR can't reach it casually. Owners who do typically share a few characteristics:

- They self-clean (recovers 100+ hours that would otherwise go to a paid cleaner).
- They self-manage all guest communication (not outsourced to a cohost).
- They handle their own minor repairs (not deferred to a contractor).
- They operate at high volume — frequent turnovers, busy seasons that demand full-time involvement during peaks.
- They live within an hour of the property (eliminates remote-management friction).

If you fit that profile, Test 1 is your default and the easiest test to defend. The records show 500+ hours and the conversation ends.

## What 500 hours looks like

Roughly 9.6 hours per week, every week. Or 12 hours per week with three weeks off. Or a heavy season (April–September) with 15 hours per week and a slow season at half that.

A 500-hour year for a single self-managed STR might break down as:

- Guest communication and inquiries: 150 hours (3 hours/week)
- Cleaning and turnover: 120 hours (2 hours × 60 turnovers)
- Maintenance and supply runs: 80 hours
- Pricing, calendar, and listing management: 60 hours
- Bookkeeping: 40 hours
- Marketing and listing improvements: 30 hours
- On-site time for projects (paint, repairs, improvements): 20 hours

Total: 500 hours. Many self-managed owners exceed this comfortably; some who think they're "active" actually don't, because they outsource cleaning and end up trimming 100+ hours that would have put them over.

## Why Test 1 is preferred when you can hit it

Three reasons.

**1. Audit story is unambiguous.** Test 1 doesn't depend on comparing your hours to anyone else's. It doesn't depend on a "substantially all" judgment. It's a single number against a single threshold.

**2. Documentation requirements are simpler.** You don't need to track non-owner hours at all if Test 1 is your test (though good practice is to track them anyway, in case a different test becomes relevant). For Tests 2 and 3, knowing your cleaner's hours or your bookkeeper's hours is critical.

**3. Resilience to surprises.** If your PM's contract turns out to have included more hours than you knew, or your cohost was busier than they reported, Tests 2 and 3 can collapse. Test 1 doesn't care.

## Why Test 1 isn't always the right pursuit

If your structure naturally produces 200–300 hours, you're not going to hit 500 by inflating logs. Trying to claim 500 when the truth is 250 is the fastest way to lose an audit and pick up a fraud penalty.

For owners who structurally fall in the 100–300 range, Test 3 is the right path. For self-cleaners who run lean operations, Test 2 is often easier. Test 1 is the natural fit only when the underlying activity actually produces 500+ hours.

## Documentation specific to Test 1

For Test 1, the contemporaneous log is your sole proof. The format covered in Chapter 7 applies, with one extra emphasis: at 500+ hours, the log will be long. A typical year produces 200–300 entries. Make sure each entry is specific and the time durations are realistic (no 12-hour days listed routinely; suspicious patterns trigger scrutiny).

A common audit tactic: the auditor picks 5 random log entries and asks for corroborating evidence. A receipt, a timestamped message, a photo. If you can produce evidence for each, the rest of the log is generally accepted. If you can't — if the entries describe work that left no trace anywhere — the auditor extrapolates and strikes large portions of the log.

Build in evidence as you go. Save photos with timestamps when you do maintenance. Keep receipts for supply runs. Save email exports for guest communication. These supplements make Test 1 unassailable.

# Chapter 5 — Test 2: The substantially-all test

Test 2 is the cleanest path to material participation for hands-on, self-cleaning, low-outsourcing STR owners. When it applies, the audit story writes itself: "I did basically all the work; the only outside help was an HVAC tech and an annual inspection." There's nothing to argue about.

When it doesn't apply, it gets confusing fast — and people sometimes claim it when they shouldn't.

## What "substantially all" means

The regulation doesn't define substantially all with a specific percentage. The phrase is intentionally fuzzy. Tax court decisions and IRS practice have settled on something close to 95% — meaning your hours represent at least 95% of all hours that anyone (you, your spouse, contractors, cleaners, bookkeepers, anyone) spent on the activity.

Some practitioners use 90% as their internal threshold; others use 95%; the IRS hasn't published a number. The conservative approach is to aim for 95% and document carefully.

## Who passes Test 2

The owner profile that passes Test 2 cleanly:

- You self-clean every turnover. Cleaning is typically the largest single source of non-owner hours; eliminating it eliminates the biggest risk to "substantially all."
- You handle all guest communication directly. No cohost, no virtual assistant.
- You manage all bookings, pricing, and calendar work yourself.
- Your only outside help is for one-off specialized work — an electrician for an actual electrical problem, an HVAC tech for the annual service, a tax preparer for the return.

This profile typically produces 200–400 owner hours per year and 5–25 non-owner hours per year. Owner share: 95–98%. Solidly substantially all.

## Who doesn't pass Test 2

If any of these are true, Test 2 is at risk:

- You hire a regular cleaner for turnovers.
- You use a cohost or property manager for messaging or dispatch.
- You have a regular bookkeeper doing monthly work.
- You contract out routine landscaping, pool service, or property monitoring.
- You operate at high enough volume that even small per-turnover help adds up to a meaningful percentage.

A representative case where Test 2 fails:

- Owner: 220 hours (handles all communication, pricing, maintenance, and books)
- Cleaner: 70 hours (2 hours × 35 turnovers)
- Bookkeeper: 15 hours

Owner share: 220 / 305 = 72%. Not substantially all. Test 2 fails. (Test 3 still works in this case — owner exceeds each individual contributor.)

## The trap of overclaiming Test 2

A specific failure mode worth flagging: owners who outsource cleaning but tell themselves "the cleaner only spends a few hours, that's not really significant." The regulation doesn't have a "not significant" exception. All non-owner hours count toward the denominator of "substantially all."

If you have any meaningful outsourced help, you should default to Test 3 unless you can document hours that genuinely satisfy the 95% threshold. Don't assume Test 2 just because you're "the main one running the place."

## When to claim Test 2 vs. Test 3

If you self-clean and your outside help is minimal (a few service calls per year), Test 2 is often the cleaner audit story.

If you have any regular outside help — a regular cleaner, a regular bookkeeper, anyone consistently logging more than 1% of total hours — claim Test 3 instead. Test 3's "not less than any other individual" framework is more durable when there are multiple contributors.

A pragmatic rule: if your owner-hours percentage is 95%+, claim Test 2. If it's 85–95%, claim Test 3 (which is easier to defend at that participation level). Below 85% but with no individual contributor exceeding you, still Test 3 — that's its sweet spot.

## Documentation for Test 2

For Test 2 specifically, you need to track *all* non-owner hours, not just yours. The denominator matters. If you can't account for the cleaner's hours, the bookkeeper's hours, the handyman's hours, you can't compute the substantially-all percentage. The audit failure mode is: "I think I did most of it, but I'm not sure how many hours my cleaner did." The auditor assumes the worst.

The companion hours-log spreadsheet has a "contributor" column for tracking this. Every time someone other than you spends time on the property, log it: date, hours, who, what they did. Cleaner invoices usually note the time spent (if not, ask). Contractor work orders typically include hours. Bookkeeper time is usually billable and tracked. The data is there if you ask for it.

# Chapter 6 — Hours that count and hours that don't

The IRS draws a sharp line between "operational" hours (which count toward material participation) and "investor" hours (which don't). Many owners log activity that falls on the wrong side of this line, then have those hours struck during an audit.

This chapter walks the line carefully.

## The regulation's exclusion

Treas. Reg. § 1.469-5T(f)(2)(ii) — the "investor activity" exclusion — says an individual is not treated as participating in an activity if their work consists of:

> activities of a type generally performed by an investor in the activity, including (A) studying and reviewing financial statements or reports on operations of the activity, (B) preparing or compiling summaries or analyses of the finances or operations of the activity for the individual's own use, and (C) monitoring the finances or operations of the activity in a non-managerial capacity.

The regulation creates an important exception: investor-type activity does count if you're "directly involved in the day-to-day management or operations of the activity." So a CEO reviewing financials counts; an absentee owner reading the same reports doesn't.

For an STR owner who's actively running the operation, most genuine work activity counts. The exclusion is aimed at activity that any absentee owner would do — checking on the investment, not running the business.

## Activities that clearly count

These are unambiguously operational and survive any audit scrutiny:

- **Guest communication.** Messaging with guests, calls, walkthroughs, addressing complaints during stays.
- **Cleaning and turnover work.** When you do it personally — stripping beds, doing laundry, restocking, vacuuming, mopping.
- **Maintenance and repairs.** Personally performed work like fixing a leaky faucet, replacing a thermostat, patching drywall, painting.
- **Supply runs.** Trips to Costco, hardware stores, Target for property supplies, with mileage logged.
- **Property monitoring.** On-site visits to check the property between bookings, walk-through inspections, inventory checks.
- **Vendor coordination, when active.** Calling a plumber, scheduling them, being on-site for the work, paying them.
- **Marketing the listing.** Photography (or photo direction), writing or editing the listing description, posting to social media for the property, channel management.
- **Calendar and pricing decisions.** Making active pricing changes, blocking dates, accepting/declining inquiries.
- **Bookkeeping for the property.** When you personally enter expenses, reconcile statements, categorize transactions.
- **Direct involvement in approving cleaner work.** Reviewing the cleaner's report, addressing issues, paying invoices after verification.

## Activities that clearly don't count

These are investor-type activities and get struck:

- **Reading reports about the property's performance.** Even your own reports.
- **Studying the broader STR market.** Researching ADR trends, occupancy benchmarks, competition pricing.
- **Watching educational content.** Webinars, YouTube videos, podcasts about STR operations or tax strategy.
- **Looking at potential additional properties to buy.** Even if you're considering scaling.
- **Reading tax law or industry analysis.** Including this manual.
- **Casual conversation with friends about your property.** Even if it sometimes covers operational topics.
- **Reviewing your own analysis without acting on it.** Producing a P&L summary for yourself; building a model of next year.
- **Travel to/from real estate seminars or conferences.**

## The fuzzy middle ground

Between clear-counts and clear-doesn't is a band where context matters. Some examples:

**Reviewing your bookkeeper's monthly report.** If you're actively working through it, identifying anomalies, questioning entries, making decisions based on what you see — operational. If you're glancing at it and filing it — investor.

**Driving to and from the property for operational work.** Counts. The regulation treats travel time directly tied to operational work as part of the work itself.

**A phone call with your CPA about the property.** Mostly investor activity. The call isn't day-to-day management; it's a meeting about strategy. Some of it might count if you're making operational decisions during the call, but most of the call is excluded.

**Setting up the property's bookkeeping system.** Counts. System setup is operational infrastructure work.

**Negotiating an insurance renewal.** Counts. Active vendor management for the property's operations.

**Browsing Airbnb to look at other properties for ideas.** Investor activity. Even if you're studying for ideas to apply.

**Photographing the property after a renovation.** Counts. Marketing work.

**Sending the cleaner the next month's calendar so they know when to clean.** Counts. Active coordination.

The pragmatic rule: when in doubt, ask whether the activity *produces an output that the operation requires.* Maintenance produces a working faucet. Cleaning produces a clean property. Reviewing a report produces nothing operational unless you act on the review. The "produce an output" test catches most of the line cases correctly.

## Side-by-side examples

Two log entries, one operational, one investor, on a similar topic:

**Operational:** "2026-09-12 14:00–14:45 (0:45). Reviewed Hospitable revenue dashboard for September pacing. Identified that two competitor properties dropped prices last week. Adjusted my listing pricing for the next 14 days down by 8%; saved screenshot of competitor data."

**Investor:** "2026-09-12 14:00–14:45 (0:45). Reviewed Hospitable revenue dashboard for September pacing. Read STR market trends article."

Same time, same dashboard. The difference is what came out of the activity. The first produced a pricing change — operational. The second produced reading material — investor.

Lean toward the operational pattern. Make sure your log entries describe outputs, not just inputs.

## The "primarily" test for mixed activities

If a single activity has both operational and investor elements, the regulation effectively asks whether the activity was "primarily" operational. A 90-minute session that included 70 minutes of doing the books and 20 minutes of reading an industry article is primarily operational; log all 90 minutes.

A 90-minute session that was primarily reading articles, with 5 minutes of actually changing something, is primarily investor; don't log it (or log only the 5 operational minutes).

When the split is genuinely 50/50, log only the operational portion. The audit risk of overclaiming is far worse than the participation cost of underclaiming.

# Chapter 7 — The contemporaneous log: gold-standard format

The single most important document in your loophole filing is the hours log. Everything else — depreciation, cost seg, expense detail — has supporting documents that originate elsewhere (statements, receipts, invoices). The hours log originates with you, and it's the document the IRS scrutinizes hardest in a material participation audit.

This chapter walks through the format that survives audit, with ten sample entries showing the right level of detail.

## Field structure

A defensible log entry has six fields:

1. **Date** (YYYY-MM-DD format).
2. **Start time and end time** — actual clock times, not just "afternoon."
3. **Duration** — calculated from start/end (helps the auditor verify, also catches your own typos).
4. **Activity** — what you did, specifically.
5. **Business purpose** — why this activity was operational, in one phrase.
6. **Contributor** — who did the work. Almost always you, but field exists so non-owner hours (cleaner, contractor) can also be logged.

Optional fields that strengthen entries:

- **Evidence reference.** "Receipt #1843" or "Email thread to guest [name] in archive" or "Photo IMG_2847 in 09-12 folder."
- **Property** (if you have multiple).
- **Mileage and expenses** if applicable to the activity.

The companion hours-log spreadsheet is built around this field structure.

## What "specific" means

The audit-defense difference between a strong entry and a weak entry is specificity. Compare:

- **Weak:** "Worked on listing"
- **Strong:** "Updated Airbnb listing description to highlight new fire pit; added 4 photos taken yesterday; rewrote the 'amenities' section to include 'pet-friendly' filter; saved revisions"

The strong version tells the auditor what happened, in language that produces a verifiable output. The weak version produces nothing they can verify. Auditors strike weak entries regularly.

A good practical test: read your entry and ask, "Could a stranger reproduce roughly what I did from this entry alone?" If no — the entry is too vague.

## Tools — pick one

Three options, all work, pick one:

**Google Sheets** (or Excel). The companion template is built for this. One row per entry, columns for the fields above. Free, works on any device, easy to export. Most owners use this.

**Toggl** (or Clockify, Harvest). Time-tracking apps with timestamp precision and clean exports. The downside: their description fields are often shorter than ideal for audit-quality narrative. Use the longer notes feature.

**Paper journal**. Old-fashioned but defensible. Each session entered in real time. Yearly, type up the entries into a spreadsheet for math. The downside: harder to search and aggregate.

The mistake: switching tools mid-year. A log split across Toggl, Google Sheets, and a notebook is harder to defend than any one used consistently.

## When to log

Same day. Within hours of doing the work, ideally. The IRS standard is "contemporaneous," which tax court has interpreted as "made at or near the time of the activity." End-of-week catch-up logging is acceptable but not ideal. Monthly catch-up is risky. Year-end reconstruction is failure.

A practical habit: at the end of every session that touched the property — guest call, cleaning, maintenance, anything — open the log and add a row. Takes 30 seconds. Saves you hours of reconstruction stress later.

## What if you missed entries

If you've gone two weeks without logging and now want to catch up, here's the protocol:

1. Pull your channel manager's message timestamps. Each guest message you sent or received is contemporaneous evidence of operational work, with a timestamp.
2. Pull your calendar. Any property-related appointments (vendor visits, blocked dates for personal projects) are contemporaneous.
3. Pull your bank/card statements. Property-related transactions imply operational work that day.
4. Reconstruct entries from these external timestamps. Note in the entry: "Reconstructed 2026-04-22 from Airbnb message thread + Home Depot receipt."

What's reconstructible from external evidence is defensible. What isn't — pure memory — should not be entered.

# Chapter 8 — Spouse, cohost, and contractor hours

The participation tests get interesting fast when more than one person is involved with the property. This chapter walks through the rules for combining and comparing hours across the people commonly involved in an STR.

## Spouse hours combine

If you and your spouse file a joint return, the tax code lets you combine your hours for purposes of every material participation test. This is the rule that makes the loophole genuinely accessible to households with one heavy W-2 earner.

The combination rule (Treas. Reg. § 1.469-5T(f)(3)) applies regardless of:

- Which spouse owns the property on title.
- Which spouse is on the LLC's operating agreement, if there is one.
- Which spouse's name is on the listing.
- Whether either spouse is on the property's mortgage.

The only requirement is that you file jointly.

In practice: a W-2-earning spouse working 50+ hours per week at their day job typically can't accumulate enough STR hours to materially participate alone. Their spouse, with more flexible time, can. Combined, they qualify; the active loss treatment offsets the W-2 earner's wages on the joint return.

This is the design pattern most STR loophole filers use. Either:

- One spouse is the "STR runner" (full hours), the other is the W-2 earner (full job, minimal STR time but combined hours pass).
- Both spouses split STR work and combine hours.

Document each spouse's hours separately even though they combine. The audit story is stronger when you can show "spouse A did 80 hours of guest comm; spouse B did 90 hours of maintenance and supply runs; combined 170 hours operational" rather than "the family did 170 hours of stuff."

## Cohost hours

A "cohost" on Airbnb's platform is someone you've granted certain access to your property's operations — typically guest messaging, calendar management, and pricing decisions. They're often paid a percentage of revenue. Cohosts vary widely in scope, from someone who handles a few specific tasks to a full property-management arrangement.

Cohost hours are non-owner hours, which means they count against you in Tests 2 and 3.

A typical cohost arrangement: 15–25% of property revenue, with the cohost handling all guest communication, dispatching cleaners, and managing the calendar. For a property doing $80,000 in revenue, that's $12,000–$20,000 of cohost services per year, which typically corresponds to 200–400 cohost hours.

If your cohost is logging 250 hours per year and you're at 80, Test 3 fails — the cohost exceeds you.

The fix, as with property managers: take work back, scope down the cohost, or accept that the loophole isn't your strategy this year.

A nuance specific to cohosts: many cohost arrangements have hours that vary widely by season. Summer is heavy; winter is light. If you're trying to assess Test 3 mid-year, project the cohost's full-year hours, not just the year-to-date.

## Property manager hours

A full-service property manager (PM) is the most common Test 3 killer. PMs typically log far more hours than the owner because they're handling the day-to-day work that defines the operation.

Industry estimates put a typical full-service PM at 250–400 hours per year on a single property they manage actively. This includes:

- Guest communication (~30% of total).
- Dispatch and oversight of cleaners and contractors (~20%).
- Calendar and pricing management (~15%).
- On-site visits and inspections (~10%).
- Bookkeeping and reporting (~10%).
- Marketing and listing maintenance (~10%).
- Miscellaneous (~5%).

If you're hiring a PM, model the hours before you commit. A 350-hour PM means you'd need 350+ hours yourself to remain the largest individual contributor. That's a tall order for a hands-off owner.

PMs sometimes resist providing detailed hours data ("we work on retainer, not hourly"). For the audit defense, you need it anyway. A reasonable approach: ask the PM for a monthly hours report or a categorized time accounting. Some PMs provide this as a standard service. Others will balk. If they balk, that's information about the audit defensibility of your filing.

## Cleaner hours

Cleaners are the second-most-common Test 2/3 killer for hands-on owners. The math is simple: 2 hours per turnover × 60 turnovers per year = 120 cleaner hours. If your operational hours are 150, the cleaner is at 80% of yours — significant for Test 2, fine for Test 3.

For Test 2, having a regular cleaner usually means you can't claim "substantially all." Default to Test 3.

For Test 3, the cleaner becomes a problem when your hours are below theirs — typically owners with 80–120 hours and cleaners with 100+. The fix: take cleaning back, or take more operational work back to push your hours over the cleaner's.

A common shortcut that doesn't work: hiring a cleaner via a turnkey cleaning service (like Tidy or MaidThis), claiming they're a "service vendor" not an "individual," and arguing their hours don't count. The regulation says hours of "any other individual" count, including individuals working through a service provider. The cleaner is still an individual, even if you pay through their employer.

## Contractor and specialist hours

One-off contractor visits are usually fine. An HVAC tech who came once for an annual service, a plumber for a single repair, an electrician for a one-time issue — each typically logs 1–4 hours per year. Even ten of these visits add up to maybe 20–40 contractor hours, well below most owner totals.

Where contractors become a Test 3 problem: a specific contractor who does recurring work. A weekly landscaper at the property is logging 50–80 hours per year just on landscaping. That's a single individual whose hours might exceed yours if you're outsourcing other categories too.

## Bookkeeper and CPA hours

These are usually small enough to be irrelevant. A bookkeeper doing monthly reconciliations might log 12–24 hours per year on your property. A CPA preparing the return logs 4–10 hours. Neither typically exceeds owner hours, so they don't threaten Test 3.

For Test 2, every hour counts toward the denominator. A bookkeeper at 18 hours and a CPA at 6 hours adds 24 hours of non-owner participation. If your owner hours are 250, that's still 91% — possibly below the substantially-all threshold, depending on interpretation. Default to Test 3 in that case.

## A worked example with multiple contributors

A typical multi-contributor STR operation:

- Owner: 200 hours
- Spouse: 90 hours (combined: 290)
- Cohost: 60 hours (handles part-time messaging on weekends)
- Cleaner: 70 hours (35 turnovers × 2 hours)
- Bookkeeper: 18 hours
- HVAC tech: 4 hours

Test 2 analysis: 290 / 442 = 65.6%. Not substantially all. Fail.

Test 3 analysis: 290 (combined spouse hours) > 70 (largest single individual contributor) > 100 minimum. Pass.

Test 1 analysis: 290 < 500. Fail.

Default test for this structure: Test 3, as it's the only passing test.

Important nuance: the spouse hours combine for the threshold but are still listed individually for the comparison test. The "largest single individual contributor" comparison is each spouse's individual hours (200 owner + 90 spouse listed separately) vs. each non-owner. In this case, the owner alone (200) exceeds the cleaner (70), so Test 3 passes even without spousal combination. With combination, Test 3 is even stronger.

The contributor framework matters because it reveals which structural changes would help. In this case, taking back the cohost work (eliminating the 60 cohost hours) wouldn't matter — Test 3 already passes. Self-cleaning (eliminating the 70 cleaner hours) wouldn't matter for Test 3 either, but would push Test 2 to 290 / 372 = 78% — still not substantially all. Test 2 isn't a viable claim for this structure.

Identifying the binding constraint is what the contributor analysis is for.

# Chapter 9 — Audit-defense protocol

If your loophole filing is selected for audit — and the IRS has been auditing STR loophole claims at meaningfully higher rates since 2022 — your records become the entire conversation. This chapter covers what the IRS asks for, what your binder should look like, and how to defend each entry.

## What an STR loophole audit looks like

The first contact is typically a notice (Letter 566 or similar) requesting documentation related to your rental real estate position. The notice asks for some combination of:

- The property's average period of customer use calculation.
- Documentation of material participation, including a "log or time records."
- Substantiation of expenses claimed.
- Cost segregation report, if one was used.
- Form 4562 detail.

The auditor is testing whether your claim of active loss treatment is supported. If the documentation is strong, the audit closes quickly. If it's weak, the audit becomes adversarial, and the auditor may expand to other tax years or other items on your return.

Response timing matters. The notice typically gives you 30 days to provide documentation. Asking for an extension is fine and usually granted. Showing up late with weak records is the worst combination.

## The audit-defense binder

Hand the auditor a single PDF binder, organized in this order:

### Section 1 — Executive summary (1 page)

A one-page cover sheet with:

- Property address and basis.
- Average period of customer use (the number, with calculation).
- Material participation test passed (Test 1, 2, or 3).
- Total operational hours.
- Largest non-owner contributor hours.
- Personal-use days for the year.
- Net taxable loss.

This page lets the auditor see your case at a glance.

### Section 2 — The 7-day calculation (1–2 pages)

- A page showing total rental days, number of rental periods, and the average.
- An attached CSV export from your channel manager (Airbnb / Vrbo / OwnerRez) showing every booking.
- A note on calculation methodology if not obvious.

### Section 3 — Hours log (the main document)

- Sorted by date.
- Each entry showing the six fields covered in Chapter 7.
- Total hours summed at the bottom, broken down by category (cleaning, maintenance, guest communication, etc.).
- Non-owner hours listed separately, with totals per individual contributor.

For Test 3 claims, include a comparison table at the end: "Owner hours: 187. Largest non-owner contributor (Cleaner): 56. Pass."

### Section 4 — Supporting evidence

For 5–10 selected log entries (a representative sample, not every entry), include corroborating evidence:

- Receipts dated to the entry's date.
- Channel manager message exports for guest-comm entries.
- Photos with EXIF timestamps.
- Mileage log entries.
- Vendor invoices.

You don't need to corroborate every entry. The auditor will spot-check; representative coverage is sufficient.

### Section 5 — Cost segregation (if applicable)

- Full cost segregation study report.
- Form 4562 detail.
- Reconciliation of bonus depreciation calculations.

### Section 6 — Personal-use day documentation

- A list of every day you (or family) stayed at the property.
- For each day, brief notation of business or personal purpose.
- Total tally relative to the 14-day cap.

### Section 7 — Property P&L

- The companion workbook's year-end summary.
- Income detail by month.
- Expense detail by category.

### Section 8 — Narrative (1–2 pages)

A short narrative explaining how the property is operated, who does what, and why the hours claimed are operational rather than investor activity.

This narrative is what most filers skip and what most auditors find helpful. It's your one chance to humanize your filing — to give the auditor a sense of the actual operation rather than just numbers on a page.

A good narrative covers:

- How you found the property and your strategy for it.
- How you run the operation day to day (cleaning approach, communication approach, vendor relationships).
- What hours you spent on what activities, in plain language.
- Why your operation looks the way it does (why you self-clean, or why you use a cleaner, etc.).

Two pages, written like an essay rather than a memo. Conversational tone.

## Defending individual entries

The auditor may pick specific entries to challenge. Common challenges and responses:

**"This entry says 'reviewed Q3 P&L for 90 minutes.' That sounds like investor activity."**

Response: "The review identified an issue with the cleaner's invoice billing — the September 14 turnover was double-charged. I emailed the cleaner immediately to correct it; the email is attached." (Defending the entry as operational because it produced an output.)

**"Your log shows 4 hours of cleaning on April 3. Is that consistent with the property's size?"**

Response: "Yes — the property is 1,400 square feet, two bathrooms, three bedrooms. A standard turnover including laundry takes 3–4 hours. The cleaner's standard time for the same property is 2 hours per turnover; the difference is that I include laundry, restocking, and inspection in my time, while their hourly invoice covers only the cleaning portion."

**"This entry says you drove to the property for 1 hour 18 minutes on April 3 to let in the HVAC tech. Why couldn't the cleaner have done it?"**

Response: "The cleaner wasn't scheduled that day, and the HVAC tech needed someone with full property knowledge to be present in case access issues arose. The trip is logged with mileage and the receipt for the service is attached."

The pattern: defend operational logic with a specific reason, evidence, or both. Don't get defensive. The auditor isn't trying to prove fraud; they're trying to understand whether the entry holds up. Help them.

## When the audit goes badly

If the auditor proposes adjustments — striking entries, reclassifying losses as passive, denying part of the depreciation — you have escalation paths:

1. **Discuss with the auditor's manager.** Many auditors are reasonable; if yours isn't, the manager often is.
2. **Request appeals.** The IRS Appeals office is independent of the audit team and looks at the case with fresh eyes. Most disputed audits are resolved at appeals favorably to the taxpayer when the documentation is reasonable.
3. **Tax court.** If appeals doesn't resolve, you can petition tax court. This is when professional representation becomes essential — at this point you're litigating, and the cost-benefit shifts dramatically.

Most loophole audits don't reach appeals, much less tax court. Reasonable documentation plus a reasonable narrative settles the vast majority at the auditor level.

## Pre-audit hardening

Don't wait for an audit to assemble the binder. Run a "self-audit" annually — typically December or January — where you assemble the binder as if you'd been notified. The exercise reveals weak entries, missing evidence, and gaps you can fix while the records are still fresh.

Many practitioners recommend the self-audit as a year-end ritual. It takes 2–4 hours. It pays dividends if the IRS ever calls.

# Chapter 10 — Quarterly and year-end reconciliation rituals

Material participation isn't a year-end exercise. It's a year-round one. The owners who lose audits typically did the right work — but didn't keep track of it as it happened. By February, the records are gone or fragmentary.

This chapter is about the rituals that prevent that. Repeatable checkpoints — quarterly and year-end — that turn material participation from a stressful pre-filing scramble into a routine.

## The quarterly checkpoint (15 minutes)

Once per quarter, on a calendar reminder, do this:

**1. Pull your hours log for the quarter.** Look at it as if you're an auditor. Are entries specific? Do they describe operational outputs? Any entries that look reconstructed or vague? Strike or revise as needed.

**2. Project your annual hours.** Year-to-date hours × 4 = projected annual. If you're below the threshold for your test, you have time to fix it. If you're tracking well, confirm the trajectory.

**3. Update the contributor table.** Pull each non-owner contributor's hours: cleaner invoices, bookkeeper time entries, any cohost data. Add to your log. Compare against your hours. If anyone's tracking ahead of you, identify the structural fix.

**4. Run the 7-day calculation.** Pull your booking export for the quarter. Calculate average period of customer use year-to-date. If it's creeping up, plan accordingly.

**5. Tally personal-use days.** Even a few days off-track can compound. Review and project to year-end.

15 minutes per quarter. 60 minutes per year. Saves the December panic.

## The October dress rehearsal (2 hours)

In late October, do a more thorough version:

**1. Run through Chapter 5 of TAX-01 (or the equivalent year-end checklist).** Item by item. What's done; what's pending.

**2. Calculate your projected year-end position.** Total hours, owner share, average stay, personal-use days, projected income and expenses.

**3. Identify any structural fixes needed.** If your test is at risk, October is your last realistic window to fix it. November is too late for most structural changes.

**4. Confirm vendor records.** Cleaner invoices for the year, bookkeeper time, contractor visits. Each should map to entries in your log or contributor table.

**5. Check the 14-day personal-use cliff.** If you're at 11–12 personal-use days, plan November and December carefully.

## The year-end reconciliation (4 hours, in early January)

Once the calendar year is complete:

**1. Final hours total.** Sum your operational hours for the year. Compare to projection. Annotate any unusual increases or decreases.

**2. Final contributor totals.** Each non-owner contributor's full-year hours, with supporting evidence (cleaner invoices, bookkeeper records, etc.).

**3. Final 7-day calculation.** Total rented days, total rental periods, average. Pass or fail.

**4. Final personal-use day count.** Confirm under 14 (or 10% of rented days, whichever is greater).

**5. Material participation test confirmation.** Which test passes? Document the math.

**6. P&L finalization.** Income and expenses totaled and categorized. Depreciation calculated.

**7. Build the audit-defense binder.** Even if you're not being audited, build it now. The information is fresh; assembly is fast.

**8. CPA handoff package.** Cover summary, hours log, P&L, depreciation detail, supporting documentation. Send to your CPA in a single PDF.

4 hours of work in January saves 20 hours of stress in March.

## The "self-audit" ritual (annual, 1 hour)

Every year, after the year-end reconciliation:

Pretend you've received an audit notice. Open the audit-defense binder you just assembled. Read it as a stranger.

- Does Section 1 (executive summary) make sense?
- Does Section 2 (7-day calculation) work?
- Does Section 3 (hours log) hold up entry by entry?
- Pick 5 random entries from the log. Can you produce supporting evidence?
- Read the narrative. Does it tell a coherent story?

Where the answer is no, fix the binder before filing. Strengthen the narrative. Add missing evidence. Strike weak entries. Better to clean up now than to have an auditor find the gaps in 18 months.

## Why the rituals matter

Material participation cases at tax court rarely turn on whether the work happened. They turn on whether the records prove it. The owners who win are the ones who treated documentation as part of the business — daily logs, quarterly reviews, year-end reconciliation, annual self-audit. Not as a year-end task to dread.

15 minutes per quarter. 2 hours in October. 4 hours in January. 1 hour for the self-audit. About 8 hours per year, total.

The amount of money at stake — typically tens of thousands of dollars in tax savings — is wildly out of proportion to the time these rituals take. They're the single highest-leverage habit an STR owner can build.

**Audit-defense binder.** The organized PDF package presented to an IRS examiner during a material participation audit. Structure covered in Chapter 9.

**Contemporaneous log.** A record of operational hours made at or near the time of the activity (same day, ideally). Tax court interpretation requires same-day or very-near-same-day entries to satisfy the contemporaneous standard.

**Contributor.** Anyone other than the owner who spends hours on the property's operation: spouse, cohost, cleaner, contractor, bookkeeper. Hours from contributors count against the owner in Tests 2 and 3.

**Investor activity.** The carve-out of activity types that don't count toward material participation under Treas. Reg. § 1.469-5T(f)(2)(ii). Includes reading reports, market research, education, looking at potential other properties.

**Operational hours.** Hours spent on activities that produce outputs the operation requires: cleaning, communication, maintenance, marketing, active vendor coordination.

**Substantially all.** The threshold for Test 2. Not numerically defined in regulation; tax court has used 95%+ as the practical threshold.

**Test 1 / Test 2 / Test 3.** Common shorthand for the three material participation tests most accessible to STR owners: 500 hours; substantially all participation; 100 hours and not less than any other individual.

For terms shared with TAX-01 (passive activity, material participation, Section 469, etc.), see the TAX-01 glossary.

# FAQ

**Do I need an app to track hours?**

No. A Google Sheet kept open in your browser works. The companion hours-log template is set up exactly the way auditors expect to see it. Apps like Toggl or Clockify also work — pick one and stick with it.

**Does my cleaner's hours count toward my participation?**

No. Cleaner hours are non-owner hours. They count *against* you (in the denominator of Test 2 and as a comparator in Test 3), not for you.

**What if I forgot to log hours all year?**

Reconstruct what you can from external evidence: channel manager message timestamps, calendar entries, receipts with dates, vendor invoices. Document the reconstruction methodology. Don't fabricate entries. Start logging contemporaneously immediately. A partial real log plus reconstructed evidence is better than a fabricated complete log.

**Does spouse hours combination work if only one of us is on the property's title?**

Yes. The combination rule (Treas. Reg. § 1.469-5T(f)(3)) is about filing jointly, not about title. The spouse not on title can still contribute hours that combine with the owner-spouse's for material participation.

**Can I pass material participation in my first partial year of ownership?**

Yes — the tests apply to the tax year, not the full year of ownership. If you bought the property in October and ran it actively for the remaining 90 days, you can hit Test 3 (100+ hours) in that timeframe. Test 1 (500+ hours) is harder in a partial year but possible if you're hands-on.

**Does an LLC change anything for material participation?**

No. The tests are about your activity, not about the entity holding title. Single-member LLC, multi-member LLC, S-corp, sole proprietor — all flow through to your individual material participation tests.

**Can I claim hours for time my property manager spent if they were "on my behalf"?**

No. The hours count to whoever spent them. Hiring a property manager doesn't transfer their hours to you. The PM's hours are non-owner hours and count against you.

**What if my cohost is my spouse?**

Spouse hours combine for material participation (Test 1, 2, and 3) regardless of how the spouse engages with the property — cohost, manager, anything. The combination is automatic with a joint return.

**Do hours spent learning about the strategy count?**

No. Reading this manual, watching educational content, attending webinars — these are excluded as investor activity. The manual makes you better at running the property; the running counts.
