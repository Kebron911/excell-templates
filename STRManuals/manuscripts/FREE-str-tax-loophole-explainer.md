## Page 2 — A short note before we start

The first time I asked a CPA whether the "STR loophole" applied to me, I got three different answers from three different people. One was technically correct but wrong in spirit. Two were wrong outright. None gave me the actual rule in a sentence I could repeat back.

So before this PDF goes anywhere, here's the rule in a sentence:

> If your property is rented to short-term guests with an average stay of seven days or fewer, and you materially participate in running it, the IRS treats it as an active business rather than a passive rental — which means losses from the property can offset your W-2 income.

That's the whole thing. The next eight pages explain what each piece of that sentence actually means, who it works for, and the three-question test for whether you should care.

## Page 3 — Why the rule exists

In 1986, Congress got mad about wealthy people using paper losses from real estate to wipe out their wage income. The fix was a section of the tax code called the Passive Activity Loss rules — Section 469.

Here's the simplified version. The rules say that losses from "passive activities" can only offset income from other passive activities. They cannot offset wages, business income, dividends, or capital gains.

By default, the law treats rental real estate as passive **no matter how active you actually are in running it.** Even if you rebuild the kitchen yourself, vet every guest personally, and answer messages at 2 AM, the IRS still classifies your rental as passive. The losses get trapped — they "suspend" and carry forward year after year, until you generate passive income to absorb them or sell the property.

That's the default. That's what makes most rentals tax-neutral or tax-disappointing in the early years.

The Passive Activity Loss rules have a few exceptions. The one we're talking about is a quiet carve-out that says short-term rentals aren't a "rental activity" at all.

## Page 4 — The carve-out in plain English

The Treasury regulations contain a rule (Treas. Reg. § 1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii) if you want the citation) that says:

> A property is **not a rental activity** — for purposes of the passive loss rules — if its average period of customer use is seven days or fewer.

Read that twice. If your average stay is seven days or fewer, the property is not a "rental activity" under the law. It's something else — a trade or business.

Trade or business losses, if you materially participate in the business, are not passive. They're active. And active losses can offset your W-2 income.

That's the loophole. A definitional carve-out, written in 1988, that turns short-term rentals into something the law considers a business rather than a rental.

It's not a hack. It's not something Airbnb hosts discovered by accident. It's been there the whole time. Real estate accountants have used it quietly for decades, and it became famous when the rise of Airbnb made it accessible to ordinary people instead of just hotel investors.

## Page 5 — What the rule actually does

The mechanical effect, in plain English:

**1.** Your STR property generates a paper loss in year one. Most of it comes from depreciation — the IRS lets you deduct a portion of the building's value every year as an expense, even though you're not paying anything out of pocket.

**2.** With cost segregation and bonus depreciation, that paper loss can be very large — $40,000–$80,000 isn't unusual on a property that costs $400,000.

**3.** Because your STR is *not* a rental activity (avg stay ≤ 7 days) and you materially participate in it, the loss is *active*, not passive.

**4.** Active losses flow through your tax return and reduce your taxable income — including W-2 wages.

**5.** If you're in the 32% federal bracket plus 5% state, every $1 of loss is worth roughly 37¢ in actual tax savings.

A high-earner couple with $300,000 of W-2 income who buys a $400,000 STR and runs cost seg in year one can save $20,000–$30,000 on their federal tax bill that same year. That's the headline. That's why this matters.

## Page 6 — What the rule is NOT

A lot of misinformation floats around on this topic. Here are the most common ones.

**It is not the same as Real Estate Professional status.** REP requires 750 hours and more time on real estate than your day job. The STR loophole requires neither. You can have a full-time W-2 job, run one short-term rental in your spare time, and still qualify.

**It does not require an LLC.** You can run an STR as a sole proprietor and still use the loophole. Entity choice matters for liability and bookkeeping, but it does not turn the loophole on or off.

**It does not require you to "actively manage" in the colloquial sense.** It requires you to "materially participate" in the legal sense — a specific test about hours, not about how engaged you feel.

**It is not a loophole the IRS doesn't know about.** The IRS knows. The Treasury wrote the rule. Tax court has addressed it dozens of times. Audits happen, and people lose them — usually because of poor documentation, not because they were doing anything wrong.

**It does not last forever in its current size.** Bonus depreciation — the thing that supercharges the loss — is on a phase-down schedule. As of 2026, bonus is at 40%, dropping to 20% in 2027 and 0% in 2028 unless Congress changes the law. The loophole itself will still exist; the size of the year-one loss will shrink.

**It is not a substitute for a property that pencils.** Buying a bad STR purely for the tax benefit is one of the worst financial mistakes you can make. The loophole reduces your tax bill on a property you'd want to own anyway. It does not turn a money-losing property into a money-maker.

## Page 7 — The three-question test

Here's a test for whether the STR loophole is worth your time. If you answer no to any of these, you can stop reading and move on with your life.

**Question 1.** Do you have, or are you about to buy, a property that is or will be rented to short-term guests with an average stay of seven days or fewer?

If you're under 7 days on average — yes. If your typical stay is 14–30 days — no, unless you can clear the "significant personal services" version of the rule (rare; it's mostly for hosted corporate housing).

**Question 2.** Are you willing to materially participate in running it — measured in real hours, real activities, contemporaneously logged?

The most common qualifying test is "substantially all participation": your hours running the property exceed everyone else's hours combined (cleaners, cohosts, contractors). For a single STR self-managed by one owner, this is achievable if you're genuinely running the place.

**Question 3.** Do you have W-2 income, business income, or other ordinary income that would benefit from offsetting losses?

The loophole reduces what you owe. If you don't owe much to begin with, it has nothing to offset.

**Three yeses** = the loophole likely applies, and the playbook will save you a lot more than $29.

**Two yeses** = adjust your situation if you can — most often by reducing how much someone else (a cohost, a property manager) is doing relative to you.

**Zero or one yes** = the loophole probably isn't your move this year.

## Page 8 — What to do next

If you got two or three yeses on the previous page, the next steps are:

**1.** Start logging your hours, **starting today.** Date, duration, what you did, business purpose. A spreadsheet is fine. Doing this contemporaneously is the difference between a tax position you can defend and one you can't.

**2.** Pull your booking platform's data and calculate your year-to-date average stay. If you're under 7 days, you're inside Door 1. If you're between 7 and 30 days, dig deeper.

**3.** Do not buy a property purely for the tax savings. Run the actual numbers on whether the property pencils as a business first. The tax loss is a kicker on a property you'd want anyway.
