## The 30-second pitch

Most STR income is rental income — Schedule E — and rental income does NOT trigger self-employment tax. But if you provide substantial services (daily cleaning during stay, meals, concierge, transportation), the IRS reclassifies you as running an active business. That triggers Schedule C, which is subject to a 15.3% self-employment tax on top of regular income tax.

If you're not sure which schedule applies, that's the FIRST tab of this workbook. If you know you're Schedule C, the rest computes your SE tax cleanly — including the 50% above-the-line deduction most hosts miss, and the Solo 401(k) shelter that active-business filers unlock.

## FAQ

**Schedule E or Schedule C — which one am I?**
The Decision tab walks the test. If your services to guests stop at "providing the building" (like a long-term landlord), you're Schedule E. If you provide substantial services (meals, daily cleaning during stay, concierge, transportation, recreation activities) — like a hotel — you're Schedule C. STR hosts cluster on the E side; specialty operators (boutique inn, glamping resort with daily housekeeping + meals) are on the C side.

**Why is the 50% SE tax deduction "above the line"?**
"Above the line" means it reduces your AGI, which reduces your income tax base AND helps you stay under thresholds for other deductions (QBI, IRA contributions, etc.). It's claimed on Schedule 1 Line 15 of your Form 1040.

**What about the additional 0.9% Medicare?**
Kicks in at $200K single / $250K MFJ AGI. The workbook auto-applies it.

**Can I use a Solo 401(k) and contribute to a workplace 401(k) too?**
Yes — separate businesses, separate plans. Annual employee deferral limit is shared across all 401(k)s, but employer contributions are per-plan.
