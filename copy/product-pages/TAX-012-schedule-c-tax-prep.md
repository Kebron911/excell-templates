## The 30-second pitch

Most STR income lives on Schedule E — and that's where most of our templates point. But a meaningful subset of hosts file Schedule C: substantial-services operators (daily cleaning during stay, meals, transportation, concierge), boutique inns, glamping resorts with daily housekeeping, and active-business STR investors who've crossed the material-participation threshold.

Schedule C triggers self-employment tax (15.3%). It also unlocks moves Schedule E filers can't use: §179 immediate expensing, Solo 401(k) contributions up to ~$70K/year, QBI deduction with safe harbor at 250+ service hours. This workbook is the year-end tax-prep file for the active cohort. It mirrors TAX-004 (the Schedule E sibling) but adds the Schedule C-specific tabs.

## Math that earns the price

**§179 immediate expensing.** Active-business hosts can §179 furniture, appliances, equipment, and qualifying vehicles in year 1. $30K of new equipment × 24% bracket = $7,200 saved THIS year vs. ~$1,440/year for 5 years on default MACRS.

**Solo 401(k) shelter.** Up to ~$70K/year in retirement contributions on the SE earnings (combined employee + employer). On $50K shelter at a 24% bracket = $12K in current-year tax saved + tax-deferred growth.

**QBI deduction.** 20% of qualified business income, below the line, when the 250-hour service threshold is met. On $40K net business income that's $8K reduced × your bracket = real tax saved.

The trade-off is the 15.3% SE tax. The math has to work for your specific situation — this workbook shows you whether it does.

## FAQ

**Should I be Schedule C?**
The Decision tab walks the test. If you provide substantial services to guests (cleaning during stay, meals, transportation, recreation activities) — like a hotel operator — you're Schedule C. If you provide only "rental of real estate" services, you're Schedule E. The middle ground is material participation: 750+ hours/year of personal involvement in an active rental business CAN trigger Schedule C treatment for tax-strategy purposes.

**What's the 250-hour QBI safe harbor?**
Rev Proc 2019-38 created a safe harbor for rental real estate to qualify as a §199A trade or business. Requires 250+ hours of "rental services" annually (you, employees, or contractors), separate books per property, and specific documentation. Most active STR operators clear it easily; the workbook tracks the hours quarterly so the year-end claim is defensible.

**Will the IRS push back on Schedule C?**
Filing C without substantial services or documented material participation is a reclassification risk on audit. The workbook's Hospitality Services tab is your audit-defense paper trail. If your services genuinely match Schedule C, the audit risk is low. If they don't, file Schedule E.

**Material participation hours — do I really need 750/year?**
For aggregate "real estate professional" status under §469(c)(7): yes, plus more than 50% of personal services in real estate. For SCH C reclassification specifically: 750 isn't a magic number — but you do need clear material participation (100+ hours AND more than anyone else, or 500+ hours, or substantially all participation). Talk to your CPA before filing if you're between thresholds.
