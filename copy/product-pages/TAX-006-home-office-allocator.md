## The 30-second pitch

Your laundry room handles cleaner pickups, supply staging, and your iPad while you manage bookings. Your dining table is where you reconcile receipts and chase contractor W-9s. There's a dedicated corner of your office that exists only for managing the STR.

The IRS allows a home office deduction for any space used regularly + exclusively for the business. Most STR hosts qualify. Most don't take it because the math feels intimidating, the simplified method feels too small, the actual method feels too risky.

This workbook runs both methods side-by-side, picks the higher one, and produces the audit-defense documentation a CPA or auditor would want. A 200 sqft home office at the simplified rate is $1,000/year, every year, on autopilot.

## FAQ

**Will this trigger an audit?**
Audit rates on the home office deduction haven't been elevated since 2014. The deduction itself doesn't flag your return. What DOES flag is taking it incorrectly — claiming a kitchen table or a guest-room/office hybrid. The workbook keeps you in the safe zone.

**Simplified or actual method?**
Run both — that's what the Comparison tab is for. Simplified is capped at $1,500. Actual can be much higher for hosts with high indirect expenses, but it requires more record-keeping and adds depreciation recapture if you later sell the home.

**Does it work for renters?**
Yes — same regular+exclusive test, except your "rent" replaces "mortgage interest + property tax + depreciation" as the indirect expense.

**What about a "principal place of business" test?**
For STR hosts, the home office IS your principal place of business if that's where you handle bookings, accounting, and contractor coordination. Pub 587 §"Principal Place of Business" walks the test; the workbook embeds it.
