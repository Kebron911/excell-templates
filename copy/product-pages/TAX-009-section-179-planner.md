## The 30-second pitch

You bought $18,000 of furniture for the new cabin in February. Default IRS treatment spreads that deduction over five years — $3,600/year. Section 179 lets you deduct the full $18,000 this year. At a 24% bracket, that's $4,320 in tax saved THIS April instead of $864 spread over five Aprils.

The catch: §179 has rules. Active-business test. Annual cap. Property-class limits (no real property — that's bonus depreciation territory). Vehicle weight thresholds. This workbook walks the rules, shows what qualifies, and produces the Form 4562 numbers ready to drop into your tax return.

## FAQ

**Schedule E or Schedule C — does it matter for §179?**
Yes. §179 requires an "active trade or business." Pure Schedule E rental activity generally doesn't qualify; you need either Schedule C OR Schedule E with documented material participation. The Eligibility tab walks the test.

**§179 vs bonus depreciation — what's the difference?**
§179 lets you elect immediate full deduction up to an annual cap. Bonus depreciation (§168(k)) applies a percentage to qualifying property automatically (currently phasing down — verify current rate). Many hosts use both: §179 first up to the cap, bonus on the rest. The Three-Way Comparison shows you the optimal play per asset.

**Can I §179 the building?**
No. Real property (buildings) doesn't qualify for §179. Use 27.5-year SL (TAX-002 P&L tracker) or accelerate via cost segregation (TAX-010).

**What's "listed property"?**
Vehicles, computers, and certain other assets that have potential personal use. Listed property has a 50%+ business-use test in year 1, and recapture rules if business use drops below 50% in any future year. The Vehicle Worksheet handles the recapture math.
