## Lesson 1.2 — Substantial services & material participation

**Length:** 10 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "1.2 — Substantial Services & Material Participation"]

[VO]

Two switches change the math by tens of thousands of dollars.

Substantial services determines whether your STR is a Schedule E
landlord operation or a Schedule C hospitality operation. Material
participation determines whether your rental losses can offset your
W-2 income.

Both are facts-and-circumstances calls, both are routinely
mis-applied, and both have decade-spanning consequences if you get
them wrong.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. The substantial services test under §1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A).

Two. The seven material-participation tests under §1.469-5T(a).

Three. The two tests that actually matter for STR hosts.

Four. How to score yourself today.

[TEACH — 5 min]

Substantial services.

Treas. Reg. §1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A) names "substantial services" as
one of the criteria that pushes a rental into hospitality territory.
The regulation does not give a numeric threshold. It describes the
character of services.

Substantial services include, in the IRS's example list:

  Daily housekeeping or cleaning during the guest's stay
  Meal service
  Concierge or personal-attention services
  Tour booking
  Transportation provided to the guest

Services that are NOT substantial:

  Turnover cleaning between stays
  Mid-stay messaging or troubleshooting
  Welcome basket or local recommendations
  Linen changes only between stays
  Standard Wi-Fi, utilities, basic amenities

The pattern is "during" versus "between." Hotels provide services
during the stay. Landlords provide services between stays.

Most STR hosts run between-stay operations. They are landlords with
a short average rental period — not hotels. Schedule E.

Material participation.

§1.469-5T(a) lists seven tests. Meeting any one establishes material
participation in an activity.

Test 1. The individual participates in the activity for more than
500 hours during the year.

Test 2. The individual's participation constitutes substantially
all of the participation in the activity (including non-owners).

Test 3. The individual participates for more than 100 hours, AND
no other individual participates more.

Test 4. The activity is a "significant participation" activity (>100
hours), and the sum of significant-participation activities exceeds
500 hours.

Test 5. The individual materially participated in 5 of the 10
preceding years.

Test 6. The activity is a personal service activity AND the
individual materially participated in any 3 prior years.

Test 7. Based on all facts and circumstances, the individual
materially participates regularly, continuously, and substantially.

The two practical tests for STR hosts are Tests 1 and 3.

Test 1 — 500 hours — is the cleanest. If you log 500+ hours of
participation across the year, you've met the test. For a host with
3+ properties or who is heavily involved, this is achievable.

Test 3 — 100 hours and most participation — is the realistic test
for hosts with cleaners, handymen, and platform-managed bookings.
The host needs more participation hours than any single other
person involved. Cleaners typically work 50-150 hours per year per
property; if you log 200 hours and your cleaner logs 120, you
qualify under Test 3.

[DEMONSTRATE — 4 min]

Score yourself.

Open the substantial-services scoring worksheet from your bonus
folder.

[Walk through the worksheet.]

Question 1. Do you provide daily housekeeping during guest stays?
Yes / No.

Question 2. Do you provide meals to guests? Yes / No.

Question 3. Do you operate a concierge desk or arrange tours? Yes
/ No.

Question 4. Do you provide transportation? Yes / No.

Question 5. Do you provide hotel-style amenities (room service,
spa)? Yes / No.

Most STR hosts answer all five "no." Substantial services do not
apply. Schedule E.

Material participation. Open the time-log worksheet.

[Walk through.]

Estimate your annual hours by category:

  Guest communication (pre-stay messaging, mid-stay support,
   post-stay reviews):                              ____
  Vendor coordination (cleaners, handymen,
   contractors):                                   ____
  Property visits and walkthroughs:                ____
  Bookkeeping and tax work:                        ____
  Pricing and revenue management:                  ____
  Marketing (listing edits, photography decisions): ____
  Education (this course, tax pro consults):       ____
                                                   ────
  Annual total:                                    ____

Compare to 500 (Test 1). Compare to 100+ "more than anyone else"
(Test 3).

If you're between 100 and 500 and worried about Test 3 — get
specific about who else is participating. Your cleaner's hours.
Your handyman's hours. Your property manager's hours, if any. The
test asks whether you participate more than any single other person.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Score yourself on both worksheets this week.

If substantial services applies, you may be filing on Schedule C —
verify with the Schedule E vs. C decision tree from Lesson 1.1 and
discuss with your CPA at the next handoff.

If material participation is uncertain, start the time log today
(see Lesson 4.3 for the four-log structure). The log doesn't fix
the past; it builds the substantiation forward.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 1.3 — Ordinary and necessary

**Length:** 8 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "1.3 — Ordinary and Necessary"]

[VO]

Lavish or extravagant is the line. Most hosts never get close to it.

Hosts under-deduct because they imagine the IRS examiner asking
"was this really necessary?" — and they imagine the answer is "no."
Wrong question. Wrong answer.

§162 of the Internal Revenue Code allows deductions for ordinary
and necessary expenses paid or incurred during the taxable year in
carrying on any trade or business. The bar is much lower than
hosts assume. The bar is "ordinary in your industry, necessary for
your operation."

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. The §162 ordinary-and-necessary test, plain English.

Two. The lavish-or-extravagant ceiling — and why most STR
expenses are nowhere near it.

Three. Five expenses, five verdicts.

[TEACH — 4 min]

The test, plain English.

§162(a) allows deduction for ordinary and necessary expenses paid
or incurred in carrying on any trade or business. The Treasury
Regulations and case law have given each word a working meaning.

Ordinary means common and accepted in the industry. Photographing
your STR listing? Ordinary — every host does it. Hiring a virtual
assistant for guest messaging? Ordinary in 2026, increasingly. A
gold-plated key fob for the front door? Not ordinary; ornamental.

Necessary means appropriate and helpful for the business. Note: not
indispensable. The IRS's test is "appropriate and helpful," and
courts have generally accepted that hosts know their own business
better than examiners do.

A repair that prevents a future problem is necessary. A welcome
gift that improves reviews is necessary. A subscription to PriceLabs
that improves revenue is necessary. None of these are
indispensable; all are appropriate and helpful.

The lavish-or-extravagant ceiling.

§274(k) and §1.162-2(a) prohibit deduction of expenses that are
"lavish or extravagant under the circumstances." The classic
examples involve five-figure dinners, private-jet travel, exotic
vacations dressed up as business retreats.

For STR hosts running operating expenses on a portfolio, almost
nothing approaches this ceiling. A $1,200 industrial vacuum for a
ten-thousand-square-foot estate rental? Reasonable. A $40
cleaning supply order at Costco? Obviously fine.

The exception: §274(k) puts a 50% limit on meal deductions
generally. Per-diem and overnight-travel meals get the 50% — see
Lesson 3.6.

[DEMONSTRATE — 3 min]

Five expenses. Five verdicts.

Expense 1. $340 paint and supplies for Cabin A bedroom walls.
Verdict: ordinary and necessary. §162 deduction.

Expense 2. $1,250 professional photoshoot for new listing.
Verdict: ordinary and necessary. §162 marketing deduction.

Expense 3. $87 Costco run that included $32 of cleaning supplies and
$55 of personal groceries.
Verdict: $32 ordinary and necessary. The $55 fails not on
"ordinary and necessary" but on the §262 personal-expenses
prohibition. Allocate at line-item.

Expense 4. $497 STR tax course (this one).
Verdict: §162 + §1.162-5. Education that maintains or improves
existing trade-or-business skills. Ordinary and necessary for an
operating host.

Expense 5. $4,200 family vacation at a resort, with one afternoon
spent walking competitor properties to "research the market."
Verdict: NOT ordinary and necessary. The primary purpose was
personal vacation. The afternoon of "research" doesn't make the
$4,200 deductible. Don't try this.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

List three expenses you've been uncertain about. Bring them to the
community channel — we run a Wednesday-evening clinic where Daniel
walks through ambiguous expenses with the cohort.

If your CPA has been disallowing things you think should be
deductible, also bring those. Sometimes the CPA is being conservative
to a fault. The cohort discussion has surfaced multiple deductions
that were being legitimately missed.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 1.4 — The Recordkeeping Pact, audited

**Length:** 10 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "1.4 — The Recordkeeping Pact, Audited"]

[VO]

Three rules underwrite forty-seven deductions.

The book introduced the Recordkeeping Pact in the front matter:
log it as you go, keep the supporting documents, back it up. The
course pressure-tests it.

These three rules are not stylistic. They are the operational
substance of §6001's contemporaneous principle. Hosts who follow
them survive examinations. Hosts who don't, don't.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. Rule one — Log it as you go. Why ninety minutes a week beats
three weekends in March.

Two. Rule two — Keep the supporting documents. The §274(d)
categories that demand strict substantiation.

Three. Rule three — Back it up. The three-two-one principle for
tax records.

[TEACH — 6 min]

Rule one. Log it as you go.

Every transaction, every trip, every decision — captured within the
week, not the month, not the quarter, and not in March of next year.

The science behind this rule isn't complicated. Memory degrades
exponentially. A receipt photographed within 48 hours, paired with a
workbook entry the same week, retains the context — what the receipt
was for, why it was bought, which property it served. The same
receipt reconstructed five months later loses two of those three
context items.

§6001 requires "sufficient" records. §1.6001-1 elaborates that the
records must be sufficient to substantiate the entries on the
return. Tax courts have generally interpreted "sufficient" to
include a contemporaneous element — records made at or near the
time of the underlying event.

Reconstructions sometimes survive. They survive worse than
contemporaneous logs.

Rule two. Keep the supporting documents.

For most expense categories, "keeping the document" means a vendor
invoice or receipt for any expense over $75. The de-minimis safe
harbor under §1.263(a)-1(f) does not eliminate the receipt
requirement; it just lets you expense rather than capitalize.

For §274(d) categories — vehicle, travel, meals, lodging, gifts —
the substantiation rule is strict. Receipts AND a contemporaneous
log entry containing amount, time, place, and business purpose. No
exceptions for small amounts.

The strict substantiation under §274(d) is the most commonly
missed standard. Hosts photograph receipts but don't log purposes.
The IRS examiner asks for purpose; the host has none. The
deduction disallows.

The Audit Dossier Template (Bonus, Module 5.2) builds the file the
right way the first time. Keep the documents in the right folder
structure from the start of the year, not at year-end.

Rule three. Back it up.

Three copies. One workbook. One cloud. One local. Monthly cadence.

The rule is from Lesson 2.8 — but the principle deserves its own
mention here as part of the frame, not just the operational layer.

The §1.6001-1 retention period is three years standard, six years
if substantial omission, indefinite for fraud. Practical retention:
seven years from filing, with backups in three locations on two
different media with one off-site copy.

The day the workbook is corrupted is not the day to discover you
have no backup.

[DEMONSTRATE — 3 min]

A clean log versus a reconstructed log.

[Show two screenshots side by side.]

The clean log. TAX-001 mileage, week of April 14 through 20. Three
trips. Each entry: date, start odometer, end odometer, miles,
business purpose ("Cleaner orientation visit, Cabin A"; "Plumber
meeting, Cabin B re master bath valve"; "Property walk-through,
Cabin C"). Each entry timestamped within 48 hours of the trip.

The reconstructed log. Same three trips. Reconstructed in March of
the following year. Date column has approximate dates — "April? Maybe
April 15-ish?" Odometers reconstructed from Google Timeline GPS
distance. Business purposes vague — "Property visit" three times,
because the host couldn't remember which trip was which.

The clean log survives §274(d). The reconstructed log does not.

The fix on the reconstructed log: paired with calendar entries (the
April 15 cleaner-orientation calendar event), vendor invoices (the
plumber's April 18 invoice), and photographic evidence (the property
walk-through photos with date stamps), the reconstruction can
become defensible. Pair-and-document is more work than logging in
real time.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Photograph the last three receipts in your wallet right now.

If your wallet has no receipts, your last three credit-card
charges count. Pull up the statement, identify the three most
recent charges, and create the substantiation right now — receipt
or statement screenshot, business-purpose note, cloud folder.

This is the Recordkeeping Pact in three minutes. Three rules
underwriting forty-seven deductions.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 1.5 — Frame self-check

**Length:** 10 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "1.5 — Frame Self-Check"]

[VO]

Three questions. If you can't answer them, you're not ready to
install the system.

Module 1 closes with a self-check. Three questions on the frame —
filing form, services character, recordkeeping discipline. Each
question is binary or numeric; each answer feeds into Module 2's
capture system.

If you can answer all three, Module 2 is operational the moment it
opens. If you can't, this is the lesson to flag the gaps and
resolve them before the spine begins.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. The three questions.

Two. How to answer them — what counts, what doesn't.

Three. What to do if any answer is "I don't know."

[TEACH — 6 min]

Question one. Schedule E or Schedule C?

You answered this in Lesson 1.1 by running the decision tree on your
own portfolio. The result is one of three:

  Schedule E — short-term rental, no substantial services
  Schedule C — substantial services, hospitality character
  Mixed — properties operate differently across the portfolio

If your answer matches what you've been filing, you're done with
this question.

If your answer doesn't match what you've been filing, you have a
conversation with your CPA. The mismatch may be:

  You've been filing Schedule C and shouldn't be — likely overpaying
   self-employment tax. Consider amending if material.
  You've been filing Schedule E and shouldn't be — operating
   substantial services. May need to switch.

If you don't know your answer, your CPA may have made the
classification on autopilot when first onboarding you. Worth
revisiting.

Question two. Do you materially participate?

You answered this in Lesson 1.2 by scoring your annual hours
against the seven material-participation tests.

The two practical tests:

  Test 1: 500+ hours per year
  Test 3: 100+ hours and more than any other person

If you clear either, you materially participate. The STR loophole
is available to you (assuming average rental period ≤ 7 days). Cost
seg's acceleration loss can offset active income.

If you don't clear either, you don't materially participate. The
STR loophole is not available. Cost seg's acceleration creates a
suspended passive loss. You can still operate; the math just changes.

If your hour estimate is between 80 and 130, the count matters more.
Start the time log today (Lesson 4.3) and run it forward through
year-end. The contemporaneous log is what defends the position.

Question three. Are you logging contemporaneously?

This is the binary question. Either you are or you aren't.

You're logging contemporaneously if:

  Receipts photographed within 48 hours and stored in property-
   tagged cloud folders
  Mileage logged within the week of each trip, all four §274(d)
   elements present
  Bank/card transactions categorized at least quarterly with rule-
   library auto-classification
  Backups running monthly to two destinations

If all four are running, you're contemporaneous. Module 2 is
operational; the spine just needs the eight lessons to walk you
through tightening the workflow.

If any of the four is not running, the gap is the priority. Module 2
is where you'll close it, but flagging the specific gap now lets
you focus when the module opens.

[DEMONSTRATE — 2 min]

[Pull up a self-check worksheet on screen.]

Three questions. Three answers.

Question one — write your answer: Schedule E / Schedule C / Mixed
                                   / I don't know

Question two — write your answer: I clear Test 1 (500+ hours) /
                                   I clear Test 3 (100+ and most) /
                                   I don't clear either /
                                   I don't know my hour count

Question three — write your answer: All four contemporaneous
                                     practices running /
                                     Some running, some not /
                                     None running /
                                     Don't know

The pattern of "I don't know" answers is the gap-list for your next
two weeks.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Post your three answers in the community channel before Module 2
opens. Doesn't have to be public if you're shy — Daniel reviews
anonymized submissions and writes a Wednesday-evening summary.

If you have any "I don't know" answers, focus on resolving those
first. Module 2 is more valuable when the frame is set.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```
