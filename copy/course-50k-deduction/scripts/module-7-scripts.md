## Lesson 7.1 — Adding property #4

**Length:** 10 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "7.1 — Adding Property #4 (and Beyond)"]

[VO]

The system stops fitting on one screen. Here's the consolidation
tab.

Years one and two of the course are about installation — getting
the seven-minute tax tab running on one, two, or three properties.
At property four and beyond, the math doesn't change but the
ergonomics do. The single-property workbook becomes harder to
navigate. The roll-up needs to consolidate cleaner.

This lesson handles the scale problem.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. Why three properties is the natural break point.

Two. The Multi-Property Consolidation Tab — what it does.

Three. When to split into multiple workbooks vs. stay in one.

Four. The §469(c)(7) aggregation election.

[TEACH — 4 min]

Why three.

The course's default workbook structure assumes three property
tabs in TAX-002 — Cabin A, Cabin B, Cabin C — because three is the
median STR portfolio for the course's audience. The roll-up tab
sums across the three.

At four properties, the workbook becomes harder to navigate. Tabs
overflow visually. Cross-property comparisons require switching
back and forth.

At six properties, single-workbook approach starts to break. The
file becomes large. Performance suffers on slower laptops. Cross-
property reconciliation gets unwieldy.

The Multi-Property Consolidation Tab.

The course-exclusive consolidation tab handles four to ten
properties cleanly. It pulls from each property's workbook (or
each property's tab in a single workbook) and consolidates into:

  A unified Schedule E roll-up — line-by-line totals across all
   properties
  A per-property summary view — quick comparison of net income,
   margin, occupancy
  A consolidated mileage log
  A consolidated 1099-NEC tracker
  A consolidated audit-dossier index

For hosts running on Notion or Airtable mirrors, the consolidation
view is built natively (database with multiple property records,
filtered/grouped views).

When to split into multiple workbooks.

Default: stay in one workbook through 5-6 properties. The
consolidation tab works fine at this scale.

Split into separate workbooks when:

  You acquire a property in a different state — state filing
   simplifies if each state's properties are in separate workbooks
  You have entity-level isolation — properties owned by different
   LLCs may be cleaner in separate workbooks
  Different operating partners on different properties — separate
   workbooks let each partner own their properties

The §469(c)(7) aggregation.

When you have multiple rental properties, you can elect under
§469(c)(7)(A) to treat all your interests in rental real estate as
a single activity for purposes of material participation.

The election is made on the timely-filed return for the year of
the election. Once made, it's binding for that year and subsequent
years until revoked.

The election helps when you can't clear the 500-hour test on any
single property but can across the aggregate. It hurts if a single
property would clear material participation alone — aggregating
forces all the properties to share the test.

For most STR hosts with 3+ properties, aggregation is the right
election. Verify with your CPA at the next handoff.

[DEMONSTRATE — 5 min]

[Open the Multi-Property Consolidation Tab on a sample 6-property
workbook.]

Six properties: Cabin A, Cabin B, Cabin C, Cabin D, Cabin E,
Cabin F.

The consolidation view shows:

Per-property summary table:
  Property | Gross Rev | Op Exp | Depreciation | Net Income | Margin
  Cabin A  | $52,400   | $28,200| $14,800      | $9,400     | 17.9%
  Cabin B  | $48,200   | $26,800| $14,200      | $7,200     | 14.9%
  Cabin C  | $54,800   | $29,400| $15,200      | $10,200    | 18.6%
  Cabin D  | $46,200   | $22,400| $13,800      | $10,000    | 21.6%
  Cabin E  | $41,800   | $19,800| $11,200      | $10,800    | 25.8%
  Cabin F  | $44,600   | $23,400| $13,400      | $7,800     | 17.5%
                                                ─────────
  Portfolio total                               $55,400

Schedule E roll-up:
  Line 3: $288,000
  Line 7: $34,200
  Line 14: $18,800
  Line 17: $24,600
  Line 18: $82,600
  Line 19: $14,200
  Plus other lines tied to the depreciation schedule

[Continue showing the tab's features for a few minutes.]

The view drives both the operational understanding (which property
is most profitable, where margins are weakest) and the tax filing
(line-by-line totals ready for Schedule E).

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Project your portfolio size 12 months out. If you'll be at 4+
properties, set up the consolidation tab now while data volume is
manageable.

If you're at 6+ already, evaluate whether to stay in one workbook
or split. The decision is operational, not tax — both work
mechanically.

## Lesson 7.2 — When to incorporate

**Length:** 10 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "7.2 — When to Incorporate"]

[VO]

Most hosts incorporate too early. Some incorporate too late. Both
are expensive.

The decision to set up an LLC, S-corp, or other entity is one of
the most-asked questions in STR communities. It's also one of the
most over-recommended. Generic advice — "always form an LLC" —
ignores that for some hosts the entity is overhead with no benefit.

This lesson runs the actual triggers.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. The three structures and what each does.

Two. The triggers for LLC formation.

Three. The triggers for S-corp election (much narrower).

Four. The decision tree.

[TEACH — 4 min]

The three structures.

Sole-prop (default).
  No formation cost
  No liability isolation
  Schedule E or Schedule C reporting in personal name
  Right answer for: small portfolios, low-risk properties, early
   stage

LLC (single-member, disregarded for tax).
  Formation cost: $50-$500 + annual fees
  Liability isolation between business and personal assets
  Tax treatment unchanged (still Schedule E or Schedule C)
  Right answer for: liability concerns, multi-property portfolios,
   out-of-state holdings

S-corp (election on top of LLC or corporation).
  Adds payroll administration overhead ($800-$2,000/year)
  Reduces SE tax on Schedule C income (no benefit on Schedule E)
  Reasonable-compensation requirement for owner-employee
  Right answer for: Schedule C STR with net income ≥ $75K,
   hospitality-style operation

LLC triggers.

Liability exposure is the primary trigger. An LLC is worth its
~$200/year overhead when:

  The property has high-risk amenities — pool, hot tub, trampoline
  Guest volume is high (many people exposed to potential injury)
  The property is high-value (more to lose in a lawsuit)
  The property is out-of-state (LLC simplifies multi-jurisdiction
   liability)
  You have multiple properties (each property in its own LLC
   isolates risk between properties)

Most hosts running 2+ STR properties form LLCs by year 3 of
operation. Hosts running 1 property in their personal name often
defer until acquisition #2.

S-corp triggers.

S-corp election is often misapplied. The election only saves SE
tax — and SE tax only applies to Schedule C income. If you file
Schedule E, S-corp election does nothing for SE tax (which is
zero anyway).

The trigger: Schedule C income of $75,000+ per year. Below that,
the payroll overhead and administrative cost of S-corp election
exceed the SE tax savings.

For most STR hosts who file Schedule E (the loophole + nonpassive
treatment via §469(c)(7)), S-corp election is not relevant. For
hosts running B&B-style hospitality on Schedule C with substantial
income, it becomes relevant.

[DEMONSTRATE — 5 min]

[Open the Decision Tree #6 — When to Incorporate (from the
Decision-Tree Pack bonus).]

Walk through three sample portfolios.

Portfolio one. Sarah. Three cabins, $156K gross, $9.5K net before
big levers, Schedule E with STR loophole. Hot tubs at all three
cabins.

  Liability exposure: high (hot tubs)
  Schedule E vs. C: E
  Net income: positive but moderate

Verdict: LLC per property (or series LLC). No S-corp election —
no SE tax to save on Schedule E income.

Portfolio two. Marcus. Single B&B, $148K gross, $32K net, Schedule
C with substantial services. Property in his name.

  Liability exposure: moderate (he runs the operation himself, no
   pool/trampoline)
  Schedule E vs. C: C
  Net income: $32K — below the $75K S-corp trigger

Verdict: LLC for liability protection. No S-corp election yet — SE
tax savings at this income level wouldn't cover the payroll
overhead. Re-evaluate at $75K+ net.

Portfolio three. Renee. Single inherited cabin, $34K gross, $2K
net, Schedule E. Property in her name. Low-risk amenities.

  Liability exposure: low
  Schedule E vs. C: E
  Net income: small

Verdict: stay sole-prop. Revisit at property #2 or if liability
profile changes (e.g., adds a pool).

When NOT to incorporate.

  Net income under $50K — entity overhead exceeds tax savings on
   Schedule C; no SE benefit on Schedule E
  Single property, low risk profile — umbrella policy may be
   sufficient
  Within 2 years of a planned property sale — transfer and
   re-titling complications
  Property qualifies for §121 exclusion as a personal residence
   sale — entity ownership disqualifies the exclusion

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Run the decision tree on your portfolio this month.

If you've already incorporated and the tree says you shouldn't
have, no immediate action — incorporation is rarely worth
unwinding. Just maintain the entity efficiently and don't add
unnecessary entities going forward.

If the tree says you should incorporate and you haven't, schedule
a CPA consult on the entity structure. Form selection (LLC vs.
LLC + S-corp election vs. C-corp) is a CPA conversation, not a
DIY decision.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 7.3 — The annual rate refresh

**Length:** 8 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "7.3 — The Annual Rate Refresh"]

[VO]

Mileage rate. §168(k) percentage. Per-diem CONUS rates. They all
move every January.

The seven-minute tax tab installs once and runs every quarter. But
the rates feeding the workbook update annually. Hosts who don't
refresh use last year's mileage rate on this year's miles —
under-deducting by a few percent each year.

The annual refresh is January 5. Half an hour. Done.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. Which rates change every year.

Two. Where to verify each.

Three. The TAX-* cells that need updating.

Four. The "What Changed" video from your bonus folder.

[TEACH — 3 min]

What changes.

  IRS standard mileage rate — set annually by Revenue Procedure
   each December. Effective January 1.
  Per-diem CONUS rates — set annually by GSA (General Services
   Administration). Effective October 1 of each year.
  §168(k) bonus depreciation rate — set by statute, phasing down
   each year. Verify the statutory rate at the start of each year.
  §179 cap and phase-out — adjusted annually for inflation.
  Standard deduction (federal) — adjusted annually for inflation.
   Doesn't directly affect Schedule E but affects overall tax.

What does NOT change (except by legislation):

  The §6041 1099-NEC threshold ($600) — last changed in 1954
  The de-minimis safe harbor ($2,500) — set in 2014, unchanged
  Most code section numbers and core tests
  The Augusta Rule (§280A(g)) 14-day cap

[DEMONSTRATE — 4 min]

[Open the workbook bundle and walk through each cell to update.]

TAX-001 Mileage Log → Year tab → Cell B2: IRS standard rate.
Source: Verify at irs.gov/tax-professionals/standard-mileage-rates.
Enter the new rate. Formula recalculates.

TAX-007 Per-Diem Meal Tracker → Year tab → Cells B2:B5: CONUS
standard, CONUS high-cost, M&IE standard, M&IE high-cost.
Source: gsa.gov/perdiem. Enter the new rates.

TAX-004 → Depreciation tab → Cell K3: §168(k) bonus rate.
Source: IRS publication or your CPA. Enter the new rate. New
placed-in-service assets use the new rate; prior-year assets
keep their original rate.

TAX-005 Quarterly Estimated Tax → Year tab → Cells B2:B6: tax
brackets, standard deduction, FICA wage base.
Source: IRS publications.

[Continue through any other cells.]

Total time: 15-30 minutes. One pass per year.

The "What Changed" video.

The course's annual update video lands in the third week of
January. 30 minutes. Walks through every rate change, every
threshold change, and any material code or guidance updates from
the prior year.

The video is the second source for the rate refresh. Watch it,
update the workbook cells, done.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Schedule January 5 as your refresh day every year. Block the
calendar. The work is uncomplicated but requires the annual
discipline.

If you missed last January's refresh, run it now. The rates
matter for the current-year transactions you've already logged.

## Lesson 7.4 — The half-day annual review

**Length:** 10 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "7.4 — The Half-Day Annual Review"]

[VO]

December 28. Half a day. Sets up next April.

The seven-minute tax tab runs quarterly. Once a year, you also run
a longer review — the Year-End Portfolio Health Check. Four hours.
Solo work. Sets the agenda for the year-end actions and the next
year's CPA handoff.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. Why December 28.

Two. The 10-question Year-End Health Check.

Three. The "before December 31" actions versus "before April 15"
actions.

Four. Year-over-year tracking.

[TEACH — 3 min]

Why December 28.

Late enough that the year is essentially closed. Early enough that
year-end actions still have a few days to execute. December 28
gives you December 29-31 to act on anything that surfaces.

Pick the date and stick with it. The annual rhythm of December 28
plus the four quarterly Saturdays creates the operating cadence
for the entire portfolio.

The 10-question scorecard.

The Year-End Health Check (Bonus, in your bonus folder) walks
through ten questions, each scored 0-3:

  Q1.  Quarterly cadence
  Q2.  Bookkeeping completeness
  Q3.  §274(d) substantiation
  Q4.  Mileage log
  Q5.  1099-NEC readiness
  Q6.  Depreciation schedule
  Q7.  Capital decisions still pending
  Q8.  Personal-use audit
  Q9.  Estimated tax
  Q10. Audit dossier readiness

Total out of 30. Above 25 is operator state. Below 22 is gap-
filling territory.

[DEMONSTRATE — 5 min]

[Open the Year-End Health Check on screen and walk through all ten
questions on a sample portfolio.]

Q1 — Quarterly cadence: 4 of 4 quarters closed on schedule. Score
3.

Q2 — Bookkeeping completeness: 3 uncategorized rows in Q4. Score 2.

Q3 — §274(d) substantiation: All trips logged contemporaneously.
Score 3.

Q4 — Mileage log: Complete and current. Score 3.

Q5 — 1099-NEC readiness: All W-9s collected, contractors flagged.
Score 3.

Q6 — Depreciation schedule: Current. New asset (HVAC replacement
on Cabin C) entered. Score 3.

Q7 — Capital decisions still pending: One — should I replace
Cabin B's water heater before December 31 or defer? Decision needed
this week. Score 2.

Q8 — Personal-use audit: All under threshold. Cabin C personal use
at 6 nights, 10% threshold is 18, well under. Score 3.

Q9 — Estimated tax: Q1-Q4 paid to safe harbor. Score 3.

Q10 — Audit dossier readiness: 4 ✗ flags from last Tabletop Drill
remain open. Score 2.

Total: 27 / 30. Operator state.

The "before December 31" actions:

  Q5: One contractor approaching $600 threshold without a W-9 —
   collect this week.
  Q7: Decision on water heater replacement — make decision and
   execute by 12/31 if proceeding (placed-in-service in current
   year for depreciation).
  Q8: No action — all under threshold.

The "before April 15" actions:

  Q2: Resolve 3 uncategorized rows.
  Q10: Close 4 ✗ flags by completing the substantiation each
   represents.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Block December 28 on your calendar. Four hours, solo work, no
calls.

If December 28 falls awkwardly for your schedule (holiday
travel), pick the closest weekend before December 31 — December
26 or 27 work fine. The exact date matters less than the
discipline.

[OUT]

## Lesson 7.5 — Graduation

**Length:** 7 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "7.5 — Graduation"]

[VO]

You read the book. You installed the system. You ran the year.
You're not the same operator.

This is the last lesson. There's no homework, no decision tree,
no worked example. Just an acknowledgment of what changes between
the host who started this course and the host who finishes it.

[FRAME — 20s]

In this lesson:

One. What a year on the system actually looks like, in retrospect.

Two. What comes after the course.

Three. A note on what the course will not have prepared you for.

[TEACH — 5 min]

What changes.

The host who started this course had a shoebox somewhere, a CPA
who filed once a year, and a vague sense that some deductions were
being missed. March was three weekends of reconstruction. The
audit fear was real but unaddressed.

The host who finishes this course has:

  Four quarterly Saturdays on the calendar, every year, going
   forward
  Three Manila folders or their digital equivalent at the front
   door
  Dedicated cards per property running clean transactions
  A workbook spine — TAX-004 — with a Schedule E roll-up that ties
   to the return
  An audit dossier that's roughly 80% built throughout the year
   and 100% ready by February 15
  A CPA handoff folder that arrives on the same date every year
  A vetted CPA who's running a strategic engagement, not a
   bookkeeping engagement
  A Pinterest pin or a wall poster of the 47 deductions next to
   the desk where the receipt photos happen
  An understanding of the §168(k) bonus rate, the §1.263(a) BAR
   test, and the §280A(d) personal-use threshold — at the level of
   knowing how to apply them, not just having read about them

The shift is from reactive to operational. From "I hope I got
everything" to "I know what I have, and I know how I'd defend it."

That shift is worth the price of the course many times over.

What comes after.

The community is yours, lifetime. Drop in when you have a question
or a win.

The annual "What Changed" update video lands every January. 30
minutes; covers rate changes, threshold updates, material code
changes. You'll get an email when it's live.

Quarterly office hours run on the last Saturday of each quarter at
11 AM Eastern. Cohort graduates can attend any session; Self-Study
graduates have standing invitations to the January and July
sessions as alumni.

The Year-End Health Check (Lesson 7.4) is the December exercise.
We'll send a reminder the week before.

If you complete the course and want to mention what worked for
another operator considering the course, the testimonial form
takes three minutes. Helpful but optional.

What this course did not prepare you for.

The course did not prepare you for state-specific filing
complications. State income-tax conformity to federal §168(k),
state-specific Augusta Rule treatment, lodging-tax compliance —
these vary by state and are out of scope for a federal-tax course.

The course did not prepare you for international tax. If you have
property abroad, a foreign-income exclusion question, or a host
who lives outside the U.S., engage a CPA with international
expertise.

The course did not prepare you for IRS examination representation.
If a correspondence examination escalates to office or field, hire
a CPA, EA, or attorney with examination experience. The course
gives you the dossier; representation is a separate skill.

The course did not prepare you to advise other hosts on tax. The
material here is for your own portfolio. If you find yourself
giving tax advice to friends, point them at the book.

[CLOSE — 1 min]

The book promised forty-seven deductions. The course installed the
system that captures them. The work going forward is operational
— quarterly Saturdays, annual rate refreshes, the December 28
review. The system runs whether or not you do.

That's the offer the course was built around. Thanks for taking
it.

— The STR Ledger
Run your rentals before they run you.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 15s]

This course has been general information about U.S. federal tax
compliance for short-term rental hosts. It is not tax, legal,
accounting, or investment advice. Specific deductions require
specific facts. Consult a qualified tax professional before filing
a return based on any material in this course.

[OUT]
