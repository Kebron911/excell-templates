## Lesson 2.1 — The shoebox

**Length:** 10 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "2.1 — The Shoebox"]

[VO]

Reliable beats elegant. Always.

Amanda's recordkeeping is not impressive to look at. Three Manila
folders sit on a shelf next to her front door. Each folder is labeled
with the cabin name and a colored sticky note — Cabin A is blue, Cabin
B is yellow, Cabin C is green. Receipts come home in a coat pocket and
get a corner-flagged with the matching color before they go into the
folder.

By quarter-end, each folder contains forty to a hundred and twenty
paper receipts. None of them are sorted. The order is whatever order
they came home in.

This is the system that recovers tens of thousands of dollars of
deductions every year. Not because the folders are sorted — because
the folders exist, and the receipts go into them within forty-eight
hours.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. Why a physical input layer matters even in 2026.

Two. The two-half rule — physical receipts plus digital receipts.

Three. How to set up your own version this afternoon.

[TEACH — 5 min]

Most STR hosts try to skip the input layer. They tell themselves
they'll photograph the receipt at the cash register, or they'll
forward the email to a folder, or they'll just remember.

They forget. Or they photograph at the register and the photo lives
in a thirteen-thousand-image camera roll where it cannot be retrieved.
Or the email forwarding works for a week and stops when life gets
busy.

The shoebox works because it lowers the activation friction to zero.
You walk in the door. The folders are right there. The receipt goes
into the matching folder. Done.

The two-half rule.

Half of your receipts are physical — Home Depot, Lowe's, gas
stations, the local plumber's handwritten invoice. Those go in the
Manila folders.

Half of your receipts are digital — Amazon, Costco online, Stripe
contractor payments, Airbnb statements, recurring SaaS charges. Those
go in an email folder you forward to. Same property tags as the
physical folders.

The system needs both halves. A physical-only system loses Amazon. A
digital-only system loses Home Depot. Neither half on its own holds.

The forty-eight-hour rule.

§1.6001-1 substantiation standard rests on contemporaneous records.
Receipts photographed and filed within forty-eight hours survive
examination. Receipts reconstructed from credit-card statements in
March do not.

Forty-eight hours is the threshold I recommend. Not because the IRS
wrote that number — they didn't. Because forty-eight hours is the
window where you still remember what the receipt was for, and
beyond which the context starts to fade.

[DEMONSTRATE — 3 min]

Walk to your front entry. Pick a shelf. Set down three Manila
folders, one per property. Put a colored sticky note on each one.
Pick distinct colors — blue, yellow, green; orange, purple, teal;
whatever you'll remember.

For your digital half: open your email. Create three labels or
folders, one per property, named the same as the physical folders.
Set up an automatic forwarding rule for any email from
"receipts@amazon.com" or "noreply@stripe.com" — anything that arrives
as a receipt — to land in those folders.

Photograph your receipts within forty-eight hours of getting them.
Use a cloud folder structure: one folder per property, sub-folders
by quarter. Lesson 2.4 walks through the photographing workflow in
detail.

That's it. The shoebox is set up. It cost nine dollars in folders and
about twenty minutes of setup time.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Stage your folders today.

Don't wait. The next receipt that comes home should have a place to
land. If it lands in a junk drawer instead, you've already started
the reconstruction-in-March habit you're trying to replace.

Photograph the last three receipts in your wallet right now. Drop
them in the corresponding cloud folder. That's your first
contemporaneous capture.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 2.2 — Dedicated cards per property

**Length:** 12 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "2.2 — Dedicated Cards Per Property"]

[VO]

One card per property. Fifteen-minute setup. Quarterly hours back.

This is the smallest investment in the entire course and the largest
recovery of bookkeeping time it produces. Hosts who mix personal and
property spending on a shared card spend three times as long
categorizing transactions as hosts who run dedicated cards. There's
no exception to that pattern.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. Why dedicated cards are not optional for the seven-minute tab to
work.

Two. What "dedicated" actually means — and what it doesn't.

Three. How to apply for one card per property in fifteen minutes
total.

[TEACH — 5 min]

Why dedicated.

A bank-card CSV with two hundred transactions per quarter takes about
ninety seconds to import and auto-categorize when every transaction
on it is a property transaction. The same CSV with a hundred property
transactions and a hundred personal transactions takes fifteen to
twenty minutes — every row needs eyeballs to determine whether it's
business or personal.

Multiply that by four quarters and three properties and you have an
extra four to six hours per year of bookkeeping work that exists
solely because you didn't separate the cards.

Plus: you cannot deduct a transaction on a personal card without an
allocation note. A mixed card forces every business deduction to
carry a substantiation receipt of "yes, this specific row was
business." A dedicated card carries an implicit substantiation:
the entire card belongs to the rental.

What dedicated means.

It does not mean a separate bank account is required, though many
hosts run separate accounts and find it cleaner.

It does mean: one credit card per property, used exclusively for
property expenses. No personal groceries. No personal Uber rides. No
personal subscriptions. The card is the property's card.

The card can be in your personal name. It does not need to be a
business credit card. A personal card you've decided to dedicate to
Cabin A works exactly as well as a business card with "Cabin A LLC"
on it.

If you have an entity — LLC, S-corp — there are reasons to put the
card in the entity's name (cleaner liability isolation, cleaner
accounting). But the cards-in-personal-name approach works fine for
the bookkeeping.

[DEMONSTRATE — 5 min]

The fifteen-minute setup.

Open your laptop. Pick one credit card application — Chase, Capital
One, American Express, your local credit union, whichever you
prefer.

Apply for the first card. Fifteen-minute application. Most cards
arrive in five to seven business days.

Repeat for each property in your portfolio. If applying for three
cards at once will spike your credit pulls, space them across two
weeks — one card per week.

When the cards arrive, label each one with a piece of tape on the
back: "Cabin A," "Cabin B," "Cabin C."

Set up online accounts. Each account becomes the source for the
quarterly CSV download.

In TAX-002, in the workbook for each property, there's a tab called
"Card Setup." Enter the card's last four digits there. The
categorization rule library uses the last-four to verify the CSV
imported into the right property's workbook.

Move recurring property charges to the new card. Utilities. Pool
service. PriceLabs. Hospitable. Stripe. The Airbnb host fee is
deducted from your gross deposits, not charged to a card, so that
one stays on the platform.

Personal charges that have somehow attached themselves to your
property's card — the streaming services, the personal Amazon — move
those off.

Sixty days from today, the cards should be running clean.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Apply for one card per property in motion this week.

If you already have dedicated cards, pull a recent statement and
audit it. Look for any personal charges that crept on. Move them
back to your personal card.

If applying for new cards triggers underwriting questions about
income — answer honestly, list rental income as the primary use case,
don't try to be clever with categorizations. Card issuers approve
hosts with rental income routinely.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 2.3 — CSV import & the rule library

**Length:** 14 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "2.3 — CSV Import & the Rule Library"]

[VO]

Eighty-five percent of your transactions categorize themselves. The
other fifteen percent take ninety seconds.

That ratio is the engine of the seven-minute tax tab. It only works
because of the rule library — sixty starter rules that ship with the
course, plus the rules you'll add as you encounter your own vendor
patterns.

[FRAME — 45s]

In this lesson:

One. How to download the quarterly CSV from your card's online
portal.

Two. How to paste it into TAX-002 so the rule library fires.

Three. How to handle the fifteen percent that didn't auto-categorize.

Four. How to add your own rules so next quarter is faster.

[TEACH — 5 min]

The rule library.

A rule is a row in TAX-002's "Rules" tab. Each row says: when a
transaction's vendor name contains a specific string, assign it to a
specific Schedule E line and category.

Example. Vendor name contains "airbnb" → Schedule E line 19, category
"Platform Fees," confidence "high." Auto-applies.

Example. Vendor name contains "home depot" AND amount is less than
five hundred dollars → Schedule E line 14, category "Repairs,"
confidence "medium." Auto-applies but flags for batched review at
quarter-end.

Example. Vendor name contains "amazon" → confidence "low." Does NOT
auto-apply; routes to the Review queue for manual line-item
allocation.

The library ships with sixty rules. About a dozen of them are
placeholders that need your customization — your specific utility
companies, your specific cleaner's name, your specific mortgage
servicer. The rest work out of the box.

Confidence levels.

High confidence rules auto-apply with no prompt. The vendor exists
for one purpose only.

Medium confidence rules auto-apply but queue a quarterly batched
review. About a minute at quarter-end to confirm or correct.

Low confidence rules do NOT auto-apply. They route to the Review
queue. Amazon, Costco, Sam's Club — vendors that genuinely span
business and personal use.

[DEMONSTRATE — 6 min]

I'm going to import a quarter's CSV right now. Watch the screen.

[Open the credit-card portal. Download CSV.]

Most card portals export CSV from the "statements" or "transactions"
section. Export the date range for the quarter you're closing. Save
to your downloads folder.

[Open TAX-002.]

In TAX-002, click the "Import" tab. Click the "Paste CSV here" cell.
Open the CSV in Excel, select all rows, copy, paste into the import
tab.

[Watch the macro fire.]

The rule library matches each row top-to-bottom. The first matching
rule fires. The transaction is tagged with its Schedule E line,
category, confidence, and the rule number that fired.

Look at the import summary at the top.

[Show summary block.]

Forty-two transactions imported. Thirty-one auto-categorized at high
confidence. Five at medium. Six routed to the Review queue.

[Click into the Review queue.]

Six items. Three Amazon orders — those need line-item splits. One
Costco — same, mixed-use. One Home Depot at eleven hundred forty
dollars — flagged for the BAR test because it's over the medium-
confidence threshold. One unknown vendor.

Walking through:

Amazon order one. Open the Amazon order email. Itemize. Forty-five
dollars Echo Dot for Cabin A — supplies, line 17. Thirty-five
dollars guest snacks — marketing under the documented welcome-supply
policy, line 19. Eighteen dollars personal book — drop. Total
deducted: eighty dollars.

Amazon order two. Quick. Print cartridges, allocated to Cabin B.
Forty-two dollars to line 19, software/office.

Amazon order three. Just batteries. Twelve dollars to line 17 supplies.

Costco. Mixed receipt. Allocated thirty-two dollars to property
supplies, fifty-five dollars personal — drop the personal portion.

Home Depot, eleven hundred forty. Dishwasher replacement. Open the
BAR-test note. Like-kind replacement of failed unit. Current expense
under §263(a). De-minimis safe harbor backstop because under twenty-
five hundred. Line 14, repairs.

Unknown vendor. Look at the amount, the date, the cabin's card. Memory
returns: it was the propane refill in October. Add to line 17 utilities,
add the vendor to the rule library so next quarter it auto-matches.

Total time on the Review queue: about three minutes.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Download last quarter's CSV from your dedicated card right now and
run the import.

If you don't have a dedicated card yet — Lesson 2.2 — set the cards up
this week. Skip the import for now and come back when the first quarter
of dedicated-card data is available.

If your import surfaces more than twenty Review-queue items, your rule
library needs customization. Identify the vendors you'll see repeatedly
and add them to the library now. Faster next quarter.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 2.4 — Receipt capture in 48 hours

**Length:** 10 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "2.4 — Receipt Capture in 48 Hours"]

[VO]

Receipts you photograph in forty-eight hours survive examination.
Receipts you reconstruct in March don't.

This lesson is the discipline. It is also, of every lesson in the
course, the one most students fail to install. Not because the
workflow is hard — because the habit is.

The habit is forty-eight hours.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. The §1.6001-1 substantiation standard, applied.

Two. The phone-camera workflow that makes forty-eight hours feasible.

Three. The cloud-folder structure receipts live in.

Four. What to do when you blow the forty-eight-hour window — because
you will, sometimes.

[TEACH — 4 min]

The standard.

§6001 of the Internal Revenue Code requires every taxpayer to keep
sufficient records to establish the items of gross income, deductions,
credits, etc. §1.6001-1 elaborates: records must be sufficient to
substantiate the entries on the return.

For STR hosts, "sufficient" means a vendor invoice or receipt for any
expense over seventy-five dollars, plus a complete receipt set for any
expense in the §274(d) categories regardless of amount — vehicle,
travel, meals, lodging, entertainment.

The IRS does not require a specific format. A photograph of a paper
receipt, stored in a way you can retrieve, is sufficient.

The contemporaneous standard.

Tax courts have interpreted "sufficient" to include a contemporaneous
element. A receipt photographed in real time, paired with a workbook
entry the same week, is contemporaneous. A receipt reconstructed at
year-end from a credit-card statement is not.

Reconstructions sometimes survive examination if paired with
corroborating evidence — calendar entries, contractor invoices, guest
messages. But the host bears the burden, and the burden is meaningful.

The forty-eight-hour rule is not in the regulation. It's a working
threshold that keeps you well inside the contemporaneous standard
without demanding same-day capture.

[DEMONSTRATE — 4 min]

The workflow.

Step one. Receipt arrives. Cash-register receipt at Home Depot, the
plumber's invoice, a hotel folio.

Step two. Within forty-eight hours, photograph it. Phone camera, good
light, frame the entire receipt including header and total. Don't
worry about glare; OCR handles surprisingly bad photos.

Step three. Drop the photo into the right cloud folder. Folder
structure:

  /receipts/
     /Cabin A/
        /2026/
           /Q1/
           /Q2/
           /Q3/
           /Q4/
     /Cabin B/
        /2026/...

Step four. The photo's filename should match the convention:

  YYYY-MM-DD-vendor-amount.jpg

Example: 2026-05-14-home-depot-1140.18.jpg

The filename convention lets the workbook find the receipt
automatically. TAX-002's transaction-detail tab has a column called
"Receipt Path" — when a CSV transaction matches a receipt by date and
amount, the workbook auto-links them.

The phone-camera shortcut.

iOS Shortcuts and Android Tasker both support a one-tap workflow:
photograph, OCR the vendor and amount, name the file, drop it in the
cloud folder. Set up once; saves about thirty seconds per receipt
forever.

The receipt-OCR n8n workflow we ship as a course bonus does the same
thing, automated, via email. Forward the receipt as an email
attachment to a dedicated address; the workflow OCRs, categorizes,
and appends to your workbook. Bonus B6 in the LMS covers setup.

When you blow the window.

You will, sometimes. A receipt sits in a coat pocket for a week.

When you find it: photograph anyway. Drop it in the right cloud
folder. Add a note in the workbook flagging the late capture. The
fact that one receipt is reconstructed doesn't ruin the dossier — it
just means that one entry is weaker on examination.

The pattern that ruins the dossier is reconstructing every receipt.
One late capture, fine. A quarter of late captures, problem.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Photograph three receipts today. Any three. Use the phone camera,
the filename convention, the cloud-folder structure.

Then set a phone reminder for "photograph today's receipts" at 9 PM
every day for the next thirty days. After thirty days the habit
either took or it didn't; either way, the reminder has done its job.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 2.5 — Mileage in real time

**Length:** 10 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "2.5 — Mileage in Real Time"]

[VO]

The IRS standard rate, multiplied by zero contemporaneous logs,
equals zero deduction.

Every year, hosts walk into examinations with a number in their
heads — six thousand miles, eight thousand, twelve thousand — and a
log that wasn't built until February. Tax courts have disallowed
mileage deductions worth tens of thousands of dollars on §274(d)
substantiation grounds. The deduction was real; the log wasn't.

The fix is forty-five seconds at the end of every business trip.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. What §274(d) actually requires.

Two. The phone-shortcut workflow for forty-five-second logging.

Three. How to handle reconstructions when you forget — and when
you can't reconstruct defensibly.

[TEACH — 4 min]

§274(d) requires four elements for every business mile: amount, time,
place, business purpose.

Amount. Miles driven. Start odometer minus end odometer.

Time. Date of the trip.

Place. Destination, with enough specificity that the location can be
verified later.

Business purpose. Why you took the trip. "Property visit" is too
generic. "Cleaner orientation visit, Cabin B" is sufficient.

These four elements live in TAX-001, the Mileage Log workbook. One
trip per row. Each row carries all four elements plus the IRS
standard rate for the year.

The standard rate is set by IRS Revenue Procedure each January.
Whatever the rate is for the year of the trip is what you multiply
by. 2026 trips use the 2026 rate; 2025 trips use the 2025 rate.

The contemporaneous rule.

§1.274-5T(c)(2) calls out four allowable substantiation methods.
The most common — and the one TAX-001 implements — is "adequate
contemporaneous records."

Contemporaneous means the log entry is made at or near the time of
the trip. Tax courts have generally accepted same-day or
within-the-week as contemporaneous. They have generally rejected
month-end or year-end reconstructions absent strong corroborating
evidence.

[DEMONSTRATE — 4 min]

The forty-five-second workflow.

Trip ends. You park back at home. Before getting out of the car:

Open TAX-001 on your phone. Or open the Notion mirror, or the
Airtable mirror — same logic, three surfaces.

New row. Date is today. Start odometer — pull from the trip's start;
most cars show trip-mileage in the dashboard. End odometer — current
reading. Miles compute automatically. Business purpose — type it
fresh while you remember.

That's it. Forty-five seconds.

The phone-shortcut.

iOS Shortcuts and Android Tasker both support a "log trip" shortcut
that opens TAX-001 to a fresh row, pre-fills the date, and prompts
for the four fields. One tap to start, four taps to complete.

[Show iOS shortcut on screen.]

Set up once; saves about thirty seconds per trip forever.

The mistakes.

Mistake one. Logging the trip "later." Later is March. The cost of
forty-five seconds is paid contemporaneously or paid in
reconstruction time.

Mistake two. Vague business purpose. "Cabin trip" doesn't survive
examination. "Cabin B repair coordination — meeting plumber re:
master bathroom shower valve replacement, vendor invoice attached" is
sufficient. Specific. Dated. Verifiable.

Mistake three. Round-tripping personal stops. You drive to Cabin B,
stop at Costco for personal groceries on the way home. The Costco
detour is personal mileage. Either log the Cabin B segment only or
note the detour and exclude its mileage.

When you forget.

If you find a trip you forgot to log: try to reconstruct from
calendar, vendor invoice, or guest message. Pair the reconstruction
with the corroborating document. Note in the workbook that the entry
was reconstructed.

If you can't reconstruct defensibly: drop the trip from your log.
Painful in the moment; cheaper than losing the deduction on
examination.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Log one trip today.

If you didn't take a business trip today, log the most recent one
you did take. Use the four §274(d) elements. Walk through the
workbook entry once.

Set a phone reminder for "log today's trips" at 7 PM every day for
the next thirty days. Same logic as the receipt reminder — the
habit either takes or it doesn't, and the reminder has done its job
either way.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 2.6 — The W-9 trap

**Length:** 10 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "2.6 — The W-9 Trap"]

[VO]

January 31 is too late. November is too late. The W-9 is collected
before the first dollar leaves your account.

Hosts who collect W-9s at engagement file their 1099-NECs on time,
deduct the contractor expenses cleanly, and never think about the
form again. Hosts who collect W-9s in January spend three weeks
chasing former contractors who have moved, lost the email, or
become unreachable — and pay late-filing penalties.

The fix is sixty seconds at the start of the relationship.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. The §6041 reporting threshold and what triggers it.

Two. The W-9 form, in plain English.

Three. The collection workflow — and the policy that makes it stick.

Four. What to do when you discover at year-end that you missed one.

[TEACH — 4 min]

§6041 requires Form 1099-NEC for any individual contractor paid six
hundred dollars or more in a calendar year for services rendered to
your trade or business.

For an STR portfolio, that includes:

  Cleaners (almost always)
  Handymen, plumbers, electricians (frequently)
  Photographers, videographers (per project)
  Lawn care, snow removal, pest control (small operators only)
  Painters, contractors on small jobs
  Bookkeepers, virtual assistants
  Co-hosts paid in cash

The threshold applies per individual contractor per year. Across
all properties combined, not per property.

What it does NOT apply to:

  Corporations (LLCs taxed as S-corp or C-corp don't need 1099-NEC)
  Material costs only — pure goods, not labor
  Payments through a 1099-K-issuing platform (Stripe, PayPal handle
   these via 1099-K to the contractor)

For ambiguous cases — an LLC of unclear tax classification, an
individual using a business name — issue the 1099-NEC anyway. There
is no penalty for issuing one that wasn't required. There is a
substantial penalty for failing to issue one that was.

The W-9 form.

Form W-9 is a four-line form: name, address, tax classification,
TIN. The contractor fills it out and signs. It takes ninety
seconds.

The W-9 collects the information you'll need to issue the 1099-NEC
in January. Without the W-9, you have a name and an address and
maybe a phone number — and no TIN, which means no 1099 can be
filed.

[DEMONSTRATE — 4 min]

The collection workflow.

Step one. Set a household policy: no money leaves your account to
any contractor without a signed W-9 on file.

Step two. When engaging a contractor, send the W-9 with the engagement
email. Two attachments: the W-9 (PDF, fillable) and a one-line
explanation: "We issue 1099-NECs at year-end; please complete and
return the attached W-9 before our first invoice."

Step three. When the W-9 returns, save it to TAX-003 with the
contractor's row. The 1099-NEC tracker stores the W-9 attachment
column directly.

Step four. As payments accumulate, TAX-003 totals year-to-date. When
a contractor crosses six hundred dollars, the workbook flags the row
for the January 1099-NEC packet.

Step five. January 1 through January 31, run the 1099-NEC export
from TAX-003. The workbook generates the form data; you e-file
through the IRS FIRE system or a service like Track1099.

The policy that makes it stick.

The single rule that prevents January chaos: no W-9, no payment.
Period.

The cleaner you've worked with for two years and never collected a
W-9 from? Email her: "We need to clean up our records. Can you fill
out the attached W-9 by Friday? We'll keep it on file." It takes
ninety seconds; cleaners answer it.

The new contractor you're engaging? "Send your invoice and a
completed W-9. We pay on receipt of both."

The contractor who declines? Don't engage. Or engage and accept
that you may need to issue a 1099 with incomplete information at
year-end, with penalty exposure.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Identify any contractor missing a W-9 today. Email each one this
week with the form attached.

If you have no contractors yet, no action needed. When you engage
your first one, follow the workflow.

If a contractor refuses to provide a W-9: that's a signal. Either
they're not who they say they are, or they're not reporting income.
Either way, the IRS interest is theirs to manage; your job is to
have collected the form and cleared your reporting obligation.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 2.7 — Your first quarter, end-to-end

> **Full long-form script in [00-course-plan.md §8.4](../00-course-plan.md). Reproduced below for the scripts folder's completeness.**

**Length:** 18 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "2.7 — Your First Quarter, End-to-End"]

[VO]

This is the lesson the rest of the course is built around.

Twenty-one minutes. Three properties. One workbook. We start with a
quarter's worth of receipts in three Manila folders and a CSV from
each property's dedicated card. We end with a Schedule E roll-up that
shows quarter-to-date net income, year-to-date cumulative, and a
projected annual.

I'm not going to teach. I'm going to do. Watch the screen, watch the
timer in the corner, and at the end you'll see what 21 minutes of
contemporaneous bookkeeping looks like.

[FRAME — 30s]

The setup: Cabin A, Cabin B, Cabin C. Each cabin has a dedicated
credit card. Each cabin has a Manila folder with that quarter's
receipts. The workbook is TAX-004 with three property tabs and a
roll-up tab.

If your portfolio looks different, the steps are identical. Just
narrower or wider.

Start the timer.

[DEMONSTRATE — 18 min]

[Real-time, single-take screen recording. The lesson is the demo.
Daniel narrates as he works. Approximate pacing:]

00:00 – 00:45  Bank/card CSV import for Cabin A. The rule library
                auto-categorizes 17 of 21 transactions. Four flagged
                for manual review.
00:45 – 02:15  Manual categorization of the four flagged. One §263(a)
                BAR test for an $1,140 dishwasher replacement —
                narrate the test live.
02:15 – 03:45  Receipt photography for §274(d) items. Two overnight
                trips, four receipts each. Forwarded to the cloud
                folder; links pasted.
03:45 – 04:30  Mileage log review. Three trips logged in real-time
                during Q1. Multiply by IRS standard rate; the cell
                does it automatically.
04:30 – 05:00  Cleaning-fee reconciliation. Pull Airbnb host
                statement. Compare.
05:00 – 05:30  Contractor 1099 tracking. Cleaning lady at $1,840 YTD
                — flagged.
05:30 – 07:00  Cabin A complete. Roll-up tab updated.
07:00 – 14:00  Repeat for Cabin B and Cabin C. Same six steps.
                Faster on B and C because the rule library has learned.
14:00 – 18:00  Roll-up review. Quarter-to-date numbers per cabin.
                Year-to-date cumulative. Projected annual. Quarterly
                estimated tax view. Backup runs.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

You just watched 21 minutes that, untrained, take 6 hours of
recovered bookkeeping in March.

Schedule your first 21-minute Saturday on the calendar event we set
up in Lesson 0.3. Bring one property to the workbook. You'll do less
```

## Lesson 2.8 — The backup pact

**Length:** 6 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "2.8 — The Backup Pact"]

[VO]

Three copies. One workbook. One cloud. One local. Monthly cadence.

The day the workbook is corrupted is not the day to discover you have
no backup. The day a hard drive dies is not the day to discover the
cloud sync stopped working three weeks ago. The day you lose the
laptop at the airport is not the day to find out the local backup
ran on a drive that wasn't plugged in.

Backup is a quiet discipline. It earns its keep once every five years
when you'd otherwise be paying a forensic data recovery firm three
thousand dollars to extract your December workbook.

[FRAME — 20s]

In this lesson:

One. The three-two-one rule applied to STR books.

Two. The §1.6001-1 retention period.

Three. The five-minute setup that makes backups automatic.

[TEACH — 3 min]

The three-two-one rule.

A standard data-management rule across IT and accounting: keep at
least three copies of your data, on at least two different storage
media, with at least one copy off-site.

For STR books, the practical implementation:

Copy one — your laptop's working file. The TAX-002 and TAX-004 you
edit on Saturday morning.

Copy two — cloud sync. Dropbox, OneDrive, Google Drive. Auto-syncs
on save.

Copy three — local backup, separate physical drive. External hard
drive, NAS, or a second computer. Refreshed monthly.

The third copy is the one most hosts skip. They reason that cloud is
their "off-site" backup, so they have two copies on two media, and
that's enough.

It isn't. Cloud sync is a real-time mirror — if you accidentally
overwrite a file, the cloud has the bad version. If a ransomware
attack encrypts your laptop, the cloud has the encrypted version.
The local-monthly copy is the snapshot that survives mistakes.

The retention period.

§6501(e) sets the IRS examination period at three years standard,
six years if substantial omission, indefinite for fraud or no
return filed.

Practical retention: keep tax-year working files for seven years
from the filing date. After seven years, the IRS examination window
has closed for that year except in fraud cases. Some hosts retain
indefinitely; storage cost is negligible relative to peace of mind.

[DEMONSTRATE — 2 min]

The five-minute setup.

Step one. Pick a cloud-sync service — Dropbox, OneDrive, Google
Drive, iCloud Drive. Install it on the laptop where you edit your
workbooks. Make sure your TAX-* and GST-* files live in the synced
folder.

Step two. Plug in an external drive. Set up a monthly backup to it.
Mac users: Time Machine, set to monthly snapshot retention. Windows
users: File History, monthly backup target. Linux users: rsync cron
job, weekly or monthly.

Step three. Verify the cloud sync is actually syncing. Open the
service's web interface and confirm your TAX-004 is dated within
the last week.

Step four. Verify the local backup ran. After the first scheduled
backup, check that the external drive contains the workbook files
and that the date stamps look right.

Step five. Set a calendar reminder for the first Saturday of every
month: "Verify backups ran." Thirty seconds of clicking around in
two interfaces.

That's the pact.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Run your first backup right now. Before you close the LMS for the
day.

Cloud sync — verify it's running. Local backup — plug in the drive,
trigger the first backup. Calendar reminder — set the monthly check.

Module 2 is now complete. Module 3 unlocks at the end of the week.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```
