## Lesson 6.1 — The handoff folder

**Length:** 10 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "6.1 — The Handoff Folder"]

[VO]

Your CPA's job is to file. Your job is to hand them a complete file.

Most STR hosts email their CPA a workbook on a random February
evening with the subject line "for the return." The CPA opens it,
finds questions, emails back, waits, the host responds late, the
cycle repeats. A two-week filing turns into eight weeks.

The handoff folder is the alternative. One delivery, organized,
complete, with a cover letter that tells the CPA exactly which
items need judgment.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. The fixed February 15 delivery convention.

Two. The 12-folder handoff structure.

Three. The cover letter.

Four. Why this approach saves 20-30% on CPA fees.

[TEACH — 5 min]

The fixed date.

CPAs prioritize clients whose materials arrive early and complete.
A reliable February 15 handoff makes you a top-of-stack client.

The 15th matters because it's after most W-2s and 1099s have
arrived (deadline January 31), after Q4 estimated tax has been
paid (January 15 deadline), and well before the March-CPA-panic
window.

If your prior-year books aren't ready by February 15, you're
behind. The Year-End Health Check (Module 7) is designed to land
you ready by January 31; February 15 is buffer.

The 12-folder structure.

  01 — Cover letter and index
  02 — Bookkeeping (TAX-002, TAX-004, reconciliation)
  03 — Supporting documents (1099-K, 1098, property tax,
       insurance dec pages)
  04 — Depreciation (schedule, cost-seg study, placed-in-service
       log, prior-year Form 4562)
  05 — Mileage and travel
  06 — Contractors and 1099s (W-9s, 1099-NECs you've already
       filed)
  07 — Cleaning fees reconciliation (Airbnb host statements)
  08 — Home office (if applicable)
  09 — Augusta §280A(g) (if elected)
  10 — Special situations (inheritance, casualty, sale, refinance)
  11 — Prior-year return
  12 — Estimated tax payments

Twelve folders. Most years, only 7-9 are populated; the others are
skipped if not applicable. The skeleton is identical every year so
the CPA's eye finds what they need without re-orienting.

The cover letter.

Two pages, max. Header section identifies you and the year.
Headline-numbers section gives the CPA your preliminary
calculation. "What's changed from last year" section flags
material differences. Items-needing-judgment section points to
the question list (which lives in 01-the-question-list.pdf).

The cover letter sets the tone. A well-organized cover letter
signals that the substance behind it is also organized.

[DEMONSTRATE — 4 min]

[Open the CPA Handoff Folder Template (Bonus, in /03-templates/
cpa-handoff/).]

Walk through populating the folders for a sample year.

Folder 01 — drop in the cover letter, the question list, and the
master index from the audit dossier.

Folder 02 — drop in TAX-002 and TAX-004 (final, frozen versions),
plus the reconciliation summary tying gross income to bank
deposits.

Folder 03 — drop in 1099-K from Airbnb, 1099-K from VRBO if
applicable, Form 1098 mortgage interest, property tax statements,
insurance declarations pages.

Folder 04 — drop in the depreciation schedule, the cost-seg study
(if year-1), the placed-in-service log, and the prior-year Form
4562.

[Continue through each folder briefly.]

Cloud-share with read-only permission for the CPA. One email with
the cover letter as the body and the share link.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Build the folder shell this week. Don't try to populate every
folder; just create the 12-folder structure on your cloud storage.

Schedule a handoff date with your CPA — February 15. Block the
date as a mutual calendar event. The accountability of the date
on both sides makes the handoff reliable.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 6.2 — The 12-question CPA interview

**Length:** 12 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "6.2 — The 12-Question CPA Interview"]

[VO]

Most STR hosts hire a generalist. The questions identify the
specialists.

If your current CPA is a generalist who treats your STR like a
duplex rental, you're paying for a service that doesn't fit your
operation. Sometimes the cost shows up as missed deductions;
sometimes as audit exposure; usually both.

The 12-question scorecard surfaces the difference between a
generalist and a specialist in 30 minutes.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. Why the 12 questions are the right 12.

Two. How to ask them — the consult format.

Three. The scoring rubric.

Four. The hire / walk decision threshold.

[TEACH — 5 min]

Why these 12.

The questions are designed to surface depth — not just whether the
CPA knows a topic exists, but whether they know it well enough to
guide your portfolio's decisions.

Each question has a specific answer pattern that distinguishes a
specialist's understanding from a generalist's pattern-match.

The 12 questions:

  Q1.  Average rental period and the Schedule E vs. C trigger
  Q2.  The seven material-participation tests
  Q3.  The STR loophole (§469(c)(7) interaction)
  Q4.  Cost segregation — when it pencils, recapture risk
  Q5.  The BAR test under §1.263(a)-3
  Q6.  Augusta Rule (§280A(g)) documentation
  Q7.  1099-NEC obligations
  Q8.  Personal-use limitation (§280A(d))
  Q9.  Bonus depreciation — current rate, §179 stack
  Q10. Audit posture — examination experience
  Q11. Software and intake process
  Q12. STR-specific experience — count and pattern of clients

Each question and the looking-for-pattern is fully detailed in the
CPA Interview Scorecard bonus. We won't repeat all twelve here —
the bonus is your reference. Pull it up before any consult call.

The format.

Schedule a 30-minute consult. Most STR-aware CPAs offer this for
$0-$150. Worth paying for either way.

Read each question out loud. Don't paraphrase. The exact wording
matters.

Listen for the answer pattern. Score 0-3 per question against the
rubric.

Ask the follow-up. Many preparers give a textbook answer to the
question and stumble on the follow-up.

Total at the end. Hire above 30 / 36. Walk below 24.

The scoring rubric.

  3 — Crisp, specific answer that demonstrates current working
      knowledge. Includes code citation or current rate where
      relevant.
  2 — Correct concept, less precision. Knows the answer but light
      on specifics.
  1 — Knows the topic exists but is fuzzy on application.
  0 — Wrong answer, evasion, or "we don't usually deal with that."

[DEMONSTRATE — 5 min]

[Show a transcript of a real (composite, anonymized) consult call.]

Sample. Question 1 — average rental period and Schedule E vs. C
trigger.

Host: "What's the average rental period at which a short-term rental
moves from Schedule E into a Schedule C analysis, and what triggers
Schedule C even when the average rental period is over seven days?"

Strong CPA answer: "Seven days is the per-se trigger under
§1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A). Above seven, you can still be pulled into
Schedule C analysis if the average is between eight and thirty
and you provide substantial services — daily housekeeping during
stay, meals, concierge. For most STR portfolios with three- to
seven-night averages and no in-stay services, Schedule E is the
correct treatment."

Score: 3.

Weak CPA answer: "Most short-term rentals file Schedule E. Some
file Schedule C. Depends on the situation."

Score: 1.

[Continue through 2-3 more sample questions with strong vs. weak
answers.]

The pattern is clear. The strong answer cites a code section, gives
a numeric threshold, and acknowledges the gray area. The weak
answer pattern-matches without depth.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Book one consult this month. Use the scorecard.

Even if you're happy with your current CPA, run the questions on
them. The scorecard tells you whether your existing engagement
fits your portfolio.

If you're searching, start with the STR-Aware CPA Directory (Bonus
B12). The Directory's listings have already passed a version of
this scorecard.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]

## Lesson 6.3 — What you should not let your CPA do

**Length:** 8 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "6.3 — What You Should Not Let Your CPA Do"]

[VO]

Bookkeeping, mileage, receipt-keeping. Not their job. Not their
hourly rate.

CPAs are expensive per hour. The right hours are the strategic
hours — depreciation election decisions, classification questions,
audit defense. The wrong hours are the hours they spend doing
data-entry your $497 course taught you to do yourself.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. The division of labor — what's your job, what's the CPA's.

Two. The misuse-of-CPA invoice that signals you're paying for the
wrong work.

Three. How to redirect a relationship that's drifted into bad
patterns.

[TEACH — 4 min]

The division of labor.

Host's job:

  Bookkeeping (categorize transactions, run TAX-002 and TAX-004)
  Receipt photography and §274(d) substantiation
  Mileage logging
  W-9 collection
  1099-NEC preparation and filing (TAX-003 generates packets)
  Quarterly cadence and the seven-minute tax tab
  The audit dossier (Module 5)

CPA's job:

  Strategic decisions: Schedule E vs. C classification, §179 vs.
   bonus, cost-seg engagement decisions, entity choice
  The actual return preparation and filing
  Form 4562 generation from your depreciation schedule
  Entity returns (1120-S, 1065 if applicable)
  State conformity questions
  Audit defense if examined
  Mid-year planning calls (proactive)

The line is "the host runs the operation; the CPA reads the operation
and translates it to the return." Both roles are necessary. They are
not interchangeable.

The misuse-of-CPA invoice.

Pull your last CPA invoice. Look at the line items. If the invoice
shows hours billed for:

  "Bookkeeping cleanup" — your CPA is doing your job
  "Categorizing transactions" — your CPA is doing your job
  "Reconstructing mileage" — your CPA is doing your job (and at a
   markup over what you should be doing)
  "Collecting W-9s" — wildly inappropriate; flag and redirect
  "Researching whether to capitalize the dishwasher" — fine if it
   was a $4K dishwasher; misuse if a $1K dishwasher with a clear
   answer

A clean CPA invoice for a 3-property STR Schedule E should run
roughly 4-8 hours of CPA time, depending on complexity. Above 12
hours typically signals one of two issues: the CPA's process is
inefficient, or you're handing them a shoebox they're cleaning up.

[DEMONSTRATE — 3 min]

[Pull up a sample CPA invoice with line items.]

Sample.

  Schedule E preparation (3 properties): 5.5 hrs × $250 = $1,375
  Form 4562 depreciation: 1.0 hr × $250 = $250
  Mid-year planning call: 0.75 hr × $250 = $188
  Strategic memo on §179 vs. bonus on HVAC: 1.0 hr × $250 = $250
  Q4 estimated tax review: 0.5 hr × $250 = $125
                                          ─────
  Total:                                  $2,188

This is a clean invoice for a host running the operating layer well.
Five and a half hours of return prep is reasonable; the strategic
hours are where the CPA earns their fee.

The contrast.

  Bookkeeping cleanup and reconciliation: 6.5 hrs × $250 = $1,625
  Categorizing un-flagged transactions: 2.0 hrs × $250 = $500
  Mileage reconstruction: 1.5 hrs × $250 = $375
  Schedule E preparation: 4.0 hrs × $250 = $1,000
  Form 4562: 1.0 hr × $250 = $250
                                         ─────
  Total:                                $3,750

Same return. $1,562 more. The host paid $1,562 for the CPA to do
work the host should have done. None of those extra hours produced
better tax outcomes.

The redirect.

If your invoices look like the second pattern, the conversation
with your CPA is:

"I'm running through Module 6 of an STR tax course and I want to
restructure how we work together. Going forward, I'll deliver a
clean handoff folder by February 15 with bookkeeping, mileage,
1099s, and a question list already prepared. Can we re-scope the
engagement to reflect that?"

Most CPAs welcome this conversation. It reduces their workload and
makes their fee more proportional to the strategic value they
provide.

A CPA who pushes back — who insists on doing the bookkeeping
themselves and charging for it — is a CPA whose business model
depends on data-entry-billing, not strategic value. Time to shop.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Pull last year's CPA invoice. Identify the hours that fall into
"things you should reclaim." Estimate the dollar reclaim.

If material — say, $500+ — schedule a conversation with the CPA
to re-scope the engagement. The handoff folder is the operational
proof that you're ready to take that work back.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 6.4 — Reviewing the draft return

**Length:** 10 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "6.4 — Reviewing the Draft Return"]

[VO]

Six lines. Verify each. Then sign.

Most hosts sign their return after a 30-second review. They scan
the refund (or balance due), confirm the dollar amount feels
right, and click. That's how returns with errors get filed.

The fix is six lines of focused review on Schedule E. Five
minutes of work. Catches the errors that show up year after year.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. Why reviewing the draft return matters more than reviewing
the books.

Two. The six lines that hold most of the errors.

Three. What to do when you find a discrepancy.

Four. The pre-signature check that's never optional.

[TEACH — 4 min]

Why review the draft.

Your bookkeeping is what you handed the CPA. The CPA's translation
to the return is where errors enter. Numbers get keyed wrong, lines
get swapped, depreciation gets computed differently than your
schedule.

Verifying the draft against your books takes 5-10 minutes and
catches errors that would otherwise propagate to filing.

The six lines.

  Line 3 — Rents received
  Line 7 — Cleaning and maintenance
  Line 17 — Utilities
  Line 18 — Depreciation expense
  Line 19 — Other (with sub-itemization)
  Plus: the depreciation schedule on Form 4562

These six lines hold the bulk of the dollar volume on most STR
Schedule E returns. They're also the most error-prone — line 3 is
the gross/net trap, line 18 depends on the depreciation schedule
being correctly imported, line 19 is the catch-all where things
get mis-itemized.

[DEMONSTRATE — 5 min]

[Open a sample draft return alongside the source TAX-004.]

Line 3 verification.

Source: TAX-004 → Schedule E roll-up tab → Line 3 cell shows
$94,200 across three properties.

Draft return: Schedule E line 3 shows $94,200.

Tie. ✓

Line 7 verification.

Source: $11,400 (cleaning fees from Airbnb passing through to
cleaner, plus standalone cleaner payments).

Draft: $11,400.

Tie. ✓

Line 17 verification.

Source: $6,240 (utilities aggregated across properties).

Draft: $6,240.

Tie. ✓

Line 18 — depreciation.

Source: TAX-004 depreciation schedule shows $24,840 — combination
of MACRS 27.5-year on three properties' building basis plus year-
one acceleration on the cost-seg reclassed assets.

Draft: $24,840.

Tie. ✓

Cross-check: Form 4562. Open the form. Section A shows §179
elections — match expected. Section B shows §168(k) bonus on
identified assets — match. Part III shows MACRS 27.5-year
depreciation by property. Tie to TAX-004's depreciation tab line
by line.

Tie across the form. ✓

Line 19 — Other.

Source: Sub-itemized in TAX-002.
  Platform fees: $2,826
  Software: $1,134
  Education: $497
  Per-diem: $283
  Bank fees: $260
  Misc: $140
                ─────
  Total:        $5,140

Draft return: line 19 shows $5,140 with attached statement
itemizing the sub-categories.

Tie. ✓

When you find a discrepancy.

Don't sign. Email the CPA: "I'm reviewing the draft and I see line
17 shows $6,400 but my books show $6,240. Can you check?"

Most discrepancies are typos or copy-paste errors. The CPA fixes
and resends.

A discrepancy that doesn't have a clear explanation — say, the CPA
re-categorized something — needs a conversation, not just an
email. Schedule the call. Resolve before signing.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Schedule your draft-return review with your CPA before signing
this year.

Build a review checklist using the six lines plus Form 4562.
Block 30 minutes for the review.

Don't sign on the same call you receive the draft. Take the draft
home, review against your books, then sign at the next interaction.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 6.5 — The post-filing debrief

**Length:** 8 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "6.5 — The Post-Filing Debrief"]

[VO]

April 16 is the most useful day of the tax year. Most hosts waste
it.

The day after the return is filed, the CPA's mind is fresh with
your portfolio. The questions that came up during preparation, the
deductions that were close calls, the items that should have been
substantiated better — all of it is at the surface of memory.

By June, that context is gone. By next February, it's reset.

The 30-minute post-filing debrief captures the lessons while they're
still accessible.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. Why the debrief is the most underrated CPA conversation.

Two. The five-question debrief script.

Three. How to action what you learn.

[TEACH — 4 min]

Why the debrief.

Your CPA just spent hours inside your return. They saw which
deductions were robust and which were thin. They noticed which
substantiation gaps caused them to ask follow-up questions. They
have a perspective on your books that you don't have.

Capturing that perspective costs you 30 minutes of CPA time —
maybe $125 at hourly rates. The information shapes how you operate
the next year.

Most hosts skip the debrief because they're tired, the return is
done, and they want to move on. The skip is the mistake.

The five questions.

Q1. Which deductions were close calls?

Some deductions on every return are clear; some are judgment.
Which ones did the CPA hesitate on this year? Their answer tells
you where to tighten substantiation in the next year's dossier.

Q2. Which substantiation gaps did you fill in for me?

Where did the CPA have to ask follow-up questions or assume in your
favor? The list is your gap-list for next year.

Q3. What should I do differently in 20XX?

Open question. Lets the CPA volunteer process improvements you
might not think to ask about.

Q4. Are there planning moves I should make in Q2 or Q3 of 20XX?

Most CPAs only think tactically during prep. The debrief is when
their mind is on your portfolio without the deadline pressure.
Mid-year planning conversations are higher-value when seeded here.

Q5. If we get an examination notice, which line is most likely to
be questioned?

Forces the CPA to predict examination posture. Their answer
identifies the deduction that needs the strongest dossier.

[DEMONSTRATE — 3 min]

[Show a sample debrief conversation, summarized.]

Sample debrief notes from a 3-cabin operator's April 16 call:

  Q1 — Close calls:
    • $2,140 partial roof repair on Cabin C — leaned toward expense
      under routine maintenance, but borderline. Recommend either
      capitalizing future similar items or strengthening the BAR-
      test note.
    • Augusta Rule election — board minutes for one of the 14 days
      were thin. Tighten next year.

  Q2 — Substantiation gaps filled in:
    • Material participation hours — assumed 487 based on time-log
      summary; would prefer to see the daily log entries
      themselves in next year's dossier.
    • One mileage trip — date of trip was reconstructed from a
      contractor invoice rather than the log itself.

  Q3 — Do differently in 2027:
    • Run quarterly bookkeeping cleaner. Two transactions in Q3
      were ambiguously categorized.
    • Consider mid-year planning call — second cost-seg study on
      Cabin D acquired April 2027.

  Q4 — Planning moves:
    • Cabin D cost seg engagement decision by July
    • Year-end personal-use audit by November 15 (proactively, not
      December 28)

  Q5 — Examination prediction:
    • Material participation under §469(c)(7) loophole — most
      likely flagged. Tighten time log discipline.

The debrief notes become the operating priorities for the next year.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Schedule your debrief on the same call as your draft-return review,
or within two weeks of filing.

Email the five questions to the CPA in advance so they can think
about answers. The call goes faster and produces better material
when the CPA isn't generating answers in real time.

The debrief notes go into your audit dossier as the year-end
perspective on the year now closed.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
