## Lesson 4.1 — Cost segregation: when it pencils

**Length:** 16 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "4.1 — Cost Segregation: When It Pencils"]

[VO]

Eight thousand four hundred and twenty-seven dollars in year-one
acceleration is real. The study costs fifteen hundred to five thousand
dollars. Math first.

Cost segregation has become a popular topic in STR communities, often
sold as a universal recommendation: every host should do one. That's
wrong. Cost seg pencils for some portfolios and is wasted money for
others. The difference is acquisition basis, ability to use the loss,
and exit horizon.

This lesson runs the math.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. What cost segregation actually is, mechanically.

Two. The three conditions that determine whether it pencils.

Three. The economic test on three real portfolios.

Four. The recapture risk on sale.

[TEACH — 7 min]

What cost segregation is.

When you buy a rental property, the IRS classifies the depreciable
basis — purchase price minus land allocation — as 27.5-year
residential rental property under MACRS. You depreciate the entire
amount over 27.5 years using the straight-line method. Year-one
depreciation on a $480,000 depreciable basis is roughly $17,500.

Cost segregation reclassifies portions of that basis into shorter-life
asset classes — five-year personal property, seven-year, fifteen-year
land improvements. Those classes are eligible for accelerated
depreciation under §168(k) bonus and §179 expensing.

A cost-seg study, performed by a qualified engineering firm, walks
through the property and identifies which components belong in which
class. Carpeting, appliances, decorative lighting, removable cabinetry
— five-year. Decks, walkways, landscaping — fifteen-year land
improvements. The structure itself remains 27.5-year.

For an STR with a $480,000 depreciable basis, a cost-seg study
typically reclassifies fifteen to thirty percent of basis into shorter
classes. On a 22% reclassification rate — about $105,000 — and a 60%
§168(k) bonus rate, year-one acceleration is roughly $63,000.

At a 32% marginal tax rate, that's about $20,000 of year-one tax
savings. The study cost about $4,500. Net first-year benefit:
~$15,500.

The three conditions.

Condition one. Acquisition basis.

The economics work when basis is at least $400,000. Below that, the
study cost eats too much of the acceleration to be worth it.

For smaller properties, the DIY workbook — TAX-010 — is the better
path. We cover it in the next lesson.

Condition two. Ability to use the loss.

The acceleration creates a tax loss. To use that loss against active
income — your W-2 salary, your business profits — you need to
qualify under one of two paths:

Path one. Real estate professional status under §469(c)(7).
Requires 750+ hours and more than half of personal services in real
property trades or businesses. For most W-2 day-job hosts, this is
not available.

Path two. The STR loophole. We dedicate Lesson 4.3 to this; the
short version is that an STR with an average rental period of seven
days or less, plus material participation, escapes the rental real
estate per-se passive rule. The acceleration loss is then nonpassive
and offsets active income.

If neither path is available, the acceleration is suspended as a
passive loss. It carries forward and offsets future passive income or
gain on sale. Cost seg still has value but the benefit is deferred.

Condition three. Exit horizon.

Cost-seg reclassifications create §1245 personal property. On sale,
§1245 recapture is taxed at ordinary rates up to the cumulative
depreciation taken — not the 25% §1250 unrecaptured-gain rate that
applies to building structure.

If you plan to sell the property within five years, the recapture on
sale partially or fully claws back the year-one acceleration benefit.
Cost seg still pencils for short holding periods if your marginal
rate at acceleration is meaningfully higher than at sale, but the
analysis is closer.

For ten-year-plus holding periods, recapture concern is minimal.

[DEMONSTRATE — 6 min]

Three portfolios. Three verdicts.

Portfolio one. Sarah. Three cabins, average acquisition basis
$640,000 each. W-2 day job. STR loophole eligible — three- to
four-night average rental periods, material participation
documented.

Cost seg pencils. Engineering studies on each cabin: roughly $4,500
per study. Reclassifications around $112,000 per cabin. At 60% bonus,
year-one acceleration of about $67,200 per cabin. Across three
cabins, $201,600 of acceleration. At a 32% rate, $64,500 in year-one
tax savings. Studies cost $13,500 total.

Verdict: cost seg, all three cabins. Net first-year benefit ~$51,000.

Portfolio two. Marcus. Single cabin, $310,000 acquisition basis.
Schedule C operation with substantial services. SE tax on the
income.

Cost seg may not pencil. Below the $400,000 threshold for engineering
studies. The TAX-010 DIY workbook is feasible — annotated worksheet
and self-prepared depreciation schedule. Reclassification potential
around $50,000 — yields about $30,000 of year-one bonus acceleration
at 60%, $9,600 in year-one tax savings. DIY workbook cost: zero
(included in the course).

Verdict: TAX-010 DIY, not engineering study. Walk through the
workbook in Lesson 4.2.

Portfolio three. Renee. Single cabin, $480,000 basis. Inherited from
her father. Stepped-up basis at date of death. W-2 day job; no STR
loophole because she only has one property and her hours are below
the material-participation threshold.

Cost seg works on the basis math. Reclassification ~$96,000. Bonus
yields ~$57,600. Tax savings at 32%: ~$18,400. Study cost ~$4,500.

But — Renee can't use the loss. She doesn't qualify for real estate
professional status, and the loophole requires material participation
she's not currently meeting. The acceleration creates a passive loss
that suspends and carries forward. She doesn't get the $18,400 in
year one; she gets it whenever passive income or sale finally
absorbs the loss.

Verdict: defer cost seg until she's grown the portfolio enough to
either meet REP or document material participation. Or, accept the
deferred benefit if her exit horizon is long.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Run the worksheet on your most expensive door this week.

The TAX-010 workbook has a "Pencil Test" tab that walks through the
three conditions and computes the year-one math. Spend twenty minutes
on it.

If the worksheet says cost seg pencils, watch Lesson 4.2 next — the
TAX-010 walkthrough. If it doesn't pencil, skip 4.2 and pick up at
Lesson 4.3.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 4.3 — The STR loophole, decoded

**Length:** 14 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "4.3 — The STR Loophole, Decoded"]

[VO]

Active losses against active income. The §469(c)(7) you-think-you-
know-it rule.

The STR loophole is the single most powerful deduction in the course
and the single most commonly misunderstood. Hosts use it without the
documentation it requires; CPAs sometimes apply the wrong test;
forums spread half-correct versions of the rule.

This lesson is the actual rule, with the documentation that defends
it.

[FRAME — 45s]

In this lesson:

One. The default rule — why rental real estate is per-se passive.

Two. The STR carve-out under §1.469-1T(e)(3) — and why it bypasses
the per-se rule.

Three. The interaction with §469(c)(7) and material participation.

Four. The four-log evidence stack.

[TEACH — 7 min]

The default rule.

§469 of the Internal Revenue Code treats rental real estate as
per-se passive. The income is passive; losses are passive. Passive
losses can only offset passive income; they cannot offset active
income from W-2 wages, business profits, or interest and dividends.

For most rental landlords, this is fine — they have rental income,
the depreciation generates a loss, and the loss is suspended (or
offsets income from other rentals). On sale, suspended losses
release.

For STR hosts who want to use the loss against active income, the
default rule is a problem.

The carve-out.

§1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii) defines six exceptions to the rental real estate
per-se rule. Two are relevant for STR hosts:

Exception A. Average rental period seven days or less.

Exception B. Average rental period thirty days or less, AND the
owner provides substantial services.

Most STR hosts qualify under Exception A. Their average rental
period is three to seven nights; they're outside the per-se passive
rule.

This is what the STR community calls "the loophole." It's not a
loophole; it's a forty-year-old regulation. But the colloquial name
has stuck.

The interaction.

Escaping the per-se rule means rental income is no longer
*automatically* passive. It still might be passive — material
participation determines that.

§1.469-5T defines seven material participation tests. The two
practical ones for STR hosts:

Test one. 500 hours of participation in the activity during the
year.

Test two. The host's participation constitutes substantially all of
the participation, including non-owners. (For a host who self-manages
and doesn't use a property manager, this is often the operative
test.)

Test seven. The host participates more than 100 hours, AND no other
person participates more.

If any of those tests is met, the host materially participates. The
escape from the per-se rule plus material participation means the
rental losses are nonpassive — and offset active income.

That's the loophole, end-to-end. Average rental period + material
participation = active losses.

The four-log evidence stack.

§1.469-5T(f)(4) allows participation to be substantiated by "any
reasonable means." Courts have generally accepted contemporaneous
logs.

The four logs that hold up:

Log one. Time log. Date, activity, duration, property. Each entry
made the same day or within the week. Annual total clearly visible.

Log two. Correspondence log. Emails, texts, messages with vendors,
guests, contractors. Time-stamped, contemporaneous. Don't print all
of them — keep them in a searchable archive (Gmail folder, Airtable,
etc.) and reference the volume in the time log.

Log three. Decision log. Choices the host made — pricing strategy
adjustments, vendor changes, marketing decisions, capital
improvements approved. One row per decision, dated.

Log four. Reservation log. Booking calendar plus the host's
involvement in each booking — pre-stay messaging, mid-stay support,
post-stay review. Most platform calendars provide the bookings; the
host's involvement is what makes it a participation log.

Together, the four logs build a defensible material-participation
file. Hosts who keep all four typically log somewhere between 200
and 600 annual hours — enough to clear at least one of the
material-participation tests.

[DEMONSTRATE — 5 min]

I'm going to set up the four logs right now.

[Open the workbook.]

Time log. New tab. Columns: date, activity, duration in minutes,
property. Sample entries from this week — 25 minutes responding to
guest message about WiFi password, 90 minutes scheduling and
coordinating cleaner for Saturday turnover, 45 minutes researching
bonus depreciation rate for the year, 20 minutes paying contractor
invoices.

Correspondence log. New tab. Header pointer: "See email folder
'STR-Cabin-A' and SMS thread with cleaner." Annual count: roughly
800 emails and 300 texts. The volume is the substantiation; you
don't need to print them all.

Decision log. New tab. Sample entries — March 14, decision to
increase Cabin A nightly rate from $189 to $215; June 22, decision
to engage new cleaner after old cleaner moved; October 8, decision
to perform deck rebuild instead of repair.

Reservation log. New tab. The Airbnb and VRBO booking history,
exported quarterly, plus a column for the host's pre-stay,
mid-stay, and post-stay touch points.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Start the time log today.

The first entry is whatever you spend on STR work in the next
hour. Open the log, add a row, write the activity, count the
minutes when the work ends.

If you've been operating without a time log, you cannot fully
reconstruct the prior year. Start today and run forward. For the
prior year, document what you can — calendar entries, email
volume, decision history — as best-efforts evidence.

The loophole only works when the documentation is there. The
documentation is there only when the logs are running.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 4.4 — The Augusta Rule

**Length:** 14 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "4.4 — The Augusta Rule"]

[VO]

Fourteen nights. Tax-free rental. Board minutes optional.
Documentation not.

The Augusta Rule — §280A(g) of the Internal Revenue Code — is one of
the cleanest deductions in the code. Up to fourteen days of rental
income from your personal residence is excluded from your gross
income; the renting entity deducts the rent as a §162 ordinary-and-
necessary business expense.

The rule sounds too good. The IRS scrutinizes it on examination.
The deduction is preserved on the strength of three documents.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. What §280A(g) does, mechanically.

Two. The three-legged-stool documentation requirement.

Three. The Augusta Documentation Pack — your file for the deduction.

Four. Who should not elect.

[TEACH — 7 min]

What §280A(g) does.

§280A is the broader Internal Revenue Code section governing
personal-use limitations on rental property. Subsection (g) is the
fourteen-day carve-out: if you rent your personal residence for
fewer than fifteen days during a tax year, the rental income is
excluded from your gross income.

The key word is "personal residence." Your STR property doesn't
qualify under §280A(g) — it's not your personal residence. Your
home, or a guest house attached to your home, qualifies.

Why this matters for STR hosts.

If you operate through an entity — an LLC, an S-corp, a C-corp —
the entity can rent your personal residence (or a portion of it,
like a home office or attached ADU) for business purposes. The
entity deducts the rent on its return. You exclude the rent income
on your return under §280A(g).

Net effect: business expense to the entity, no income to you. Up
to fourteen days at a defensible daily rate.

For an entity renting an ADU at $800/night for fourteen days, that's
$11,200 of business expense and $11,200 of excluded personal income.
At a 32% combined marginal rate at the entity's pass-through level,
the deduction is worth roughly $3,500 in tax savings.

The three-legged stool.

The deduction rests on three documentation items. Pull any one leg
out and the deduction collapses on examination.

Leg one. Reasonable rate. The rate paid by the entity must be
reasonable for the space and use. "Reasonable" is fact-specific;
the IRS has historically accepted rates in line with comparable
event venues, meeting spaces, and short-term rental rates for
similar capacity.

Leg two. Legitimate business purpose. The entity must have a real
reason to use the space. Board meetings. Strategy retreats.
Training sessions. Generic "office work" doesn't survive
scrutiny.

Leg three. Discrete days, contemporaneously documented. The
fourteen days must be specific dates with specific business
purposes, recorded as they happen. Reconstructing a calendar in
February to fit fourteen days into the year is the pattern that
gets disallowed.

[DEMONSTRATE — 5 min]

The Augusta Documentation Pack.

[Open the pack folder.]

Three templates ship with the course.

Template one. FMV Comparable-Rate Worksheet.

[Open the worksheet.]

Filled out before the first rental day of the year. Three
comparable venues — Peerspace, local hotel meeting room, Splacer
listing — with daily rates, dates observed, source citations.
Median of the three is your defensible rate. If your proposed rate
falls at or below the median, no further justification needed.

Template two. Board Minutes Template.

[Open the template.]

One filled copy per Augusta-rented day. Date, time, location,
attendees, agenda, decisions, resolutions. Specific business
agenda — not generic. Signed and dated the same day.

Template three. Personal-Use Calendar.

[Open the calendar.]

One row per day in the year. Each day marked as personal,
Augusta-rented, home-office only, mixed, or vacant. Annual totals
auto-summed. Compliance checks flag the moment the Augusta day
count exceeds fourteen.

The three together build the dossier. Filed in the audit dossier
under folder 13.

Who should not elect.

Sole proprietors. §280A(g) requires entity-to-homeowner transaction.
A sole proprietor renting their own home to themselves doesn't
work.

Hosts whose only entity is dormant. The entity needs to actually
have business activity that justifies meetings.

Hosts whose proposed rate is well above comparable-rate evidence.
If you can't substantiate the rate, don't take the deduction.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Identify whether you can elect this year.

If yes: open the Augusta Documentation Pack, fill out the FMV
worksheet first (before the first rental day), then build the
board-minute habit alongside your existing meeting cadence.

If no: skip Augusta. Don't elect just because the deduction looks
attractive. The deduction is small enough that the audit-defense
burden isn't worth carrying without legitimate facts.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 4.5 — The 14-day personal-use line

**Length:** 12 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "4.5 — The 14-Day Personal-Use Line"]

[VO]

One bad night flips your STR loophole into a vacation home.

§280A(d) — different from §280A(g) we covered in the last lesson —
limits rental deductions when personal use of a property exceeds a
threshold. Cross the threshold and the property is reclassified as a
"residence." Rental deductions are limited to rental income — no
loss allowed, no offset of active income.

For hosts using the STR loophole to recover tens of thousands of
dollars, this threshold is the cliff.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. The §280A(d) threshold — and why it's not a flat fourteen days.

Two. The repair-day carve-out under §280A(d)(2)(C).

Three. The contemporaneous calendar that defends the threshold.

Four. The mistake hosts make at year-end and how to avoid it.

[TEACH — 6 min]

The threshold.

§280A(d)(2) says a property is a "residence" if personal use during
the year exceeds the *greater of*:

  Fourteen days, OR
  Ten percent of the days the property is rented at fair rental
   value.

The word "greater" matters. Hosts often remember it as "fourteen
days" and stop there. That's wrong.

If the property rents for 200 nights, ten percent is 20 nights — and
the threshold is 20, not 14. You have more headroom.

If the property rents for 80 nights, ten percent is 8 nights — and
the threshold is still 14 (the greater of 14 or 8). Less headroom.

If the property rents for 365 nights — every night booked — ten
percent is 36.5 nights. The threshold rounds to 37.

Higher rental volume gives more headroom. Lower rental volume
narrows it.

The cliff.

If personal use crosses the threshold, the property is reclassified
as a residence under §280A(d)(2). Rental deductions are then limited
to rental income — meaning you can deduct expenses up to the income
amount, but not beyond. No loss is allowed; no offset of active
income via the STR loophole; no acceleration of losses via cost seg.

For a host using the STR loophole and cost seg to recover, say,
$60,000 of active-income offset, crossing the §280A(d)(2) threshold
costs roughly $20,000 in real tax dollars. Over a single overnight
stay.

The repair-day carve-out.

§280A(d)(2)(C) provides relief: days the host stays at the property
"principally for the purpose of rendering work performed in
connection with the rental" do not count as personal use.

Coordinating a deck rebuild for three days? Not personal. Painting
the bedrooms over a weekend? Not personal. Meeting the plumber for
a master-bath renovation? Not personal.

The phrase "principally for the purpose" is the test. If you go to
the property to coordinate repairs and incidentally enjoy the
property, the day is repair-day, not personal-use day. If you go to
the property for vacation and incidentally fix a doorknob, the day
is personal.

Document repair days the same way you document everything else —
contemporaneously. Photograph the repair work, save the contractor
invoices, log the time you spent on repair coordination. The
substantiation lives in the audit dossier.

[DEMONSTRATE — 4 min]

The contemporaneous calendar.

[Open the personal-use calendar from the Augusta Documentation
Pack.]

The calendar has one row per day in the year. Each day is marked as
personal use, repair day, rented, or vacant. The Excel version
auto-sums per category at year-end and flags compliance against
both §280A(d) (10% / 14-day rule for the rental property) and
§280A(g) (fourteen-day cap for Augusta on the personal residence).

[Walk through entering several days.]

Run the calendar weekly during the year. At the end of every quarter
review the running total. If you're approaching the threshold, you
have advance warning.

Most hosts who blow the threshold do so in late December — they
escape to the cabin for a week of relaxation between Christmas and
New Year, push past the threshold, and find out in March when they
calculate their return.

The fix is calendar awareness in October. Pull the year-to-date
personal-use total. Identify whether any planned year-end stays
will push you past the threshold. Cancel or relocate as needed.

The mistake.

The mistake hosts make at year-end is that they don't pull the
calendar. They reach December 28, look at their planned stays, and
estimate "yeah, I'm fine."

The estimate is often wrong by two to four days. By the time they
run the actual count in March, the threshold is already crossed.

The fix: pull the count on October 1, again on November 15, again on
December 15. Three checkpoints. Catches every year-end miscount
before it happens.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Open the personal-use calendar today and audit the last six
months.

Go day by day if you have to. Mark each day. If you find you're
already above threshold, that's a sober conversation with your CPA;
the rest of the year is constrained.

If you're under threshold, set the three calendar checkpoints —
October 1, November 15, December 15 — to pull the count and confirm
you stay under.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 4.6 — Stacking the levers

**Length:** 16 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "4.6 — Stacking the Levers"]

[VO]

Amanda's fifty-thousand-dollar year, deconstructed.

This is the lesson the book opens with. The chapter that promised
$50,000 of legitimately-earned deductions on a three-cabin portfolio
in a single year. We've spent five modules building toward the
mechanics. Now we run the math.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. Amanda's portfolio specifics.

Two. The four levers stacked: cost seg + §168(k) bonus + STR
loophole + Augusta.

Three. The order-of-operations that determines whether the math
works.

Four. The substantiation file that defends every dollar.

[TEACH — 8 min]

The portfolio.

Three cabins. Average acquisition basis $640,000 each. Average
rental period 4 nights — STR loophole eligible. Material
participation documented across all three properties; Amanda's
annual hours total 487, well clear of the 500-hour test
individually but clearing the 100-hour-and-most-participation test
on each property separately.

Day-job income: $185,000 W-2 base salary. Combined federal and
state marginal rate: 32%.

Lever one. Operating expenses.

Standard Schedule E line items across three properties. Cleaning,
utilities, supplies, repairs, insurance, mortgage interest,
property tax. Annual total: roughly $94,000 across three properties.
Standard depreciation pre-cost-seg: roughly $52,500 across three
(MACRS 27.5-year on $480,000 depreciable basis × 3, year-one).

Operating expenses + standard depreciation ≈ $146,500 of deductions.

Against $156,000 of rental income (52,000/cabin × 3), this leaves
roughly $9,500 of net rental income before the big levers.

Lever two. Cost segregation + §168(k) bonus.

Cost-seg studies on all three cabins. Reclassifies about 22% of
basis on each — roughly $105,000 per cabin into shorter classes.

§168(k) bonus at the current rate accelerates a substantial portion
of that into year one. Across three cabins, year-one bonus
acceleration totals roughly $189,000.

The cost-seg studies cost about $13,500 total — separate §162
deduction. Engineering studies fully deductible in the year paid.

Lever two adds $189,000 of acceleration plus $13,500 of study
costs. Total: $202,500.

Lever three. The STR loophole.

The acceleration loss is meaningful — $193,000 minus $9,500 of net
rental income = $193,000 of loss after applying operating
deductions. Without the STR loophole, this would be a passive loss,
suspended.

With the STR loophole — average rental period 4 nights, material
participation documented — the loss is nonpassive. It offsets W-2
```
