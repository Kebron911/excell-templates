## Lesson 5.1 — What an audit looks like

**Length:** 12 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "5.1 — What an Audit Looks Like"]

[VO]

An IDR is a letter. The letter is not the audit. The audit is what
you put in the response.

The word "audit" carries dread that's mostly cinematic. STR Schedule
E examinations rarely involve an examiner walking through a home or
demanding a sit-down meeting. Most are correspondence examinations
— a letter arrives, you respond with documents, the IRS reviews and
either closes the case or asks follow-up questions.

This lesson walks through what actually happens.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. The IRS examination flow — DIF score, IDR, response, closure.

Two. The 30-day timeline that controls the response.

Three. Correspondence vs. office vs. field examinations.

Four. What hosts do wrong on the first response.

[TEACH — 6 min]

The flow.

Step one. Selection. The IRS Examination Division selects returns
through a combination of computer-scoring (the Discriminant
Inventory Function score, or DIF score) and information-matching
mismatches. A return with depreciation deductions, large repair
expenses, or a Schedule E loss against active income may score
higher than average.

Step two. Notification. The IRS sends a letter. The letter is
typically Letter 525, 692, or 915, depending on the type of
examination. The letter tells you:

  Tax year(s) being examined
  Areas of focus (e.g., "your repairs deduction" or "vehicle
   expense" or "passive vs. nonpassive treatment of rental losses")
  Documents requested (an Information Document Request, or IDR)
  Response deadline — typically 30 days

Step three. Response. The taxpayer (or representative — CPA, EA,
attorney) submits the requested documents and a written response.

Step four. Examiner review. The examiner reviews the response,
either closes the case ("no change"), proposes adjustments, or
requests additional documents.

Step five. Resolution. Closure with no change, agreed adjustment
(taxpayer signs Form 870), or contested adjustment (taxpayer
appeals).

The 30-day timeline.

The 30-day clock starts on the date of the letter, not the date
you opened it. Hosts who let the letter sit on the kitchen counter
for ten days lose a third of the response window before they begin
working.

If the 30 days isn't enough — and it often isn't, especially for
hosts who weren't running a clean dossier — the taxpayer can
request an extension. Extensions are routinely granted for
reasonable cause, but they're not automatic; the request needs to
be made before the original deadline.

Correspondence vs. office vs. field.

Correspondence examination. Mail-only. Most STR Schedule E
examinations are correspondence. Resolved without an in-person
meeting.

Office examination. The taxpayer (or representative) meets with the
examiner at an IRS office. Typically reserved for more complex
returns or when correspondence response was inadequate.

Field examination. The examiner comes to the taxpayer's place of
business. Reserved for the largest, most complex examinations —
generally not STR Schedule E unless the portfolio is very large or
the issues are unusual.

If correspondence escalates to office or field, the case is more
serious. Engage representation immediately.

[DEMONSTRATE — 4 min]

[Show a redacted IDR on screen.]

Sample IDR for an STR Schedule E examination:

  Tax year: 2024
  Areas of focus:
    1. Repairs deduction on Schedule E line 14 ($8,432 claimed)
    2. Vehicle expense on Schedule E line 6 ($4,202 claimed)
    3. Passive vs. nonpassive treatment under §469(c)(7)

  Documents requested:
    A. All vendor invoices and proof of payment for line 14 repairs
    B. Mileage log with §274(d) elements (amount, time, place,
       purpose)
    C. Documentation of average rental period for each property
    D. Documentation of material participation hours

  Response deadline: 30 days from date of letter

The IDR is specific. The examiner has identified three issues. The
documents requested are exactly the documents your audit dossier
should already contain.

Hosts running the system from Module 5.2 onward can respond by
forwarding folder 04 (repairs), folder 05 (mileage), and folder
14 (material participation) — plus a cover letter pointing to
each. Total response time: an hour.

Hosts who haven't been running a dossier face days or weeks of
reconstruction.

What hosts do wrong on the first response.

Mistake one. Sending too much. Hosts dump every receipt for the
year and hope the volume looks substantiating. It looks the
opposite — disorganized, undocumented. The examiner sees a
shoebox.

Mistake two. Sending too little. Sending only a summary spreadsheet
with no underlying receipts. The examiner has nothing to verify.

Mistake three. Responding from emotion. Long letters arguing the
deductions, defending intent, explaining context. Examiners read
documents, not narratives.

The right response: a one-page cover letter, the requested
documents organized in the IDR's order, and a brief "let me know
if you need anything else." Calm, organized, complete.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Pull your last 3 returns into a folder this week. Mark them with
tax year. Keep them where you can find them on 24-hour notice.

If an IDR ever arrives, you have triage time before the 30-day
clock becomes a problem.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 5.2 — The audit dossier

**Length:** 14 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "5.2 — The Audit Dossier"]

[VO]

If it's not in a folder a CPA can find in sixty seconds, the IRS
doesn't care that you remember it.

The dossier is the tangible defense layer. Every line on your return
paired with the substantiation that supports it, organized in the
order an examiner reads.

The dossier is built once per tax year. It snapshots the year. After
filing, it's archived and never reopened unless an IDR arrives.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. The §6001 retention period and what triggers each window.

Two. The 14-folder structure of the Audit Dossier Template.

Three. The master index — single source of truth.

Four. How the dossier is built throughout the year, not at year-end.

[TEACH — 5 min]

§6001 retention.

§6501(a) sets the standard examination period at 3 years from the
filing date. §6501(e) extends it to 6 years if the return omits more
than 25% of gross income. §6501(c) makes it indefinite for fraud or
no return filed.

Practical retention: 7 years from filing. Three years standard, six
years if substantial omission, plus a one-year buffer.

Some hosts retain indefinitely. The marginal cost of cloud storage
is negligible relative to the cost of having to reconstruct.

The 14-folder structure.

The Audit Dossier Template (Bonus, also in /03-templates/audit-
dossier/) ships with 14 numbered subfolders:

  01 — Return and supporting forms
  02 — Bookkeeping snapshot (TAX-002, TAX-004 frozen at filing)
  03 — Receipts (Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4)
  04 — Repairs vs. improvements (BAR-test notes)
  05 — Vehicle and mileage
  06 — Travel, meals, lodging (§274(d) overnight trips)
  07 — Contractors and 1099s
  08 — Cleaning fees reconciliation
  09 — Depreciation and cost seg
  10 — Home office
  11 — Insurance
  12 — Entity and legal
  13 — Augusta §280A(g)
  14 — Material participation

Plus 99 — Correspondence (IRS notices, CPA correspondence — only
populated if triggered).

The numbers are deliberate. Examiners read in order — return first,
bookkeeping next, then drill into the lines they're questioning.
The dossier is in the order they read.

The master index.

00-index.xlsx sits at the top of the dossier. Each row is a Schedule
E line; each row points to the sub-folder and file(s) that
substantiate that line.

Status flags:

  ✓ Fully substantiated
  ◐ Partially substantiated (action needed before filing)
  ✗ No substantiation (drop the deduction or build it now)
  – Not applicable
  ? Substantiation exists but position is uncertain (CPA review)

A clean dossier ships with zero ✗ flags. ✗ flags mean the deduction
is at risk on examination.

The build cadence.

The dossier is not a year-end project. It builds throughout the
year:

  Receipt photos go directly into folder 03 — Receipts at capture
  Mileage log lives in folder 05 — Vehicle, updated daily
  W-9s go into folder 07 — Contractors at engagement
  BAR-test notes get written into folder 04 — Repairs whenever an
   item over $1,000 hits the workbook
  Augusta board minutes go into folder 13 — Augusta the same day
   as the meeting
  Time-log lives in folder 14 — Material participation, updated
   weekly

If Module 2's capture system is running, the dossier is roughly
80% built by year-end automatically. The Year-End Health Check
(Bonus, Module 7) finishes the remaining 20% — snapshots, index
finalization, return-to-substantiation tieout.

[DEMONSTRATE — 7 min]

[Open an empty Audit Dossier Template folder. Walk through
populating it for a sample year.]

Open folder 01. Drop in: schedule-e-as-filed.pdf, form-4562-
depreciation.pdf, form-1099-k-airbnb.pdf, form-1098-mortgage.pdf,
property-tax-statement.pdf.

Open folder 02. Drop in: TAX-002-final.xlsx, TAX-004-final.xlsx,
reconciliation-summary.pdf.

Open folder 03/Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4. Drop in receipts named per the
convention: YYYY-MM-DD-vendor-amount.jpg.

Open folder 04. Drop in: repairs-line-14-summary.pdf and any
bar-test-notes/ for items over $1,000.

[Continue through each folder, briefly.]

Now open the master index. 00-index.xlsx.

[Walk through the index.]

Each Schedule E line has a row. Each row points to a folder. Each
row carries a status flag.

Sample entry. Line 14 — Repairs: $8,432.
  Substantiation: 04/repairs-line-14-summary.pdf
  Plus: 04/bar-test-notes/ for items >$1,000
  Plus: 03/Q1-Q4/ matching receipts
  Status: ✓

The index is the document a CPA — or an examiner — receives first.
Everything else is just navigation.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Build the folder shell this week. Don't try to populate every
folder; just create the 14-subfolder structure on your cloud
storage.

For each folder, drop a single placeholder file ("README.md" or
"this-folder-is-empty.txt") so the structure is visible.

When Module 2's capture system has been running for a quarter, the
folders start auto-filling. The shell is the precondition.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 5.3 — The repairs-vs-improvements defense

**Length:** 12 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "5.3 — The Repairs-vs-Improvements Defense"]

[VO]

The §263(a) determination must be made by you, not the examiner.

If you treat a $1,140 dishwasher replacement as a current-year
repair on your return, the examiner will ask you to justify why
it isn't a capital improvement. Your answer needs to be in the
folder, not in your memory.

The BAR-test paper trail is the defense.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. What the §263(a) determination requires you to document.

Two. The BAR-test note — the one-paragraph defense.

Three. The de-minimis safe harbor election as a backstop.

Four. Building the file for an examination.

[TEACH — 5 min]

The §263(a) determination.

§263(a) and the §1.263(a)-3 regulations require capitalization for
expenditures that result in betterment, adaptation, or restoration.
The host claims a current-year repair on Schedule E line 14. The
examiner's job is to verify the BAR test was applied.

To defend the position, the host needs:

  The vendor invoice
  Proof of payment
  A BAR-test note explaining why the expenditure failed all three
   prongs (or why a safe harbor applied)

The third item is the one most hosts skip. The invoice and payment
prove the expense happened; the BAR-test note proves the
classification was made deliberately.

The BAR-test note.

A one-paragraph note in plain English. Date, item, dollar amount,
and the analysis:

Sample. April 14, 2026. $1,140 dishwasher replacement at Cabin A
(unit failed mid-stay; replaced with like-capacity model).

Analysis: Like-kind replacement of a failed appliance. Not a
betterment — no improvement in capacity, quality, or productivity
of the property as a whole. Not an adaptation — no change in the
property's use. Not a restoration — replacement of a single
appliance is not the replacement of a major component or
substantial structural part of the property under
§1.263(a)-3(k)(1)(vi). Backup: de-minimis safe harbor under
§1.263(a)-1(f) at $2,500-per-invoice threshold. Treated as
current expense on Schedule E line 14.

That's the note. 4-6 sentences. Saved as a PDF in folder 04 of the
audit dossier.

The de-minimis safe harbor as backstop.

§1.263(a)-1(f) — the de-minimis safe harbor — provides a clean
fallback for items under $2,500 per invoice. If you've elected the
safe harbor on a timely-filed return (the election is made by
attaching a statement to the return), every qualifying item under
$2,500 expenses without BAR-test analysis.

The election is annual; once made, it applies to all qualifying
purchases for that year. Most STR hosts should make the election
every year — it eliminates BAR-test arguments on small items and
costs nothing.

[DEMONSTRATE — 5 min]

[Open folder 04 of a sample audit dossier.]

Two files inside.

File one. repairs-line-14-summary.pdf.

[Show the summary.]

A one-page summary. Lists every line-14 expenditure in
chronological order. Vendor, date, dollar amount, brief
description, link to the BAR-test note (if over $1,000) or
"de-minimis safe harbor" (if under $2,500).

Total at the bottom ties to Schedule E line 14.

File two. The bar-test-notes/ folder.

[Show subfolder contents.]

One PDF per item over $1,000. Named: YYYY-MM-DD-item-bar-test.pdf.
Each PDF is the BAR-test note we walked through above.

For the sample portfolio: 17 line-14 expenditures totaling $8,432.
3 of them are over $1,000 — the dishwasher, a $2,140 partial roof
repair, an $1,820 hot-water heater. Each has its own BAR-test note.
The other 14 are under the de-minimis safe harbor and don't need
individual notes.

The total dossier-build time per year: maybe 30 minutes for the BAR
notes plus 5 minutes for the summary.

The defense in practice.

When the IDR arrives asking about line 14, the response is:

  Cover letter pointing to folder 04
  repairs-line-14-summary.pdf as the table of contents
  bar-test-notes/ as the per-item analysis
  Receipts already in folder 03 by date

The examiner can verify each item in under 5 minutes. The case
typically closes with no change.

The contrast: a host without BAR-test notes responds with a
spreadsheet of expenditures. The examiner asks "why did you treat
the dishwasher as a repair instead of capitalizing?" The host has
to explain in real time, often years after the fact, with memory
gaps. Some adjustments inevitably stick.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Pick one item over $1,000 from last year's line 14. Write the
BAR-test note now.

If you can't write the note clearly — either because you don't
remember the item or because you're not sure it should have been
expensed — that's a signal. Either reconstruct with corroborating
evidence (the receipt, the contractor's notes) or amend the return
to capitalize the item.

The note-writing exercise itself is a self-audit for past
classifications.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 5.4 — The material-participation defense

**Length:** 12 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "5.4 — The Material-Participation Defense"]

[VO]

Four logs hold up. Three logs don't.

If you're using the STR loophole — treating rental losses as
nonpassive against active income — the material-participation
position is the highest-stakes defense in your dossier. Cost-seg
acceleration, §168(k) bonus, and the loophole's $50,000+ of annual
tax savings all rest on it.

The four-log evidence stack is what makes the position defensible.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson:

One. §1.469-5T(f)(4) — substantiation by "any reasonable means."

Two. The four logs that hold up.

Three. What three logs (or two, or one) look like — and why they
don't.

Four. The defense in an examination response.

[TEACH — 5 min]

The substantiation rule.

§1.469-5T(f)(4) allows participation to be established "by any
reasonable means." The regulation explicitly does not require
contemporaneous time records as the sole proof.

That sentence has been the source of confusion. Hosts read it and
conclude they don't need contemporaneous records. They're wrong.

The "any reasonable means" language opens the door to alternative
substantiation — calendar entries, vendor invoices, reservation
records — but tax courts have generally favored taxpayers who pair
those alternatives WITH contemporaneous logs. The pairing is the
defense; either one alone is weaker.

The four logs.

Log one. Time log.

One row per activity, contemporaneous. Date, activity, duration,
property. Annual total clearly visible. Categorization that can
withstand the §1.469-5T(f)(4) "any reasonable means" standard.

Sample entry: 2026-04-15, scheduling cleaner for Saturday turnover
+ confirming with guest re early check-in, 35 minutes, Cabin A.

The time log is the headline document. Hours total clearly visible.

Log two. Correspondence log.

Emails, texts, messages with vendors, guests, contractors. Time-
stamped, contemporaneous. Don't print all of them — keep them in a
searchable archive (a Gmail folder, an Airtable base, an SMS
backup) and reference the volume in the time log.

Sample entry: "See Gmail folder STR-Cabin-A. Annual volume: ~3,200
emails. Sample dates pulled in subfolder /samples/ — May 14 plumber
coordination, June 22 cleaner replacement, October 8 deck-rebuild
decision."

The correspondence log is the proof that the host is actively
involved in the operation, not a passive owner.

Log three. Decision log.

Choices the host made — pricing strategy adjustments, vendor
changes, marketing decisions, capital improvements approved. One
row per decision, dated.

Sample entries:
  2026-03-14: Increased Cabin A nightly rate $189 → $215 based on
   PriceLabs analysis showing market shift.
  2026-06-22: Engaged new cleaner (J. Martinez) after prior cleaner
   moved out of state.
  2026-10-08: Approved deck rebuild at Cabin C ($4,800) over patch
   repair after structural inspection.

The decision log proves the host is the operator, not just the
title-holder.

Log four. Reservation log.

Booking calendar plus the host's involvement in each booking —
pre-stay messaging, mid-stay support, post-stay review.

Most platform calendars provide the bookings; the host's
involvement is what makes it a participation log. A host who
exports the Airbnb reservation history and adds a column for
"messages sent / time spent per booking" has the participation
evidence.

[DEMONSTRATE — 5 min]

[Open folder 14 of a sample audit dossier.]

Four files inside.

  time-log-final.xlsx
  correspondence-log.xlsx (pointer document, not full export)
  decision-log.xlsx
  reservation-log.xlsx

[Open the time log.]

Sample annual total: 487 hours across three properties.

By property: Cabin A 168 hours, Cabin B 142 hours, Cabin C 177
hours.

By activity category: guest communication 124 hours, vendor
coordination 138 hours, property visits 96 hours, bookkeeping 42
hours, pricing/revenue 38 hours, marketing 24 hours, education
25 hours.

The host clears Test 1 (500 hours) by aggregating across all three
properties under the §469(c)(7) election to treat all rental real
estate as a single activity. (If electing not to aggregate, each
property is tested individually under Test 3 — 100+ hours and most
participation. All three clear.)

Three logs versus four.

Without the correspondence log: the time log looks invented. No
external evidence corroborates the hours.

Without the decision log: the host looks like a passive recipient
of contracted services rather than the operator making business
decisions.

Without the reservation log: there's no proof the host is
involved in the actual rental activity, as opposed to only the
back-office.

Three logs survive examination weaker than four. Two logs survive
examination at substantial risk. One log (just the time log) often
gets disallowed under the §1.469-5T(f)(4) reasonable-means
standard, especially if the time log looks reconstructed.

The defense.

When the IDR asks for material-participation evidence, the response
is:

  Cover letter pointing to folder 14
  time-log-final.xlsx as the headline
  correspondence-log.xlsx pointing to the Gmail / messaging archive
  decision-log.xlsx with the year's strategic decisions
  reservation-log.xlsx with the booking-level involvement

A complete response. The examiner reviews and either closes or
asks specific follow-up questions on a specific entry.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Start the time log today.

If you've been operating without a log, you cannot fully
reconstruct the prior year. Start today and run forward. For the
prior year, document what you can — calendar entries, email
volume, decision history — as best-efforts evidence.

If your hour count appears tight (under 200), set a phone reminder
to log every weekday's STR activity. Most hosts under-estimate
their hours by 30-50% before tracking.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```

## Lesson 5.5 — The tabletop drill

> **Full long-form script in [00-course-plan.md §8.4](../00-course-plan.md). Reproduced below for completeness.**

**Length:** 14 min

```
[OPEN — title card: "5.5 — Tabletop Drill"]

[VO]

We're going to simulate the conversation.

I'm the examiner. You're the host. The IRS has selected your 2024
return for correspondence examination. The Information Document
Request, the IDR, asked for substantiation on three items: your
repairs deduction, your vehicle expense, and your material-
participation claim under §469(c)(7).

I'm going to ask the questions an examiner actually asks. After
each one, I'll pause for ten seconds. Pause the video, answer out
loud, then keep playing to hear the model answer. The point isn't
to score perfect — it's to find the gaps in your dossier before
the examiner does.

[FRAME — 30s]

Pull up the Tabletop Drill Worksheet from your bonus folder.
Fifteen questions. Score yourself a one if you have full
documentation, a half-point if you have partial documentation, and
zero if you have nothing.

A perfect score is fifteen. Below twelve and there's a gap to fill
before filing the next return.

Begin.

[QUESTIONS — 12 min, alternating examiner / model answer cadence]

Question one. Walk me through the eight-thousand-four-hundred-
dollar repairs deduction on Schedule E line 14.

[10s pause]

Model answer: "I have a folder labeled '2024 Repairs.' Inside are
seventeen line items, each with a vendor invoice, a payment record
from my dedicated property card, and a one-line description of the
repair purpose. The total sums to $8,432 with rounding. I've also
flagged any item over a thousand dollars with the §263(a) BAR test
result and a note explaining why I treated it as a current expense
rather than capitalized."

What that answer demonstrates: contemporaneous records. Vendor
verification. Self-administered §263(a) test.

[Continue through 14 more questions. Categories:]

Q2.  Vehicle expense — show me the §274(d) substantiation for the
     6,420 miles claimed.
Q3.  Material participation — what's your hour total and how was
     it logged?
Q4.  Substantial services — what services were provided, when, by
     whom?
Q5.  Average rental period — how do you compute it?
Q6.  The dishwasher replacement — repair or improvement, and why?
Q7.  Cleaning fees — gross income or net? Show the reconciliation.
Q8.  1099-NEC — show me each contractor over $600 and the
     corresponding W-9.
Q9.  Home office — exclusive use. Convince me.
Q10. Augusta Rule election — show me the 14 days, the FMV
     comparable, the board minutes.
Q11. Cost segregation — show me the study or the engineering
     basis.
Q12. Bonus depreciation — what was placed in service, when, and
     where on the depreciation schedule?
Q13. Personal use — how many nights did you stay there? Show the
     calendar.
Q14. Insurance — was the premium business or personal? Show the
     policy.
Q15. Education expense — show me how this course relates to the
     trade or business under §162.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Score yourself out of 15. Post your score in the community channel
anonymously if you prefer. The class median is 9 on the first
attempt. Hosts who run the drill twice — once now, once after
building the dossier in 5.2 — typically score 13 or 14 the second
time. That's not a brag — it's the gap closing.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s]

[OUT]
```
