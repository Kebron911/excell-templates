## 1. Why a course (and why this one)

The book teaches *what* the 47 deductions are. Buyers finish it inspired and 80% never act, because reading a tax book and running a tax system are different skills. The funnel today drops them into a 21-day nurture and offers workbooks à la carte; the missing rung is a **paid, supervised installation** — the equivalent of "I'll sit next to you until your portfolio is on the system."

The course earns its price by collapsing the gap between *reading about Amanda's seven-minute tax tab* and *being Amanda*. It is not a re-recorded book. It is the workshop the book points at.

### What the course is not

- Not a chapter-by-chapter video reading of the book
- Not a CPA replacement (every module ends with the same disclaimer the book uses)
- Not a tax-season-only course — built for year-round operating cadence
- Not a beginner's intro to STR investing — assumes 1+ door already operating

### What it is

- A 7-module guided installation of the recordkeeping pact, the 47-deduction checklist, the quarterly cadence, and the CPA handoff
- ~9 hours of structured video + ~6 hours of optional deep-dive
- A complete bundle of the existing TAX-* and GST-* workbooks ($300+ value at retail) integrated as the course's working layer
- Purpose-built tools the book can't carry — interactive decision trees, audit-defense drills, scenario library, an Airtable/Notion mirror for hosts who don't live in Excel
- Optional cohort wrap with live Q&A and a CPA panel call

## 8. Video script library

Every lesson has a script. Scripts use a consistent 5-beat structure so they're fast to write, fast to record, and fast to re-shoot when something changes.

### 8.1 — Script structure (every lesson)

```
1. HOOK            (15–25 sec)  The tension. One number, one stakes line.
2. FRAME           (30–45 sec)  What we're going to do in this lesson, and what we're not.
3. TEACH           (3–6 min)    The core lesson. Always tied to a code citation or a workbook tab.
4. DEMONSTRATE     (1–3 min)    Screen-share or worked example. Show, don't tell.
5. ASSIGNMENT      (20–40 sec)  The one action the viewer takes before the next lesson.
                   + DISCLAIMER (10 sec, on-screen card, not voiced after Module 0)
```

**Voice rules** (inherited from `brand-decisions.md` §6):
- Editorial-finance register. Calm authority.
- Specific dollar figures. Specific code citations.
- No exclamation points except in dialogue.
- No emoji.
- "Host," "operator," "portfolio." Never "side hustler."
- Disclaimer cadence at the close of every Part-II/Module-3+ lesson.

### 8.2 — Production specs

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Format | 1920×1080, 30fps, H.264 |
| Audio | 48kHz, mono voiceover at -16 LUFS |
| Voiceover | Faceless: AI voice (ElevenLabs, premium voice) OR contracted female VO matching persona register; founder-arm: Daniel on-camera |
| B-roll / visuals | Screen recordings (workbook-first), keynote slides in brand palette (Harbor Navy + Parchment + Muted Gold), no stock footage |
| Lower-thirds & cards | Cormorant Garamond display + Inter body, gold rule motif, monogram corner watermark |
| Hosting | Vimeo Pro (private, embed-only) — or Mux for analytics + DRM at scale |
| Captions | Burned-in optional + .vtt sidecar required (accessibility + watch-rate boost) |

#### Sample 1 — Lesson 1.1 (Schedule E or Schedule C)

```
[OPEN — title card: "1.1 — Schedule E or Schedule C"]

[VO]

Eight thousand four hundred and twenty-seven dollars.

That's what one host — call her Sarah — left on the table the year she filed
Schedule C when she should have filed Schedule E. Self-employment tax she
didn't owe. A fifteen-point-three-percent surcharge on a portfolio that wasn't
self-employment income in the first place.

Sarah is a composite. The number is real. The decision behind it is binary,
and most hosts make it on autopilot.

[FRAME — 30s]

In this lesson we're going to run the Schedule E versus Schedule C decision tree
on your own portfolio. We're not going to debate the tax theory in the
abstract — the book did that in Chapter One. We're going to use the Entity
Decision Flowchart from your bonus folder, answer five questions, and produce a
recommendation by the end of the next four minutes.

Pull up the flowchart now. The PDF lives in your bundle, in the folder labeled
04-decision-trees, file name "01-schedule-e-or-c.pdf."

[TEACH — 4 min]

The IRS treats short-term rental income one of two ways. Schedule E says you're
a landlord — passive activity, no self-employment tax. Schedule C says you're
running a hospitality business — active income, fifteen-point-three percent
self-employment tax on top of regular income tax.

The line between the two is not the rental period alone. It's substantial
services. The IRS, in Publication 527 and in §1.469-1T(e)(3), points to two
tests that decide the question.

Test one: average rental period. Seven days or fewer, you're outside the
default Schedule E lane. Eight to thirty days with substantial services, also
out of the default lane. Thirty-one days or more, you're firmly in Schedule E.

Test two: substantial services. Daily housekeeping during a stay. Meals
provided. Concierge. Tour booking. The kind of services a hotel provides, not
the kind a landlord provides. Note the word "during." A clean between guests is
not substantial. A clean every morning during a guest's stay is.

Most STR hosts run an average rental period between three and seven days, with
no in-stay services. That puts them on Schedule E — passive rental activity,
no SE tax. Some hosts run shorter average rental periods with white-glove
services and qualify for, or are forced into, Schedule C.

The decision is not optional. It is a function of how you operate.

[DEMONSTRATE — 2 min]

Open the flowchart. The first decision node asks for your average rental
period across the prior twelve months. To compute it, take total rental days
and divide by total bookings. If that number is seven or below, you proceed
down the left branch. Eight to thirty, the middle branch. Thirty-one and up,
the right branch.

The next node asks about substantial services. Walk through the four
checkboxes. If you check none, you stay on the Schedule E recommendation. If
you check two or more, the flowchart routes you to a "consult a tax
professional" outcome — that's not us hedging, it's the genuine fact-pattern
question that requires a CPA's review.

I'm going to run it on Amanda's portfolio. Three cabins, average rental period
of four nights, no in-stay services. Four nights routes left. No services
keeps us on Schedule E. Recommendation: Schedule E.

[ASSIGNMENT — 30s]

Run the flowchart on your own portfolio. If the result matches what you've
been filing, you're done. If it doesn't, flag it — bring the discrepancy to
Module Six, where we cover the CPA handoff.

If your CPA has been filing you on Schedule C and the flowchart says
Schedule E, you have a question to ask. We'll script that question in 6.2.

[ON-SCREEN DISCLAIMER CARD — 10s, no VO]

This lesson is general information, not tax advice. State law varies and
facts-and-circumstances determinations like substantial services should be
reviewed with a qualified tax professional before filing.

[OUT]
```
