# Bonus B2 — The Audit Tabletop Drill

**Format:** 60-min interactive video + this printable worksheet
**Course module:** Module 5 (Lesson 5.5)
**Use case:** Pressure-test your audit dossier before the IRS does

> **Run this drill twice.** Once before you build the dossier (Lesson 5.2), once after. The gap between the two scores is the value the course delivered.

---

## Setup

You are the host. The IRS has selected your prior-year Schedule E for correspondence examination. The Information Document Request (IDR) lists three areas of focus:

1. **Repairs deduction** (Schedule E line 14)
2. **Vehicle expense** (line 6)
3. **Material participation claim** under §469(c)(7) — the STR loophole

The video walks through 15 questions an examiner actually asks. Pause after each question, answer out loud or write your answer, then resume the video to hear the model answer.

This worksheet captures your score and the gap-list.

---

## Scoring

For each question:

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **1** | Full documentation. You can produce the records, organized, on demand. |
| **½** | Partial. You have the data but it's scattered or incomplete. |
| **0** | Nothing. You can't substantiate. |

A perfect score is 15. Below 12 = gaps to fill before next filing.

## The 15 questions

### Q1 — The repairs deduction

**Examiner:** *"Walk me through the $8,432 repairs deduction on Schedule E line 14. I'd like to see the supporting records."*

**What you should be able to produce:**
- A folder labeled "20XX Repairs"
- One sub-record per line item: vendor invoice, payment record (check, credit card statement, ACH confirmation), a one-line description of the repair purpose
- For any single item over $1,000, a §263(a) BAR-test note explaining why you treated it as a current expense
- A running total that matches Schedule E line 14

**Model answer:** *"I have a folder called '2024 Repairs' with seventeen line items. Each has the vendor invoice, the credit-card statement showing payment from the dedicated property card, and a brief note describing the repair. The total sums to $8,432 with rounding. Items over $1,000 — the dishwasher replacement and the deck rail repair — have a BAR-test note explaining the current-expense treatment under §1.263(a)-3."*

**Your score:** ☐ 1   ☐ ½   ☐ 0

### Q2 — Vehicle expense substantiation

**Examiner:** *"Schedule E line 6 shows $4,202 in vehicle expense — 6,420 miles at the standard rate. I need to see the §274(d) substantiation."*

**What you should be able to produce:**
- A contemporaneous mileage log (TAX-001) with one row per business trip
- Each row: date, start/end odometer, miles, business purpose, destination
- Logged within the week of the trip, not reconstructed at year-end

**Model answer:** *"My mileage log is in the workbook tab labeled 'Vehicle Expense.' Each trip has the date, start odometer, end odometer, miles, business purpose, and destination. Trips were logged the week they occurred — the most recent log entry is dated within ten days of the year-end. The IRS standard rate for the year is $0.655. 6,420 × $0.655 = $4,205.10, which rounds to the $4,202 reported."*

**Your score:** ☐ 1   ☐ ½   ☐ 0

**Gap to close:** _______________________________________________

### Q3 — Material participation hours

**Examiner:** *"You're claiming the rental loss is nonpassive under §469(c)(7). Show me your hour total and how those hours were logged."*

**What you should be able to produce:**
- A time log with one row per activity, contemporaneous
- Each row: date, activity, duration, property
- Annual total clearly visible
- Categorization that can withstand the §1.469-5T(f)(4) "any reasonable means" standard

**Model answer:** *"My time log is in a separate workbook tab. 487 hours for the year across all three properties. Each entry has the date, the activity — guest communication, vendor coordination, repairs, decision-making — duration, and which property it relates to. Logged the same day or within the week. Under §1.469-5T(a)(7), I qualify as materially participating because I devoted more than 100 hours and more than any other person, including my cleaning vendor and my handyman."*

**Your score:** ☐ 1   ☐ ½   ☐ 0

### Q4 — Substantial services claim

**Examiner:** *"Walk me through the services provided to guests during their stays. Daily housekeeping? Meals? Concierge?"*

**What you should be able to produce:**
- Description of services provided
- Distinction between services provided *between* stays (not substantial — typical landlord) versus *during* stays (potentially substantial — moves toward Schedule C)
- Documentation of which side of the line your operation falls on

**Model answer:** *"Standard turnover cleaning between stays. Welcome basket and a printed welcome book on arrival. No daily housekeeping during the stay. No meals provided. Guest messaging support during the stay but no in-person concierge. These are not substantial services under §1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A). The operation is properly classified as rental, reported on Schedule E."*

**Your score:** ☐ 1   ☐ ½   ☐ 0

### Q5 — Average rental period

**Examiner:** *"What's the average rental period for each property, and how did you compute it?"*

**What you should be able to produce:**
- Per-property total nights rented
- Per-property total bookings

All three are under the 7-day threshold for the rental real-estate per-se passive rule, which is what allows the §469(c)(7) treatment of losses as nonpassive when combined with material participation."*

### Q6 — Repairs vs. improvements judgment

**Examiner:** *"The dishwasher replacement was $1,140. Why current-expense rather than capitalized?"*

**What you should be able to produce:**
- The §263(a) BAR-test note from your repairs file
- Explanation tied to the regulation
- Reference to a safe harbor if applicable

**Model answer:** *"Like-kind replacement of a failed appliance. Under the §263(a)-3 BAR test, it's not a Betterment (no improvement in capacity or quality), not an Adaptation (no change in use), and not a Restoration (no rebuild after a casualty, no replacement of a major structural component). The unit replaces a failed component and returns the property to its operating condition. I also elected the de-minimis safe harbor under §1.263(a)-1(f) at the $2,500-per-invoice threshold, which provides an alternate route to the same current-expense result."*

**Your score:** ☐ 1   ☐ ½   ☐ 0

**Gap to close:** _______________________________________________

### Q7 — Cleaning fee gross/net

**Examiner:** *"Your gross income on line 3 is $94,200. Your 1099-K from Airbnb shows $94,200 — which matches. But your bank deposits total only $74,840. Reconcile."*

**What you should be able to produce:**
- Reconciliation: gross income → host fees → cleaning fee deductions → net deposits
- Each component sourced (Airbnb statement, cleaner invoices, bank deposits)

**Model answer:** *"Gross income $94,200 matches the 1099-K. Airbnb host service fees of $2,826 are deducted on line 19. Cleaning fees collected from guests, $11,400, were paid through to the cleaner — the cleaner expense on line 7 is $10,800; the $600 difference is a cleaning supplies subtotal that flows to line 17. Net to the bank: $94,200 minus $2,826 minus $11,400 plus the $600 supplies (which I paid directly, not via cleaner) equals approximately $80,574 — the remaining gap to the $74,840 bank-deposit figure is a $5,734 platform-held escrow that hadn't released by year-end. I have the Airbnb transaction history showing each component."*

### Q10 — Augusta Rule

**Examiner:** *"You excluded $11,200 in rental income under §280A(g) — the Augusta Rule. Walk me through the documentation."*

**What you should be able to produce:**
- Calendar of the 14 days
- FMV substantiation (comparable rate analysis)
- Board minutes or business-purpose documentation for each rental day
- Rent invoice/receipt showing the entity paid the homeowner

**Model answer:** *"14 days at $800 per day. The calendar shows the 14 specific dates — two two-day strategy retreats in February and August, plus ten quarterly board-meeting days spread across the year. FMV comparable rate was established by surveying three local meeting-space and event-rental venues at similar capacity; the $800 rate falls at the median. Board minutes documenting the business purpose, attendees, and agenda for each meeting are in the entity's corporate-records folder. The S-corporation paid the rent to me as the homeowner; I excluded the $11,200 under §280A(g) on my Schedule E; the entity deducted the rent on its 1120-S as ordinary and necessary business expense."*

### Q11 — Cost segregation

**Examiner:** *"You took $67,200 of bonus depreciation in the placement-in-service year. Show me the cost-segregation study."*

**What you should be able to produce:**
- Engineering cost-seg study from a qualified firm OR a documented self-study with full asset-class detail
- Allocation of basis across 5-, 7-, 15-, and 27.5-year property
- Form 4562 reflecting the bonus election

**Model answer:** *"Engineering study performed by [firm] on the cabin acquired in 20XX. Total depreciable basis $480,000 — $160,000 land excluded. Of the $480,000, the study reclassified $112,000 into 5-, 7-, and 15-year asset classes — appliances, carpeting, decorative lighting, decks and walkways. Bonus depreciation rate of 60% applied to the reclassified $112,000 yields $67,200 of year-one acceleration. The remaining $368,000 depreciates over 27.5 years on the standard MACRS schedule. Form 4562 reflects both the bonus election and the §168(k) eligibility."*

## Verdict

| Score | Verdict |
|---|---|
| **14–15** | **Audit-ready.** Your dossier holds up. Run the drill again 90 days before next filing. |
| **12–13** | **Mostly defensible.** A few gaps. Close each one in the next 30 days. |
| **9–11** | **At risk.** The substantive deductions are likely fine; the documentation isn't ready for examination. Build out the audit dossier (Module 5.2) before next filing. |
| **5–8** | **Material exposure.** If audited today, you'd lose deductions you actually earned because you can't substantiate them. Stop. Build the dossier. Don't file again until it's done. |
| **Below 5** | **Stop and rebuild.** This is a system-level gap. Module 2 (Capture System) is your priority — substantiation begins there, not at audit. |

## When to re-run the drill

- **Now (baseline).**
- **30 days after building the audit dossier (Module 5.2).** Compare scores. Most students improve by 4–6 points.
- **30 days before next filing.** Final check before the return goes out.
- **After any IRS notice or correspondence.** Even an innocuous notice is a signal to sharpen.
