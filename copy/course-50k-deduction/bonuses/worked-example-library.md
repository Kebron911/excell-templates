## How to use this library

The book teaches **rules**. The course teaches **judgment**. This library is the bridge.

Every example below is a real-world scenario a host might face: a receipt, a vendor invoice, a question from a CPA, a line item on last year's return. Each one is structured the same way:

1. **The scenario** — what the host is looking at
2. **The wrong answer** — what most hosts (and some CPAs) do
3. **The right answer** — what survives an examiner
4. **The reasoning** — the code citation and the test that drives the answer
5. **Where it goes** — the workbook tab + the Schedule E line

When you watch the matching video, you'll see the receipt or screenshot on the screen and walk through the verdict in real time. Use the PDF as a reference when the same scenario shows up in your own books.

## The 25 examples

### Example 1 — The mid-stay dishwasher

**Scenario.** Cabin A's dishwasher dies on a Saturday morning during a guest stay. Host buys a $1,140 replacement at Lowe's the same day, pays the install $180, and the unit is running by Sunday afternoon.

**Wrong answer.** "It's $1,000+, so I'll capitalize and depreciate it over five years."

**Right answer.** Current-year repair expense. Schedule E line 14. Total $1,320.

**Reasoning.** §263(a) and §1.263(a)-3 use the BAR test — Betterment, Adaptation, Restoration. A like-kind replacement of a failed unit is none of those. The unit doesn't *better* the property, doesn't *adapt* it to a new use, and isn't *restoring* a property after a casualty. It's a routine repair. The de-minimis safe harbor (§1.263(a)-1(f)) lets you elect to expense items under $2,500 per invoice anyway, which is a belt-and-suspenders position here.

**Where it goes.** TAX-002 → "Repairs" tab → Schedule E line 14. Photograph the Lowe's receipt within 48 hours. Note the install in the receipt log.

### Example 2 — The deck rebuild

**Scenario.** Cabin B's back deck is 12 years old and starting to splinter. Host pays a contractor $4,800 to demo and rebuild — same dimensions, same layout, pressure-treated lumber instead of cedar.

**Wrong answer.** "Repair. The deck was already there." Schedule E line 14.

**Right answer.** Capitalize and depreciate. Improvement under the Restoration prong of the BAR test.

**Reasoning.** §1.263(a)-3(k)(1)(vi) treats the replacement of a *major component* or *substantial structural part* as a Restoration — therefore an improvement, therefore capitalized. A full deck rebuild qualifies. Note: a deck *repair* (replace 6 boards, reseal) at $400 would be a current expense. The size and the "complete replacement" character flip the answer.

**Where it goes.** TAX-004 → Depreciation tab → 27.5-year MACRS class as a structural component. Or, with cost segregation, potentially a 15-year land-improvement asset class.

**Note.** Some practitioners argue land-improvement (15-year, eligible for bonus). The conservative position is structural component on the building (27.5-year). A cost-seg study can sustain the 15-year position with documentation.

### Example 3 — The Home Depot paint run

**Scenario.** $340 receipt: 2 gallons interior paint, 3 brushes, drop cloth, painter's tape, 1 quart trim paint. Cabin A bedroom walls.

**Wrong answer.** Capitalize as part of an improvement. (Some hosts overthink this.)

**Right answer.** Repair. Schedule E line 14.

**Reasoning.** Routine maintenance painting is a current expense under §1.263(a)-3(i) — a routine maintenance safe harbor explicitly covers activities a taxpayer expects to perform "more than once" during the property's class life. Repainting bedrooms qualifies cleanly.

**Where it goes.** TAX-002 → "Repairs" → line 14. Tag the receipt with the property name in the photo log.

### Example 4 — The mixed Costco trip

**Scenario.** $87 Costco receipt. Line items: paper towels (4-pack), hand soap (3-pack), trash bags (2 boxes), peanut butter, two boxes of cereal, a bag of clementines.

**Wrong answer.** Deduct the whole $87. (The host plans to "use the snacks at the cabin if guests want them.")

**Right answer.** Allocate. Paper towels, soap, and trash bags are property supplies — Schedule E line 17. Peanut butter, cereal, clementines are personal — not deductible unless they're explicitly stocked for guests in a documented guest-supply program.

**Reasoning.** §162 ordinary and necessary, applied honestly. Personal groceries don't become deductible because they ride to the property. If the host has a *documented* guest-supply policy ("I leave a welcome basket of branded snacks for every check-in"), the snacks become a marketing/guest-experience expense (line 19, "Other") — but it requires the policy and the contemporaneous receipts showing the snacks went to guests.

**Where it goes.** Allocate at the receipt. ~$32 supplies (line 17), ~$55 personal (not deducted). Annotate the photographed receipt with the split.

**Watch the video for:** how to handle the allocation in TAX-002's split-receipt column.

### Example 5 — The cash cleaner with no W-9

**Scenario.** Host pays cleaner $1,840 in cash across the year — five-cabin turnover days, no consistent rate, no W-9 collected. December comes and the host realizes the threshold question.

**Wrong answer.** Skip the deduction "because there's no paper trail."

**Right answer.** The deduction is still legitimately yours, but you have a 1099-NEC obligation. File the 1099-NEC late (penalty: $60–$310 per form depending on how late), collect the W-9 retroactively, and document the payments. Skipping the deduction is the wrong fix; missing the form is the wrong process.

**Reasoning.** §6041 requires Form 1099-NEC for any individual contractor paid $600+ in a year. The deduction itself is preserved under §162; only the information-reporting form was missed. Penalty is the cost of the lapse, not the loss of the deduction.

**Where it goes.** TAX-003 1099-NEC Tracker → flag for January 1099 packet (or late filing). Schedule E line 7 (cleaning and maintenance) for the deduction.

**Process fix going forward.** Lesson 2.6 protocol: W-9 before the first dollar leaves your account. No exceptions.

### Example 6 — The Airbnb gross/net trap

**Scenario.** Host's Airbnb statement shows $32,400 in gross bookings. Host fees of $972 deducted. Cleaning fees collected from guests of $5,600. Net deposit: $25,828. The host's bank statement only shows $25,828.

**Wrong answer.** Report $25,828 as gross income on Schedule E line 3.

**Right answer.** Report $32,400 (gross) on line 3. Deduct $972 (host fees, line 19) and any cleaner payment separately (line 7).

**Reasoning.** Gross is gross. The IRS computer-match expects to see your 1099-K from Airbnb (which shows gross) reconcile to your line 3 income. Reporting the net creates an automatic mismatch flag. The mechanically correct way is gross income, then expenses on the expense lines — same arithmetic result, audit-clean reporting.

**Where it goes.** TAX-002 → Reconciliation tab. Pull host statement, pull bank deposits, reconcile to gross and itemize the deductions.

### Example 7 — The cost-seg study on a $640K cabin

**Scenario.** Host bought a $640K cabin in 2026. Pays a cost-seg engineering firm $4,800 to perform a study. Study reclassifies $112,000 of the $480K depreciable basis (assume $160K land) into 5-, 7-, and 15-year asset classes. Bonus depreciation rate that year is 60%.

**Wrong answer.** Skip the study, depreciate everything 27.5-year. Or — pretend the host can self-study without engineering documentation.

**Right answer.** Engineering-based cost-seg study with §168(k) bonus depreciation election on the eligible property classes. Year-one acceleration ≈ $67,200 (60% of $112,000). The $4,800 study cost is a separate §162 deduction. Net first-year benefit at a 32% marginal rate ≈ $20,000+ in tax savings.

**Reasoning.** §168(k) allows bonus depreciation on property with a recovery period of 20 years or less. Cost segregation reclassifies portions of building basis into 5-, 7-, and 15-year property — all eligible. The study is the substantiation.

**Where it goes.** TAX-010 Cost Seg DIY Workbook (for hosts doing the analysis themselves) or the engineering firm's deliverable file. Either way, the depreciation schedule lives in TAX-004.

**Caveat.** Cost-seg only pencils when (a) basis is meaningful, (b) the host can use the loss against active income (STR loophole) or has passive income to absorb it, and (c) the recapture risk on sale is acceptable. Run lesson 4.1's worksheet first.

### Example 8 — Reconstructed mileage

**Scenario.** Host drove between three properties throughout the year. Logged nothing in real time. In March, opens Google Timeline and reconstructs ~6,420 business miles. Wants to claim $4,202 in vehicle expense (6,420 × $0.655).

**Wrong answer.** Claim it. "I have Google Timeline data."

**Right answer.** Don't claim what you can't substantiate. If Google Timeline shows the trips and you can pair each trip with a *contemporaneous* business purpose record (a calendar event, a guest message, a contractor appointment), you may be able to defend a portion. The unsubstantiated remainder is at risk on examination.

**Reasoning.** §274(d) requires *contemporaneous* substantiation: amount, time, place, business purpose. Reconstruction, even with GPS data, fails the contemporaneous standard. Tax courts have allowed partial mileage claims with corroborating evidence; pure reconstructions have been disallowed.

**Where it goes.** TAX-001 Mileage Log — but only entries you can pair with contemporaneous business-purpose records.

**Process fix.** Lesson 2.5 — log the trip the day you take it. A 30-second entry saves a $4,000 audit problem.

### Example 9 — Meals with no log

**Scenario.** Host drove 4 hours each way for a 3-day repair trip. Stayed in a hotel ($420). Ate at restaurants — kept receipts totaling $360. No log of who, what, why.

**Wrong answer.** Claim 50% of $360 = $180.

**Right answer.** Receipts alone don't substantiate §274(d) meals. You need a log entry per meal: amount, date, place, business purpose. If you can fill in the log retroactively from the trip records (the repair invoice, the property visit calendar entry), you can substantiate the meals as part of overnight business travel.

**Reasoning.** §274(d) overnight travel requires the same four elements as mileage. Receipts establish amount and place; the log establishes time and business purpose. The 50% meal limitation under §274(n) applies after substantiation, not before.

**Where it goes.** TAX-007 Per-Diem Meal Tracker → consider whether the federal per-diem method is a cleaner substitute for actual receipts on overnight travel.

**Tip.** Many hosts find per-diem easier than receipt-keeping for overnight repair trips. Lesson 3.6 covers when to elect.

### Example 10 — The home-office chair

**Scenario.** Host buys a $720 office chair specifically for the home-office space where she manages the portfolio.

**Wrong answer.** Add to home-office "actual expenses" calculation, allocating by square footage.

**Right answer.** Section 179 deduction (or de-minimis safe harbor). $720 expensed in full in the year placed in service. The home-office allocation is for *shared* expenses (mortgage interest, utilities, insurance). A piece of furniture used 100% in the home office isn't shared — it's 100% business.

**Reasoning.** §179 allows the host to expense business equipment (within annual limits, currently very generous). §1.263(a)-1(f) de-minimis safe harbor allows expensing under $2,500 per invoice. Either path works.

**Where it goes.** TAX-002 → equipment line → Schedule E line 19 ("Other") with a description, OR depreciation schedule with §179 election.

### Example 11 — The umbrella that covers both

**Scenario.** $4,200/year umbrella policy. Covers both 3 rental properties and the host's personal home.

**Wrong answer.** Deduct the whole $4,200 on Schedule E.

**Right answer.** Allocate. Most umbrella carriers itemize coverage by underlying property; if yours doesn't, allocate by underlying-policy values. Common allocation lands at 65–80% rental for a host with 3 STRs and one primary residence.

**Reasoning.** §162 — only the portion ordinary and necessary to the trade or business is deductible. Personal coverage is not. The allocation method needs to be reasonable and documented.

**Where it goes.** TAX-002 → Insurance line → Schedule E line 9 (insurance). Document the allocation method in the workbook notes.

### Example 12 — LLC formation costs

**Scenario.** Host paid $620 to form an LLC, $180 first-year registered agent, plus $2,000 for an attorney to draft an operating agreement and hold initial member meeting. All in 2026. Properties transferred to LLC mid-year.

**Wrong answer.** Deduct everything on Schedule E line 19.

**Right answer.** §195 startup costs apply to amounts paid before the trade/business begins. Once the business is operating, ongoing entity costs (annual franchise fee, registered agent renewal) are §162 deductions. Formation costs are §195 (deduct up to $5,000 in year 1, amortize the rest over 15 years). The attorney's operating-agreement work is borderline — likely §195 if pre-operational, §162 if mid-year reorganization.

**Reasoning.** §195(b) caps the year-1 startup deduction at $5,000 (phasing out above $50K total startup spend). Amounts above the cap amortize over 180 months.

**Where it goes.** TAX-002 → Entity Costs line. Mark §195 vs §162 in the notes column.

**Tip.** Lesson 7.2 covers when to incorporate at all. Many hosts incorporate too early.

### Example 13 — This very course

**Scenario.** Host pays $497 for *The 47-Deduction Operator* course in 2026.

**Wrong answer.** "Education isn't deductible."

**Right answer.** §162 ordinary-and-necessary education expense — *if* it maintains or improves skills required in your existing trade or business. For an operating STR host, a course on STR tax compliance and bookkeeping qualifies cleanly.

**Reasoning.** §162 + Treas. Reg. §1.162-5. Education that qualifies you for a *new* trade or business is not deductible. Education that maintains or sharpens skills in your *existing* business is. Operating an STR portfolio is the existing business; this course sharpens the bookkeeping skill within it.

**Where it goes.** TAX-002 → Education line → Schedule E line 19. Save the receipt and the course description.

### Example 14 — Professional photography for the listing

**Scenario.** Host pays $1,250 for a professional photoshoot for a new listing.

**Wrong answer.** Capitalize as a startup cost.

**Right answer.** §162 marketing expense. Schedule E line 8 (advertising) or line 19.

**Reasoning.** Photography for an active listing is ordinary and necessary marketing. The asset (the photos) doesn't have a useful life beyond the listing's marketing horizon — it's not capitalized. If the property is brand-new and the photoshoot is part of *placing it in service*, it can be capitalized into basis as a placement-in-service cost; in practice for an existing STR, it's a current marketing expense.

**Where it goes.** TAX-002 → Marketing line → Schedule E line 8.

### Example 15 — The mixed Amazon order

**Scenario.** $185 Amazon order. Items: Echo Dot for cabin guest use ($45), welcome-basket snacks for guests ($35), the host's personal copy of a novel ($18), batteries ($12, both for cabin and personal), a host-personal headphone case ($75).

**Wrong answer.** Deduct $185. Or: deduct nothing because "it's mixed."

**Right answer.** Allocate at the line-item level. Echo Dot ($45, line 17 supplies), welcome-basket snacks ($35, marketing/guest experience under a documented guest-supply policy), batteries (~$6 of $12, allocated). Novel and headphone case: personal, not deducted. Total deducted: ~$86.

**Reasoning.** Mixed receipts require honest line-item allocation. Aggregating up creates a §162 problem; aggregating down (deducting zero because it's mixed) leaves money on the table.

**Where it goes.** TAX-002 supports line-item splits in the receipt-detail tab. Annotate the photographed receipt with the allocation.

### Example 16 — The HVAC replacement

**Scenario.** Cabin C's HVAC unit (15 years old) fails in July. Host replaces it with a like-capacity unit for $9,400.

**Wrong answer.** "Big number — capitalize 27.5 years."

**Right answer.** Capitalize and elect §168(k) bonus depreciation OR §179. HVAC is *eligible §179 property* under post-2017 rules (PATH Act extended §179 to HVAC, fire systems, and roofs on nonresidential — but residential rental HVAC is generally 27.5-year property unless it qualifies as a separate land-improvement-class asset). For *short-term rental* property classified under MACRS as nonresidential (because of the 7-day average rental period), HVAC may be §179-eligible — fact-pattern question. Conservatively, depreciate over 27.5 years and consider a cost-seg study to support reclassification.

**Reasoning.** §263(a) — replacement of a major component is restoration, capitalized. The classification (27.5 vs §179-eligible) depends on whether the property is residential or nonresidential under MACRS, which depends on average rental period. STR with <7-day average is often nonresidential, opening up §179 on HVAC.

**Where it goes.** TAX-004 depreciation schedule. Flag the §179 question for CPA review.

**This one explicitly belongs in the CPA conversation.** Lesson 6.4.

### Example 17 — The 14-day personal-use trap

**Scenario.** Host calls a property a "vacation rental" and stays there 14 nights/year. The property rents 180 nights/year on Airbnb.

**Wrong answer.** Continue treating it as an STR business. Deduct full expenses.

**Right answer.** §280A(d)(2) — if personal use exceeds the *greater of* 14 days OR 10% of rental days, the property is a "residence" and rental deductions are limited to rental income (no loss allowed). At 180 rental days, 10% = 18 days; the 14 days of personal use is *under* the threshold here. So the host is fine — but barely. One more night flips it.

**Reasoning.** The exact mechanics of §280A(d)(2) trip up many hosts who think 14 days is an absolute cap. It's the greater of 14 OR 10%. Higher rental volume gives more headroom; lower rental volume narrows it. A property rented 100 days has 10 days of personal-use headroom, not 14.

**Where it goes.** Augusta Documentation Pack → Personal-Use Calendar tab. Track contemporaneously.

### Example 18 — Augusta Rule election

**Scenario.** Host's S-corp rents her home office (a separate-entrance ADU on the same property as her primary residence) from her for 14 days at $800/night. Total: $11,200. She holds two board-strategy retreats and twelve quarterly business meetings in the space across the year.

**Wrong answer.** Take the deduction without documentation. Or — claim 30 days because "we used it more than 14."

**Right answer.** §280A(g): up to 14 days of rental income to the homeowner is excluded from income; the renting entity (the S-corp) deducts the rent as a §162 ordinary-and-necessary business expense. Documentation required: (1) FMV substantiation showing $800/night is reasonable for the space, (2) board minutes for each meeting documenting the business purpose and attendance, (3) a contemporaneous calendar.

**Reasoning.** §280A(g) is one of the cleanest deductions in the code — but the IRS scrutinizes it. The three documentation items are the difference between a clean deduction and an examiner-disallowed one.

**Where it goes.** Augusta Documentation Pack — all three templates (FMV worksheet, board-minute template, calendar). The rent income flows to Schedule E for the homeowner (excluded under §280A(g)); the rent expense flows to the S-corp's Form 1120-S deductions.

### Example 19 — Platform and software fees

**Scenario.** Host pays the following throughout the year: PriceLabs $240, Hospitable $300, Stripe processing fees $400 net (after gross-net reconciliation).

**Wrong answer.** Forget Stripe fees because "they're already netted."

**Right answer.** Deduct all three. PriceLabs and Hospitable as line 19 (other) or line 8 (marketing/management). Stripe as a platform fee — but only if you're tracking gross revenue (see Example 6); otherwise the netting handled it.

**Reasoning.** §162 — software and platform fees ordinary and necessary to running the portfolio.

**Where it goes.** TAX-002 → SaaS / Platform-fees line.

### Example 20 — The attorney for a guest dispute

**Scenario.** Guest damages the property; host pays $3,400 to an attorney to pursue the damage claim and recover from the guest.

**Wrong answer.** "Legal is always deductible."

**Right answer.** Deductible §162 — legal expense ordinary and necessary to the trade or business. The recovery, when received, is income (or a basis adjustment depending on the nature). Net the legal cost in the year paid; record the recovery separately when received.

**Reasoning.** Legal fees follow the activity they relate to. Trade-or-business legal = deductible. Personal legal (divorce, will) = not deductible. Acquisition-of-property legal = capitalized into basis.

**Where it goes.** TAX-002 → Legal/Professional line → Schedule E line 10.

### Example 21 — The cell phone allocation

**Scenario.** Host's cell phone bill is $260/month, $3,120/year. Estimates 70% business use (guest communication, contractor coordination, listing management).

**Wrong answer.** Deduct 100% with no allocation.

**Right answer.** $3,120 × 70% = $2,184 deductible. The 70% allocation needs a contemporaneous basis — call log analysis, day-in-the-life records, or a sampling method.

**Reasoning.** §262 disallows personal expenses. Mixed-use assets require allocation. The phone is a §274(d)-style listed-property issue if used heavily for business, though Congress simplified the rules in 2010 — but the *deduction* still requires an honest allocation.

**Where it goes.** TAX-002 → Mixed-use line, with the allocation method noted. Schedule E line 19.

**Tip.** A second line dedicated to the rental business eliminates the allocation problem and is often cheap. Operational fix often beats accounting fix.

### Example 22 — §179 on a multi-property mower

**Scenario.** Host buys a $1,800 riding mower used at all 3 cabins. Election: §179 for full year-1 deduction.

**Wrong answer.** Skip §179, depreciate over 7 years.

**Right answer.** §179 election on the full $1,800. Allocate use across the 3 properties (e.g., 33% each).

**Reasoning.** §179 expensing is available on tangible business equipment. The annual limit is generous (well above $1,800); election is per-asset, made on Form 4562. Allocation across properties is tracked in the depreciation workbook.

**Where it goes.** TAX-004 Depreciation tab → §179 election column → allocated across property tabs.

### Example 23 — The cleaning fee pass-through

**Scenario.** Cabin B collects $5,600 in cleaning fees from guests across the year. Host pays the cleaner $4,200. Net to the host: $1,400.

**Wrong answer.** Report $1,400 as cleaning-fee income.

**Right answer.** Report $5,600 as gross income (line 3 alongside rental income). Deduct $4,200 as cleaning expense (line 7). Net effect: $1,400 — but the audit-clean reporting matches the 1099-K from Airbnb showing $5,600 included in gross.

**Reasoning.** Same as Example 6 — gross reporting matches information returns. Netting creates mismatches.

**Where it goes.** TAX-002 → Cleaning-fee reconciliation tab.

### Example 24 — The inherited rental

**Scenario.** Host inherited a property in 2024. Rented it as STR starting 2025. No closing statement, no original cost-basis records. Wants to start depreciating in 2026.

**Wrong answer.** Skip depreciation because "I don't know the basis."

**Right answer.** Step-up basis at date of inheritance. Use a contemporaneous appraisal (or a retrospective appraisal if none exists) to establish FMV at date of death. That FMV becomes the depreciable basis (less land allocation). MACRS 27.5-year clock starts at the date placed in service as a rental.

**Reasoning.** §1014 — inherited property gets a stepped-up basis equal to FMV at date of decedent's death. The depreciable basis is FMV minus land. The lack of an original closing statement is irrelevant; the inheritance reset the basis.

**Where it goes.** TAX-004 → Depreciation tab. Document the FMV source (appraisal, comparable sales analysis) in the basis-substantiation column.

**Caveat.** A retrospective appraisal costs $300–$800 but is the substantiation that holds up. Pay it.

### Example 25 — Paying the spouse

**Scenario.** Host pays her spouse $920 across the year for bookkeeping help — categorizing receipts, running the quarterly Saturday with her. No payroll, no W-9, no 1099.

**Wrong answer.** Deduct $920 as contractor labor.

**Right answer.** Generally, no deduction for spouse labor unless the spouse is an actual employee (on payroll, with W-2) or a documented sole-proprietor contractor (W-9, 1099, separate business). Most spouse-helping arrangements are not deductible. The cleaner alternative is to *not* pay the spouse and accept that the help is part of the marriage, OR set up a real employee relationship if the dollars justify the payroll overhead.

**Reasoning.** §162 requires the expense to be ordinary, necessary, and *paid or incurred*. Paid is real money flowing to a real worker on real terms. Spouse-helping arrangements often fail the substance test. If you do pay the spouse, the §3121 family-employment payroll-tax rules apply, and the W-2 has to be issued.

**Where it goes.** Most hosts: don't deduct, don't pay. If you do pay: payroll on a W-2, and treat as wages on Schedule E line 7.

**Caveat.** This is the single most-asked spousal question hosts bring to CPAs. The honest answer is "either do it right or don't do it." Half-measures don't work.
