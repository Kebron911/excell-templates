# Bonus — The 12-Question CPA Interview Scorecard

**Format:** 4-page printable PDF
**Course module:** Module 6 (Lesson 6.2)
**Use case:** Vetting a tax preparer before you hire them

> **Most STR hosts hire a generalist CPA and wonder why their return feels generic. The questions below identify the specialists.**
>
> Run this on every preparer you consider — including the one you've been using for years. A 30-minute consult costs $0–$150 and is the cheapest tax decision you'll make this decade.

---

## How to use this

1. Schedule a consult call with the prospective CPA — 20–30 minutes is enough.
2. Read the question out loud. Don't paraphrase. The exact wording matters.
3. Listen to the answer. Score against the rubric below.
4. Ask the follow-up. Many preparers give a textbook answer to the question and stumble on the follow-up.
5. Total the score at the end. Hire above 30. Walk below 24.

---

## The 12 questions

### Question 1 — Average rental period

**Ask:** *"What's the average rental period at which a short-term rental moves from Schedule E into a Schedule C analysis, and what triggers Schedule C even when the average rental period is over seven days?"*

**Looking for:**
- Knows the 7-day average
- Knows the 8–30 day with substantial services secondary trigger
- Mentions §1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)
- Doesn't reflexively recommend Schedule C "to deduct more expenses"

**Follow-up:** *"How do you compute average rental period?"*
*(Answer: total rental days ÷ total bookings, not nights divided by guests.)*

| Score | Answer pattern |
|---|---|
| 3 | Hits all four cleanly, including the §1.469 cite |
| 2 | Hits 7-day rule and substantial services concept; misses the cite |
| 1 | Knows there's a difference but is fuzzy on triggers |
| 0 | "We just file Schedule C for short-term rentals" |

---

### Question 2 — Material participation

**Ask:** *"What are the seven material-participation tests under §1.469-5T, and which two are most defensible for a part-time STR host?"*

**Looking for:**
- Names at least 4 of the 7 tests without prompting
- Identifies the 100-hour test (§1.469-5T(a)(7) — "any test, plus more participation than any other person") and the 500-hour test (§1.469-5T(a)(1)) as most commonly used
- Knows that the STR loophole *bypasses* the material-participation question for the rental real estate per-se rule but still requires material participation under §469(c)(7) interaction

**Follow-up:** *"What logs do you ask clients to keep to substantiate material participation?"*
*(Answer should mention: time log, correspondence log, decision log, reservation log — or equivalent four-log structure.)*

| Score | Answer pattern |
|---|---|
| 3 | Names 4+ tests, identifies the practical two, asks for the four logs |
| 2 | Names the 500-hour and 100-hour tests; doesn't get to the logs |
| 1 | Knows the concept of material participation but is light on tests |
| 0 | Doesn't distinguish material from active participation |

---

### Question 3 — The STR loophole

**Ask:** *"Explain the §469(c)(7) real-estate-professional rules versus the so-called STR loophole. Which one applies to my client profile of three short-term rental properties with a W-2 day job?"*

**Looking for:**
- Distinguishes the §469(c)(7) real-estate-professional carve-out (750 hours + more than half of personal services) from the STR loophole
- Recognizes that *with* a W-2 day job, the real-estate-professional path is rarely available; the STR loophole (treating STR income as nonpassive due to short average rental period and material participation) is the typical path
- Knows the loophole rests on the average rental period being short enough to remove the per-se rental classification

**Follow-up:** *"What evidence would you ask my client to keep contemporaneously to defend the loophole on examination?"*

| Score | Answer pattern |
|---|---|
| 3 | Crisp distinction, identifies the loophole as the W-2 host's path, lists evidence |
| 2 | Distinguishes the two but recommends the wrong path for the W-2 host |
| 1 | Conflates the two |
| 0 | "What loophole?" |

---

### Question 4 — Cost segregation

**Ask:** *"At what acquisition basis does cost segregation typically pencil for an STR host, and what are the recapture risks at sale?"*

**Looking for:**
- General threshold around $400K–$500K acquisition basis where engineering studies become economically rational
- Distinguishes engineering studies from DIY/self-prepared studies and acknowledges DIY's audit-risk profile
- Mentions §1245 recapture on sale of reclassified personal-property components — and §1250 unrecaptured gain on the building portion

**Follow-up:** *"How do you handle cost-seg with the STR loophole? Does it interact with §469?"*

| Score | Answer pattern |
|---|---|
| 3 | Threshold + recapture + §469 interaction |
| 2 | Threshold + recapture, no §469 nuance |
| 1 | Threshold only |
| 0 | "I don't usually recommend cost seg" or evasive |

---

### Question 5 — Repairs vs. improvements (the BAR test)

**Ask:** *"Walk me through the BAR test under §1.263(a)-3 and the routine-maintenance safe harbor. How do you decide on a $1,200 water heater replacement?"*

**Looking for:**
- Names Betterment / Adaptation / Restoration cleanly
- Identifies water heater replacement as a unit-of-property issue — likely a current expense as routine maintenance or under the de-minimis safe harbor (if elected)
- Doesn't reflexively capitalize because of dollar amount alone

**Follow-up:** *"Have you elected the de-minimis safe harbor for any of your STR clients? At what threshold?"*
*(§1.263(a)-1(f) — $2,500 per invoice or $5,000 with applicable financial statement.)*

| Score | Answer pattern |
|---|---|
| 3 | BAR clean, water heater = expense, de-minimis election in the toolkit |
| 2 | BAR named but answer is "depends" on the water heater |
| 1 | Vague on the BAR test |
| 0 | "Anything over $1,000 we capitalize" — wrong policy |

---

### Question 6 — Augusta Rule

**Ask:** *"How do you handle a §280A(g) Augusta Rule election for a host who wants to rent her home office space to her S-corp for 14 days? What documentation do you require before you'll let her take the deduction?"*

**Looking for:**
- Knows §280A(g) — 14 days, excluded from income to homeowner, deductible to entity
- Requires FMV substantiation, contemporaneous calendar, and board-minute documentation
- Asks about the relationship between the rented space and the homeowner's primary residence

**Follow-up:** *"Have you ever had a §280A(g) deduction challenged? What held up?"*
*(Best answer: documentation of FMV via comparables and contemporaneous board minutes.)*

| Score | Answer pattern |
|---|---|
| 3 | All three requirements + has battle-tested experience |
| 2 | Mentions §280A(g) and at least one of the three requirements |
| 1 | "Augusta Rule? Sure, we do those." with no specifics |
| 0 | Doesn't know §280A(g) by section number or by name |

---

### Question 7 — 1099 obligations

**Ask:** *"What 1099 forms am I responsible for issuing as an STR host with three properties, and what's the deadline?"*

**Looking for:**
- 1099-NEC for any contractor paid $600+ in a calendar year (cleaners, handymen, photographers, attorneys, accountants billing as individuals/sole-props) — January 31 deadline
- 1099-MISC if applicable (rent paid to a property owner, if the host is leasing in to sublease — uncommon for STR)
- Mentions the W-9 collection at start of relationship

**Follow-up:** *"What happens if the host paid a cleaner $1,200 in cash and has no W-9?"*
*(Answer should describe retroactive W-9 collection, late filing, and penalty exposure — but the deduction is still preserved.)*

| Score | Answer pattern |
|---|---|
| 3 | Form, deadline, threshold, retro-fix |
| 2 | Form and threshold |
| 1 | "Some kind of 1099" |
| 0 | "You don't need to issue 1099s for cleaners" — wrong |

---

### Question 8 — Personal use limitation

**Ask:** *"How does §280A(d) apply when a host stays at her own STR for personal use? At what point do rental deductions get limited?"*

**Looking for:**
- Knows the *greater of 14 days or 10% of rental days* threshold
- Knows that crossing the threshold reclassifies the property as a "residence" and limits deductions to rental income (no loss allowed)
- Mentions the contemporaneous calendar requirement

**Follow-up:** *"How do you handle a host who uses the property for a 7-day repair trip? Personal use or business use?"*
*(Answer: business use if the primary purpose is repairs/management — §280A(d)(2)(C) provides the carve-out for days "principally for the purpose of repair or maintenance.")*

| Score | Answer pattern |
|---|---|
| 3 | Threshold cleanly + repair-day carve-out |
| 2 | Threshold; misses repair-day nuance |
| 1 | Knows there's a 14-day rule but conflates it with the Augusta Rule |
| 0 | Doesn't know §280A(d) is distinct from §280A(g) |

---

### Question 9 — Bonus depreciation

**Ask:** *"What's the current §168(k) bonus depreciation rate, and how does it interact with §179 for STR property?"*

**Looking for:**
- Cites the current rate accurately (verifies it; doesn't wing it)
- Knows §179 election is per-asset and capped; §168(k) is automatic on eligible property unless elected out
- Order of operations: §179 first (within limits), then bonus on remainder

**Follow-up:** *"Can a host bonus-depreciate a roof replacement on a residential STR?"*
*(Roof on residential rental is generally part of the 27.5-year structure, *not* eligible for bonus. On nonresidential STR — average rental period <7 days, treated as nonresidential — roof can qualify under post-2017 §179 rules. Fact-pattern question.)*

| Score | Answer pattern |
|---|---|
| 3 | Current rate, stack order, residential vs. nonresidential nuance |
| 2 | Rate and stack order |
| 1 | Knows there's a bonus rate but is fuzzy on it |
| 0 | Cites a years-old rate without checking |

---

### Question 10 — Audit posture

**Ask:** *"If a client's STR Schedule E gets flagged for correspondence examination, what's your first move?"*

**Looking for:**
- Reads the IDR carefully, identifies which line items are at issue, computes the dollar exposure
- Pulls the client's contemporaneous records and assesses substantiation gaps
- Manages the timeline; the 30-day clock is not negotiable
- Doesn't escalate to in-person field exam if it can be resolved by correspondence

**Follow-up:** *"How many examinations have you handled for STR or rental clients in the last 3 years?"*

| Score | Answer pattern |
|---|---|
| 3 | Process answer + experience count |
| 2 | Process answer, no concrete experience |
| 1 | "We'd handle it" — vague |
| 0 | Refers everything to a tax attorney without trying |

---

### Question 11 — Software & process

**Ask:** *"What software do you use to receive client books, and how do you prefer hosts deliver Schedule E backup?"*

**Looking for:**
- Has a defined intake — client portal, secure file transfer, structured folder format
- Comfortable receiving Excel-based bookkeeping (TAX-004, TAX-002 format) — doesn't insist on QuickBooks Online
- Provides a checklist of what they want by mid-February

**Follow-up:** *"How do you charge — flat-fee, per-form, or hourly?"*
*(For STR hosts with 1–10 doors, flat-fee or per-property pricing is the norm; hourly often signals a generalist.)*

| Score | Answer pattern |
|---|---|
| 3 | Modern intake + flexible on format + flat-fee or per-property pricing |
| 2 | Modern intake; insists on a specific software |
| 1 | "Email me the QuickBooks file" with no checklist |
| 0 | "Bring me your shoebox in March" |

---

### Question 12 — STR-specific experience

**Ask:** *"How many STR or short-term rental clients do you currently serve, and what do they have in common?"*

**Looking for:**
- A specific number (more than zero, ideally 10+)
- A pattern in the client base — "most are 2–5 properties, mid-tier acquisition basis, W-2 day job" or similar
- Has opinions on how STR hosts should structure their books
- Has a STR-specific intake checklist or onboarding doc

**Follow-up:** *"What's the most common mistake you see new STR clients bring you?"*
*(Best answer: gross-vs-net cleaning fee reporting, missed depreciation, no W-9s, reconstructed mileage. Any of these signals real exposure.)*

| Score | Answer pattern |
|---|---|
| 3 | Specific count + pattern + opinions + common-mistake answer |
| 2 | Has STR clients but doesn't articulate a pattern |
| 1 | "We have a few" |
| 0 | "We don't specialize but we can figure it out" |

---

## Scoring & decision

| Score (out of 36) | Decision |
|---|---|
| 30–36 | **Hire.** This is a STR-aware preparer. Get the engagement letter signed. |
| 24–29 | **Hire with caution.** Solid generalist; will need you to supply the STR-specific structure. Consider as a backup or for simpler returns. |
| 18–23 | **Probably skip.** You'll spend more time educating them than they save you. Keep interviewing. |
| Below 18 | **Walk.** Generalist with no STR exposure. The fee will be small and the cost of missed deductions will be large. |

---

## Other signals to weigh alongside the score

- **Responsiveness.** A CPA who takes 5 days to reply in November will take 3 weeks in March.
- **Engagement letter clarity.** Vague scope is the biggest source of fee disputes. Look for specificity.
- **Proactive vs. reactive.** Does the CPA reach out in October with planning ideas, or only in February with a tax-prep request? Proactive is worth a 20% premium.
- **Continuing education.** STR-tax CPE in the last 24 months is a strong signal. Ask.
- **References.** Two STR-host references with permission to call. If the CPA can't or won't provide them, that's a signal.

---

## After the interview

1. Score the call within 4 hours, while you remember the answers.
2. If you're hiring: ask for the engagement letter and a sample prior-year return (redacted).
3. If you're not hiring: send a brief thank-you note. Networks are small; the CPA you don't hire today refers a friend tomorrow.
