## What is Schedule E?

Schedule E is the IRS form used to report income and expenses from **passive activities**, including rental real estate. It's attached to your Form 1040 personal tax return.

For most STR hosts, Schedule E is the default form. You'll report:
- Gross rental income per property
- Expenses per property (mortgage interest, utilities, depreciation, repairs, etc.)
- Net income or loss per property

The resulting net number flows to your 1040 as rental income (or loss). If you have losses from your STR, those losses are generally **limited** by passive activity rules — meaning you can't use them to offset your W-2 income, except in specific circumstances.

## When does Schedule E apply?

Schedule E applies to **rental of real estate** when:
1. You're **not providing substantial services** to guests, AND
2. You don't meet the IRS definition of a **real estate professional** with material participation, AND
3. Your average guest stay is **more than 7 days** OR you don't meet the material participation tests

In practice, if you run a hands-off Airbnb where you just rent the property, don't offer concierge services, and don't spend hundreds of hours per year managing it → Schedule E.

## Schedule E vs Schedule C: the most important decision you'll make

**This is where most STR hosts get it wrong.** Schedule E treats STR income as passive. Schedule C treats it as self-employment income (active). The differences matter:

| | Schedule E (passive) | Schedule C (active) |
|---|---|---|
| Losses offset W-2 income? | No (usually) | Yes |
| Self-employment tax (15.3%) | No | Yes |
| QBI deduction eligible? | Sometimes | Yes |
| Sec. 199A deduction | Complex | Straightforward |
| Home office deduction | Limited | Full |

In short: Schedule E saves you ~15% in self-employment tax, but limits your ability to use losses. Schedule C lets you use losses against W-2 income, but adds 15.3% self-employment tax on profits.

**When should you file Schedule C instead of E?**

The IRS says: if you provide **substantial services** similar to a hotel (daily cleaning, meals, concierge, etc.), your STR is a **trade or business**, not a rental. That means Schedule C.

The threshold isn't perfectly bright-line, but here are signals that push you toward Schedule C:
- **Average stay < 7 days** AND material participation
- You provide daily housekeeping during stays
- You provide meals or food service
- You have a front-desk-equivalent (in-person check-in, concierge)
- Your STR operation is clearly your primary business (10+ units, dedicated team, full-time effort)

Most single-family Airbnb operators with turnkey self-check-in fall under Schedule E. Boutique inn operators, luxury concierge rentals, or operators running STRs at hotel-intensity fall under Schedule C.

## The 7 material participation tests (why they matter)

Even on Schedule E, whether you **"materially participate"** determines whether your losses are fully deductible against other income or limited as passive losses. The IRS has seven tests — you only need to pass **one**.

1. You participate in the activity for **more than 500 hours** during the tax year
2. Your participation is **substantially all** the participation by anyone (you do the work yourself)
3. You participate more than **100 hours** and more than anyone else
4. You have **significant participation activities** totaling more than 500 hours across all such activities
5. You materially participated in **any 5 of the last 10 years**
6. (For personal service activities only — rarely applies to STR)
7. You participate on a **regular, continuous, and substantial basis**

**Why this matters:** if you pass at least one test AND your average guest stay is 7 days or less, your STR may qualify as a **non-passive activity** → losses offset your W-2 income. This is the "STR loophole" you've heard about in real-estate investing circles.

**Real-world example:** Amanda owns 3 Smokies cabins. Average stay is 4 nights. She personally handles pricing, guest communication, maintenance coordination, and books 600 hours/year doing it. She passes test 1 (500+ hours) and meets the ≤7-day average stay rule. Her cabins are treated as non-passive for tax purposes — and when she took a cost segregation study resulting in $47,000 of first-year depreciation losses, those losses offset her husband's W-2 income.

**This is specific. If you're claiming this treatment, document your hours in a contemporaneous log — not reconstructed at tax time.**

## How to report Airbnb/VRBO income on Schedule E — line by line

Open IRS Schedule E. Each property you rent goes in one column (A, B, C). You can have up to 3 properties per Schedule E — with 4+, attach additional copies.

### Part I — Income or Loss From Rental Real Estate

**Line 1a — Physical address**
Full address of the rental property.

**Line 1b — Type of property**
Code 1 for residential rental (standard for most STRs). Code 3 for vacation/short-term if you want to flag specifically — IRS doesn't formally distinguish, but accuracy matters.

**Line 2 — Fair rental days / Personal use days**
Days the property was rented at fair market value vs days you personally used it. If you used the property for more than 14 days AND more than 10% of rental days, you have a **mixed-use property** with complex expense allocation — consult a CPA.

**Line 3 — Rents received**
Gross rental income, before platform fees. **This is where most hosts make the first error.** Include:
- All payment from guests
- Cleaning fees you charged guests
- Taxes you collected from guests (if you're responsible for remitting — otherwise leave out)

Do NOT net out Airbnb/VRBO service fees — those go on Line 5 as an expense. The 1099-K you receive from Airbnb typically reports gross payments, so your Line 3 should match.

**Line 4 — Royalties received**
Skip for STR hosts.

### Expenses

**Line 5 — Advertising**
Airbnb/VRBO service fees go HERE. Also:
- Platform boost fees (Airbnb's "promoted listing" option)
- Professional photography
- Social media ads for your listing
- Listing optimization tools (PriceLabs, Wheelhouse)

**Line 6 — Auto and travel**
Mileage to the property at the IRS standard rate (70¢/mile for 2026, confirm current-year rate). Also rental car costs for STR-related trips. Requires a contemporaneous mileage log.

**Line 7 — Cleaning and maintenance**
Your cleaner's fees, handyman costs, landscaping, pool service, pest control. Note: if you pay any contractor more than $600/year, you must issue a 1099-NEC.

**Line 8 — Commissions**
Usually blank for STR. Only applies if you paid a booking agent or realtor commission.

**Line 9 — Insurance**
Homeowners / landlord / STR-specific insurance (like Proper or Obie). Also umbrella policies covering the rental.

**Line 10 — Legal and other professional fees**
Attorney fees, accountant fees, tax preparation costs, bookkeeper fees, STR permit/license fees (some file here, some on Line 16 — consistency matters).

**Line 11 — Management fees**
Co-host fees, property manager fees. If you're using Hostfully or a similar PMS, the subscription goes on Line 19 "Other expenses" — but a per-booking percentage to a human manager goes here.

**Line 12 — Mortgage interest paid to banks**
Interest portion of your mortgage payment (principal goes into basis for depreciation, not a deduction). Use Form 1098 from your lender.

**Line 13 — Other interest**
Interest on credit cards or lines of credit used specifically for the STR (purchases, furniture, rehab).

**Line 14 — Repairs**
Repairs that restore the property to its prior condition without adding value. Fixing a broken AC = repair. Replacing the AC with a new unit = capital improvement (depreciated, not expensed here).

**Line 15 — Supplies**
Cleaning supplies, linens, guest consumables (coffee, water, paper products). Also minor equipment items under ~$200.

**Line 16 — Taxes**
Property taxes. Some hosts put local STR license fees here (acceptable) vs Line 10 (also acceptable).

**Line 17 — Utilities**
Electric, gas, water, sewer, trash, internet, streaming services, cable — anything you pay on behalf of the guest experience.

**Line 18 — Depreciation**
Calculated via Form 4562. This is a big deal — covered in depth in our [depreciation blog post](thestrledger.com/blog/str-depreciation).

For the building: 27.5-year straight-line MACRS.
For furniture, appliances, electronics: 5-year.
For landscaping/paving: 15-year.

**Line 19 — Other expenses** (list specifically)
- Airbnb/VRBO host fees (if not on Line 5)
- HOA fees
- PriceLabs/Hospitable/Turno subscriptions
- Smart home service fees (Airbnb-managed smart lock services)
- Transient occupancy tax remittance
- Background check services for guest screening

Itemize each with clear labels. Don't lump "misc" — IRS finds "misc" suspicious.

### Lines 20–26 — calculations

The form walks you through totaling expenses, calculating income/loss, applying passive loss limitations (Form 8582 for complex cases), and summing across properties.

## The 10 most common Schedule E mistakes STR hosts make

1. **Reporting net payout as income instead of gross.** The 1099-K is gross. Deduct platform fees separately on Line 5.
2. **Missing cleaning fees as income.** When Airbnb charges the guest a cleaning fee and passes it to you, that's income. If you then pay your cleaner, the cleaner's fee is an expense on Line 7.
3. **Lumping all expenses into "Other."** Itemize per line. Audit-friendlier and often legal requirement.
4. **Forgetting depreciation.** Missing 27.5-year depreciation = leaving $5K–$20K/year on the table. Use Form 4562.
5. **Claiming 100% personal use deductions when property is mixed-use.** If you use the property personally >14 days OR >10% of rental days, expenses must be allocated — full deduction is wrong.
6. **Filing Schedule E when Schedule C is correct (or vice versa).** Revisit the material participation tests and your services level.
7. **Not issuing 1099-NECs to contractors paid $600+.** Auditable expense otherwise.
8. **Missing local/state filings.** Transient occupancy tax, STR permit fees — jurisdiction-specific, separate from federal Schedule E.
9. **Not tracking mileage contemporaneously.** IRS requires date-of-trip documentation, not reconstructed year-end estimates.
10. **Ignoring the STR material participation "loophole" when it applies.** If you pass 500+ hours with ≤7-day average stays, losses may offset W-2 income — potentially $10K–$50K in first-year savings via cost segregation.
