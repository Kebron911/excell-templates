The fastest way to lose money on a short-term rental is to buy the property *before* you read the city's STR ordinance. The slowest way is to buy without understanding the difference between the ordinance, the zoning code, the lodging-tax statute, and the HOA covenants — because all four can shut you down independently, and only one of them is on the city website's STR page.

This post is the buyer-side regulation read. Four questions every code answers, four named-city walkthroughs, and a checklist for the property you're underwriting this week.

> **Not legal advice.** STR ordinances change. Verify current status with the city's planning department or a local STR-specialist attorney before signing any LOI. The summaries below are illustrative and assume Q1 2026 status.

## The four questions every STR ordinance answers

Before you read any city's specific code, know what you're looking for. Every meaningful STR ordinance answers these four:

1. **Is the use permitted at all?** Some cities ban unhosted STRs entirely. Some ban them in residential zones but allow them in resort or commercial zones. Some allow them everywhere with a license.
2. **What's the night cap?** Many cities cap unhosted STR nights per year (Los Angeles caps at 120; some Vermont towns at 60).
3. **Is there a primary-residence rule?** Cities like Honolulu and Los Angeles restrict unhosted STRs to the operator's primary residence — investor-owned non-resident rentals are not allowed.
4. **What's the registration / licensing path?** Most permissive cities still require a business license, lodging-tax registration, fire-inspection sign-off, and platform-listed permit number. The cost ranges from $0 to several thousand dollars annually.

If a city's code answers any of these four with "no" or "highly restricted," the deal economics for an investor-owned rental change materially.

## Walkthrough A — New York City (Local Law 18 of 2022)

Effective enforcement: September 5, 2023.

**What it does:** requires hosts to register short-term rentals (less than 30 days) with the Mayor's Office of Special Enforcement. Registration is restricted to hosts who **occupy the unit themselves while guests are present**, with no more than two paying guests at a time. Platform companies (Airbnb, Vrbo) must verify a registration number before listing.

**Practical effect:** the unhosted-STR business model in NYC is effectively non-viable. AirDNA reported active short-term listings in the five boroughs collapsed approximately 90% in the months following enforcement. Listings shifted to ≥30-day stays (mid-term rental territory) or off the platforms entirely.

**Source:** [NYC Office of Special Enforcement — Short-Term Rental Registration Law](https://www.nyc.gov/site/specialenforcement/registration-law/local-law-18-of-2022.page).

**Buyer takeaway:** if your underwriting model assumes unhosted nightly bookings in NYC, the model is wrong. Underwrite as a 30-day-minimum medium-term rental and the math is different by an order of magnitude.

## Walkthrough B — Los Angeles (Home-Sharing Ordinance, HSO)

In effect since 2019, with periodic amendments.

**What it does:**
- Requires the property to be the host's **primary residence** (lived in at least 6 months of the prior year)
- Caps unhosted nights at **120 per year** (extended-permit holders may go higher with public-hearing approval)
- Requires HSO registration plus business tax registration
- Requires the registration number on every listing

**Practical effect:** investor-owned, non-primary STR rentals in the City of Los Angeles are not permitted. The HSO is enforced; non-compliant listings receive cease-and-desist letters and accumulating fines.

**Source:** [LA Department of City Planning — HSO](https://planning.lacity.gov/) (search "home-sharing ordinance" for the current code section).

**Buyer takeaway:** non-primary-residence STR investing in the City of LA is the wrong product. County-area unincorporated zones may have different rules. The exact municipal boundary matters — verify the parcel sits in the City vs. County before you assume the rule applies.

## Walkthrough C — Honolulu (Bill 41, Ordinance 22-7)

Effective October 2022.

**What it does:**
- Sets a **30-day minimum stay** in most non-resort districts of the island of Oahu
- Maintains shorter stays in designated resort zones (Waikiki, Ko Olina, etc.)
- Restricts unhosted under-30-day rentals outside resort zones

**Practical effect:** the typical 3–7 night Airbnb model is restricted to resort-zoned properties in Oahu. Outside those zones, the operating thesis becomes 30-day-minimum corporate housing or extended-stay travelers, which materially changes ADR, turnover, and operating expense.

**Source:** [Honolulu Department of Planning and Permitting — Short-Term Rentals](https://www.honolulu.gov/dpp/short-term-rentals.html).

**Buyer takeaway:** if you're underwriting a non-resort-zoned Oahu property, your unit economics are 30-day-stay economics, not nightly STR economics. Comps need to be drawn from corporate-housing comps, not Airbnb comps.

## Walkthrough D — Austin, TX (Type 2 STR licensing)

**What it does:** Austin classifies STRs into Type 1 (owner-occupied), Type 2 (non-owner-occupied, residential), and Type 3 (commercial-zoned). Type 2 — the relevant category for most investors — is **capped citywide** and requires annual licensing.

**Practical effect:** new Type 2 licenses are limited; existing licenses transfer with property in some configurations. License availability fluctuates.

**Source:** [City of Austin — Short-Term Rental Licensing](https://www.austintexas.gov/department/short-term-rentals).

**Buyer takeaway:** verify Type 2 license availability for the specific parcel *before* LOI. A property that cannot obtain a Type 2 license has zero STR revenue potential under the current code.

## The four-layer check for any city

When you're evaluating a city you don't already know, walk these four layers in order. Don't stop at the first.

### Layer 1 — The STR-specific ordinance

This is the thing most buyers find first. Search "[city name] short-term rental ordinance" and read the actual code section, not the news article. The ordinance answers the four questions in the opening section.

### Layer 2 — The zoning code

The STR ordinance can say "STRs are permitted with a license," but the zoning code can say "this parcel is residential-only and overnight commercial accommodations are not a permitted use." When the two conflict, zoning typically wins.

Pull the parcel from the city or county GIS portal, identify the zoning designation, and read the permitted-use list for that zone. STRs may be a permitted use, a conditional use (requires public hearing), or a prohibited use depending on the zone.

### Layer 3 — Lodging tax registration

Most STR-active states require operators to register with the state Department of Revenue and remit lodging tax. Some states require county-level registration on top. The platform may or may not collect on your behalf.

[strhost.tools' 50-state lodging tax index](https://strhost.tools/lodging-tax) tracks which states have full Airbnb-side collection vs. which leave gaps the host has to remit manually.

Buyers underestimate the registration burden. In some markets it takes 30–60 days from registration application to first legitimate listing.

### Layer 4 — HOA / condo declaration

The often-fatal one. A condo declaration or HOA covenant can prohibit STRs even when the city ordinance permits them. The HOA rule is enforced privately by lawsuit; the city ordinance is enforced publicly by code enforcement. They are independent.

Pull the recorded CC&Rs and the current HOA bylaws for the property *before LOI*. Search for terms: "short-term," "transient," "vacation rental," "minimum lease," "rental restriction." A 30-day or 6-month minimum lease in the CC&Rs is a death sentence for an STR thesis.

## A practical checklist for the parcel you're underwriting

For each candidate property, answer these in writing before LOI:

- [ ] What is the parcel's zoning designation?
- [ ] Are STRs a permitted, conditional, or prohibited use in that zone?
- [ ] Does the city require an STR-specific license? At what cost? With what cap or limit?
- [ ] Is there a primary-residence requirement?
- [ ] Is there a night cap?
- [ ] Has there been any STR-related ordinance change in the last 24 months? Any pending?
- [ ] Does the state require lodging-tax registration? Does the platform collect, or is it on the host?
- [ ] Does the HOA / condo / co-op declaration permit short-term rentals? At what minimum stay?
- [ ] Is there a parking, occupancy, or noise ordinance that disproportionately affects STRs?
- [ ] Have neighboring property owners filed STR-related complaints recently?

Anything that scores "unclear" gets a phone call to the city planning department before LOI. Better to spend an hour on hold now than 14 months in litigation later.

## Pending changes — markets to watch

Not exhaustive, and verify current status before underwriting:

- **Several mid-Atlantic and Northeast suburban markets** have proposed primary-residence restrictions on the model of LA's HSO. Track local council agendas.
- **Texas Hill Country** — Fredericksburg, Wimberley, and surrounding towns have active STR ordinance debates. Status varies.
- **Florida Gulf Coast condominium associations** — many post-2024 declarations have added 30-day minimums.
- **Mountain West ski-resort towns** (Park City, Telluride, Crested Butte) — periodic moratorium proposals on new STR licenses.

The municipal STR registry, where one exists, is the authoritative source. The [BiggerPockets STR forum](https://www.biggerpockets.com/forums/535-short-term-and-vacation-rental-discussions) is often the leading indicator — operators report enforcement and ordinance changes in real time, weeks before the trackers update.

## Trend direction

Three patterns visible across U.S. STR regulation since 2022:

1. **Primary-residence restrictions are spreading.** LA's model has been copied or adapted in dozens of mid-size cities. Investor-owned unhosted STRs are losing ground in larger metros.
2. **Resort-zone carve-outs are stable.** Designated resort or tourism zones generally preserve STR rights even as residential zones tighten. Buying inside a resort zone is more durable than buying outside one in the same city.
3. **Rural and small-town markets remain permissive but volatile.** A town can vote in restrictions on six months' notice. The smaller the local government, the more political risk per cycle.

The implication for buyers: in a 10-year hold, regulation is the single largest non-financial risk on the table.

## Where this leaves the deal

Three outcomes from a regulation read:

1. **Open / stable** — proceed with normal underwriting.
2. **Restricted but workable** — proceed with adjusted underwriting (30-day-minimum thesis, primary-residence model, etc.) and re-run [DSCR](/dscr-calculator/), [cash-on-cash](/cash-on-cash-calculator/), and [year 1 cash needs](/year-1-cash-needs/) on the adjusted thesis. Often the deal still works at a different price.
3. **Restricted and not workable** — kill the deal. Move to the next market. The [market score framework](/blog/airbnb-market-score/) screens these out at the city level before you reach property selection.

## Frequently asked

**The city's STR page says no restrictions, but the HOA forbids it. Who wins?** The HOA, in practical terms. City permits don't override private deed restrictions. Verify CC&Rs before LOI.

**My loan officer said it's fine to operate as STR even though the loan documents call it owner-occupied. Is it?** No. Conventional second-home and primary-residence loans have occupancy requirements. Operating against those terms can trigger acceleration. Use a DSCR loan or investment loan if STR is the actual use.

**Are platform-collected lodging taxes always sufficient?** No. In some counties Airbnb collects state-level sales tax but not the county tourist development tax. Operators in those counties must register separately and remit the county portion. Get specific by state.

**Can grandfathered properties keep operating after restrictions pass?** Sometimes. Many ordinances grandfather licensed operators at the date of enactment. The grandfather right is rarely transferable to a new owner — verify before assuming a regulatory grandfather travels with the property.

## Closing

Regulation is the input that compounds slowly until it doesn't. The market with permissive rules today can be the market with primary-residence restrictions in 18 months — the cycle from "first proposal" to "enforcement" runs about 14–24 months in most U.S. cities.

Underwrite at the point in the regulatory cycle the city is *headed*, not the point it currently sits. A market that just passed restrictions is more predictable than a market the day before a council vote.
