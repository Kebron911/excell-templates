Most short-term rental "deal analyses" you see online stop at projected revenue. ADR times occupancy times 365, less a fee, and the spreadsheet spits out a green number. That is not an analysis. That is a hope.

A deal-underwriting screen for an STR — the kind a junior analyst at a small REIT would run before recommending the LOI — has five separable layers: revenue, operating cost, debt cost, working-capital cost, and the regulatory tail risk. Each one can kill the deal independently. This piece walks the layers in order and shows the math at each step.

> **Not investment advice.** Every market and every deal is fact-specific. The numbers below are illustrative. Do your own due diligence and review with a CPA and licensed lender before signing anything.

## Layer 1 — Revenue you can actually defend

Revenue projection is the input every other layer compounds against. If you start optimistic, every downstream output is wrong by the same multiple. Three numbers feed it:

- **ADR** — Average Daily Rate. Pull comps from at least three sources: AirDNA's free city snapshots, Mashvisor estimates, and three live competitor listings on Airbnb itself. Take the trimmed mean.
- **Occupancy** — for an unfurnished new listing in a competitive market, plan on 50–55% in months 1–6 even if the market average is 65%. New listings are punished by the booking algorithms until they accumulate reviews.
- **Seasonality** — most non-urban STR markets have ADR variance of 1.6–2.4× between peak and trough months. Annual averages hide it.

Worked example, a hypothetical 2-bed cabin near Pigeon Forge, TN:

- ADR: $215 (trimmed mean of 9 comps, Q1 2026)
- Occupancy, year 1 stabilized: 58%
- Annual gross revenue: 215 × 365 × 0.58 = **$45,524**

Note that this is *gross collected from the platform*, not what hits your bank. We strip platform fees and cleaning pass-through next.

### The "stabilized" qualifier matters

A deal pro forma that uses stabilized year-2 occupancy in year 1 is overstating cash flow by 8–15%. Underwrite year 1 conservatively. Use a stabilized number in years 2 and beyond. The spread between the two is your ramp risk and it should be priced in.

## Layer 2 — Operating cost, not just "expenses"

Hosts typically underestimate four cost lines:

1. **Cleaning, paid versus collected.** You pass through a cleaning fee to the guest, but you pay the cleaner. The two are rarely identical. Budget the gap.
2. **Utilities at occupancy.** A cabin at 58% occupancy in August costs roughly 1.4× the residential utility baseline. Especially HVAC and water.
3. **Capex reserve.** STR turnover cycles every 2–4 nights versus every 12 months for a long-term rental. Furniture, paint, appliances, and HVAC depreciate faster. Plan 1–3% of property value per year. Skip it and one $9,000 HVAC bill erases a profitable quarter.
4. **Lodging tax pass-through gaps.** In some counties Airbnb collects state sales tax but *not* the county tourist development tax. Our sister site has the [50-state lodging tax index](https://strhost.tools/lodging-tax) flagging which states have full platform collection.

Same Pigeon Forge example, monthly operating cost:

| Line | Monthly | Annual |
|---|---|---|
| Platform fee (3% standard split) | $114 | $1,366 |
| Cleaning paid (over collected) | $200 | $2,400 |
| Utilities + internet | $385 | $4,620 |
| Insurance (STR policy, not residential) | $190 | $2,280 |
| Repairs + capex reserve (2.5% of value) | $625 | $7,500 |
| Property tax | $190 | $2,280 |
| Software (PMS, dynamic pricing) | $55 | $660 |
| **Total operating cost** | **$1,759** | **$21,106** |

Net operating income before debt: 45,524 − 21,106 = **$24,418/yr**.

For a deeper walk through profit math the way an operator does it, the [strhost.tools profitability post](https://strhost.tools/blog/how-to-calculate-airbnb-profitability) is the closest sibling read.

## Layer 3 — Debt cost and DSCR

Most STR purchases under $750k are financed with one of four products:

- **Conventional second-home loan** — lowest rate, but stricter occupancy rules and 10% minimum down.
- **DSCR loan** — non-QM, qualified by the property's projected income, not your W-2. Visio, Kiavi, Lima One are the active lenders. Rates run 75–150 bps above conventional.
- **Conventional investment loan** — 20–25% down minimum, qualified by your DTI.
- **Cash-out refi from a primary** — viable if you have equity, awful if rates have moved against you since you bought the primary.

The DSCR calculator at [/dscr-calculator/](/dscr-calculator/) outputs the ratio most lenders price off:

```
DSCR = Net Operating Income / Annual Debt Service
```

Visio's standard tier requires DSCR ≥ 1.0; their preferred pricing kicks in at 1.20+. Below 1.0, you're either taking a higher rate, putting more down, or being declined.

Our worked Pigeon Forge example, $480,000 purchase, 25% down ($120,000), 30-year fixed at 7.75% on $360,000:

- Monthly P&I: $2,580
- Annual debt service: $30,960
- DSCR: 24,418 / 30,960 = **0.79**

That deal does not finance at standard DSCR pricing. Two ways out: more equity (drop loan to $300,000 → debt service $25,800 → DSCR 0.95, still tight) or a higher-revenue thesis (different property, different submarket, different operating model).

This is the moment most amateur underwriters stop and call the deal dead. A professional underwriter stops, recalculates the maximum supportable loan, and *then* decides whether the equity check still hits the return target. The latter is what the [break-even purchase price](/blog/str-break-even-purchase-price) post walks through.

## Layer 4 — Working-capital cost

The line buyers underestimate most: how much cash hits the table *after* the down payment and *before* the first guest pays.

- Closing costs (2–4% of purchase): $9,600–$19,200
- Furnishing package (3-bed cabin, mid-tier): $28,000–$42,000
- Photography, listing setup, smart locks, Wi-Fi router class upgrade: $2,500–$4,500
- Operating reserve (3 months opex + debt service before stabilization): $14,000–$18,000

For the worked example, total Year-1 cash needs come to roughly $175,000–$200,000 above the $120,000 down. The [year 1 cash needs calculator](/year-1-cash-needs/) breaks this out.

Most failed STR deals are not cash-flow failures. They are working-capital failures — the operator runs out of cash in month 4 because furnishing went $11,000 over budget and the first 90 days of bookings undershot.

## Layer 5 — Regulatory tail risk

The deal sheet that ignores regulation is the one that gets stopped six months in by an enforcement letter. A non-exhaustive recent set:

- **New York City Local Law 18 of 2022** — effectively banned non-hosted STRs under 30 days. Listings collapsed roughly 90% in 2023.
- **Los Angeles HSO ordinance** — primary-residence-only for unhosted rentals, capped at 120 nights/year unless extended permit.
- **Hawaii Bill 41 (Honolulu)** — minimum 30-night stays in most non-resort districts.
- **Austin, TX** — Type 2 STR licensing, capped citywide.

Read the city's STR code yourself before LOI. A free starting point is the [BiggerPockets STR forum's regulation thread](https://www.biggerpockets.com/forums/535-short-term-and-vacation-rental-discussions) — operators report enforcement faster than ordinance trackers update.

We cover the regulation read in more depth in [Short-Term Rental Regulation by City](/blog/str-regulation-by-city/).

## Putting the five layers together

A deal passes the screen when:

1. Stabilized year-2 NOI is positive at conservative ADR/occupancy
2. DSCR ≥ 1.0 at the lender's actual rate (not the headline rate)
3. Year-1 cash needs are within 1.4× the down payment
4. Cap rate clears your alternative-investment hurdle (more on that in the [vacation rental cap rate](/blog/vacation-rental-cap-rate/) post)
5. Regulation is either permissive or stable for the foreseeable horizon

Pigeon Forge example fails on (2) at the original $480,000 ask. It would pass at $410,000 with the same operating thesis. That's the offer.

## The single biggest analyst error

Treating the projection as the deal. The projection is a *thesis*. The deal is whatever survives a 20% adverse move in occupancy, a 100 bps rate move, and a single $7,500 unexpected capex hit in year 1. Underwrite the deal with all three of those happening at once. If it still cash-flows, the thesis has slack.

Most pro formas have zero slack. That is the bug.

## Frequently asked

**How conservative should year-1 occupancy be?** For a brand-new listing, 12–18 percentage points below market average for the first 90 days, then ramp. For a property with transferable reviews, market average minus 4 points.

**Is a DSCR loan worth the rate premium?** Sometimes. The premium runs 75–150 bps. If using a DSCR loan lets you keep your DTI clean for the next deal, it can be the right move on a portfolio path even when the rate looks worse standalone.

**Should I include personal labor in operating cost?** Yes. Charge the deal $25–$45 per booking for guest comms / dispatch, even if you do it yourself. Otherwise you'll never know whether the deal was profitable or whether *you were* profitable.

**Cap rate or cash-on-cash — which matters more?** Cash-on-cash for the equity decision; cap rate for the comparable-deal screen. They answer different questions. The [cap rate post](/blog/vacation-rental-cap-rate/) walks the distinction.

## Closing note

Deal analysis is a discipline of subtraction. You start with the gross revenue thesis, subtract every line of cost honestly, subtract again for adverse-scenario stress, and what's left is the deal. If your spreadsheet starts with revenue and ends green without an adverse-stress block, it isn't an analysis. It's a brochure.

Build the brochure for the seller. Run the analysis for yourself.
