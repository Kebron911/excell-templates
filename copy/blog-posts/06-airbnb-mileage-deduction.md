## The two ways to claim STR mileage

You can choose, per vehicle per year, either:

### Method 1: Standard mileage rate (simpler)

Multiply business miles × the IRS standard rate.

```
2025 STANDARD RATE: 67¢/mile (business / self-employment)
2026 STANDARD RATE: TBD — IRS publishes in late December
```

That's it. No tracking gas, depreciation, insurance, maintenance separately. The standard rate is intended to bake all those in.

**Example:**
1,800 business miles × 67¢ = **$1,206 deduction**

### Method 2: Actual expense method (more record-keeping, sometimes more deduction)

Track every vehicle expense for the year (gas, insurance, depreciation, oil, tires, repairs, registration, lease payments). Multiply by the business-use percentage:

```
Business use % = Business miles ÷ Total miles
```

**Example:**
- Total vehicle expenses (gas + insurance + depreciation + maintenance): $7,200
- Total miles driven: 14,000
- Business miles: 1,800
- Business use %: 12.86%
- Deduction: $7,200 × 12.86% = **$926**

In this case the standard rate ($1,206) wins. For high-cost vehicles (trucks, EVs with rapid depreciation, leased high-end cars), the actual expense method sometimes wins by hundreds of dollars.

### Which method should you choose?

| Choose Standard Rate if... | Choose Actual Expense if... |
|----------------------------|------------------------------|
| You drive a paid-off, cheap-to-operate vehicle | You drive a new, high-depreciation vehicle |
| You track mileage but not every receipt | You're disciplined about every vehicle receipt |
| <40% business use | >50% business use AND high actual costs |
| You'll switch vehicles often | You'll keep the same vehicle 5+ years |

**Trap:** if you choose actual expense in year 1, you cannot switch back to standard rate later for that vehicle (with some narrow exceptions). The standard rate is the "default" because it preserves your flexibility.

## Which trips count as "business miles"?

The IRS standard: any trip with a primary business purpose for your STR.

### YES — these count (track every one)

- **Property visits**: any trip to your STR for any STR-related reason
- **Supply runs**: Costco/Target/Amazon-pickup for STR supplies
- **Cleaner / handyman meetings**: even if you only meet them briefly
- **Vendor meetings**: meeting your CPA, attorney, photographer specifically for STR business
- **Inspection / permit / city-hall trips**: anything regulatory
- **Showings if you're considering buying another STR**: yes (acquisition-related travel is deductible for active investors)
- **Driving guests** between the STR and a service you provide (rare, but counts)
- **Picking up a forgotten item** at the STR (laundry, leftover bedding, etc.)
- **Bank deposits / mail runs** specifically for STR business

### NO — these don't count

- Commuting from your home to a *fixed primary workplace*. (For most solo STR owners, there is no fixed workplace, so this is rarely an issue. But if you have a home office, the office-to-property trip *is* deductible.)
- Personal stays at the STR (= personal-use days, separate trap)
- Trips that combine personal + business if business was secondary

### The "mixed-purpose trip" rule

If you drive 18 miles to your STR, and on the way back stop at the grocery store for your home, the **outbound trip is fully deductible**; the return trip's *additional* mileage to swing by the store is not.

The cleanest way to handle this: track each trip as either "STR business" or "personal" at the moment it happens. If a trip is mixed, log the STR-business portion only.

## What you must track (the IRS substantiation rules)

To deduct any business mileage, you must maintain a **contemporaneous mileage log** with:

1. **Date** of the trip
2. **Starting odometer or starting location**
3. **Ending odometer or ending location**
4. **Total miles driven**
5. **Business purpose** (specifically: "STR — meeting cleaner at Beach House #1" is good; "Airbnb stuff" is not)
6. **Property** (if you own multiple STRs)

The IRS is allowed to disallow your *entire* mileage deduction if your log is missing any of these — and they have. Cohan v. Commissioner is the case hosts point to for relief from the perfect-record requirement, but post-1996 changes to IRC §274(d) effectively closed that escape hatch for vehicle expenses.

**Bottom line:** track every trip. The 30 seconds of logging saves you $200-$1,200 at audit time.

## The 3 most-missed STR trips

### Trip 1: Costco / supply run

Most STR hosts do 6-15 supply runs a year. Each one is typically 8-25 miles round-trip. That's **48-375 miles/year**, worth **$32 to $251**. Almost no host tracks these because they feel like personal trips ("I was going anyway"). But if the run included buying for the STR — even mixed with personal items — the proportional STR mileage counts.

### Trip 2: Property visit to deliver/retrieve a single item

You drove 24 miles round-trip to drop off a forgotten guidebook. That's a $16 deduction. You'll do this 8-15 times a year. Annual value: **$128 to $241**.

### Trip 3: Cleaner / handyman meeting

These are short trips (often <30 min) but happen 12-25 times a year. **$96 to $419 in deductions** typically.

**Combined: a typical STR host leaves $256 to $911 on the table just from these three trip types.** Over 5 years, that's $1,280 to $4,555.

## The home office multiplier

If you have a **dedicated home office** for your STR (a room used exclusively and regularly for STR business), the home office unlocks a side benefit for mileage:

Without a home office, the trip from your home to your STR counts as commuting (= not deductible). With a home office, that same trip starts FROM your "office" (i.e., your business location) and becomes fully deductible.

This is most impactful for hosts whose property is far from home — a 60-mile-each-way property visit can be the difference between $0 and **$80** per trip.

To qualify the home office:
- The room is used *exclusively* for STR business (it can't double as a guest room)
- It's used *regularly* (not just "sometimes")
- It's your principal place of business for STR (= you don't have another office for it)

Form 8829 captures the home-office calculation. Cross-reference [IRS Pub 587](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/p587.pdf).

## Where mileage lands on your tax return

On **Schedule E** (rental real estate):
- Line 6 (Auto and travel)
- Cross-reference to Form 4562 if you're using the actual-expense method and claiming depreciation on the vehicle

On **Schedule C** (if your STR is a substantial-services / B&B-style operation):
- Line 9 (Car and truck expenses)
- Same Form 4562 link if actual-expense method

Most STR hosts file Schedule E. If you're not sure which schedule, see [Schedule E vs Schedule C for Airbnb](thestrledger.com/blog/airbnb-schedule-e).

## The 6 mistakes that get mileage disallowed

1. **No log, just an estimate.** "I drove about 2,000 miles for the STR" is not a deduction; it's a flagrant audit invitation.
2. **Reconstructed log.** Building the log AFTER the IRS notice arrives is technically allowed but the IRS heavily discounts it. Track contemporaneously.
3. **Claiming standard rate after using actual.** Once you depreciate the vehicle on actual-expense, you can't go back to standard for that vehicle in most cases.
4. **Claiming the full vehicle for STR.** Unless your vehicle is 100% used for STR (unusual), you must allocate by business-use %.
5. **Mixing tax-year miles.** Each year's miles are claimed in that year. You can't roll prior-year miles into a current return.
6. **Same trip claimed by two co-owners.** Joint owners must split the deduction (typically 50/50 unless ownership % differs).

## The 90-second annual ritual

Every December 31:

1. **Read the odometer**, photograph it. Date stamp.
2. **Export your tracking app** to CSV (or photograph your handwritten log).
3. **Tally business miles.** Apply the IRS standard rate. Save to `templates/_delivery/your-bookkeeping/mileage/{{ year }}.xlsx`.
4. **Tell your CPA the number.** They drop it on Schedule E line 6.

That's the whole annual workflow. The work happens at the trip; year-end is just tallying.
