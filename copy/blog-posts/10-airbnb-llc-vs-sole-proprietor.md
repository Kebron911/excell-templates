## The single most important thing to understand about LLCs and taxes

**A single-member LLC, by default, is taxed exactly the same way as a sole proprietor.**

When you form a single-member LLC and don't elect anything else, the IRS treats it as a **disregarded entity** for federal income tax purposes. That means:

- Your STR income still goes on **Schedule E** of your personal 1040 (or Schedule C if substantial-services)
- You still pay the same federal income tax
- You still pay the same self-employment tax (zero if Schedule E; 15.3% if Schedule C)
- You still claim the same deductions
- Form 1099s, W-9s, and all reporting works the same

**The total federal tax you pay on Airbnb income with an LLC is identical to what you'd pay without one.** The IRS calls this "disregarded" for a reason: from a tax-collection standpoint, the LLC is invisible.

This isn't a loophole the LLC structure is supposed to close. It's the default. Forming an LLC, by itself, saves you exactly $0 in federal income tax.

So why do people still form them? Two reasons:

1. **Asset protection** (the real reason)
2. **Operational hygiene** (a secondary but valuable reason)

---

## Reason 1: Asset protection

If a guest slips on your STR's icy front steps and sues, who do they sue?

**Without an LLC:** They sue you personally. If their judgment exceeds your STR insurance limit (typical $1M/$2M), they can come after your other assets — your primary home, your other properties, your bank accounts, your wages.

**With an LLC:** They sue the LLC. If the judgment exceeds the LLC's assets (your STR + the cash in its checking account), it generally stops there. Your personal assets are protected by the **limited liability** that gives the LLC its name.

This protection has caveats:

### What can pierce the LLC veil (= remove asset protection)

- **Commingling funds.** Paying personal expenses from the LLC's bank account, or vice versa. This is the most common veil-piercer.
- **Personal guarantees on debt.** If you signed personally for the STR mortgage, the lender can come after you personally despite the LLC.
- **Fraud or knowing wrongdoing.** Hiding from a known dangerous condition.
- **Not following corporate formalities.** Not maintaining the LLC operating agreement, not filing annual reports in the state, etc.

### When LLC asset protection is most valuable

- Higher-value STR (> $500K)
- High-traffic STR (more guests = more incident odds)
- Risky features (pool, hot tub, fire pit, dock, steep deck)
- Operator with significant personal assets to protect
- Operator already at high risk of lawsuits (medical professional, business owner with public visibility)

### When LLC asset protection is overkill

- Small, low-value STR (<$200K) with no risky features
- Operator with no significant personal assets to protect (= judgment-proof anyway)
- Personal home that's already heavily mortgaged (= no equity for plaintiff to attach)

---

## Reason 2: Operational hygiene

Even without litigation risk, an LLC gives you operational benefits:

| Benefit | What it does |
|---------|--------------|
| Dedicated business bank account | Cleaner books, easier tax prep, separation of personal and business |
| Dedicated EIN | No more giving out your SSN on W-9s, vendor forms, etc. |
| Professional appearance | "Beach House LLC" reads more business-like to vendors and platforms |
| Clear ownership transfer | Selling the STR can be done by transferring LLC membership interests instead of titling the property — cleaner closing |
| Multi-owner structure | If a partner co-owns the STR, LLC operating agreement governs profit splits, responsibilities, exit |

These are real benefits, but they're achievable without an LLC (just open a separate business checking account in your name, get an EIN as a sole prop, etc.). The LLC just packages them cleanly.

## The cost of an LLC

LLCs are not free. State-specific costs vary wildly. Some examples (verify current with your state's Secretary of State):

| State | Formation fee | Annual fee | Notes |
|-------|---------------|------------|-------|
| Texas | $300 | $0 (under $1.23M revenue) | One of the cheapest |
| Wyoming | $100 | $60 | Popular for anonymity reasons |
| Florida | $125 | $138.75 | Annual report due May 1 |
| Tennessee | $300 | $300 minimum | Surprisingly expensive |
| California | $70 | $800 (franchise tax) | Hostile to small LLCs |
| New York | $200 | $25 every 2 years | Plus newspaper publication requirement (~$200-800 one-time) |
| Delaware | $90 | $300 (franchise tax) | Popular for big corps; less useful for small STR |

Plus:
- **Registered agent:** $50-300/yr if you use a service (or free if you accept service of process at your own address — but doxxes your home address)
- **Operating agreement:** $200-500 if you have an attorney draft it (templates available cheaper but state-specific tweaks help)
- **Bookkeeping:** A separate set of books for the LLC, possibly more CPA time

**Typical all-in annual cost for a single STR LLC:** $300-$1,500/yr, depending on state.

For most STR hosts, that's well worth the asset protection.

## The S-corp election conversation (the hot take that's mostly wrong for STR)

You've probably read on Reddit: "Elect S-corp status for your LLC and save 15.3% in self-employment tax!"

For STR hosts, this is **almost always wrong**, and here's why:

### S-corp election only helps if you have self-employment income

The 15.3% self-employment tax applies to **Schedule C** income (= substantial-services / B&B-style STR). For **Schedule E** rental income (= 90% of typical Airbnb hosts), there is no self-employment tax to save. The S-corp election can't reduce a tax that doesn't apply.

### Even for Schedule C STRs, the S-corp path is full of traps

- S-corp owners must take a "reasonable salary" via W-2 payroll, which forces you to run payroll software, pay state unemployment insurance, comply with state-specific employer rules. Easily $500-1,500/yr in compliance costs.
- The "savings" only kicks in past a certain income level — typically $40K+ net.
- Some states (CA notably) tax S-corp passthrough income at the entity level too, eroding savings.
- If the IRS later reclassifies your STR as Schedule E (= no SE tax to begin with), the S-corp election was unnecessary overhead.

### When S-corp election DOES make sense for STR-adjacent income

- You're a property manager (you charge other STR owners management fees) → that's Schedule C income, and the S-corp election is genuinely valuable past ~$60K
- You operate a B&B with substantial services (meals, daily housekeeping) → same logic
- You operate a "boutique stay" with hotel-level services

Otherwise: skip the S-corp election. Single-member LLC, default disregarded-entity tax treatment, file on Schedule E.

## Multi-member LLCs (you + a partner)

If you're co-owning an STR with a spouse, partner, or investor, the LLC default tax treatment is **partnership taxation**:

- You'll file **Form 1065** (partnership return) at the entity level
- Each member gets a **Schedule K-1** showing their share of income/loss
- Each member reports the K-1 income on their personal Schedule E

The partnership filing adds ~$400-1,500 of annual CPA cost to your tax prep but gives you a clean operating-agreement structure to govern the partnership. For investor-partner setups, this is usually the right structure.

**Spouse-only owners in community property states** can elect a "qualified joint venture" to skip the partnership return, but the rules are state-specific. Confirm with CPA.

## The decision tree

```
Is your STR worth more than $300K?
└── YES → LLC strongly recommended
└── NO →
    └── Does your STR have risky features (pool, hot tub, fire pit, etc)?
        └── YES → LLC recommended
        └── NO →
            └── Do you have significant personal assets outside the STR?
                └── YES → LLC recommended
                └── NO →
                    └── Are you in a state with low LLC costs (TX, WY, FL)?
                        └── YES → LLC recommended (cheap insurance)
                        └── NO → 
                            └── Operate as sole prop with strong STR insurance
                                Revisit when STR value or income grows
```

**One more layer:** if you have multiple STRs, the *series LLC* structure (or a holding-company / subsidiary structure) can isolate each property's liability from the others. Worth a conversation with an attorney if you cross 3 properties.

## What to do this week

Regardless of which structure you choose:

1. **Open a separate business bank account** for your STR. Stop commingling funds today.
2. **Get an EIN** (free, takes 10 minutes at IRS.gov) — use this on W-9s, vendor forms, instead of your SSN.
3. **Buy STR-specific insurance** (Proper Insurance, Obie, CBIZ) — your homeowners policy almost certainly does NOT cover commercial rental use.
4. **Set up STR-only credit card** — separates expenses for bookkeeping AND avoids 1099-NEC obligations on contractor payments.
5. **Track every personal-use day** at the STR — this matters for both tax and entity-veil considerations.

These five steps achieve 80% of the operational benefit of an LLC, even if you never form one. They're free or near-free, and they don't require an attorney.

## When to consult an actual attorney

The cost of a 1-hour STR-attorney consult is typically $250-500. Worth it when:

- You're forming the LLC (state-specific templates exist but operating agreements should be reviewed)
- You're buying STR #2+ and considering series LLC or holding company structures
- You're forming with a non-spouse partner
- You're in a state with hostile LLC rules (CA, NJ, NY)
- You've already had one liability scare and want to upgrade your protection

## What an LLC will NOT do for you

To save you Reddit research time, the LLC will NOT:

- Reduce your federal income tax bill
- Reduce your state income tax bill (in most states)
- Eliminate self-employment tax (you don't pay SE tax on Schedule E income anyway)
- Make you eligible for the QBI deduction (rental income's QBI eligibility depends on different factors — the "real-estate-business safe harbor" or material participation, not entity choice)
- Protect you from your own negligence (you're still personally liable if YOU were the one who did the negligent act)
- Hide your identity (some states require LLC ownership disclosure; only a few states + privacy structures shield you fully)
