## Section 1: The deductions every host knows (but often misuses)

Start here because these are table-stakes. If your current tax return doesn't include them, stop reading and fix that first.

### 1. Mortgage interest (Schedule E, Line 12)

Deductible in proportion to the rental use of the property. If you personally use the STR for 15+ days per year, you must allocate between personal-use days and rental-use days. Most pure STR operators have zero personal-use days and deduct 100%.

### 2. Property taxes (Schedule E, Line 16)

Same allocation rule as mortgage interest. Pro-rate if you have personal-use days.

### 3. Insurance (Schedule E, Line 9)

Covers your homeowners/landlord/STR-specific insurance (like Proper or Obie). Pro-rate with personal-use days. Don't forget to include umbrella policies if specifically covering the rental.

### 4. Depreciation (Schedule E, Line 18)

The building itself depreciates over 27.5 years (residential rental property). This is automatic — it doesn't require cash outflow — and most hosts handle it correctly. What most miss is **accelerating it via cost segregation** (see #23).

### 5. Repairs and maintenance (Schedule E, Line 14)

Anything that restores property to its previous condition without adding value. Fixing a broken AC unit = repair. Replacing it with a new one = capital improvement (depreciated, not expensed).

### 6. Utilities (Schedule E, Line 17)

All utilities paid for the STR: electricity, gas, water, sewer, trash, internet, streaming services, cable. Internet and streaming are often missed.

### 7. Cleaning and maintenance labor (Schedule E, Line 14)

Payments to your cleaners, handymen, landscapers. Must issue 1099-NEC if paying any individual contractor more than $600/yr.

### 8. Management fees (Schedule E, Line 11)

If you use a co-host or property manager, their fees are deductible. Includes platform-side "professional hosting" services.

## Section 2: The deductions most commonly missed

These are the big ones. If you're not claiming all of these, start today.

### 9. Airbnb/VRBO service fees (Schedule E, Line 19 — "Other Expenses")

Every time Airbnb takes their 3% host fee off your payout, that's a deductible expense — not gross income you then write off. Your actual gross income is the total guest payment; the service fee is a deduction.

If your 1099-K from Airbnb reports the total guest payment (including their service fee), verify you're not double-counting. This is one of the most common errors.

### 10. Furniture and furnishings (depreciation, not expense)

Every couch, bed, table, and decor item you bought for the rental is depreciable. **5-year MACRS class** — faster than the 27.5-year building. Plus you can typically take bonus depreciation or Section 179 on the first-year cost.

### 11. Appliances (5-year property)

Refrigerator, washer, dryer, microwave, coffee maker, dishwasher. 5-year depreciation, eligible for bonus/Section 179.

### 12. Electronics and small equipment

TVs, smart speakers, smart locks, thermostats, security cameras, gaming consoles (if in the rental). 5-year property, bonus-eligible.

### 13. Linens and textiles

Sheets, towels, pillows, comforters, shower curtains, rugs. Technically these are "supplies" if replaced regularly — deducted in full when purchased.

### 14. Cleaning supplies

Laundry detergent, dish soap, bathroom cleaner, trash bags, paper towels, toilet paper. 100% deductible when purchased.

### 15. Guest consumables

Coffee, tea, bottled water, single-serve snacks, welcome-basket items. 100% deductible.

### 16. Marketing and advertising

Platform-side boost fees, professional photography, social media ads, listing-optimization tools. Fully deductible.

### 17. Professional photography

One-time photography investment (often $300–$1,500 for an STR) is typically a current-year deduction, not depreciation. If it's a major "re-shoot" project it may be capitalized — talk to your CPA.

### 18. Listing software

PriceLabs, Wheelhouse, Beyond Pricing, Hospitable, Hostfully, OwnerRez, Furnished Finder premium subscriptions, Turno — all deductible software/services.

### 19. Smart home / IoT devices

Smart locks, smart thermostats, smart TVs, motion sensors, leak detectors. 5-year property.

### 20. Outdoor furniture and improvements

Patio sets, grills, fire pits, hammocks, outdoor lighting. Some furniture = 5-year; some landscape-integrated improvements = 15-year.

### 21. Pool and hot tub maintenance

Monthly pool service, chemicals, filters, resurfacing. Current-year expense unless it's a major renovation.

### 22. HOA fees

If you own an STR in an HOA, those fees are fully deductible as a rental expense.

### 23. Cost segregation accelerated depreciation

This is the big one. Instead of depreciating the entire acquisition cost over 27.5 years, a cost segregation study separates out:

- **5-year property:** furniture, appliances, decorative elements, some fixtures
- **7-year property:** certain business equipment
- **15-year property:** landscaping, outdoor lighting, paved surfaces, fencing
- **27.5-year property:** the building itself

For a typical STR under $500K acquisition cost, a self-segregated analysis can accelerate **$15K–$50K of depreciation into year one** — especially if you combine it with bonus depreciation.

Engineer-led studies ($3K–$8K service fee) are appropriate for properties over ~$500K. Under that threshold, a self-done study is acceptable to the IRS — it just requires good documentation.

### 24. Bonus depreciation

In 2026, you can take 40% bonus depreciation on qualifying 5-year and 15-year property. Combined with cost segregation, this can meaningfully reduce your first-year tax bill. Note: the bonus percentage is on a phase-down schedule — confirm current-year rate.

### 25. Section 179 deduction

Allows up to $1,160,000 (2026 limit — confirm) of qualifying property to be expensed entirely in year one. For STRs, particularly useful on appliances, furniture, and some equipment. Has income limits and recapture rules — coordinate with your CPA.

## Section 3: The travel, vehicle, and home-based deductions

### 26. Mileage to and from the property

70 cents per mile in 2026 (confirm current IRS standard mileage rate). Every trip to the STR for maintenance, turnover, cleaning inspection, or guest support counts. Requires a mileage log — date, purpose, start/end odometer, total miles.

One reliable mileage log, used consistently, commonly produces $2,000–$8,000 per year in deductions for hosts with multiple trips.

### 27. Per-diem meals when traveling to a property

When staying overnight at or near your rental for maintenance/management, the meals-and-incidentals per-diem rate applies. Varies by location — check GSA.gov rates.

### 28. Airfare / lodging when managing remotely

If you own STRs in multiple cities and travel to them, both airfare and your (non-rental) lodging qualify as ordinary business expenses.

### 29. Rental car costs

If you rent a car for STR-related trips, the full cost is deductible.

### 30. Home office deduction

A dedicated portion of your home used exclusively for STR management qualifies. Two methods:
- **Simplified:** $5 per square foot, up to 300 sq ft ($1,500 max)
- **Actual:** proportional share of home expenses — often higher for larger offices

### 31. Cell phone (business-use portion)

The business-use percentage of your cell phone bill is deductible. Most hosts use 40–70% for STR management — document your methodology.

### 32. Internet (home office portion)

Same concept — the portion of home internet used for STR management is deductible.

## Section 4: The professional-services deductions

### 33. Legal fees

Attorney fees for LLC formation, rental agreement drafting, regulatory compliance, tenant disputes. All deductible.

### 34. Accounting and bookkeeping fees

Your CPA, bookkeeper, and tax-prep software are deductible in the year paid.

### 35. Banking fees

Business bank account fees, wire transfer fees, Stripe fees on payouts (if not already netted).

### 36. Credit card fees

Business credit card annual fees, if the card is used exclusively for STR.

### 37. Tax preparation software

TurboTax, H&R Block Online, TaxAct — if used for the STR return. If personal use overlaps, allocate.

### 38. Professional development

STR courses, webinars, conferences, books specifically about running an STR business.

### 39. Industry subscriptions

AirDNA, BiggerPockets Pro, industry publications, mastermind memberships.

## Section 5: The compliance and protective deductions

### 40. STR permit and license fees

Short-term rental permit applications, renewals, business license fees. Deductible.

### 41. Transient occupancy tax (TOT) registration

If your jurisdiction requires TOT registration fees, those are deductible. (Note: the tax you collect and remit is pass-through, not your expense.)

### 42. Airbnb insurance add-ons

Aircover supplementary coverage, host guarantee fees (if charged separately).

### 43. Security services

Alarm monitoring, security cameras (ongoing service fees), guest verification services.

### 44. Background check services

Services used to screen guests (Autohost, Guesty Guest Vetting, SafelyStay).

### 45. 1099 filing fees

Services like Track1099 or your payroll software for issuing contractor 1099s at year end.

---

## Section 6: The overlooked structural deductions

### 46. LLC maintenance fees

Annual state filing fees to maintain your LLC (varies by state — $50 in some, $800 in California).

### 47. Business-entity startup costs

If you formed an LLC or S-corp for your STR, the $5,000 first-year startup cost deduction applies. Excess is amortized over 15 years.
