## What is depreciation, really?

Depreciation is the IRS's recognition that physical assets wear out over time. Instead of deducting the full cost of a building in the year you buy it, you deduct a portion each year over the asset's "useful life."

For residential rental real estate (including most STRs), the building is depreciated over **27.5 years** straight-line. That means if you paid $300,000 for the building portion of an STR, you deduct roughly $10,909/year ($300,000 / 27.5) from your taxable income for 27.5 years.

Depreciation is "non-cash" — it doesn't represent money leaving your bank account. It's a **deduction that reduces taxable income without reducing cash**. For a host in the 24% tax bracket, $10,909 of depreciation = ~$2,618 of tax savings per year, every year, on that one property.

Miss this, and you're paying taxes on income you don't owe taxes on.

## The four depreciation classes every STR host should know

Not every asset in your STR depreciates over 27.5 years. The IRS has distinct **asset classes** with different recovery periods:

### Class 1: 27.5-Year Property — The Building

- Physical structure: foundation, walls, roof, plumbing, electrical (as part of the structure)
- Land improvements embedded in the structure
- Method: straight-line (equal deduction each year)
- **This is the default** most hosts use for everything — and it's where the money-leaving happens

### Class 2: 15-Year Property — Land Improvements

- Landscaping, paved driveways, fencing, exterior lighting, retaining walls, outdoor pools (the shell, not the pool equipment)
- Recovery period: 15 years
- Method: accelerated (150% declining balance, switching to straight-line)
- **Eligible for bonus depreciation**

### Class 3: 7-Year Property — Certain Equipment

- Office furniture and fixtures in the rental
- Some equipment not specifically in 5-year class
- Recovery period: 7 years
- Method: 200% declining balance, switching to straight-line

### Class 4: 5-Year Property — The Big One for STRs

- Furniture (beds, couches, tables, chairs)
- Appliances (refrigerator, washer, dryer, dishwasher, microwave)
- Electronics (TVs, smart speakers, smart locks, thermostats)
- Decorative fixtures
- Carpeting (sometimes, depending on integration)
- Small equipment, kitchenware
- Recovery period: 5 years
- Method: 200% declining balance, switching to straight-line
- **Eligible for bonus depreciation AND Section 179**

**The key insight:** a typical STR has **$30,000–$80,000 of 5-year property** (all the furniture, appliances, electronics you bought to make the house rentable) and **$5,000–$20,000 of 15-year property** (landscaping, outdoor improvements). If you're lumping all of that at 27.5 years, you're spreading $35K–$100K of deductions over 27.5 years when you could be taking most of it in year one.

## Bonus depreciation in 2026

Bonus depreciation lets you deduct a large percentage of **qualifying property's cost in the year you place it in service**, rather than depreciating it over multiple years.

Qualifying property includes:
- 5-year property (furniture, appliances, electronics)
- 15-year property (land improvements)
- NOT the 27.5-year building itself

The bonus percentage is on a **phase-down schedule** post-2022:
- 2023: 80%
- 2024: 60%
- 2025: 40%
- 2026: 20% (confirm current rate at publish time)
- 2027: 0%

**Verify current year percentage before publishing — this is on an active phase-down schedule.**

Bonus depreciation stacks on top of regular depreciation. If you place $50,000 of 5-year property into service in 2026 at the current bonus rate (confirm percentage at publish time):
- Bonus depreciation year 1: $10,000 (20% × $50,000)
- Regular depreciation on remaining $40,000 over 5 years

Without bonus: regular depreciation on full $50,000 over 5 years, much slower.

## Cost segregation — the real game-changer

This is where STRs get interesting.

**Cost segregation** is the analysis that separates a property's total cost basis into its component parts — building, 5-year property, 7-year property, 15-year property — for depreciation purposes. Most CPAs don't do this by default unless you ask.

When you buy an STR, you're not just buying a building. You're buying:
- The structure ($X at 27.5 years)
- All the landscaping, fencing, driveway ($Y at 15 years)
- Any furniture, appliances, and fixtures that came with the property ($Z at 5 years)

If your purchase agreement bundled it all under one price, a default tax return depreciates it all at 27.5 years. A cost segregation study breaks it out and applies the shorter lives to each component.

### Real example

Amanda bought a Smokies cabin for $480,000. Her CPA depreciated the full $480,000 minus land value ($80,000, which never depreciates) over 27.5 years = **$14,545/year** for 27.5 years.

After Amanda switched CPAs, they ran a cost segregation analysis on the cabin:
- Building structure: $280,000 at 27.5 years = $10,182/yr
- 15-year land improvements: $35,000 at 15 years = $2,333/yr + eligible for 20% bonus = **$7,000 bonus + remaining over 15 years**
- 5-year property (furniture, appliances, existing fixtures): $85,000 at 5 years = $17,000/yr + eligible for 20% bonus = **$17,000 bonus + remaining over 5 years**
- Land: $80,000 — non-depreciable

Year 1 deduction:
- Building: $10,182
- 15-year bonus portion: $7,000
- 15-year regular: $1,867 (on the remaining $28K)
- 5-year bonus portion: $17,000
- 5-year regular: $13,600 (on the remaining $68K)
- **Total year 1 depreciation: $49,649**

Compare to the "default" approach at 27.5 years:
- $14,545 from just the building
- **Extra $35,104 in deductions** in year 1 from cost segregation

At a 24% tax bracket, that's **~$8,425 in Year 1 tax savings** from one cost segregation analysis. Recurring benefits in subsequent years from faster depreciation schedules.

### DIY vs engineer-led cost segregation

Cost segregation studies historically cost $3,000–$8,000 from engineering firms. For most STRs under $500K acquisition cost, an **engineer study isn't cost-justified** — the tax savings outweigh the cost, but barely.

**DIY cost segregation** (also called "self-segregated" or "rule of thumb") is acceptable to the IRS for smaller properties if done with reasonable methodology. You allocate costs based on:
- Purchase documents (itemized furniture inventory from seller)
- Appraisal reports (often break out improvements)
- Reasonable component estimates (benchmarks from published sources)

It takes a few hours and captures most of the benefit. For properties over ~$750K, the engineer-led study generally pays for itself.

**Amended returns:** you can file amended returns going back 3 years to apply cost segregation retroactively. This is called a "change in accounting method" via Form 3115. For hosts who've been filing "default" returns for 2–3 years, this is worth exploring with a CPA.

## Section 179 vs bonus depreciation

These are often confused. Both let you accelerate depreciation, but they work differently:

| | Section 179 | Bonus Depreciation |
|---|---|---|
| Limit (2026) | ~$1,160,000 (confirm current) | No dollar limit |
| Applies to | Qualifying 5-year + 7-year property | 5, 7, and 15-year property |
| Income limit | Yes — can't create a net loss | No — can create a tax loss |
| Phase-out | Above $2.9M total purchases | None (percentage decreasing) |
| Application | Election per asset | Automatic unless opted out |
| Year purchased used | 100% of eligible asset cost | Current-year bonus % |

**For most STR hosts:**
- Section 179 is best when you have profit to offset and your purchases are under the threshold
- Bonus depreciation is useful when you want to create losses (potentially offsetting W-2 income if you meet material participation tests per our [Schedule E guide](https://thestrledger.com/blog/airbnb-schedule-e))

Combining them strategically:
1. Use Section 179 on the most valuable 5-year items until you hit the income limit
2. Apply bonus depreciation to everything else 5/7/15-year
3. Regular MACRS schedules on whatever's left

## How to report depreciation on your tax return

Depreciation flows from **Form 4562** → Schedule E Line 18.

### Form 4562 structure

- **Part I:** Section 179 expense election (dollar amount, asset details)
- **Part II:** Bonus depreciation (MACRS-eligible property placed in service this year)
- **Part III:** MACRS depreciation for property placed in service this year (asset-by-asset or in groups)
- **Part IV:** Summary totaling the three above
- **Part V:** Listed property (vehicles, computers used <100% for business) — typically skip for STRs
- **Part VI:** Amortization (startup costs, loan fees) — applies Year 1 after purchase

You need to track, per asset:
- Date placed in service
- Cost basis
- Recovery period (5, 7, 15, or 27.5 years)
- Method (MACRS typically)
- Convention (half-year, mid-quarter, or mid-month)
- Bonus % claimed

**Mid-month convention** applies to 27.5-year residential property placed in service — you get a half-month of depreciation in the month placed in service, full months thereafter.

**Half-year convention** applies to 5, 7, and 15-year property by default.

## The 7 most common depreciation mistakes

1. **Depreciating the whole property at 27.5 years.** Covered above — this is the big one.
2. **Forgetting to separate land value from building.** Land is never depreciable. Typical split: 80% building, 20% land (check your county assessor's ratio for accuracy).
3. **Missing bonus depreciation on purchase-year improvements.** Renovations after purchase are bonus-eligible if they qualify.
4. **Claiming Section 179 on property the rule doesn't cover.** 27.5-year property is NOT Section 179-eligible.
5. **Re-claiming assets you've already fully depreciated.** 5-year furniture bought in Year 1 is fully depreciated by Year 5. Re-entering it in Year 6 = audit flag.
6. **Inconsistent placed-in-service dates.** Use the actual date the asset was ready for use (not purchase date if different).
7. **Skipping the mid-month convention for 27.5-year property.** A property placed in service on March 15 gets March (mid-month, ½ month) + April–Dec = 9.5/12 of a full year's depreciation in Year 1, not 10/12.

## Amended returns — reclaiming lost depreciation

Filed returns without cost segregation for the past 2–3 years? You can file an amended return or a **Form 3115 change in accounting method** to apply retroactively.

- **Simple amended return (Form 1040X):** 3-year statute of limitations
- **Form 3115 (Change in Accounting Method):** allows you to catch up **ALL missed depreciation** in the current year via a "481(a) adjustment" — no time limit

For a host 5 years into ownership who's been depreciating at 27.5-year straight-line and wants to switch to cost segregation, Form 3115 is the right tool. It's CPA-complex but can result in a single-year deduction of $50K+ of "catch-up" depreciation.

This is a legitimate, IRS-blessed procedure — it requires proper forms and documentation but doesn't trigger special audit scrutiny if done correctly.

## Final action items

1. **Confirm your property's current depreciation approach.** Pull last year's Form 4562 — what's there?
2. **Decide if cost segregation fits your property.** For most STRs under $750K: yes, at DIY level.
3. **Track depreciable assets as you acquire them.** The first week after buying a property is the highest-value window — inventory everything before anyone touches it.
4. **Build a real depreciation schedule.** Whether DIY spreadsheet or CPA-maintained, the schedule is what matters for decades of future returns.
5. **Consider Form 3115 if you've been under-depreciating.** Don't leave years of missed deductions on the table.
