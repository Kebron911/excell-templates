# Chapter 1 — Schedule E or Schedule C

*The choice that changes everything.*

---

Megan ran four cabins in the Colorado high country and filed Schedule C on her returns for three years before her new CPA stopped her in the middle of a sentence.

"Why are you filing this on Schedule C?"

"That's what my old CPA told me to do. Vacation rentals are a business."

"Some of them are. Yours isn't."

The cabins averaged four-day stays. Megan didn't provide meals, didn't run a concierge, didn't change linens during anybody's stay. What she ran was a furnished short-term rental portfolio — the kind of business that under the Treasury Regulations is *not* a Schedule C trade-or-business at all.

For each of the three years she had been filing Schedule C, Megan had paid self-employment tax — 15.3% on the first portion of net earnings up to the Social Security wage base, then 2.9% Medicare-only on earnings above it — on roughly $120,000 of annual net rental income. The total she had paid in SE tax across three years was approximately **$50,000.** None of it had been owed.

She filed three Form 1040-X amended returns the following month and recovered most of it.

This chapter is about the rule Megan's old CPA didn't know — and about why filing on the right schedule is worth more, in many cases, than every line-item deduction in the rest of this book combined.

---

## What the schedules actually are

Schedule E and Schedule C are both attachments to Form 1040. They report business and rental income to the IRS. They look similar from a distance and they produce some of the same line items. The differences that matter are at the edges.

**Schedule E** (Supplemental Income and Loss) is the IRS's form for reporting rental real estate, royalties, partnerships, S corporations, estates, trusts, and a handful of other passive-investment-style activities. Income reported on Schedule E is **not subject to self-employment tax**. Losses are subject to the passive activity rules of §469 unless an exception applies.

**Schedule C** (Profit or Loss from Business) is the IRS's form for reporting income from a sole-proprietor trade or business in which the taxpayer materially participates. Income reported on Schedule C **is subject to self-employment tax** under §1402 — 15.3% on net earnings up to the Social Security wage base, 2.9% above it, plus an additional 0.9% Medicare surtax on high earners. It qualifies for the §199A qualified business income (QBI) deduction. Schedule E rentals also qualify for §199A when the activity rises to a §162 trade or business — most non-passive STRs under the 7-day rule meet that bar, and Rev. Proc. 2019-38 provides a 250-hour rental real-estate safe harbor for hosts who want a bright-line. (OBBBA made §199A permanent in 2025; it had been scheduled to sunset at the end of that year.)

Most STR hosts read those two paragraphs and conclude that Schedule E is "better" because it skips the 15.3% SE tax. That's the right intuition. But the choice between the two is not a preference — it's a determination the IRS makes based on three rules. Filing on the wrong schedule does not save you anything. It exposes you. The IRS gets to recharacterize your filing on audit, and the version they pick is the one that costs you more.

The three rules that decide which schedule applies are:

1. **Average rental period** — how long does the average guest stay?
2. **Substantial services** — do you provide hotel-like services beyond what a long-term landlord would?
3. **Material participation** — do you put real hours into the activity, and how do those hours compare to anyone else's?

Each rule has a specific Treasury Regulation behind it. None of them are matters of opinion.

---

## Rule one — the average rental period (the 7-day rule)

Under Treasury Regulation **§1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A)**, an activity is treated as **not a rental activity** for purposes of §469 if the average period of customer use is seven days or less. The rule is mechanical. You compute it by dividing total days rented by the number of separate rentals in the year. If the result is 7.00 or less, the activity is — for §469 purposes — treated like a service business rather than a passive rental.

There is also a 30-day cousin to this rule: an activity with an average period of customer use of 30 days or less is also treated as non-rental if the owner provides "significant personal services."

This is the regulation that produces the so-called "STR loophole." It is not a loophole in any meaningful sense — it is the IRS deliberately treating short-stay rentals differently from long-term landlording. The reason is straightforward: the economic substance of a one-week cabin rental is closer to a hotel than to an apartment lease. The Treasury wrote the regulation to reflect that.

For most STR portfolios, the average stay calculation comes out comfortably below 7 days. Three- and four-night stays dominate. Weekly stays push the average up. Monthly stays — common in some markets — push it past 7 and into the standard rental category.

**Computing your average:** sum the nights actually rented across all reservations in the tax year. Divide by the count of reservations. Cleaning gaps and unrented nights are excluded. The figure goes in your tax file alongside your closing statements; the workbook in Chapter 9 captures it automatically.

This is the simple-average proxy. The regulation at §1.469-1T(e)(3)(iii) actually contemplates a **weighted-average** computation using rental periods, which matters in portfolios mixing one-night and 14-night stays. For typical three-to-four-night STR portfolios, the simple and weighted averages produce equivalent answers; if your stay distribution is bimodal or you're sitting near the 7-day line, run the weighted version too.

The 7-day rule does not, by itself, push you into Schedule C. It pushes you out of the default §469 passive-rental treatment. Where you land next depends on the second and third rules.

## Rule two — substantial services

If your average period of customer use is short enough to escape the default rental category, the next question is whether you provide **substantial services** to your guests. The technical phrase from §1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A) is services "primarily for the convenience of the customer" of a type that "would not be expected to be provided in connection with the rental of real property for occupancy as a residence."

In plain English: would a hotel concierge do this, or would a long-term landlord?

Services that count as substantial — the ones that push you toward Schedule C — include:

- Daily housekeeping during the guest's stay
- Meal service, breakfast provision, or stocked food
- Concierge, sightseeing, or transportation arrangements
- Linen changes during a guest's stay
- Personal greetings, property tours, or staffed check-ins
- Tour-guide or chauffeur services
- Equipment rental that is staffed during the stay

Services that **do not count** as substantial — the ones that keep you in Schedule E — include:

- Cleaning between guest stays
- A pre-stocked welcome basket
- Local recommendations in a printed welcome book
- Linens provided at check-in
- Self check-in via a smart lock
- Bicycles, kayaks, or grills left unattended for guest use
- Standard utilities (Wi-Fi, electricity, water, basic cable)

The line is the question of whether the service is *during the stay* and *of a type a hotel provides*. Cleaning the cabin between Alice and Bob is not substantial. Cleaning the cabin while Alice is sleeping in it is.

Most STR hosts running self-managed portfolios provide no substantial services. Most boutique cabin operators who advertise "concierge" packages do. Bed-and-breakfast operators almost always do.

**If you provide substantial services, you file Schedule C.** Average stay length doesn't save you — substantial services override. The activity is a hotel-like trade or business, and the IRS treats it as such regardless of how short or long your stays are.

If you do not provide substantial services, the next question is whether you materially participate.

---

## Rule three — material participation

Material participation is a §469 concept that determines whether a non-rental activity is **passive** or **non-passive**. The distinction matters because passive losses can only offset passive income — they can't shelter your W-2 paycheck or your spouse's bonus. Non-passive losses can.

For an STR with average stay ≤ 7 days and no substantial services, material participation determines whether the activity is reported on Schedule E as **passive** (limited loss treatment) or as **non-passive** (full loss deductibility against ordinary income). The form is the same. The character of the activity changes.

You materially participate if you meet **any one** of the seven tests in Treasury Regulation §1.469-5T(a):

1. **The 500-hour test.** You worked **more than 500 hours** on the activity during the year. (501 hours qualifies; 500 flat does not.)
2. **The substantially-all test.** Your participation was substantially all of the participation by anyone — owners, employees, contractors, anyone — in the activity.
3. **The 100-hour, no-less-than-anyone test.** You worked **more than 100 hours** AND **not less than** any other individual. (Equal counts qualifies — if you logged 120 hours and your cleaner logged 120, you still pass.)
4. **The significant participation test.** The activity was a "significant participation activity" (more than 100 hours) and your aggregate hours across all such activities exceeded 500.
5. **The 5-of-10 test.** You materially participated in any 5 of the prior 10 tax years.
6. **The personal service test.** The activity is a personal service activity in which you participated for any 3 prior years. (Rare for STR.)
7. **The facts-and-circumstances test.** Your participation was regular, continuous, and substantial — **more than 100 hours** — based on all facts. This is the hardest to defend; do not lean on it.

For most owner-operators, **test 3 is the most accessible.** A self-managed STR portfolio at four properties with three- to four-day average stays generates somewhere between 200 and 400 hours of work per year for the owner: messaging guests, coordinating cleaners, restocking, handling maintenance issues, reviewing pricing, handling damage, processing refunds, fielding the 11 PM "the heater won't turn on" call. If you are the only person making those decisions and your cleaners are contractors who don't otherwise participate in the rental decision-making, you almost certainly meet test 3.

But meeting it isn't the same as proving you met it. The IRS has the right to ask for substantiation. The substantiation is an hour log: a contemporaneous record of hours worked, with brief notes about the activity. "March 14 — 1.5 hours — guest messaging, cleaner coordination, restock supply order." It does not need to be elegant. It needs to exist.

The material participation hour log isn't a deduction itself — it's the **enabling discipline** that protects deduction #1 and every line that flows from non-passive treatment. Without the log, you cannot defend the non-passive Schedule E filing. Logs are cheap. Reclassifications on audit are not.

## The three outcomes

Putting the three rules together produces three possible outcomes for a short-term rental host. Each outcome is a different filing.

**Outcome A — Schedule E (passive activity).**
Average stay > 7 days *or* (8–30 days without substantial services) *or* 30+ days. Default treatment. No SE tax. Losses limited under §469 unless you qualify as a real estate professional under §469(c)(7). Most long-term and longer-stay rentals land here. (The 7-day-rule path to non-passive treatment in Outcome B does not apply once average stay exceeds 7 days — at that point §469(c)(7) REPS is the only escape from §469.)

**Outcome B — Schedule E (non-passive).**
Average stay ≤ 7 days, no substantial services, AND material participation under §1.469-5T(a). The "STR loophole" outcome. No SE tax. Losses are fully deductible against W-2 income, business income, and other non-passive sources. This is where most actively-managed STR portfolios should be — and where many are not, because the tax preparer never asked the right questions.

**Outcome C — Schedule C (active trade or business).**
Substantial services provided, regardless of average stay length. Subject to SE tax under §1402. Often qualifies for the §199A QBI deduction. Common for bed-and-breakfast operators, full-service vacation rentals with concierge, and any STR that crosses into hotel-like service.

The numbers behind these outcomes vary by host, but the rough orders of magnitude on a $120,000 net-income portfolio are:

| Outcome | Federal income tax | SE tax | Net to host |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A — Sch E (passive)** | At marginal bracket | $0 | Best on income; loss treatment limited |
| **B — Sch E (non-passive)** | At marginal bracket | $0 | Best overall — losses sheltered, no SE tax |
| **C — Sch C** | At marginal bracket, possibly with §199A | ~$16,000–$17,000 | Worst — SE tax adds 14.1% effective burden |

The difference between Outcome B and Outcome C, on $120K of net income, is about $16,000 a year — *every year you file the wrong schedule*. That is what Megan paid. That is what most hosts who filed Schedule C without the substantial-services trigger pay.

## Filing the wrong schedule, in both directions

There are two ways to get this wrong, and both cost real money.

**Filing Schedule C when you belong on Schedule E.** This is Megan's mistake. She paid SE tax she didn't owe. Three years × $16,000 ≈ $48,000–$55,000, depending on her exact income and the wage base in those years. The path back is Form 1040-X amended returns, filed within the three-year statute of limitations from the original due date. Hosts in this situation should pay a CPA $400–$800 per year to file the amendments — the recovery typically dwarfs the cost ten-fold.

**Filing Schedule E when you belong on Schedule C.** This is the rarer mistake but also the more dangerous one. A bed-and-breakfast operator filing Schedule E to dodge SE tax is on the wrong side of §1402, and on audit the IRS will recharacterize the filing, assess the back SE tax, and add interest and penalties. The §199A QBI deduction available to Schedule C filers may also have been lost in the meantime. If you provide substantial services, you file Schedule C. There is no creative path around it.

**Filing Schedule E (passive) when you qualify for Schedule E (non-passive).** Common, costly, and quiet. The form looks identical on paper — the difference is whether your losses are sitting trapped on Form 8582 (passive activity losses, carried forward) or flowing onto Schedule 1 of Form 1040 to offset other income. A host whose property is generating $30,000 of paper losses through depreciation each year is leaving $30,000 of W-2-offsetting deduction on the table for as long as they file as passive. With the cost-segregation move from Chapter 7 layered on top, that figure can be much larger.

This is **deduction #1 of the 47**: filing on the schedule the regulations actually assign to your activity, with the participation status the regulations actually permit. It is not a line item. It is the framing decision that determines whether every other line item in this book lands at full strength or at half.

## What this chapter unlocks

Before you file your next return — or amend a prior one — work through these three checks in order.

1. **Compute your average period of customer use.** Total nights rented divided by number of separate rentals. If it's ≤ 7, the §1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A) rule applies and you are out of the default rental category.
2. **Confirm you provide no substantial services.** Run through the list earlier in this chapter. If anything you do during a guest's stay is hotel-shaped, you may be on Schedule C. Be honest with yourself.
3. **Verify your material participation.** Pick the test you most clearly meet — for most owner-operators, test 3 (100 hours and more than anyone else). Then start logging hours contemporaneously, today, if you haven't been.

If you completed those three checks and the answer surprises you, talk to a CPA before filing this year and consider whether prior-year returns are amendable. You have three years from the original filing deadline.
