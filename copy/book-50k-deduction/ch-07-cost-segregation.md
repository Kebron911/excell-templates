# Chapter 7 — Cost Segregation for the Rest of Us

*The Amanda chapter.*

---

Amanda owned three cabins in the Smokies for four years before her new CPA flagged it.

She had everything else right. Schedule E filed clean, the buildings depreciated on the standard 27.5-year line, the cleaning fees and platform fees in the right boxes, even the start-up costs taken in year one. Her old CPA was not negligent. He was just doing the work the way most CPAs do it for most clients: a single line for "27.5-year residential rental," then a calm move to the next page.

Her new CPA — a woman who had spent two years inside a real-estate accounting firm before going independent — opened the engagement with a question.

> "Did you run a cost seg on these?"

Amanda had not. She had not because nobody had told her she was supposed to. She had not because the term *cost segregation* sounded, to her, like the kind of thing a hedge fund would do — engineers walking through a Class-A office tower with clipboards, billing six figures for the privilege.

By the end of that meeting Amanda was filing a Form 3115 for one of her cabins, claiming a §481(a) adjustment that put roughly **$53,000** of previously-undepreciated value on her current-year return — which at her marginal federal rate cut her current-year tax by about **$12,700**. No amended returns. No engineering firm. A Saturday afternoon and a workbook.

This chapter is about how she did it. More importantly, it is about why most short-term rental hosts can do the same thing — and why the rule of thumb that cost segregation is "for big real-estate operators" is one of the most expensive misunderstandings in this industry.

---

## What cost segregation actually is

In plain English: the IRS lets you depreciate a residential rental property over 27.5 years. That number — 27.5 — is a compromise. It is the average useful life of the *building*, treated as a single asset.

But the building is not a single asset. The building is a building, plus a roof, plus an HVAC system, plus appliances, plus furniture, plus carpets, plus a driveway, plus landscaping, plus a deck, plus a hot tub, plus the strands of bistro lights you ran across the back yard for the listing photos. Some of these things wear out in five years. Some in fifteen. Treating all of them as if they wear out in twenty-seven and a half years is bookkeeping fiction.

Cost segregation is the practice of separating — *segregating* — these components from the building they sit inside, so each one can be depreciated on the schedule the tax code actually assigns it. It is not aggressive. It is not creative. It is, in fact, the **default treatment** the IRS has assigned to these asset classes since *Hospital Corporation of America v. Commissioner*, 109 T.C. 21 (1997). Cost segregation is one of the few areas of the tax code where doing it right makes you *more* compliant, not less.

The myth — and it is a myth — is that you need an engineer-led study to do it. You don't. You need an engineer-led study to do it on a $25 million Class-A office tower. For a $300,000 cabin in the Smokies, what you need is a workbook, a contractor's invoice, and a Saturday afternoon.

---

## Why short-term rental hosts qualify uniquely

There are two reasons cost segregation is unusually generous to STR operators specifically.

The first is **asset density**. A long-term rental is mostly building. The tenant brings their own couch, their own bed, their own toaster. A short-term rental is the opposite — the host brings everything. Every furnished STR ships with somewhere between $20,000 and $80,000 of personal property baked into the acquisition or commissioning cost: furniture, mattresses, linens, dishware, smart locks, televisions, light fixtures, appliances, outdoor furniture, hot tubs, fire pits, decorative everything. All of this is **5-year property** under MACRS. Almost none of it is supposed to depreciate over 27.5 years. But on the standard Schedule E worksheet — the one most CPAs run by default — that's exactly what happens to it.

The second is **§168(k) bonus depreciation**. When property is reclassified into a recovery period of twenty years or less — which captures all of the personal-property and land-improvement reclassifications cost seg generates — it becomes eligible for bonus depreciation in the year the property is placed in service. Bonus depreciation lets you take a percentage of the full asset value as a deduction in year one, instead of spreading it across the recovery period. The rate has moved over time and you must use the rate in effect for the year the property was placed in service. For property Amanda placed in service in 2022, that rate was 100%. **Under OBBBA §70301, 100% bonus depreciation was restored permanently for property acquired after January 19, 2025 and placed in service from that point forward** — so for hosts placing property in service in 2026 the rate is 100% by default. For property placed in service between the post-9/27/2017 ramp-down and the OBBBA restoration, look up the current rate in the IRS instructions for Form 4562 for the specific tax year. The point is not the percentage. The point is that the entire reclassified amount becomes deductible in year one, instead of trickling out at $1/27.5 per year.

When you stack those two — high asset density and bonus depreciation — the year-one deduction on a typical STR acquisition can be three to ten times what a default Schedule E filing produces. That is the deduction Amanda's old CPA wasn't taking.

## The four buckets

Every dollar spent acquiring or commissioning an STR property falls into one of five recovery periods. The first one is the only one your CPA used by default; the other four are where the deduction lives.

| Bucket | Recovery period | What lives here | STR examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Land** | Non-depreciable | The land itself — never deductible | Lot value at acquisition, separated from improvements |
| **5-year property** | 5 years (MACRS, 200% DB) | Tangible personal property | Furniture, appliances, decorative lighting, carpeting, window treatments, hot tubs, removable cabinetry, smart-home electronics, outdoor furniture, BBQ grills |
| **7-year property** | 7 years | Office furniture & equipment | Owner's office desk, files, computers used for the portfolio (rare for pure STR; applicable for hosts who run from home) |
| **15-year property** | 15 years (MACRS, 150% DB) | Land improvements | Driveways, parking pads, fences, exterior lighting, planted landscaping, retaining walls, sidewalks, decks attached to grade |
| **27.5-year property** | 27.5 years (straight-line) | The building's shell | Foundation, framing, roof, exterior walls, plumbing system (not fixtures), electrical system (not fixtures), HVAC system, windows, doors |

Two boundary calls hosts get wrong:

A **deck** built on grade with concrete footings is 15-year land improvement. A **balcony** structurally tied to the building is 27.5-year. The footing detail decides it.

A **window treatment** — drapery, blinds, shades — is 5-year personal property. A **window itself** — glass and frame — is part of the 27.5-year building. Hosts frequently lump these together as "window costs" on a remodel invoice. Don't. The contractor's line items already separate them; honor that separation in your books.

## The DIY method — what the IRS allows

There is a useful distinction between an **engineering-based study** (the gold standard, $5,000–$15,000, defensible against any examination) and a **residential cost segregation done by the host with reasonable allocation** (the realistic standard for properties under roughly $500,000 in acquisition cost).

The IRS does not require the engineering version. What it requires is that your allocation be **reasonable, supported, and consistently applied**. For a small residential rental, that means:

1. **Start with the closing statement.** Separate land value from building value using the ratio your local property tax assessor uses. Your county assessor publishes this. It is the cleanest defensible split available without paid help.
2. **Pull the personal-property line items** from your contractor invoices, furniture-package receipts, and renovation records. Every dollar with a paper trail to a 5-year asset goes into the 5-year bucket.
3. **For unreceipted items** — the existing fixtures, fittings, and appliances at acquisition — use a reasonable percentage method. The practitioner-accepted range for a furnished short-term rental is between 15% and 30% of building value reclassified into 5-year and 15-year property combined. (This is a working rule of thumb from the engineering-study literature, not an IRS-published threshold.) Below 15%, you are likely under-claiming. Above 30%, you are inviting questions you don't want to answer.
4. **Document your method.** Write a one-page memo explaining how you arrived at each bucket. Save the contractor invoices, the assessor's land-value page, and the photos of the finished property. The memo is what survives an examination. It does not have to be elegant. It has to exist.

The further you push beyond 30%, the more you should consider paying for an engineering study. For a $300K cabin at 25% reclassification, the deduction recovery is large enough to justify the work and small enough to defend without a study. That is the shape of most STR acquisitions.

## A worked example — Amanda's smallest cabin

Amanda's smallest cabin was a **$305,000 acquisition** in 2022. The county assessor's land/improvement ratio split it 18% / 82% — so $54,900 to land, **$250,100 to depreciable building basis**.

Without cost segregation, her depreciation on this property looked like this:

```
Year 1:   $250,100 ÷ 27.5  ×  partial-year convention  ≈ $7,955
Year 2+:  $250,100 ÷ 27.5                              ≈ $9,094 each
... and so on for 27.5 years.
```

After cost segregation, the same $250,100 was reallocated:

| Bucket | % of building basis | Allocation | First-year deduction |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5-year personal property *(furniture, appliances, fixtures, treatments)* | 18% | $45,018 | Full Y1 (bonus depreciation eligible) |
| 15-year land improvements *(driveway, deck-on-grade, landscaping, exterior lighting)* | 7% | $17,507 | Full Y1 (bonus depreciation eligible) |
| 27.5-year building (shell) | 75% | $187,575 | Standard MACRS Y1 ≈ $5,966 |
| **Year-one deduction (with cost seg)** |  |  | **~$68,491** |

The same property. The same closing statement. The same contractor invoices. A different worksheet.

The difference in year-one deduction is approximately **$60,536** — roughly $68,491 with cost seg versus $7,955 without. At Amanda's 24% marginal federal rate, that's about $14,529 of additional federal tax savings in year one alone, before state tax and before the QBI deduction interaction.

But Amanda was not in year one. She was in year five, four years past the placed-in-service date, with the building sitting on the standard 27.5-year line in her old depreciation schedule. To capture the deduction now, she needed the look-back trick.

## The look-back trick — Form 3115 instead of amended returns

Most hosts believe — and most general-practice CPAs imply — that recovering missed depreciation requires amending prior years' returns. It does not. It is, in fact, the *worse* way to do it.

The right way is **Form 3115 — Application for Change in Accounting Method**. The IRS treats a missed cost segregation as a "change in method of accounting," which means you take a single §481(a) adjustment on your **current** return. The adjustment captures all of the previously-missed depreciation from the placed-in-service date forward, deductible in the current year as a cumulative catch-up.

Why this matters:

- **No amended returns.** You don't reopen prior years. No statute-of-limitations risk. No prior-year audit exposure.
- **All of the missed deduction lands in one year.** This concentrates the deduction at your highest marginal rate (assuming the current year is not abnormally low income).
- **Automatic consent.** For most cost-seg method changes, Form 3115 is filed under "automatic consent" — meaning the IRS approves it by default, without a discretionary review, as long as you follow the procedural requirements in the current Rev. Proc.

The trade-off: Form 3115 is a procedurally fussy form. The §481(a) adjustment calculation is exacting. Most hosts are well-served paying a CPA $400–$800 to file this *one form* in the year they implement cost seg, even if they did the underlying allocation themselves. The form is a specialist task; the allocation isn't.

Amanda's catch-up adjustment for her smallest cabin — four years of missed accelerated depreciation — totaled roughly **$53,000** of additional depreciation on her current-year return, worth approximately **$12,700** of federal tax savings at her marginal rate. She filed the same form for the other two cabins on her own timeline; the cumulative recovery was larger, but she kept that number to herself. The point is not the aggregate. The point is the method.

## Mistakes hosts make even after they know better

Five things go wrong even among hosts who know cost segregation exists.

**1. They under-allocate to personal property.** A furnished short-term rental almost never has less than 15% of building basis in 5-year personal property. Hosts new to the technique routinely allocate 8–10% because they're nervous. This is leaving money in the wrong bucket. The defensible floor is your contractor invoices plus a furnished-rental factor — and a furnished STR's factor is materially higher than a long-term rental's.

**2. They forget land improvements.** The driveway. The fire-pit pad. The strands of bistro lights. The fence. The retaining wall. The landscaping that was put in at commissioning. All 15-year. All bonus-eligible. Every host we have ever talked to forgets at least one of these on their first allocation.

**3. They ignore the Tangible Property Regulations on remodels.** Under §1.263(a)-3 (routine maintenance and small-taxpayer safe harbors) and §1.263(a)-1(f) (de minimis safe harbor), certain "improvements" can be expensed rather than capitalized. Hosts who do annual freshening — a new vanity, a paint job, a re-flooring of one room — frequently capitalize when they could expense. If your average freshen-up bill per item is under the de minimis safe harbor (currently $2,500 per item or per invoice for taxpayers without an applicable financial statement, under §1.263(a)-1(f)), it may belong on Schedule E line 14 (Repairs) rather than on the depreciation schedule. This is a separate technique from cost seg, but it lives next door.

**4. They cost-seg a property they're going to sell within a year.** If you sell soon after cost segregation, you trigger **depreciation recapture** — and the rate depends on which bucket: §1245 personal property (the 5-year bucket) recaptures at full **ordinary income rates**, while 15-year land improvements are §1250 property and individual taxpayers' §1250 recapture is **"unrecaptured §1250 gain" capped at 25%**. The mix matters: a cabin with most of its reclassified value in 5-year furniture has a higher effective recapture rate than one weighted toward 15-year land improvements. Cost seg is still a hold-period play, but don't abandon a defensible study on the basis of a recapture estimate that assumes everything recaptures at ordinary rates. Run the actual numbers.

**5. They never document the method.** They run the workbook, take the deduction, and don't write the one-page memo. Then, three years later, the IRS sends a request for substantiation, and the host is rebuilding the allocation from memory. Write the memo. Save the contractor invoices. Photograph the finished property at acquisition. The work to defend the deduction takes ten minutes if you do it as you go and three weeks if you do it later.

---

## The boundary you do not want to cross

Cost segregation is aggressive without being abusive. There is a line beyond which it becomes abusive. Two markers:

- **No reclassification of the building shell.** The framing, foundation, exterior walls, roof system, primary electrical, and primary plumbing systems remain 27.5-year. A host who reclassifies "30% of the structural shell" into 5-year property is inviting an examination they will not enjoy.
- **Reasonable consistency with industry data.** The IRS has access to engineering-study data across thousands of similar properties. If your allocation is wildly outside the norm for a furnished short-term rental of comparable size and price point, you should expect to defend the difference. The norm for an STR is in the 20–30% reclassification range; the norm for a long-term rental is in the 8–12% range. Use the right reference.

When in doubt, document, allocate conservatively, and pay a CPA to file the Form 3115 the first time. The ceiling is high. There's no need to walk to the edge of it.
