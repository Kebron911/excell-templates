# Chapter 9 — Mileage, Per Diem, and Lodging on Property Visits

*The §274(d) chapter. Strict substantiation, no exceptions.*

---

Jeremy ran two cabins in Sedona and lived in Tucson, four hours each way. He drove up about three times a month for cleaner check-ins, supply runs, and the occasional repair he could handle without a contractor. He logged every trip the same week he made it: date, purpose, start and end odometer, miles. The log lived in a workbook tab he opened every Sunday evening.

In April of his fourth year, the IRS opened an examination of his prior-year return. The first item on the auditor's list was vehicle expense. Jeremy had claimed roughly **$8,400** of mileage on that year — about 11,000 business miles at the prevailing IRS standard rate.

The auditor asked for the log. Jeremy emailed the file. The auditor opened it, scanned six pages, and closed the line.

> "This is fine. Moving on."

The mileage portion of the audit closed in **twelve minutes.** The rest of the audit closed in another two days, with no adjustments.

This chapter is about the deductions Jeremy claimed and the log that defended them. It is also about the four other deductions in this category — per-diem meals, actual vehicle expense, lodging on overnight trips, and the once-per-vehicle method election that locks you in for the life of the car. All five live under **§274(d)**, the strict-substantiation regulation introduced in Chapter 3. None of them survive without the log.

These are **deductions #26 through #30** of the 47.

---

## The §274(d) standard, restated

For any deduction in this chapter, the substantiation requires:

- **Amount** of the expense
- **Time and date** of the activity
- **Place** (origin and destination for travel; address for lodging; restaurant or location for meals)
- **Business purpose**
- **Business relationship** of any persons involved (for meals shared with others)

The Cohan rule does not apply. No estimate, however reasonable, will rescue a missing entry. The deduction is disallowed.

The cost of compliance is small — a log entry per trip, contemporaneous capture of receipts in §274(d) categories, and the discipline to do it weekly. The cost of non-compliance is the disallowance of the entire category.

## Deduction #26 — Standard mileage rate

The simplest method, and the one Jeremy used. Multiply business miles by the IRS standard mileage rate for the year. The rate is announced annually by Rev. Proc. (e.g., Rev. Proc. 2024-08 set the 2024 rate, with successor procedures for each subsequent year). For tax year 2024 the rate was 67¢ per mile, for 2025 it was 70¢, and for 2026 it is **72.5¢** (verify against the IRS announcement for the current year before filing). You must use the rate in effect for the year of the trip.

The standard rate is a *blended* rate — it includes deemed depreciation, gas, oil, repairs, insurance, and registration. Once you elect the standard mileage method for a vehicle in its first year of business use, you can switch to actual expense in later years (with depreciation method consequences), but you cannot switch back. Choose carefully.

What counts as a business mile for an STR host:

- Trips to and from the property for cleaning oversight, repairs, supply restocks, contractor meetings
- Trips to home-improvement stores, supply stores, or specialty stores for the property
- Trips to the bank for rental-account business
- Trips to your CPA for the rental return
- Trips to a closing or property inspection during acquisition

What does **not** count:

- **Commuting** between your home and your "principal place of business," if the property is your principal place of business (rare for STR — the property usually isn't where the host conducts the activity)
- Personal trips that incidentally pass the property
- Trips made entirely for non-rental purposes that happen to be in the property's region

Jeremy's 11,000 miles were each documented with origin (Tucson, his home) and destination (Sedona, the property), purpose (cleaner check-in / supply run / repair), and date. Every entry passed §274(d) on its own.

## Deduction #27 — Actual vehicle expense method

The alternative to standard mileage. Track every dollar spent on the vehicle during the year:

- Gas
- Oil and routine maintenance
- Repairs
- Insurance
- Registration and licensing
- Lease payments (if leased)
- Depreciation (if owned) — MACRS 5-year on the business-use portion of basis

Multiply the total by the **business-use percentage** of the vehicle for the year (computed as business miles ÷ total miles).

The actual method generally produces a larger deduction for:

- Newer vehicles with high depreciation
- Vehicles with high maintenance costs in a given year
- Vehicles used >50% for business

The standard method generally produces a larger deduction for:

- Older fully-depreciated vehicles
- Vehicles used <50% for business
- Hosts who don't want to track every gas receipt

The **first-year election** matters. If you start with actual expense for a vehicle, you cannot switch to standard mileage in later years for that vehicle. If you start with standard mileage, you can switch to actual in later years (but the depreciation rules get fussy). For most STR hosts with a personal vehicle used part-time for the rental, **starting with standard mileage is the safer first-year election** — it preserves flexibility and reduces tracking overhead.

## Deduction #28 — Per-diem meals

Meals on overnight property trips are deductible at **50% of the amount** under §274(n). Daytime meals on a same-day trip are not deductible — the §162 trade-or-business test fails because there's no overnight stay creating a "travel away from tax home" status.

Two methods:

**Actual receipts method.** Keep receipts for every meal during overnight travel. Deduct 50% of the actual cost. §274(d) substantiation applies — date, place, amount, business purpose, business relationship if dining with others.

**Per-diem method.** Use the IRS standard per-diem rate for the destination city, published annually in IRS Notice 2024-68 (and successors). The high-low method allows a simplified two-tier rate (high-cost localities vs. all others). Per Rev. Proc. 2019-48, self-employed taxpayers can use per-diem for *meals* but not for *lodging* (lodging requires actual receipts).

The per-diem method is simpler — no receipts to track per meal — and is often higher than actual cost in modest-meal scenarios. For a host who genuinely eats at Waffle House on the late drive home, actual receipts may be lower than per diem; the per-diem election then pays a kind of administrative dividend.

What does **not** qualify for the meal deduction:

- Day-trip meals (no overnight)
- Meals taken at the property itself (not "travel away from tax home")
- Lavish or extravagant meals under §274(k)
- Personal meals that happen to occur during business travel

## Deduction #29 — Lodging on overnight trips

Fully deductible — 100%, not 50% — if the trip qualifies as overnight business travel under §162. §274(d) substantiation applies.

Lodging deduction examples for STR hosts:

- A motel room near the property when the host is doing a multi-day repair or commissioning push and the property is occupied or unavailable
- A hotel room during property closing (acquisition trip)
- A short-term rental booked elsewhere because the host's own property is occupied during a repair window

**Receipts only — no per diem for lodging.** Per Rev. Proc. 2019-48 §4.04, self-employed taxpayers cannot use a per-diem method for lodging. Actual receipts are required (the per-diem option in the meals section above does not extend here). This is the same point flagged in Deduction #28 — it bears repeating because hosts who use per-diem for meals frequently assume the lodging side works the same way. It does not.

Lodging is often missed because hosts assume "I have a property — why would I pay for a hotel?" The answer is exactly the situation above: a multi-day visit where the property itself is booked. The deduction is genuine and frequently not claimed.

What does **not** qualify:

- Lodging on personal trips
- Lodging that's "lavish or extravagant" under §274(a) — the IRS standard is generally lenient, but $800/night five-star hotels invite questions
- Lodging at the host's own property (the host doesn't pay themselves)

## Deduction #30 — The vehicle-method election

Not a dollar deduction in itself, but a substantiation deduction — the value created by *making the right method election in year one* and then *documenting the method consistently every year after.*

The election is binary: standard mileage or actual expense. Once chosen for a vehicle in its first year of business use:

- **Standard mileage chosen first** → can switch to actual in later years (with adjusted depreciation rules)
- **Actual expense chosen first** → locked in actual for the life of the vehicle

This election is what most hosts get wrong by accident, because they don't know it's an election. Their CPA picks one in year one, doesn't explain it, and the host doesn't realize they're locked in.

The defensive move: ask your CPA, in writing, which method was elected for each vehicle in its first business-use year. Save the answer. If the answer is "actual expense" and you don't recall consenting to it, it may be reversible — but the window is small and the procedure is fussy.

## What "tax home" means

Several deductions in this chapter — meals, lodging — depend on the host being "away from their tax home." The IRS definition matters:

**Tax home** is the host's regular or principal place of business, regardless of where their personal residence is. For most STR hosts, the principal place of business is *the home where management, bookkeeping, and decision-making occur* — typically the host's residence. The property is not the principal place of business unless the host runs a Schedule C operation from the property itself (rare).

Practical implication: trips from Jeremy's Tucson home to the Sedona properties are *travel away from tax home* once they involve an overnight stay. Day trips don't qualify for meal or lodging deductions. Overnight stays do.

A host whose principal place of business is unclear — for instance, someone who genuinely operates from the property — should establish tax home in writing in their books and consult a CPA. The wrong tax home determination can disallow an entire category of travel deduction.

## Common mistakes

**Reconstructing a mileage log in March.** This is the most expensive recordkeeping mistake in the entire book. A reconstructed log fails §274(d) on credibility grounds — file metadata, handwriting consistency, and odometer/calendar cross-checks all flag retrospective creation. The deduction is disallowed even when the underlying trips were genuine. Log contemporaneously or don't claim.

**Mixing personal and business mileage on the same trip.** A trip that's 60% rental supply run and 40% personal grocery shopping is not 100% business mileage. The defensible move is to deduct only the marginal business miles — the additional miles incurred *because* of the business stop — or to allocate by the trip's primary purpose if it tips clearly one way.

**Claiming day-trip meals.** No overnight stay means no meal deduction. The host who buys a $14 sandwich on the drive home from the property is not entitled to a $7 deduction. The §162 framework does not allow it.

**Mixing the standard mileage rate with separate gas deductions.** The standard mileage rate already includes gas. Hosts who claim 70¢/mile *and* itemize gas receipts double-deduct. The IRS catches this on audit immediately.

**Forgetting the lodging deduction.** Multi-day repair trips where the property is booked and the host stays elsewhere — fully deductible lodging, frequently missed. Re-read the lodging deduction examples and audit your prior years for un-claimed instances.

**Not collecting receipts for meals over $75.** §1.274-5(c)(2)(iii) requires receipts for travel and meal expenses of $75 or more, regardless of method. The standard mileage rate doesn't trigger this requirement (it covers vehicle expenses, not meals), but actual meal receipts during overnight travel do. Save the receipts.
