# Chapter 8 — §179 and §168(k) Bonus Depreciation

*When to take it all in year one.*

---

Devin closed on a four-bedroom mountain cabin in October. The next sixteen days were a controlled blur — furniture delivery, mattress install, smart-lock programming, hot-tub commissioning, professional photography, the listing live by Halloween. By December 31 the cabin had three bookings on the books and another eight on the calendar.

The commissioning invoice totaled **$52,000** in furniture, appliances, fixtures, decor, and outdoor equipment. Pure 5-year property — every dollar of it.

Devin's CPA, working from the standard playbook, set up a 5-year MACRS schedule. The year-1 deduction (mid-quarter convention, fourth-quarter placement) came out to roughly **$2,600**. The remaining $49,400 would deplete over five years.

Devin asked, almost in passing, "Couldn't I take more of this now?"

The CPA paused. Pulled up the prior year's IRS bonus-depreciation rate. Reran the math.

The year-1 deduction went from $2,600 to **$52,000.**

This chapter is about the two regulations that produce that swing: **§179** (the expensing election) and **§168(k)** (bonus depreciation). They both let you deduct in year one what would otherwise spread over multiple years. They have different rules, different limits, and different gates. Used together correctly, they front-load deductions on furniture, fixtures, and qualified improvements that would otherwise trickle out over half a decade or more.

---

## The two regulations at a glance

**§179 — Election to expense.**
Allows the taxpayer to elect to expense up to a dollar limit of qualifying property in the year of purchase. The election is annual. The dollar cap is statutory (it indexes for inflation). The deduction is limited to the taxpayer's taxable income from active trades or businesses — §179 cannot create or increase a loss. Available only for property used in the **active conduct of a trade or business** under §162.

**§168(k) — Bonus depreciation.**
Allows a percentage of the cost of qualifying property to be deducted in the year placed in service. Automatic — applies by default to all qualifying property unless the taxpayer elects out by class. The percentage has shifted over recent years; you must use the rate in effect for the year the property was placed in service. **No taxable-income limit** — bonus depreciation can create or increase a loss. **No dollar cap.** Available regardless of trade-or-business status.

The two regulations overlap. Most STR placements that qualify for one qualify for the other. The order of operations matters, and §179's narrower availability is the gate that catches more hosts than the dollar cap ever does.

These are **deductions #23, #24, and #25** of the 47.

---

## §179 — the trade-or-business gate

The most consequential limit on §179 for STR hosts is not the dollar cap. It is the *active conduct of a trade or business* requirement under §179(d)(1).

A passive Schedule E rental is not a §162 trade or business in most circuits. The host who files Schedule E (passive) under the framework in Chapter 1 is generally **not eligible for §179**, even on $400 of decorative pillows.

A non-passive Schedule E rental — the "STR loophole" outcome under Chapter 1, with average stay ≤ 7 days plus material participation — is generally treated as a trade or business and **does qualify for §179.** Worth a CPA confirmation: §179 availability assumes the rental rises to a §162 trade or business under the standard in Chapter 2, which is a facts-and-circumstances determination based on your hour log and operating profile. TCJA repealed the pre-2018 §179(d)(1)(B)(ii) "lodging" carve-out, so the structural answer for a non-passive furnished STR is yes — but the IRS examination position on §162 status for non-passive STRs is not always automatic.

A Schedule C rental — substantial-services operator under Chapter 1 — is by definition an active trade or business and qualifies.

**The §179 availability map for STR hosts:**

| Filing form (Ch 1 outcome) | §179 available? |
|---|---|
| Schedule E (passive) | No |
| Schedule E (non-passive, material participation) | Yes |
| Schedule C | Yes |

This is why Chapter 1 is the frame chapter for the entire book. The filing-form decision in Ch 1 determines whether §179 is on the table at all in Ch 8.

### §179 dollar limits

Within the gate, §179 is generous, and recent legislation has made it more so. The annual cap was $1,160,000 in 2023 and $1,220,000 in 2024. **Under OBBBA §70306, the §179 cap was raised to $2,500,000 (with phase-out beginning at $4,000,000 of qualifying property placed in service) for tax years beginning after December 31, 2024**, with inflation indexing thereafter — the 2026 figures track this base. For any STR host placing under several million dollars of qualifying property in one year, the dollar cap is not a meaningful constraint — and post-OBBBA it is even less so. The cap is well above any realistic single-year STR commissioning budget.

The constraint that matters is the **taxable-income limit**: §179 deductions cannot exceed the taxpayer's aggregate taxable income from all active trades or businesses for the year. Excess §179 carries forward indefinitely. This means §179 is most useful for hosts with material non-rental income (W-2, business income from other ventures); a host whose only income is the rental itself may find §179 produces little year-one benefit beyond what the rental's positive net income permits.

### What §179 covers for STR hosts

Most furniture, appliances, fixtures, decor, electronics, and equipment with recovery period of 20 years or less. Examples that qualify on a non-passive Schedule E or Schedule C STR:

- Furniture and bedding
- Appliances (refrigerators, washers, dryers, dishwashers, microwaves)
- Electronics (TVs, smart-home equipment, security cameras)
- Hot tubs, fire pits, BBQ grills
- Outdoor furniture
- Decor, lighting, art

What §179 does **not** cover for residential rental:

- The building itself (27.5-year property is excluded from §179)
- HVAC systems on residential rental property (§179 was opened to HVAC for *non-residential* property only)
- Roofs on residential rental (same)
- Land improvements (15-year, but not §179-eligible for residential rental)

For land improvements and building components reclassified through cost segregation (Chapter 7), §179 is generally unavailable on a residential STR — but §168(k) is. The two regulations work together because of the gap.

## §168(k) — bonus depreciation

§168(k) is the broader, more powerful, and more forgiving of the two. The gates are simpler:

- The property has a recovery period of 20 years or less, **and**
- The property was placed in service after September 27, 2017 (the post-TCJA window).

That's it. No active-trade-or-business requirement. No taxable-income limit. No dollar cap. Bonus depreciation can take a profitable property to a loss; the loss flows through subject to the §469 passive activity rules (Ch 1).

For STR hosts, §168(k) qualifying property includes:

- All 5-year property (furniture, appliances, fixtures, electronics, decor)
- All 7-year property (office furniture)
- All 15-year property (land improvements: driveways, decks-on-grade, landscaping, exterior lighting, fences)
- Any property reclassified into 5-year, 7-year, or 15-year classes through cost segregation (Chapter 7)

**§168(k) does not apply to** the 27.5-year building itself. The shell stays on the standard MACRS 27.5-year line.

### The rate has moved

The §168(k) bonus rate has shifted multiple times in the post-TCJA era. The full schedule:

| Placed in service | Rate |
|---|---|
| 2017 (post-9/27) – 2022 | 100% (TCJA original) |
| 2023 | 80% |
| 2024 | 60% |
| 2025 (acquired before 1/20/2025) | 40% |
| 2025 (acquired after 1/19/2025) – 2026+ | **100% (permanent under OBBBA §70301)** |

**OBBBA §70301 restored 100% bonus depreciation permanently** for property *acquired* after January 19, 2025 and placed in service from that point forward — so for hosts placing property in service in 2026, bonus is 100% by default. The acquisition-date test matters: property acquired before January 20, 2025 but placed in service in 2025 is still under the prior 40% phase-down rate.

**You must use the rate in effect for the year (and acquisition date) the property was placed in service.** The IRS publishes the applicable rate annually in the Form 4562 instructions. Do not assume the rate is the same as last year. Verify before filing.

The rule of thumb: for any STR commissioning over the past several years, look up the bonus rate for the placed-in-service year and apply it. For 2026-and-forward placements, 100% is the default — but the number you need is still one Google search away, and it is not a number you should carry in your head from a prior return.

### §168(k) is automatic — and you can elect out

Bonus depreciation applies *by default* to all qualifying property. If you do nothing, you take it. The election is to *opt out* — which a host might do to spread depreciation across years for taxable-income smoothing reasons.

The opt-out is by class, not by individual asset. If you elect out of bonus on 5-year property in 2024, all 5-year property placed in service in 2024 takes standard MACRS instead. The election is binding for the year and irrevocable.

Most STR hosts should not elect out. The opportunity cost of forgoing year-one expensing — and the time-value-of-money math behind it — almost always favors taking bonus.

## The interaction — §179 first, then §168(k)

When both regulations apply, the canonical order is:

1. **Apply §179 first**, up to the lesser of (a) the §179 dollar cap or (b) the host's taxable income limit.
2. **Apply §168(k) bonus** on whatever remains of qualifying property.
3. **Apply standard MACRS** on whatever still remains after both.

Why this order? §179 is more restrictive (taxable-income-limited, trade-or-business-gated). Using §179 first preserves §168(k) for the residual that §179 couldn't absorb. The reverse — bonus first, §179 on residual — wastes the §179 election on items §168(k) would have handled anyway.

For most STR hosts placing property in a year when the §168(k) rate is 100%, the order doesn't change the result — both regulations cover the same property at full year-one expense. When the §168(k) rate is below 100%, the order matters: §179 covers 100% and bonus covers a fraction, so §179 should pick up the high-priority items first.

## Devin's worked example

Devin placed $52,000 of 5-year property in service in October. He files Schedule E non-passive (material participation, average stay ≤ 7 days). The §168(k) bonus rate that year was 100%.

| Step | Calculation | Year-1 deduction |
|---|---|---|
| §179 election (assuming sufficient taxable income) | $52,000 expensed under §179 | $52,000 |
| §168(k) bonus | $0 remaining to apply against | — |
| Standard MACRS | $0 remaining | — |
| **Total year-1 deduction** | | **$52,000** |

Without the election, on a default 5-year MACRS schedule with mid-quarter convention (October placement = fourth quarter, mid-quarter required when more than 40% of the **aggregate basis** of all property placed in service during the year is placed in service in the last three months), the year-1 deduction would have been $52,000 × 5% = **$2,600**.

Same property. Same year. Two paragraphs of election language on the return. **Year-one deduction differential: $49,400.**

If Devin had filed Schedule E *passive* instead — same property, same year, but failed the material-participation test — §179 would have been unavailable. §168(k) at 100% would still have applied (no trade-or-business gate). The year-1 deduction would still have been $52,000, just routed through a different code section. The outcome holds either way when the bonus rate is 100%.

If the bonus rate were 60% instead, the math would diverge:

| Filing | §179 available? | Bonus available? | Year-1 deduction |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sch E non-passive | Yes | Yes | $52,000 (via §179) |
| Sch E passive | No | Yes (60%) | $31,200 (60% bonus) + $4,160 (5% MACRS on remainder) ≈ $35,360 |

This is why filing form (Chapter 1) and bonus rate (current year) are both inputs to the year-one deduction question. Neither is a constant.

## What §179 and §168(k) do not cover

A short list of common confusions:

- **The 27.5-year building itself.** Neither regulation applies. Standard MACRS only.
- **HVAC and roofs on residential rental.** Excluded from §179 specifically (the 2017 expansion of §179 covered non-residential improvements only).
- **Cost-segregation reclassifications on residential rental for §179.** §168(k) applies; §179 generally does not for residential rental's reclassified components.
- **Property converted from personal use.** Bonus depreciation under §168(k)(2)(E)(ii) is not available for property that was used for any purpose before being placed in service for business — including the host's prior personal use.
- **Used property acquired from related parties.** §168(k) was extended to used property in 2017, but related-party transactions are excluded.

When in doubt, run the question past the chapter that owns it: Chapter 6 for base depreciation, Chapter 7 for cost segregation, this chapter for the year-one expensing layer.

## Common mistakes

**Filing Schedule E passive when non-passive was available — and losing §179.** This is the Chapter 1 mistake re-emerging in Chapter 8. A host who could have qualified as non-passive but didn't document hours forfeits §179 on every commissioning purchase. For a host placing $40,000 of furniture in a year when bonus is 60%, that's roughly $14,000 of year-one deduction left on the table.

**Forgetting §168(k) on 15-year land improvements.** Driveways, decks-on-grade, exterior lighting, landscaping, fences — all 15-year property, all bonus-eligible. Hosts who correctly classify these (per Chapter 6) but never apply bonus take them on a slow 15-year schedule when they could have expensed most or all of the cost in year one.

**Electing out of bonus without realizing it.** Some tax preparers elect out of §168(k) by default as a "conservative" practice. If your prior-year return shows a §168(k) opt-out you didn't request, ask why.

**Misapplying the order of operations.** Using §168(k) before §179 wastes the §179 election. The order is §179 first, bonus second, standard MACRS last. Always.

**Capitalizing items under $2,500 that should have been expensed under the de minimis safe harbor (§1.263(a)-1(f), Chapter 5).** §179 and §168(k) are for items that *do* require capitalization. The de minimis safe harbor is one rung lower — for items that don't require capitalization at all. Use the right tool for the right item.
