# Chapter 14 — Marketing, Guest Experience, and Education

*The deductions that compound the next booking, not the current one.*

---

Lena ran six cabins in the Smoky Mountains and prided herself on the welcome-bottle of local wine she left for every guest. The bottles were nothing special — local Tennessee blends, $50 retail, picked up in cases at a winery near the highway. Across the year she gave out **80 bottles**: $4,000 of welcome gifts, dropped clean into "Supplies" on her Schedule E.

Her CPA, working through the year-end books, paused on the line.

> "How are you classifying these?"
> "Welcome gifts. Each guest gets one at check-in."
> "How much per bottle?"
> "Fifty."
> "Then half of it isn't deductible."

This was the §274(b) gift cap, and Lena had been on the wrong side of it for the entire year. The deduction wasn't disallowed on origin — the bottles were ordinary and necessary in Lena's segment. The deduction was *capped*. Per recipient, per year, the maximum business-gift deduction is **$25.** The other $25 of every bottle was below the line.

For 80 bottles at $50: **$2,000 disallowed.**

The next year Lena made two changes. She dropped to a $24 bottle — under the cap, fully deductible. And she added a $30 in-cabin coffee bar (locally-roasted beans, a French press, mugs in the brand color) — which is not a "gift" under §274(b) at all. It's a *property amenity*, deductible in full as a supply.

Same emotional impact for the guest. $2,000 of restored deduction.

This chapter is about the three categories that determine whether your *next* booking arrives at all — marketing, guest experience, and education — and the small regulatory traps that quietly disallow part of the deduction unless you know where they live.

These are **deductions #44 through #46** of the 47.

---

## The framework — §162, §274(b), and the "new business" line

Most expenses in this chapter clear §162 on origin. Marketing the listing is ordinary. Welcoming the guest is necessary. Education that maintains or improves the host's skills is appropriate and helpful.

Three regulatory edges trim the bulk:

1. **§274(b) — business gifts limited to $25 per recipient per year.** Affects Lena's wine and any per-guest gift practice.
2. **§274(k) — meals must not be lavish or extravagant.** Affects conference and travel meals from Chapter 9.
3. **§162 / Welch — education for a *new* trade or business is not deductible.** Affects the line between continuing-education and starting-something-new.

Get the framing right; the deductions land at full strength.

---

## Deduction #44 — Listing and marketing spend

Schedule E line 19 (Other) labeled "Advertising" or line 8 (Advertising) where state/preparer convention places it.

The clean cluster, all fully deductible, no special caps:

- **Professional photography** for listing photos. Universal. Highest-ROI marketing dollar most STR hosts spend.
- **Copywriting and listing optimization** services. Listing-description audits, headline rewrites, tag optimization.
- **Paid promotion** through platform-internal ads (Airbnb's promoted listings, VRBO's premier-partner placement) or third-party advertising (Google Ads to a direct-booking site, Facebook/Instagram ads).
- **SEO and direct-booking website** development and maintenance — domain, hosting, website builder, plugins.
- **Email marketing** services (Mailchimp, ConvertKit, Klaviyo) for past-guest remarketing.
- **Branded materials** — door signs, exterior signage with the listing name, business cards if used.
- **Pinterest, Instagram, TikTok content production** if the host runs the property's social presence.
- **Public-relations services** for press placements and feature coverage.

For a typical six-property portfolio, marketing spend often runs **$3,000–$15,000 a year.** For host-photographer single-property operations, less. For hosts running active direct-booking funnels with paid ads, more.

The deduction is uncontroversial. The only question is capture discipline — most marketing spend hides on a personal credit card, and hosts forget it.

## Deduction #45 — Guest experience

The category Lena got wrong. Schedule E line 15 (Supplies) for amenities; line 19 (Other) labeled "Guest gifts" for the §274(b)-capped portion.

Three sub-categories with different regulatory treatment:

### Welcome book printing and guest-facing collateral

The physical printed welcome book left in the cabin. Brochures. House rules placards. Local-attraction guides. These are **property amenities**, fully deductible as supplies. No §274(b) cap — they aren't gifts to specific recipients; they live in the property and are used by every guest who passes through.

For a six-property portfolio with quality printed welcome books refreshed annually: **$300–$800/year of fully-deductible supply.**

### Per-guest gifts (the §274(b) cap)

Anything *given specifically to a guest as a gift* — a welcome bottle, a custom souvenir, a hand-written card with a $20 gift card to a local restaurant — is a §274(b) business gift. Deduction is capped at **$25 per recipient per year**, regardless of actual cost.

The cap is generous in two ways:

- **"Per recipient" not "per gift."** If you give the same guest a $25 welcome bottle on arrival and a $25 thank-you card on departure, the deduction is still capped at $25 *for that recipient for the year* (assuming both qualify as gifts). Multiple gifts to the same person stack against the same cap.
- **Incidental costs don't count toward the cap — *if* they don't add substantial value.** Per Treas. Reg. §1.274-3(c)(2), gift wrapping, packaging, shipping, and delivery generally qualify as incidental and are deductible separately. Engraving and similar additions are excluded from the cap **only if** they "do not add substantial value" to the gift — engraving on a watch generally adds substantial value and counts against the $25 cap; a host name engraved on a $25 cutting board likely does too. A $25 bottle wrapped in $4 of branded packaging is a $29 deduction; $4 of engraving on a $25 cutting board may not be.

The cap is not a guess. It is statutory and mechanical. A $50 wine bottle is a $25 deduction with $25 disallowed, every time. A $20 wine bottle is a $20 deduction. A $25.01 wine bottle is a $25 deduction.

### Property amenities (no §274(b) cap)

Anything that **stays in the property** for use by all current and future guests — coffee beans in a stocked coffee bar, snacks in a basket, bath salts on the tub, robes in the closet, books on the shelf — is a *supply*, not a gift. Fully deductible as a supply expense, regardless of dollar amount.

The line is whether the item leaves with the guest or stays in the property. The IRS hasn't blessed this distinction in a published ruling specifically for STR amenities, so it is a practitioner-accepted teaching frame rather than a bright-line rule — a stocked pantry the guest consumes is functionally similar to a per-guest gift basket. Most CPAs accept the framing below; for outlier cases (e.g., a $300 in-room consumable bar that is fully restocked for each guest), expect a hedge from a careful CPA.

- A $50 wine bottle the guest opens and consumes: gift, capped at $25. (The bottle effectively leaves with the guest, even though the empty stays.)
- A $50 coffee-bar setup with locally-roasted beans, a French press, and ceramic mugs: amenity, deductible in full.
- A $20 welcome card with a $50 restaurant gift card inside: gift to specific guest, $25 cap (the card has a specific recipient).
- A $300 welcome basket of jams, honeys, and local snacks left in the kitchen for the guest's stay: gift, capped at $25.
- A $300 stocked pantry of staples the host refreshes between every booking: amenity, deductible in full.

Lena's wine bottles were gifts. Lena's coffee bar was an amenity. Both are legitimate guest-experience investments. Only one fits cleanly inside the §274(b) cap, and the other doesn't have to.

For most STR hosts, **the structural answer to the gift cap is to shift the welcome-touch budget from per-guest gifts to in-property amenities.** Guests respond similarly. The deduction is fully restored. Most hosts make this shift as soon as they hear about §274(b).

## Deduction #46 — Education and professional development

Schedule E line 19 (Other) labeled "Education" or rolled into "Professional services."

Categories that qualify under §162's "ordinary and necessary" framework, applied through Chapter 2's segment-context test:

- **STR-specific conferences** — VRMA, Hostfully Connect, ShortyZ, Vrolio, regional Airbnb host meetups. Travel + lodging + 50% meals all deductible per Chapter 9.
- **Online courses** specifically about short-term rental operations, pricing, marketing, or tax strategy.
- **One-on-one coaching or mentorship** with operating short-term-rental hosts or specialists.
- **Books on STR operations, real-estate investment, hospitality management, and tax strategy.** Including this book.
- **Subscriptions to industry publications** (newsletters, podcasts with paid tiers, market-research services like AirDNA Pro — though pure software lives in Chapter 10).

For most STR hosts, education spend runs **$200–$3,000 a year.** Hosts in active growth mode or hosts running coaching businesses themselves can run higher.

### The "new business" trap

Education is deductible under §162 if it **maintains or improves skills required in the host's existing trade or business.** It is *not* deductible if it qualifies the host for a *new* trade or business.

The line is fact-and-circumstances. Examples:

- A current STR host taking a course on STR pricing optimization → maintains/improves existing skills, deductible.
- A current STR host taking a real-estate-agent licensing course → qualifies the host for a new trade (real estate brokerage), **not deductible** under §1.162-5(b)(2). The course expense becomes a §195 start-up cost of the new business if the host actually launches as an agent, or a non-deductible personal expense if not.
- A current STR host taking a cost-segregation engineering course → arguable. If the education is to improve the host's own portfolio's tax outcomes, deductible. If to start a cost-seg consulting practice, not deductible against the rental.
- A current STR host taking a CPA review course or attending tax-attorney CLE → not deductible (qualifies for a new profession).

When the answer is unclear, the safer treatment is to defer the deduction and consult a CPA. The IRS examines education deductions when the topic strays from the existing business.

### Conferences — the §274(k) lavish-or-extravagant gate

Conference attendance is fully deductible if the conference is ordinary and necessary. Travel, registration fees, lodging, and 50% of meals all flow through (per Chapter 9 for the §274(d) and §274(n) mechanics).

The §274(k) lavish-or-extravagant rule applies to conference meals just as it applies to travel meals — but the IRS standard is generally lenient for genuine business attendance. A $90 conference banquet ticket is fine. A $400/plate winemaker's-dinner add-on at a five-star restaurant is questionable.

The substantiation rule from Chapter 3 applies: receipts, dates, business purpose, names of attendees if dining with others. A conference badge plus the receipts is the standard package.

## Common mistakes

**Treating §274(b) gifts as supplies.** The Lena mistake. Year-end CPA review catches it; mid-year capture discipline prevents it. The fix is structural — shift to property amenities for most of the welcome budget.

**Forgetting that the $25 cap is per-recipient-per-year.** Multiple small gifts to the same guest add to the same cap. Two $20 gifts to the same guest in the same year is a $25 deduction, not a $40 deduction.

**Failing to allocate listing-photography costs to multiple properties.** A $1,200 photoshoot covering two properties is $600 per property, not $1,200 on one. Allocate cleanly at capture.

**Capitalizing marketing spend that should be expensed.** Marketing services (photography, copywriting, ads, SEO) are current-year expenses under §162 — not capital improvements under §263(a). Even a one-time $4,000 listing optimization investment is fully deductible in the year incurred.

**Trying to deduct "real estate education" that's actually for a different business.** A host getting a real estate license, a property-management certification for a separate PM business, or a hospitality MBA is paying for *new business* qualification. Deduction not allowed against the existing rental.

**Taking conference attendance without §274(d) substantiation.** A conference is travel under §274(d). Receipts, dates, places, business purpose. The conference badge alone is not enough — keep the registration receipt, the lodging receipt, and the meal receipts.
