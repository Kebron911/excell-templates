# Chapter 13 — Insurance, Umbrella, and Entity Costs

*The deductions that exist whether you claim them or not.*

---

Renee owned a single short-term rental on a barrier island. For the first eighteen months she ran it, the house was insured under her standard HO-3 homeowner policy — the same policy that covered her primary residence elsewhere, with the rental property simply listed as a second home.

A guest broke an ankle on the back stairs in the second summer. The guest's lawyer called. The lawyer's letter went to Renee's insurance company. Three weeks later, the insurance company wrote back: the policy excluded coverage for losses occurring while the property was occupied by paying guests. The carrier wasn't denying out of malice. They were following the language in the policy Renee had signed eighteen months earlier and never re-read.

Renee personally settled the claim out of pocket — about $9,400 — and made three changes that month:

- Switched to a **commercial dwelling policy** with explicit short-term rental coverage ($1,800/year, vs. $1,200 for the HO policy)
- Filed an **LLC** for the rental ($350 formation, $200/year registered agent, $50 annual report)
- Bought a **$1M personal umbrella liability policy** ($420/year)

The total annual cost of the new stack: **about $2,820.** All of it deductible, every year. The cost of *not* having it the previous year: $9,400 plus eighteen months of unrecognized exposure.

This chapter is about the four deductions in that paragraph and the regulation behind each one. They are workhorse deductions — small individually, real in aggregate, almost universally available to STR hosts who set the operating shell up correctly.

These are **deductions #41 through #43** of the 47.

---

## The framework — §162 plus §195

Insurance premiums paid in connection with the rental are deductible under §162 as ordinary and necessary business expenses. Schedule E line 9 (Insurance).

Entity costs split into two categories:

- **Annual operating costs** of the entity (registered agent, annual report fees, business license fees) — deductible under §162 in the year incurred. Schedule E line 19 (Other) or line 16 (Taxes).
- **One-time formation costs** (filing fees, legal fees for organization documents, initial state filing) — treated under **§195** as start-up expenditures if incurred before the rental activity begins, or under §709 (for LLCs taxed as partnerships) for organizational expenses. Both regimes allow up to $5,000 of immediate deduction in the first year, with the remainder amortized over 180 months (15 years).

The accounting can be fussy. The deductions are real either way; the only question is whether they hit the return immediately, slowly, or in some combination.

---

## Deduction #41 — Property insurance (the right policy)

Schedule E line 9. The clean rule: insurance premiums for coverage of the rental property are fully deductible, period.

The catch is having the right policy in the first place. Three policy types commonly cover STR-adjacent risks, and only one of them actually does:

**Standard homeowner (HO-3) policy.** Covers owner-occupied or part-time-secondary-home use. Most policies *exclude* coverage during periods of paying guest occupancy. Reading the policy's "business pursuits" exclusion and the "rental of premises" provisions is the only way to know what's actually covered. Renee's mistake was assuming "second home" coverage extended to guest occupancy. It didn't.

**DP-3 dwelling-fire policy.** Designed for landlord rentals where the owner doesn't occupy. Better than HO-3 for rentals, but most DP-3 forms assume long-term tenancy and may exclude or limit short-term-rental scenarios. Verify with the carrier.

**Commercial dwelling / STR-specific policy.** Designed for short-term rental use explicitly. Carriers including Proper Insurance, CBIZ, and several major insurers' specialty divisions write policies that cover guest-occupancy losses, business interruption, contents at higher limits, and liability tailored to STR.

For most STR hosts, the commercial dwelling policy is the only one of the three that actually pays out when a guest claim hits. It's also the most expensive — typically 30–80% more than the HO-3 it replaces.

**The full annual premium** of whichever STR-appropriate policy the host carries is deductible. There is no allocation, no mixed-use percentage, no business-vs-personal split. The property is a rental; the policy covers the rental; the deduction is 100%.

What's NOT deductible:

- Personal residence insurance for the host's home (unless allocated through the home office in Chapter 12)
- Title insurance (capitalizes into basis at acquisition — Chapter 6)
- Mortgage insurance premiums (sometimes deductible as part of mortgage interest treatment under varying rules — verify with CPA)

## Deduction #42 — Umbrella liability

A personal umbrella policy provides liability coverage above the limits of underlying policies (homeowner, auto, dwelling). For an STR host, the umbrella sits behind the commercial dwelling policy and catches catastrophic claims.

Annual premiums for $1M of umbrella coverage typically run **$200–$500.** $2M coverage runs **$300–$700.** $5M is rarely cost-effective for individual hosts.

The deduction wrinkle: an umbrella policy covers both the host's personal life (auto, primary residence) and the host's business life (the rental). The IRS allows deduction of the **business-use portion** of the umbrella premium under §162.

The defensible allocation method: weight the business-use percentage by the relative liability exposure. If the rental represents 40% of the host's overall liability profile (one rental property versus a primary home, two cars, and personal life), 40% of the umbrella premium is deductible. The percentage isn't precise, but it should be defensible — a host with three rentals and a single primary residence has higher rental exposure than a host with one rental and two cars.

For a $400 annual umbrella premium with a 40% business allocation: **$160/year of deduction.** Small dollars. Universal availability. Frequently missed because the host pays the premium from a personal account and never thinks to allocate.

## Deduction #43 — Entity costs (LLC, registered agent, annual reports)

Schedule E line 19 (Other) for current expenses; Schedule E line 18 (Other) labeled "Amortization" for §195/§709 amortized portions.

Three sub-categories:

### Annual operating costs (current expense)

- **Registered agent fees** — typically $100–$300/year per state where the LLC is registered. If the LLC is registered in multiple states (the property is in one state, the host lives in another and registered there too — common for asset-protection planning), each state's registered agent is deductible.
- **Annual report fees** — varies by state; typically $50–$200/year. Some states (Delaware, California, Massachusetts, others) charge franchise or annual taxes that exceed $500.
- **Business license fees** — local jurisdictions vary. Often $50–$300/year.
- **State franchise taxes** — California's $800/year LLC tax, for instance. Deductible on Schedule E line 16 (Taxes).

All of the above are deductible in full in the year paid. No allocation, no amortization.

### Formation costs (§195 or §709)

The one-time costs of creating the LLC:

- State filing fees (Articles of Organization) — typically $50–$500
- Legal fees for organizational documents (Operating Agreement, etc.) — typically $200–$2,000 if attorney-prepared
- Initial registered agent setup — first-year fee
- Federal EIN — free
- Any pre-formation consulting — legal review, accountant consultation on structure

If the formation occurs **before** the rental activity is "active" (often the case when forming an LLC in anticipation of buying the first property), §195 applies. The host can deduct up to $5,000 of start-up costs in the year the activity begins (phased out for total start-up costs above $50,000 — rarely an issue) and amortize the remainder over 180 months (15 years).

If the formation occurs **after** the rental activity is already operating (the host is adding the entity layer to an existing portfolio), the costs are typically deductible as ordinary §162 expenses in the year incurred — though the IRS sometimes scrutinizes large entity-formation costs claimed as current expenses. For amounts under $1,000, current-expense treatment is generally accepted; for larger amounts, the safer treatment is amortization under §709 (for LLCs taxed as partnerships) over 180 months.

For Renee's $350 formation fee post-rental-launch: deduct as current expense on Schedule E line 19.

For a host who pays $3,000 to a tax attorney to set up a multi-LLC structure before buying their first property: §195 start-up costs apply. Deduct $3,000 entirely in year one (under the $5,000 cap).

### What does NOT belong here

- **Personal will or estate planning** that incidentally references the rental — not deductible
- **Asset-protection planning** unrelated to the rental's operation — not deductible (subject to a fact-specific test)
- **Tax planning** consultations — these belong in Chapter 11 (professional services), not entity costs

## A note on entity choice

This chapter is about deducting the *costs* of an entity. It is not about choosing which entity structure to use. That decision — single-member LLC, multi-member LLC, S-corp election, partnership, sole-proprietor with no entity at all — is a separate analysis that depends on liability exposure, state-specific tax treatment, asset-protection goals, and whether the host wants to file separate entity returns.

The high-level rule of thumb most STR-focused tax attorneys offer:

- **No entity at all** — fine for hosts with one property and modest liability exposure, who carry adequate commercial dwelling and umbrella insurance. The deductions in this chapter all still apply at the individual level.
- **Single-member LLC** — the default recommendation for most hosts. Disregarded entity for tax (no separate return), provides liability shielding, and the costs are modest.
- **Multi-member LLC** — for spouses or partnerships, with the additional complexity of a 1065 partnership return.
- **S-corp election** — rarely beneficial for pure rental operations on Schedule E (since rental income isn't subject to SE tax anyway, the S-corp's main benefit doesn't apply). Sometimes useful for active hosts on Schedule C — but the "reasonable salary" requirement and payroll administration usually outweigh the savings until net income is well above $80,000–$100,000. **OBBBA made §199A permanent in 2025**, which shifts this break-even higher: the QBI deduction continues to apply to Schedule C net income but **not** to S-corp wages, so the S-corp election now permanently sacrifices a 20% deduction on the wage portion. The historical $80K–$100K floor likely moves up post-OBBBA — confirm with your CPA on the actual SE-tax-saved-vs-§199A-lost math at your specific income level before electing.

The structural choice is best made with a tax attorney who knows your state and your portfolio. The deductions in this chapter apply regardless of which structure you choose; the only thing that changes is which return the costs land on.

## Common mistakes

**Carrying the wrong policy.** The Renee mistake. The premium is irrelevant if it doesn't cover guest-occupancy losses. Read the policy. If unclear, ask the carrier in writing.

**Failing to allocate the umbrella premium.** Hosts who pay umbrella from a personal account and never allocate to the rental forfeit the deduction. The allocation is approximate but defensible — claim it.

**Treating large LLC formation costs as current expenses.** A $3,500 attorney-prepared multi-LLC structure deducted in full as a current expense on Schedule E line 19 may invite questions on audit. The safer approach is §195 amortization for pre-launch costs or §709 amortization for post-launch organizational costs. For amounts under $1,000, current-expense treatment is generally fine.

**Forgetting state franchise taxes.** California's $800 LLC tax is the most expensive single line item in this category. Hosts who own properties through California LLCs but never see the franchise tax bill (because their accountant pays it from the LLC bank account) sometimes don't realize it's a deductible expense at the individual level when the LLC is disregarded. It is. Capture it.

**Mixing personal liability insurance with rental coverage.** A personal liability policy that covers the host's personal life is not deductible. An umbrella that sits behind both personal and rental policies requires allocation. Don't claim 100% of an umbrella as a rental expense unless 100% of the underlying exposure is rental-related (extremely unusual).

**Skipping renewals.** Several deductions in this chapter renew annually — registered agent, annual report, insurance premiums. Hosts who set up the entity once and forget to keep it active can lose LLC status in their state, exposing themselves to personal liability and forfeiting the deduction in subsequent years. Calendar the renewal dates.

---

## A note on chapter scope

This chapter covers the *operating shell* — insurance and entity. Two related categories live elsewhere:

- **Mortgage interest** — deductible on Schedule E line 12, Chapter 4-style operating expense (covered in passing in Ch 4 / Ch 6 since it's universally claimed)
- **Property taxes** — Schedule E line 16, also universally claimed

Both of those are big deductions that almost no host misses. They're not in this chapter because no one needs to be told to claim them. The deductions in this chapter — the right insurance, the umbrella allocation, the entity-cost amortization — are the ones routinely under-claimed because the host doesn't know the regulation or doesn't think to apply it.
