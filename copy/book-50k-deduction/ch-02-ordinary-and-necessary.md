# Chapter 2 — What "Ordinary and Necessary" Actually Means

*The phrase that gates every business deduction in the tax code.*

---

Priya owned two cabins outside Asheville. Both rented in the $180–$220 a night range, three- and four-night stays mostly. After a guest complained that the coffee in cabin two was "Folgers-tier," Priya bought a $4,500 prosumer espresso machine — a Rocket Appartamento, with a doser grinder — and put it on the kitchen counter. On her return that year, she dropped the $4,500 into "Supplies" and moved on.

Her CPA, reviewing the books that December, paused on the line item.

> "Tell me about this espresso machine."
> "Guest complained about the coffee. I bought a nicer one."
> "On a $200-a-night cabin."
> "Yes."
> "This isn't ordinary."

This was true. It was also not the end of the conversation — but it was the start of one Priya hadn't expected to have. The deduction wasn't disallowed outright. It was *reframed*. The $4,500 came out of "Supplies" and went into the depreciation schedule as a 5-year asset, recoverable over five years instead of one. Priya took $900 in year one instead of $4,500. She also walked out of the meeting understanding something she hadn't before — that "deductible" is not the same as "deductible in full, this year."

This chapter is about the phrase that gates every business deduction in the tax code: **ordinary and necessary.** The phrase is from §162 of the Internal Revenue Code. Its interpretation has been litigated for nearly a century. And almost every deduction question an STR host has — *can I deduct this?* — is, at its core, an ordinary-and-necessary question.

---

## What §162 actually says

The operative language of §162(a) reads:

> *"There shall be allowed as a deduction all the ordinary and necessary expenses paid or incurred during the taxable year in carrying on any trade or business..."*

Two requirements. Ordinary. Necessary. Both apply. Both have specific meanings in tax law that don't quite match the everyday meanings of the words.

**Ordinary** does not mean common. It does not mean cheap. It means *customary in your particular trade or business, at your scale and segment.* The leading case is *Welch v. Helvering*, 290 U.S. 111 (1933), where the Supreme Court held that an expense is "ordinary" when it is "of common or frequent occurrence in the type of business involved." Critically, that test is contextual. What is ordinary for a $200-a-night cabin is different from what is ordinary for a $1,200-a-night chalet, and different again from what is ordinary for a luxury beachfront with a private chef.

**Necessary** is the lower bar. It does not mean strictly required. It means *appropriate and helpful* to the business. Courts have repeatedly held that the taxpayer's good-faith judgment that an expense was useful clears the necessary hurdle in most cases. Most CPAs spend almost no time on the *necessary* test. They spend almost all of their time on the *ordinary* test.

This is **deduction #3** of the 47 — not a line item, but the legal frame that decides whether every other line item is allowed. Get the frame right and the rest of the book is mechanical. Get it wrong and your line items get reclassified, depreciated over multiple years instead of expensed, or disallowed outright.

---

## The contextual test — and why STR is unusual

The "ordinary in your trade or business" test produces a paradox most hosts don't notice: an expense can be ordinary for one of your properties and not ordinary for another. The benchmark is not "STR hosts in general." It is the specific operating segment your specific property competes in.

Three quick examples.

**A $4,500 espresso machine.**
- In a $200/night cabin: not ordinary. Reclassify or capitalize.
- In a $700/night design-forward boutique listing where "café-grade espresso bar" is in the description and the photos: ordinary. Expense in full (subject to capitalization rules — see Ch 5).
- In a $1,500/night ski chalet that markets a "barista experience": ordinary, defensible, and probably under-spec relative to the segment.

**A $90/month gym membership for the host.**
- For most hosts: not ordinary. Personal expense, not deductible regardless of how much the host needs the gym to manage portfolio stress.
- For a host who provides yoga or fitness instruction as a substantial service to guests (Schedule C territory — see Ch 1) and uses the gym for credentialing: arguably ordinary. Defensible with documentation.

**A $1,200 listing photographer.**
- For any STR at any price point in any market: ordinary. Listing photography is universally accepted as a customary cost of marketing a short-term rental.
- Even at the absurd end of the spectrum — a $50/night roadside cabin — listing photography is ordinary. It's also probably the highest-ROI dollar the host will spend.

The reason this matters is that the IRS and the tax courts apply the test the way it's written. They don't ask "do other STR hosts buy espresso machines?" They ask "do other STR hosts *operating in this segment, at this price point, for this guest profile* buy this kind of equipment?"

**The defense is the segment.** When you take a deduction that might raise an examiner's eyebrow, your justification is not that the deduction is common across STR. Your justification is that the deduction is common for *the kind of property you actually run.* Document the segment — the listing description, the photo standards, the guest reviews referencing the relevant feature — and the deduction has a story.

---

## Necessary — the bar most hosts misunderstand

If "ordinary" is the contextual test, "necessary" is the everyday-use test. The bar here is much lower than hosts assume.

The Supreme Court in *Welch* and again in *Commissioner v. Tellier*, 383 U.S. 687 (1966), held that "necessary" requires only that the expense be "appropriate and helpful" to the business. The taxpayer does not need to prove the expense was indispensable. The taxpayer does not need to prove the expense produced revenue. The taxpayer only needs to show the expense was reasonably aimed at advancing or protecting the business.

In practice this means:

- **A $300 marketing course** does not need to have generated a single booking to be deductible — only to have been reasonably aimed at improving marketing.
- **A $1,500 hot tub maintenance contract** does not need to have prevented a single repair to be deductible — only to have been reasonably aimed at protecting the asset.
- **A $400 software subscription** that the host evaluated for two months and abandoned is still deductible for those two months — the test is the host's good-faith judgment at the time of purchase, not 20/20 hindsight.

Hosts routinely under-claim because they hold themselves to a much higher bar than the regulations require. *Necessary* is not *indispensable*. *Necessary* is *appropriate and helpful*. If you bought it because you thought it would help your business, and your judgment was reasonable, the necessary test is met.

## The capitalization trap — when ordinary expenses must be spread

Priya's espresso machine was reclassified, not disallowed, because of a separate rule that lives next door to §162. **Section 263(a)** requires that expenditures producing "permanent improvements or betterments" — assets with a useful life beyond one year — be capitalized rather than expensed. Capitalized expenditures recover their cost over the asset's depreciation life rather than in the year of purchase.

The line is not the dollar amount. The line is the useful life.

- A $200 set of dish towels: useful life under one year. Expense.
- A $200 lamp: useful life over one year. Capitalize, then depreciate over 5 years (5-year MACRS class — Ch 6 has the table).
- A $4,500 espresso machine: useful life over one year. Same.
- A $4,500 contractor invoice for a one-time deep clean: useful life zero. Expense.

The IRS has built two safe harbors that ease this distinction for small purchases:

1. **De-minimis safe harbor under §1.263(a)-1(f).** Tangible property under **$2,500 per item or per invoice** can be expensed in the year of purchase, regardless of useful life, if the host elects the safe harbor on a timely-filed return. (Higher-amount thresholds apply for taxpayers with audited financial statements; not relevant for almost any STR host.)
2. **Routine maintenance safe harbor under §1.263(a)-3(i).** Maintenance expected to be performed more than once over the property's useful life — HVAC service, gutter cleaning, repainting on a cycle — can be expensed even though the underlying property is capitalized.

Most STR purchases below $2,500 per item, properly invoiced, qualify for the de minimis safe harbor and become same-year expenses. Most STR purchases above $2,500 must be capitalized regardless of how clearly they pass the ordinary-and-necessary tests.

Priya's espresso machine cleared §162 (in the right segment). It failed §263(a) because its useful life was beyond one year. The deduction was correct in concept and wrong in timing.

## Mixed-use — the prorate principle

Many STR expenses are partially personal and partially business. The phone bill that handles guest comms and your dentist appointments. The car that drives to the property and to school pickup. The home internet that powers your reservation software and your kid's homework.

The §162 framework allows the **business-use percentage** of mixed-use expenses, provided you can substantiate the percentage. The phone bill that's 60% guest comms is 60% deductible. The car that's 35% portfolio business is 35% deductible (or, more commonly, deducted at the standard mileage rate per §1.274-5(j) — see Ch 9). The home internet that's 40% rental software is 40% deductible.

The substantiation method varies by category:

- **Phone:** maintain a usage log for a representative two-week sample period each year, then apply the percentage. The IRS has accepted the sampling method in practice for decades.
- **Vehicle:** mileage log under §274(d) — strict substantiation required. (Treated in detail in Ch 9.)
- **Home internet, utilities:** square-footage allocation if there's a dedicated home office — see Ch 12. Otherwise, a reasonable use-percentage method.

The mistake hosts make is not the percentage. The mistake is failing to claim the partial deduction at all because the expense is mixed. *Mixed-use is not non-deductible.* It is deductible at the business-use percentage. Claim the percentage. Document the method.

## What's almost always not deductible

A short list of the things hosts try to deduct that almost never clear §162:

- **Commuting** between your home and your only-or-primary place of business. Disallowed under §1.262-1(b)(5) regardless of mileage. (Travel from your home to a *property* is generally deductible — the property is not your place of business in the commuting sense — but document carefully and read Ch 9.)
- **Clothing** that's wearable outside the business. The "uniform" exception (§1.162-5) requires the clothing to be both required for the business and not adaptable to general use. A polo with your STR brand logo on it — borderline yes. A nice blazer you wear to CPA meetings — no.
- **Personal meals during ordinary travel.** Meals on a one-day trip to your property are not deductible — see §274(n) and the case law on overnight travel. Meals during overnight trips are 50% deductible (Ch 9).
- **Country club, gym, social club dues.** §274(a)(3) categorically disallows. No carve-out for business networking.
- **Lobbying or political contributions.** §162(e). Categorically disallowed.
- **Fines and penalties paid to government.** §162(f). Categorically disallowed. This includes parking tickets, late-filing penalties, and HOA violations even if technically business-related.

When something appears on this list, no documentation strategy rescues it. The deduction is structurally unavailable.

## The "would a competent CPA defend this" test

The practical test most experienced tax preparers use, and that this book recommends as a discipline, is to ask:

> *Would a competent CPA, given the full context, defend this deduction in writing?*

If the answer is yes — claim it. Document the segment, document the necessity, document the use percentage if mixed.

If the answer is *probably*, with caveats — claim it, but build the documentation file before you file the return, not after the IRS asks for it.

If the answer is no — don't claim it. The cost of an aggressive deduction that gets disallowed isn't just the deduction. It's the disallowed amount, plus interest from the original due date, plus a 20% accuracy-related penalty under §6662 if the IRS finds the position was unreasonable. On a $4,000 deduction, the all-in cost of getting it wrong can exceed $1,500.

The good news for STR operators is that the segment-based test, properly applied, is generous. STR is a service-rich business that competes on guest experience. Many expenses that would be hard to justify in a long-term rental — design upgrades, premium amenities, professional photography, guest gifts under $25, event-driven cleaning supplies — clear §162 cleanly when the segment supports them.

Claim what's yours. Don't claim what isn't. Document the difference.
