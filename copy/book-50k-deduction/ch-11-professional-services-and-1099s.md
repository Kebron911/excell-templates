# Chapter 11 — Professional Services and 1099 Contractors

*The deduction the IRS expects you to file paperwork for.*

---

Sloane ran a five-property portfolio and paid four contractors more than $600 each in 2023 — her cleaner, her handyman, her landscaper, and her pool-and-hot-tub service. Before the first payment to each, her property manager emailed a W-9 form template and required it back as a prerequisite for getting paid. By the third week of January 2024, Sloane had filed four clean 1099-NEC forms with the IRS and mailed the contractor copies. Total time spent across the year: about an hour. Total cost: zero — her bookkeeping software files 1099s for $4 each.

Ahmad ran two properties and paid his cleaner $14,400 across 2022 and 2023 — twelve hundred a month, all of it via Zelle, all of it without a W-9 on file. The cleaner moved away in late 2023 and Ahmad lost contact. When the IRS opened an examination of Ahmad's 2023 return, the auditor questioned the $7,200 cleaner deduction: no 1099-NEC filed, no W-9 on file, no signed contractor agreement.

The cleaning had been real. The deduction had been real. The *substantiation* hadn't.

The auditor disallowed $4,800 of the $7,200 deduction (the portion the auditor concluded Ahmad couldn't prove was for business cleaning rather than personal). Add §6722 penalties for failure to file 1099s (two years × the per-form rate, which exceeded $300 per form by then). Add interest. Add a 20% accuracy-related penalty under §6662 on the disallowed deduction. **Ahmad's all-in cost: about $5,200.**

This chapter is about Sloane's hour and Ahmad's $5,200. They are the same chapter, played out from opposite ends.

These are **deductions #35 through #38** of the 47.

---

## The framework — §162 plus §6041

Every dollar paid to a non-employee service provider in connection with the rental is, on its face, deductible under §162. That part is easy. The complication is **§6041**, the information-reporting regulation that requires the host to file a 1099-NEC for any unincorporated payee paid above the threshold in a calendar year for services. The threshold was $600 for 2025-and-prior payments; **OBBBA §70433 raised it to $2,000 for payments made after December 31, 2025**, with inflation indexing for years beginning after 2026. The W-9 discipline below is unchanged at either threshold — the deduction stands or falls separately from the information-return obligation, and the cost of having the W-9 on file is zero whether or not the payee crosses the year's reporting line.

The regulations divide the work into two streams:

1. **The deduction itself** — the dollar amount paid for services. Captured in the appropriate Schedule E line at year-end.
2. **The information return** — the 1099-NEC filed with the IRS by January 31 of the following year, with a copy to the contractor.

The deduction stands or falls on §162. The 1099 stands or falls on §6041. The two are separate obligations. Ahmad's mistake was not the deduction; it was the missing W-9 that, on audit, created the credibility gap that let the auditor disallow part of the deduction. Without the W-9 and the 1099, Ahmad couldn't prove the payee was a contractor at all.

## Deduction #35 — Professional services

Schedule E, line 17 (Legal and other professional fees). The clean cluster:

- **CPA fees** for preparing the rental return — including the portion of your personal return preparation allocable to the rental schedule
- **Legal fees** for rental-related matters: lease drafting, eviction proceedings, dispute resolution, contract review
- **Bookkeeping services** for the rental, including outsourced bookkeeping software costs that include CPA-level oversight
- **Tax-planning consultations** that produce reasonably-aimed-at-the-business advice

CPA fees specifically: if your CPA charges $1,800 for the personal return and $400 of that is the rental schedule work, the $400 is deductible on Schedule E. The remaining $1,400 personal-portion is a 2017+ TCJA suspension casualty (miscellaneous itemized deductions are no longer deductible for individuals in most cases) and lives nowhere on the return. The split matters — ask your CPA to invoice rental-allocated work as a separate line item, and the deduction is automatic.

For most STR hosts, professional services on a single-property portfolio run **$400–$1,500 a year.** Multi-property: **$1,500–$5,000.** Hosts with active legal matters: higher. The deduction is iron-clad ordinary under Chapter 2; the only failure mode is forgetting to capture it.

What does **not** belong on this line:

- Personal income-tax preparation that doesn't allocate to the rental
- Estate planning fees (unless directly rental-related)
- Investment advisory fees not tied to the rental property

## Deduction #36 — 1099 contractor payments

Schedule E, line 7 (Cleaning and maintenance) for cleaners and most repair contractors; line 11 (Management fees) for property managers; or line 14 (Repairs) for ad-hoc repair contractors.

Every dollar paid to a non-employee for services performed at or for the rental is deductible. The categories are uncontroversial:

- Cleaners (between-stay turnover crews)
- Handymen and repair contractors
- Landscapers and lawn services
- Pest control companies
- Snow removal contractors
- Pool and hot tub maintenance services
- HVAC service technicians
- Locksmiths and security installers
- Marketing freelancers (photographers, copywriters, listing optimizers)
- Virtual assistants

The deduction is on the host's return whether the host pays directly or pays through a property manager. The form changes (line 11 vs line 7), the dollar amount doesn't.

## Deduction #37 — The 1099-NEC obligation

Under §6041, if a host pays an unincorporated payee at or above the §6041 threshold in a calendar year for services, the host must:

1. **Collect a Form W-9** from the payee, on or before the first payment
2. **File Form 1099-NEC** with the IRS by January 31 of the following year, reporting the gross amount paid
3. **Send a copy of the 1099-NEC** to the payee by the same January 31 deadline

The threshold is per *calendar year* and per *payee*. The figure: **$600** for 2025-and-prior payments; **$2,000** for payments made after December 31, 2025 under OBBBA §70433 (with inflation indexing for years beginning after 2026). A host paying a cleaner $50/turnover and doing thirteen turnovers under the old regime crossed the threshold at the thirteenth turnover; under the post-2025 $2,000 floor, the same cleaner crosses at the forty-first. There is no exception for "below the radar" amounts — the rule kicks in at the threshold mark for the relevant year. **A W-9 on file regardless of threshold is the right operational default**: it costs nothing, and the threshold is a moving target.

**Who is exempt** from the 1099-NEC filing requirement:

- Corporations (C-corps and S-corps) — except for legal-services payees, which always require 1099-NEC regardless of incorporation status
- Tax-exempt organizations
- Government agencies
- Payees paid via credit card (the card processor handles the 1099-K reporting; the host does not also file 1099-NEC)
- Payments under the §6041 threshold for the year per payee ($600 for 2025-and-prior; $2,000 for 2026+ under OBBBA §70433)

Most STR contractors are unincorporated sole proprietors or single-member LLCs (which default to disregarded entity status — treated as sole proprietors for 1099 purposes). They are *not* exempt. The "they're an LLC, I don't need to file" rule is a common misconception. Single-member LLCs require 1099-NEC. Multi-member LLCs taxed as partnerships also require 1099-NEC. Only LLCs that have specifically elected S-corp or C-corp tax treatment escape the requirement.

The W-9 makes the determination clean — the form's check-the-box section indicates the payee's tax classification. Collect the form. The W-9 either lists "C corporation" or "S corporation" on the entity-classification line (no 1099 needed) or it lists individual / sole proprietor / partnership / LLC (1099 required).

### The penalties for non-filing

§6722 — failure to furnish correct payee statements. The penalty is per missed form and is indexed annually under the Rev. Proc. for the year of filing. The current tier structure (information returns required to be filed in 2026):

| Late by | Penalty per return |
|---|---|
| ≤ 30 days | ~$60 |
| 31 days – August 1 | ~$130 |
| > August 1 or not filed | ~$340 |
| Intentional disregard | ~$680 (no annual cap) |

§6721 — failure to file information returns with the IRS. Mirrored penalty structure.

The figures index up each cycle — verify against the current Rev. Proc. for the year of filing before assuming the table above. A host who skipped four 1099-NECs for two years could face penalties in the $2,500–$5,000 range before the underlying deduction issue is touched.

The penalties are *separate* from the deductibility question. A host who paid $14,400 in cleaning, captured the receipts properly, and won the §162 question on audit can still face $1,200+ in 1099-related penalties for the failure to file. The deduction and the information return are independent obligations.

## Deduction #38 — Property management fees

Schedule E, line 11. The cleanest line item in this chapter. If you pay a property manager — whether a full-service local manager or a remote co-host — the management fee is fully deductible.

Three sub-categories:

- **Percentage-of-revenue fees** (typical for local PMs): often 18–28% of gross bookings
- **Flat monthly fees** (typical for remote co-hosts): often $100–$300/month per property
- **One-time setup or onboarding fees**: deductible in year incurred

The 1099 obligation applies to property managers the same as any other contractor. If you paid a single PM more than $600 in the year and they're unincorporated, you owe a 1099-NEC.

**The pass-through-payment subtlety:** if the property manager invoices you for management fees AND for vendor payments they made on your behalf (cleaning, supply runs, repair contractors), the deduction is allocated by line:

- Management fee → line 11
- Cleaner payment passed through → line 7
- Repair contractor pass-through → line 14
- Supply purchases pass-through → line 15

Most PMs provide an annual statement that itemizes by category. Don't lump everything onto the management line — itemize at the source level for cleaner Schedule E reporting.

A side issue: if the PM is paying contractors on your behalf with discretion and from PM-controlled trust funds, the PM is generally the §6041 obligor under Treas. Reg. §1.6041-1(e) (the "person who has management or oversight of payment") and you do not also file. The cleaner the PM's discretion, the cleaner the rule. Where the PM is acting as a *bare agent* — following your instructions, with your funds clearly identified — the question is murkier and some practitioners counsel the owner to file as well. Confirm in writing with your PM each year that they handled the filings, and keep the confirmation in the contractor file. That confirmation is your documentation if the IRS asks.

## The W-9 discipline

The single highest-leverage operational habit for this chapter is requiring a completed W-9 *before* the first payment to any contractor. Two paragraphs of process:

1. **Build a payment-prereq email template.** When a new contractor first invoices you, send a template email: "Thanks for the invoice. Before I can issue payment, please return the attached W-9. We process payment within 24 hours of receipt." Most established contractors complete and return the form same-day; they're used to it. One-time handymen, vacation-market cleaners with informal businesses, and contractors who only work cash markets are less reliable here — calibrate expectations and have a fallback ready.

2. **Save the W-9 to the contractor file.** A folder per contractor, the W-9 in there, every invoice and payment confirmation in there. The folder is your audit-defense package for that vendor.

3. **Have a backup-withholding fallback for the contractor who refuses.** If a contractor declines to provide a W-9, §3406 requires the payer to withhold **24% of the payment as backup withholding** and remit it to the IRS. In practice, the refusal usually evaporates once the contractor learns they're about to receive 24% less than invoiced. If it doesn't, withhold; do not skip the W-9 just to keep the relationship friction-free.

Hosts who establish this discipline at first payment never have an Ahmad-shaped problem. Hosts who try to chase down W-9s after a year of payments find that 30% of contractors are unreachable, having moved or changed names or simply gotten too busy to fill out a form for a one-time job from last year.

The pre-payment requirement isn't punitive. It's the same arrangement every payroll service uses for new employees — you don't get the first paycheck without filling out the W-4. For 1099 contractors, the W-9 is the equivalent.

## Common mistakes

**Paying contractors in cash.** Cash payments are not, on their face, undeductible — but they are extraordinarily hard to substantiate. The auditor's first question for a cash deduction is "show me the trail." Without bank records, Zelle/Venmo/PayPal records, or signed receipts, the host is at the mercy of the auditor's discretion. Use traceable payment methods. Cash is the worst choice the modern STR host can make.

**Missing the credit-card carve-out.** Payments to contractors made via credit card don't require a 1099-NEC from you — the card processor handles the 1099-K. If you pay your cleaner via credit card consistently, you don't owe them a 1099-NEC. The exemption is real but narrow; mixed payment methods (some cash, some card, some Zelle) require treating the non-card portion as 1099-NEC-eligible.

**Lumping property manager line items.** Per the pass-through subtlety above. Itemize by source category, not by who wrote the check.

**Skipping 1099s "because the contractor said they don't need one."** Contractors don't get to opt out of 1099 reporting. Their preference is irrelevant. The host's obligation is the host's obligation.

**Filing 1099-MISC instead of 1099-NEC.** Since 2020, contractor payments for services go on **1099-NEC**, not 1099-MISC. 1099-MISC is for rents paid, prizes and awards, and other miscellaneous income. Use the right form.

**Forgetting state filing.** Many states require their own copy of the 1099-NEC. Check your state's combined federal/state filing program — most STR-operating states (Florida, Tennessee, North Carolina, Colorado, Arizona) participate in the federal program and don't need separate filings, but some don't. Verify once for your state.
