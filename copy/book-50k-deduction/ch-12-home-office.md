# Chapter 12 — Home Office

*Exclusive use, regular use, principal place. Not optional.*

---

Maya managed three properties from her dining room table. The laptop lived on the same table where her family ate dinner. Reservation messaging happened during morning coffee on the same surface where last night's plates had sat. There was a desk in the corner of her bedroom too — sometimes she used it.

When her CPA brought up the home office deduction at year-end, Maya started talking square footage. The CPA stopped her three sentences in.

> "Wait. Exclusive use?"
> "What do you mean?"
> "The space has to be used *only* for the rental business. Nothing else. Not dinner. Not crafts. Not where the kids do homework."
> "Oh."
> "Yeah."
> "...Is the dining room a no?"
> "It's a no."

Maya didn't claim a home office deduction that year. The dining room was disqualified by the regulation. The bedroom desk was disqualified by inconsistency — sometimes the rental, sometimes Maya's freelance graphic design, sometimes Maya's husband paying bills.

That fall Maya converted the spare bedroom — previously a guest room used twice a year — into a dedicated rental-management office. Desk. File cabinet. Dual monitors. A door she could close. Four months later, on the next year's return, the home office deduction was real.

This chapter is about the regulation Maya didn't know — **§280A(c)(1)** — and the path from "I work from home" to a defensible deduction.

These are **deductions #39 and #40** of the 47.

---

## The framework — §280A(c)(1)

Section 280A is the home office regulation. The default rule under §280A(a) is that no deduction is allowed for a dwelling unit used by the taxpayer as a residence during the year. The home office deduction is an exception under §280A(c)(1), which requires the area in question to be:

1. **Used exclusively** for the trade or business
2. **Used regularly** for the trade or business
3. **Either**:
   - The principal place of the trade or business, OR
   - A place where the taxpayer meets clients or customers in the normal course of business, OR
   - A separate structure not attached to the dwelling

For STR hosts, the third prong's "principal place of business" route is the standard path. The "meet clients" route doesn't apply (guests check in at the property, not the host's home). The "separate structure" route applies only for hosts with a true detached office building — a converted backyard ADU used as a home office, for instance.

All three of the first three requirements must be met. **Exclusive. Regular. Principal place** (or the alternatives). Failing any one of the three disqualifies the deduction.

---

## The trade-or-business gate (read this before the rest of the chapter)

§280A(c)(1) opens with a phrase that sinks more home-office deductions than the exclusive-use trap ever will. The space must be used "in connection with the taxpayer's **trade or business**." Not "production of income." Not "investment activity." Trade or business under **§162**.

This is the same gate that decides whether §179 is on the table in Chapter 8. For STR hosts it produces three different answers depending on which Chapter 1 outcome you land in:

| Chapter 1 filing outcome | §280A(c)(1) home-office available? |
|---|---|
| Schedule C (substantial services) | **Yes** — by definition a §162 trade or business |
| Schedule E **non-passive** (Outcome B — 7-day rule + material participation) | **Defensible** — non-passive STRs generally rise to a §162 trade or business |
| Schedule E **passive** (Outcome A) | **Generally no** — the IRS classifies standard rental activity as §212 production-of-income, not §162 trade or business |

The Schedule E passive case is the one to be careful with. The IRS's published examination position is that ordinary rental activity is not a §162 trade or business — it is a §212 income-production activity. §280A(c)(1) does not extend to §212 activities. A host filing Schedule E passive who takes the home-office deduction is ripe for a clean disallowance on examination, even if the exclusive-use, regular-use, and principal-place-of-business prongs are otherwise immaculate.

Maya's situation in this chapter assumes she lands in the non-passive Schedule E bucket — three properties, materially participated, 7-day-rule average stay, hour log on file. The worked example below presupposes she has cleared the trade-or-business gate. **If you file Schedule E passive, do not lift the worked example. The deduction is not available to you under §280A(c)(1) on that filing posture, and the chapter's framework is from Maya's posture forward.**

When in doubt, this is one of the cleanest places to ask your CPA the §162 / §212 question explicitly. The home-office line is small enough that it isn't worth defending on a position-taking call.

## The exclusive-use trap

The most common failure point — and the one Maya hit — is **exclusive use**. The regulation is unforgiving: the area must be used *only* for the trade or business. Not "primarily." Not "mostly." Only.

What disqualifies a space:

- A dining table that doubles as a workspace and a meal location → fails
- A bedroom desk used for both rental management and personal email → fails
- A guest room that's a workspace 11 months and a guest bedroom 1 month → arguably fails (the IRS has accepted "incidental" personal use in narrow cases, but this is a fact-and-circumstances determination — don't rely on it)
- A garage that's part workshop, part rental supply storage → fails (mixed use)
- A laptop on the kitchen counter → fails (the kitchen is not exclusively used)

What qualifies:

- A spare room with a closeable door, used only for rental work → qualifies
- A dedicated section of a basement, physically separated from family living areas, used only for rental work → qualifies (the regulation allows for portions of rooms, but the portion must be clearly delineated)
- A converted detached structure (shed, ADU, garage corner with a permanent partition) used only for rental work → qualifies

The IRS does not require a dedicated room. It requires a dedicated *area*. A clearly-delineated corner of a larger room — partitioned with shelving or a screen, used for nothing else — qualifies. But the cleaner the delineation, the easier the audit defense.

---

## The regular-use requirement

"Regular use" means the area is used on a continuing basis — not occasional or sporadic. There is no minimum-hours threshold in the regulation, but the practical test is: would a reasonable person observing the space conclude that it is in active, ongoing use for the business?

For STR hosts who manage their portfolios actively — guest messaging, cleaner coordination, reservation review, marketing, bookkeeping — regular use is straightforwardly met. A few hours a week, every week, is regular use.

What fails regular use:

- A room used twice a year during tax season → fails
- A space the host plans to use but hasn't yet → fails
- Space used only during the host's annual portfolio review → likely fails

For most actively-managed STR portfolios, regular use is the easiest of the three requirements. Hosts who fail it are typically in a different setup category — passive Schedule E filers with a property manager handling everything, where the host's own active hours are minimal.

## The principal-place-of-business prong

For STR hosts, the home office is "the principal place of business" if **the host conducts the administrative or management activities of the trade or business there** AND **there is no other fixed location where the host conducts substantial administrative or management activities.**

This is the "soft principal place" rule established in §280A(c)(1) (last sentence) — added by Congress in the Taxpayer Relief Act of 1997 (effective for tax years beginning after 12/31/1998) to fix the Supreme Court's decision in **Commissioner v. Soliman, 506 U.S. 168 (1993)**, which had narrowed the home office deduction unreasonably. For STR hosts, the rule is generous. Administrative and management activities — guest communication, cleaner coordination, bookkeeping, reservation review, pricing decisions — are exactly what the home office handles. The property itself is the *operating* location, not the *administrative* location. Most hosts who do their books at home meet this test.

The trap: if you also rent commercial office space for your rental administration, the principal-place-of-business prong may shift to the commercial space. The home office becomes secondary and may be disqualified. Most STR hosts don't have this complication.

## Deduction #39 — The regular method

The regular method allocates **actual home expenses** to the business based on the **business-use percentage** of the home.

**Step 1 — Compute business-use percentage.** Two common methods:

- **Square-footage method**: business area square feet ÷ total home square feet. A 200 sq ft office in a 2,000 sq ft home is 10%.
- **Number-of-rooms method**: business rooms ÷ total rooms (excluding bathrooms, hallways, closets). A 1-room office in an 8-room house is 12.5%. Generally weaker than square-footage; use only when rooms are roughly equal in size.

**Step 2 — Apply the percentage to actual home expenses.** Categories that qualify:

- Mortgage interest (or rent)
- Real estate taxes
- Homeowner's insurance
- Utilities (electric, gas, water — *only if not separately metered for the office*)
- Depreciation (for owned homes — straight-line over 39 years, not 27.5)
- Repairs and maintenance directly attributable to the office area, plus the percentage-share of whole-home repairs

**Step 3 — Apply the §280A(c)(5) income limit.** The home office deduction cannot create a net loss for the home-office-related activity. Excess deductions carry forward indefinitely under §280A(c)(5) and apply against future home-office-related income.

A worked example: Maya's converted spare bedroom is 240 sq ft in a 2,400 sq ft home — 10% business use. Her annual home expenses:

| Category | Annual cost | Office share (10%) |
|---|---|---|
| Mortgage interest | $14,200 | $1,420 |
| Property taxes | $4,800 | $480 |
| Insurance | $1,800 | $180 |
| Utilities | $4,400 | $440 |
| Repairs | $1,200 | $120 |
| Depreciation (39-year, $400K basis × 80% improvement allocation × 10% × ~2.56% = ~$820) | $8,200 | $820 |
| **Total regular-method deduction** | | **$3,460** |

The depreciation portion creates the largest potential complication on eventual sale of the home — recapture applies to the depreciation taken on the home office portion. For most hosts, this is a manageable trade.

---

## Deduction #40 — The simplified method

Introduced in 2013 to reduce the recordkeeping burden. The simplified method is:

```
Deduction  =  $5  ×  business-use square feet  (capped at 300 sq ft = $1,500/year)
```

Maya's 240 sq ft office under the simplified method: 240 × $5 = **$1,200/year.**

The simplified method advantages:

- No depreciation tracking (and no recapture on eventual home sale)
- No allocation calculation
- No actual-expenses recordkeeping
- One line on Form 8829 (or directly on Schedule E for some configurations)

The disadvantages:

- Capped at $1,500/year regardless of actual expenses
- No carryforward of unused deduction (the §280A(c)(5) income limit just truncates rather than carrying)
- For larger or more expensive home offices, leaves money on the table

The election is annual. A host can use the simplified method one year, switch to the regular method the next, and switch back. There is no permanent commitment.

**Decision rule for choosing methods:**

- If your business-use square footage exceeds 300, or your actual home expenses × business % exceeds $1,500, use the **regular method**.
- If you're under both thresholds and want to skip the depreciation tracking, use the **simplified method**.

For Maya at 240 sq ft and $3,460 of regular-method deduction, the regular method is clearly the move — it's nearly 3× the simplified deduction and well within the §280A income limit.

## The home-sale recapture issue (regular method only)

When the home is eventually sold, the depreciation taken under the regular method's home office deduction is subject to **§1250 unrecaptured gain treatment** — taxed at up to 25% on sale, regardless of overall capital gains rate. The §121 primary-residence exclusion ($250K single / $500K joint) does **not** shelter post-May-6-1997 home-office depreciation: depreciation taken on the home-office portion is excluded from §121 protection and recaptures separately. This is the regulation that makes the recapture math actually bite, and it's why the regular-method/simplified-method choice matters more for hosts who plan to use §121 on eventual sale.

For Maya, claiming $820/year of home office depreciation for, say, ten years before selling, she would have ~$8,200 of unrecaptured §1250 gain to report on sale. At a 24% rate, that's roughly $1,968 of tax cost on sale. Spread across ten years of ~$820 deductions at her marginal bracket (24% × $820 = ~$197/year of tax savings), the lifetime savings are roughly $1,970 — almost exactly equal to the recapture cost.

The math comes out roughly neutral over a decade. For shorter holds, the deduction is a clear win (the time value of money favors the early deduction). For longer holds, it's still net-positive in nominal dollars but less dramatic.

The simplified method avoids this entirely — no depreciation taken means no depreciation to recapture.

For hosts who plan to sell their home within five years and want to dodge the recapture math, the simplified method is the safer choice. For hosts who plan to stay in the home long-term, the regular method's larger deduction wins.

## Common mistakes

**Claiming exclusive use when the space is mixed.** The Maya mistake. Don't claim a home office for a dining room, a guest room used as a guest room, or a kitchen counter laptop spot. The IRS examines this category aggressively.

**Claiming square footage for the entire home when only a portion qualifies.** The percentage applies to the business-use area, not the whole home. A host with a 2,400 sq ft home and a 240 sq ft office claims 10% — not 100%.

**Forgetting to take the home office deduction at all.** The most common mistake by far. Hosts who genuinely qualify (regular use of an exclusive area as the principal place of business) but never claim because they think it's "an audit flag." The audit-flag concern is largely outdated — the simplified method has reduced IRS scrutiny because the recordkeeping is minimal. If you qualify, claim.

**Mixing the home office with the rental property's separate office.** If you have a dedicated office at one of your rental properties (rare for STRs, but it happens for hosts running operations from a converted unit), that's a different deduction — it goes on Schedule E directly as a rental expense, not on Form 8829 as a home office.

**Confusing the §280A home office deduction with the §280A "Augusta Rule" 14-day deduction.** Two different sections of the same regulation. The home office is §280A(c)(1). The Augusta Rule is §280A(g). They don't interact directly. Augusta is covered in Chapter 15.
