# Chapter 15 — The Augusta Rule and the 14-Day Personal-Use Line

*Two rules. Same regulation section. Opposite outcomes.*

---

Caleb owned four cabins in the Tennessee mountains. In late summer he finished the renovation on the newest one — a four-bedroom on a ridge — and decided to host an extended-family reunion there before listing it. Eighteen people. Fifteen days. The cabin was ready, the family was excited, the listing could wait one more month.

The cabin went live on Airbnb in mid-September. By December 31 it had been rented for 36 nights at strong off-season rates. Caleb's bookkeeper put the property's expenses on Schedule E along with everything else.

In January, Caleb's CPA looked at the depreciation schedule, the operating expenses, and the timeline of personal use, and stopped at the personal-use figure.

> "Eighteen days?"
> "Family reunion. Just one weekend."
> "It was fifteen actual days."
> "Plus three for setup and cleanup. Why?"
> "That's over the 14-day line. This property's now treated as a residence."

The cabin's $11,400 of year-one depreciation was suspended. The operating losses for the year — about $7,200 of expenses over rental income, given that the property only ran half a season — became deductible only against rental income from the same property. There were no losses to deduct because the income wasn't there yet.

Caleb's mistake wasn't using the cabin. His mistake was using it for **18 days** when the line is **14**.

This chapter is about that line — and about its sibling regulation, the **Augusta Rule**, which lives in the same statute (§280A) and produces the opposite outcome for hosts who structure things correctly. Two rules. Same regulation section. Opposite consequences.

This is **deduction #47** of the 47 — the final entry on the master list and one of the more nuanced rules in this book.

---

## The framework — §280A in two halves

Section 280A is the home-and-vacation-property regulation. The default rule under §280A(a) is that no deduction is allowed for a dwelling unit used by the taxpayer as a residence during the year. The full statute then carves out exceptions, and for STR hosts two of those exceptions matter:

- **§280A(c)(1)** — the home office deduction (Chapter 12)
- **§280A(g)** — the Augusta Rule
- **§280A(d)** — the 14-day personal-use line that flips a rental into a residence
- **§280A(e)** — the rental-vs-personal expense allocation that follows from §280A(d)

This chapter handles the second pair — Augusta and the 14-day line.

---

## The 14-day personal-use line — §280A(d)

A property is treated as a "residence" of the taxpayer — and therefore subject to the deduction-limiting rules of §280A(c)(5) — if **either** of the following is met for the year:

1. **Personal use exceeds 14 days**, OR
2. **Personal use exceeds 10% of the days the unit was rented at fair rental value**

Whichever produces the larger number is the threshold the host must stay below to keep the property classified as a pure rental.

Once a property crosses into "residence" status, three things happen:

1. **Deductions are limited to rental income.** The property cannot generate a Schedule E loss. Excess deductions carry forward indefinitely under §280A(c)(5) and apply against future rental income from the same property.
2. **Expenses must be allocated** between rental and personal use under §280A(e). Rental-period expenses are deductible (subject to the income limit); personal-period expenses are not.
3. **The §469 passive-activity rules still apply on top** — and so does the **7-day-rule framework from Chapter 1** (Treas. Reg. §1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A) plus the §1.469-5T(a) material-participation tests). If the property is also non-passive under that framework, the dual treatment can be complicated. (Note: §469(c)(7) is the separate real-estate-professional exception, which uses different tests; Chapter 1's STR loophole hangs on the 7-day rule, not REPS.)

For Caleb, the cabin's 18 days of personal use put it over the 14-day line. The 10% test didn't help him — 10% of 36 rental days is 3.6 days, far below 14, so the 14-day floor governed. The property became a "residence," its $11,400 of depreciation was capped at rental income (which was thin in a half-year of operation), and the loss was suspended.

### What counts as "personal use"

Under §280A(d)(2), personal use includes:

- Any day the property is used by the taxpayer or any member of the taxpayer's family
- Any day the property is used by anyone under a reciprocal-use arrangement
- Any day the property is rented to someone at *less than fair rental value*
- Any day the property is used by a co-owner

What does **not** count as personal use:

- Days spent on **repairs, maintenance, or property improvement**, even if the host stays overnight, *as long as the host engages in repair and maintenance on a **substantially full-time basis** for that day and the host substantially documents this*. (§280A(d)(2) requires substantially full-time work, not merely a "principal purpose" of repair — a half-day of patching followed by an afternoon at the lake doesn't qualify, even if family members happen to be there.)
- Days the property is donated to charity for use (subject to other rules)

The repair-and-maintenance carveout under §280A(d)(2) is real and useful — but it requires substantiation. Caleb's "three days for setup and cleanup" might have qualified for the carveout if (a) the principal purpose was genuine repair/maintenance and (b) it was documented. But Caleb mixed the setup with family-reunion enjoyment, and on audit the days would all be reclassified as personal use. The carveout is for genuine work trips, not for travel that happened to start and end with maintenance tasks.

### The renting-to-yourself trap

A common host attempt to dodge the 14-day rule is to "rent" the property to oneself or a family member at fair-market rate during the personal-use period. This does not work. §280A(d)(2)(C) explicitly treats any rental at less than fair-market value as personal use, AND treats rentals to family members at any price as personal use unless the family member uses it as their principal residence.

If your sister stays at your cabin for a week and pays you full nightly rate, that week is still personal use under §280A(d)(2). The regulation closes the loophole.

## The Augusta Rule — §280A(g)

The companion regulation, and the one that pays the host instead of penalizing them.

Under §280A(g), a host who rents a dwelling unit for **fewer than 15 days during the year** can **exclude the rental income from gross income entirely.** No tax. The corresponding rental expenses are not deductible (except for items deductible regardless, like mortgage interest and property taxes), but the trade is favorable: tax-free income in exchange for forfeiting a small expense deduction.

The rule's name comes from Augusta, Georgia, where homeowners traditionally rent their houses to spectators during the Masters Tournament for a week each year. Congress carved out the rule so those homeowners wouldn't owe tax on a week of exceptional rental income.

For STR hosts, the Augusta Rule produces a specific strategic move when the host has both a primary residence and an LLC operating the rentals:

1. **Host owns a primary residence** (separate from the rental properties)
2. **Host operates rentals through an LLC**
3. **LLC needs occasional space** for legitimate business activities — annual planning meeting, board retreat, training event, owner offsite
4. **Host rents the primary residence to the LLC** for up to 14 days during the year, at fair market rate

The result:

- **The host receives rent personally — tax-free under §280A(g).**
- **The LLC pays rent — deductible under §162 as an ordinary business expense.**
- **Net effect:** income is shifted from the LLC to the host without personal tax liability. The host's overall tax cost on the shifted income drops to zero (Augusta-side) plus the LLC's deduction (savings at the LLC's marginal rate).

For a host whose LLC saves taxes at, say, 24% marginal rate, a $4,000 fair-rent payment for 8 days of legitimate business use shifts $4,000 from the LLC to the host while reducing total tax by **$960** (the LLC's deduction at 24%). The $4,000 to the host is tax-free. Net household tax savings: $960 in pure timing/character arbitrage.

### What the Augusta Rule requires

The IRS does not bless this strategy unconditionally. Three substantiation requirements:

1. **Genuine business purpose.** The LLC must have a real reason to rent the space. "Annual operations review with vendors and contractors," "team strategic planning offsite," "training session for property managers" — all legitimate. "Family BBQ that the LLC paid for" — not legitimate.

2. **Fair market rent.** The rate the LLC pays must be defensible against comparable local commercial rates for similar space-and-duration use. Document the rate-finding methodology — pull comparable Airbnb business-meeting-space rates, pull commercial event-venue rates, average and document. A $2,000/day rate for a primary residence is defensible if local conference centers charge $2,500/day and Airbnb event spaces in the area charge $1,500/day. A $5,000/day rate for the same property is not.

3. **Written rental agreement.** A short rental agreement between the host (as individual landlord) and the LLC (as commercial tenant), specifying the dates, the daily rate, the business purpose. Signed by both parties. Filed with the LLC's books.

4. **Stay under 15 days.** The rule is "fewer than 15." 14 days qualifies; 15 days does not.

### What the Augusta Rule does NOT cover

A common confusion: the Augusta Rule allows tax-free rental income from the host's *own dwelling unit.* It does **not** apply to rental of the host's STR properties to anyone, including the LLC. The STR properties are already rental property; renting them to the LLC produces ordinary rental income subject to the regular Schedule E (or Schedule C) rules.

The Augusta Rule is specifically about renting the *host's primary residence or vacation home* (a non-rental dwelling) to a business that has a legitimate need for the space. It's a primary-residence move, not an STR-portfolio move.

## How Caleb fixed his year going forward

The cabin was already a residence under §280A(d) for the year of the family reunion. Caleb's options for the suspended losses were:

- **Hold the property and rent it more aggressively in subsequent years.** The carryforward of suspended deductions under §280A(c)(5) applies indefinitely. As long as the property stays in the rental activity, future rental income will eventually absorb the carryforward — potentially even sheltering future-year income above the basis.
- **Keep personal use at 14 days or fewer in subsequent years.** Once the property's annual personal use is back under 14 days (and under 10% of rental days), the property re-converts to pure rental status. Future-year deductions are no longer income-limited.

Caleb adopted both. The next year his personal use of the cabin was zero (he stayed at his primary residence and took family vacations elsewhere). Rental days hit 280. The property generated full Schedule E losses, and the prior year's suspended losses started absorbing against rental income.

He also implemented the Augusta Rule on his primary residence the same year. His LLC held an annual operations review for 6 days at his primary residence at a documented fair rate of $1,800/day — total payment $10,800. The LLC took a $10,800 §162 deduction. Caleb received $10,800 tax-free. Documented business purpose, written rental agreement, comparable-rate analysis on file.

Two regulations. One bad outcome the prior year, one good outcome the current year. Same statute. Opposite ends.

## Common mistakes

**Counting setup and cleanup days as repair days.** The repair-and-maintenance carveout requires the principal purpose of the day to be repair or maintenance. Travel days that combine personal enjoyment with maintenance tasks are personal use days. The carveout is narrow.

**Renting at "fair market rate" to family.** §280A(d)(2)(C) treats family rentals at any price as personal use. The family-discount-pretending-to-be-arms-length game does not work.

**Thinking the Augusta Rule applies to STR properties.** It applies to the host's *non-rental* dwelling — typically the primary residence. STR properties are already rental property and don't qualify for §280A(g) treatment.

**Skipping the substantiation on the Augusta Rule.** The strategy is legitimate but only if documented. No written agreement, no comparable-rate analysis, no business purpose memo means the IRS can recharacterize the payment as a non-deductible distribution from the LLC, undoing the entire benefit.

**Overpaying yourself under the Augusta Rule.** Setting a daily rate well above local commercial comparables flags the strategy as a tax-avoidance device. Stay defensible. The IRS has examined Augusta Rule arrangements in recent years, and the cases that hold up are the ones with disciplined documentation and reasonable rates.

**Counting the host's repairs to the property as personal use.** The carveout at §280A(d)(2) explicitly excludes repair days. Don't accidentally count yourself as personal-use for days you were genuinely working at the property — but document the work, since the IRS may ask.

**Confusing §280A(g) with §1031 like-kind exchange or with §121 home-sale exclusion.** Three different statutes, three different mechanics. Don't mix them.

## A note on chapter scope

This chapter handles **two of §280A's deduction edges.** The home office deduction lives in Chapter 12. The §1.469-1T 7-day rule that determines whether a rental escapes default rental treatment lives in Chapter 1.

For STR hosts, §280A is sometimes the most-cited regulation in the book — it shows up in Ch 1 (the framing through §469's interaction), Ch 12 (home office), and this chapter (Augusta + 14-day). All three chapters cite the same statute for different purposes.
