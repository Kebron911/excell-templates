## Section 3 — Foreword: Why this book is short

*Recto opener after copyright. Cormorant Garamond display headline, Inter or serif body. Drop-cap "M" in Muted Gold opens the foreword.*

### Why this book is short

Most tax guides for landlords are four hundred pages long and useless. They march through the entire Internal Revenue Code, hedge every position, and surface the actually-useful deductions for short-term rental hosts at maybe a twelve percent density. The reader puts the book down unsure of what to do next.

We did not write that book.

This one is seventeen chapters and roughly a hundred and sixty pages. Every chapter teaches between one and ten specific deductions a short-term rental host can claim, organized by where the deduction lives in the tax code. Every chapter cites the regulation behind it. Every chapter ends with the workbook that captures it. We left out everything that did not directly produce a deduction or directly enable one.

The forty-seven deductions in this book are not random. They are the deductions we have watched our own portfolios use, that our clients' portfolios use, and that our CPAs ask us about every January. They are not exotic. The exotic ones — cost segregation, the Augusta Rule, §168(k) bonus depreciation — are in here, but they sit alongside cleaning fees and utilities and trash service. The big numbers are real. The small numbers, summed across a portfolio across a decade, are bigger.

We wrote this book for a specific kind of reader. You run between one and ten properties. You treat the portfolio as a real business. You have asked your CPA, at some point, "did we get all the deductions?" — and you weren't entirely satisfied with the answer. You may already file Schedule E, or you may be one of the hosts filing Schedule C who shouldn't be (Chapter 1 will tell you which). You almost certainly use a workbook of some kind, and you almost certainly want a better one.

If that's you: this book is the field guide. The forty-seven deductions are the checklist. The system in Chapter 16 is the workflow that runs the checklist in twenty-one minutes a quarter. The CPA handoff in Chapter 17 is what you send your tax preparer in February so the year's filing takes weeks, not months.

A note on what this book is not. It is not a substitute for a tax professional. We cite IRS code calmly because the regulations are knowable, but we are not standing in for the CPA who knows your specific facts and your specific state. Every chapter ends with a disclaimer on this point because the disclaimer is genuine — every position in this book deserves a thirty-second sanity check from someone who has reviewed your return.

It is also not a tax-season-only book. The deductions in this book are operational — they live in your bookkeeping discipline twelve months a year, not in your stress in March. The hosts who recover the most every April are the hosts who logged contemporaneously every Sunday of the prior calendar year. Run the system year-round and tax season is, as one of our hosts puts it, *seven minutes per property.*

We have heard people describe forty-seven deductions as a lot. It is not. It is the standard. The hosts in this book — composites of real people whose stories shape every chapter — recovered tens of thousands of dollars each, in some cases over six figures, because they ran the list. The list is generous. The book is short. Read it once. Then run it.

— *The STR Ledger*

## Section 4 — How to use this book

*Recto, follows the foreword. Editorial-finance tone, practical. Cormorant Medium 32 pt headline + Inter 400 body.*

### How to use this book

**Read in any order after Part I.** Chapters 1, 2, and 3 are the frame: filing-form decision, what "ordinary and necessary" means, and the recordkeeping standard that everything else relies on. Read those three first; they take maybe thirty minutes. After that, the deduction chapters are independent — you can jump to Chapter 7 (cost segregation) or Chapter 9 (mileage) or Chapter 15 (the Augusta Rule) in any sequence and the references will work both ways.

**Every chapter ends with a *Capture* sidebar.** The sidebar names a specific workbook — Schedule E Workbook, Mileage Log, Cost Seg DIY Workbook — and the URL where it lives. The workbooks are the operational layer this book points at. You can read the book without the workbooks; you cannot run the system without them. Each sidebar URL is a tracked redirect — if you scan or type one, you will land on the relevant product page directly.

**Claim the reader bonus once.** At the back of this book is a QR code and a URL: `thestrledger.com/47-book`. That page delivers a free printable PDF and Excel checklist that maps every deduction in this book to the specific Schedule E line it belongs on, plus the workbook tab that captures it. The bonus is free for readers and exists once — claim it whenever, but you only need to do it once for the entire book.

**IRS code citations are calm.** When this book cites §469 or §168(k) or §1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A), the citation is for substantiation in case you want to verify or your CPA wants to look it up. You can skim past every code citation and still run the system. The citations are there because the regulations are knowable; you do not need to memorize them.

**Use this as a year-round reference, not a tax-season manual.** The deductions live in your bookkeeping discipline every quarter. The annotations and *Capture* sidebars are where you'll return: in March when you're prepping the CPA handoff, in October when you're commissioning a new property, in June when you're reviewing the Q2 numbers. Mark the chapters relevant to your current month; come back to the others when your portfolio reaches them.

**One page worth memorizing.** Turn to The Recordkeeping Pact on the next page. The three rules on that page underwrite every deduction in this book. If you read nothing else, read those three rules.

## Section 5 — The Recordkeeping Pact

*Standalone page, designed for visual prominence. Parchment background, Harbor Navy frame, Cormorant Medium 56 pt headline at top, Cormorant Italic subhead, Muted Gold rule above each rule, Inter 400 18 pt body. Generous whitespace.*

### The Recordkeeping Pact

*If you remember nothing else from this book, remember these three rules.*

———

#### One. Log it as you go.

Every expense, every trip, every contractor payment, captured within the week — not the month, not the quarter, and not in March of next year. Contemporaneous logs survive examination. Reconstructions don't. Ninety minutes a week is the cost of compliance. Fifteen to thirty hours of March panic is the cost of skipping it.

———

#### Two. Keep the supporting documents.

Receipts for every expense over $75. All §274(d) categories — travel, meals, vehicle expenses, lodging — regardless of amount. W-9s for every contractor before the first payment. The closing statement, the depreciation schedule, the prior-year return. Sufficient, not elegant. Photograph receipts within forty-eight hours; store them in a folder you can find.

———

#### Three. Back it up.

One workbook. One cloud copy. One local copy. Monthly cadence. The day the workbook is corrupted is not the day to discover you have no backup.

———

> If any of these three feels onerous, you are running a portfolio without books. Fix that before you read another chapter. The deductions in this book recover real money for hosts who can document them. The hosts who can't, can't.

*The STR Ledger.*
