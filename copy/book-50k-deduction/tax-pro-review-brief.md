A 17-chapter book for short-term rental hosts about 47 federal-tax deductions that apply to their portfolios. The book ships as a $9.99 paperback and $2.99 Kindle, sold under the brand The STR Ledger. It will reach an audience of Schedule E and Schedule C STR filers nationwide.

## What I'm asking you to do

Read the manuscript with one question in mind: **Is anything in here factually wrong, misleading, out-of-date, or likely to mislead a reasonable host who reads it?**

I'm not asking you to write the book or rewrite the prose. The voice is locked. The cold-open characters, dollar figures, and editorial choices are mine. I'm asking you to flag tax statements that are wrong or that need a year-current update.

There are three tiers of flag:

- **🔴 Must fix before print** — A statement is incorrect, misleading, or no longer current under post-publication law. The book cannot ship until this is addressed.
- **🟡 Worth fixing** — A statement is technically correct but loose, ambiguous, or could be tightened. Useful but not blocking.
- **🟢 Optional polish** — A nuance, a missing nuance, or a "you could also mention" suggestion. Ignore if you're tight on time.

Use the tier labels in your feedback so I can triage at a glance.

---

## What I'm NOT asking you to do

- **Don't rewrite for voice.** The voice is The STR Ledger's house style: first-person plural ("we"), specific dollar figures, calm authority on tax, plain English. If a sentence sounds different from how you'd write it, that's probably intentional. Flag *factual* issues only.
- **Don't expand scope.** If a chapter doesn't mention a deduction you would have included, that's an editorial choice. The book is intentionally 47 deductions, not 60. Don't suggest additions unless the omission creates a misleading framework.
- **Don't fix; flag.** Mark the line, note the issue in 1–3 sentences, and move on. I'll update the manuscript based on your flags.
- **Don't re-verify the cold-open numbers.** The character stories (Megan's $50K, Amanda's $8,427, etc.) are illustrative composites and disclosed as such on the copyright page.

## What "good enough" means

The book is a non-professional tax guide. Every chapter ends with a "this is general information, not tax advice" disclaimer. Hosts who read it are expected to consult their own CPA before filing positions. Your review is not a substitute for that — but it is the floor.

The bar I'm holding the manuscript to:

- **No statement that a competent CPA reading the book would call wrong.** If you would correct a host who said it back to you, flag the statement.
- **No statement that an examining IRS agent would call indefensible.** If a position would draw a question on examination *because of how the book describes it,* flag it.
- **No safe-harbor amount, dollar limit, or rate that is out of date** as of the publication month.

Anything below that is acceptable looseness for a popular-press book. The reader will consult their CPA; we just have to set them up to ask the right questions.
