# Chapter 5 — Repairs vs. Improvements

*The line that changes the year.*

---

Marcus replaced the back deck on his cabin in mid-September. The old deck — pressure-treated pine, original to the build, twelve years in — had sagged enough at one corner that a guest left a one-star review with a photo. The contractor's invoice came in at **$14,200.** Marcus's bookkeeper, working from the invoice, dropped the line into "Repairs and Maintenance" on Schedule E.

His CPA, reviewing the books two months later, pulled it back out.

> "You replaced the entire deck."
> "Yes."
> "This isn't a repair."
> "What is it?"
> "It's a betterment. We're capitalizing it."

The deduction wasn't lost. It was rescheduled. Instead of $14,200 on his current-year Schedule E, Marcus would take roughly $930 a year for the next 15 years, depreciating the deck as a land improvement. The total deduction was the same. The *timing* was different by about $13,000 of current-year cash flow.

This chapter is about that line — the line between a *repair* and an *improvement.* The line is governed by Treasury Regulation **§1.263(a)-3.** Most CPAs draw the line consistently. Most hosts don't. And most STR maintenance falls cleanly on one side of it once you know the test.

---

## The framework — the BAR test

Under §1.263(a)-3, an expenditure on tangible property must be capitalized — and depreciated over the relevant recovery period — if it produces a **betterment, adaptation, or restoration** of the property. The acronym tax preparers use is **BAR**, and any one of the three triggers capitalization.

**Betterment.** The expenditure ameliorates a material condition or defect that existed before the property was acquired or arose during production, *or* it materially adds to the property, *or* it materially increases the property's capacity, productivity, efficiency, strength, quality, or output.

**Adaptation.** The expenditure adapts the property to a new or different use that is not consistent with the property's original intended use.

**Restoration.** The expenditure (a) restores damage from a casualty for which a basis adjustment was made, (b) returns the property to ordinary operating condition after deterioration to the point of disrepair, (c) results in the rebuilding of a component to a like-new condition, or (d) replaces a major component or substantial structural part of the property.

If a single expenditure passes any of the three tests, capitalize. If it passes none of them, the expenditure is a deductible repair under §162.

Marcus's deck failed restoration test (d): a complete deck replacement is the rebuilding of a major component. The fact that it was the same material, same dimensions, same finish was not the test. The test was whether a major component had been replaced. It had. Capitalize.

A different expenditure on the same deck — say, replacing four rotted boards and re-staining — would have been a repair. Same deck. Same surface. Different scope. Different result.

## Three safe harbors that rescue ordinary repairs

The §263(a)-3 regulations include three safe harbors that explicitly allow expense treatment for expenditures that would otherwise raise BAR-test questions. All three can be elected on a timely-filed return; once elected, they apply automatically to qualifying expenditures.

### Safe harbor #1 — De minimis (§1.263(a)-1(f))

Tangible property under **$2,500 per item or per invoice** can be expensed in the year of purchase, regardless of useful life, if the host elects the safe harbor.

This is the most important and most underused election available to STR hosts. Once elected:

- A $1,800 microwave: expense.
- A $2,300 patio furniture set: expense.
- A $2,600 patio furniture set: capitalize. (The cap is per item, not aggregate.)

The election is made by attaching a written statement to a timely-filed (including extensions) original return. Once made, it applies for the year and must be re-elected each year.

Most STR purchases below $2,500 per item — the vast majority of furniture, appliances, electronics, and decor — qualify. Make the election every year. The administrative cost is one paragraph on a return.

### Safe harbor #2 — Routine maintenance (§1.263(a)-3(i))

Expenditures for the **recurring activities** that the host expects to perform to keep the property in its ordinary efficient operating condition can be expensed, even if they would otherwise be considered a betterment, adaptation, or restoration.

The qualifying conditions:

- The maintenance is performed because of the property's use
- The maintenance is reasonably expected to be performed *more than once* during the property's class life (for residential rental, more than once over 27.5 years)
- The maintenance keeps the property in *ordinary efficient operating condition*

Examples that qualify:

- Repainting the interior every five years
- Recoating the deck (not replacing it) every three years
- Replacing HVAC filters quarterly
- Re-grouting tile every seven years
- Repaving the driveway as routine refresh

Examples that don't:

- A first-ever repainting after twenty years (not "more than once" in the property's life)
- An HVAC system replacement (major component)
- A roof replacement (major component, restoration test (d))

This safe harbor is what allows hosts to expense the $5,000 every-five-years interior repaint without depreciating it over 27.5 years. Use it when the work is genuinely recurring. Do not use it for one-time major work and call it "routine."

### Safe harbor #3 — Small taxpayer (§1.263(a)-3(h))

For buildings with an unadjusted basis of **$1 million or less**, a *qualifying small taxpayer* can elect to expense — rather than capitalize — improvements up to the lesser of:

- $10,000 per building per year, or
- 2% of the unadjusted basis of the building

**Eligibility footnote:** Under §1.263(a)-3(h)(4), the host must qualify as a small taxpayer — average annual gross receipts of $10 million or less over the three preceding tax years. For solo and small-portfolio STR hosts, this test is automatic; the gating constraint is the $1 million per-building basis cap, not the gross receipts cap.

This is the safe harbor that rescues mid-size renovations on smaller properties. A $300,000 cabin (unadjusted basis: $300,000) qualifies for $6,000 of expense-treatment improvements per year (2% of basis). A $700,000 cabin qualifies for $10,000 per year (the dollar cap). A $1.2 million property fails the eligibility threshold and must capitalize all improvements.

The election is per-building, per-year. It does not apply automatically; it must be elected on a timely return.

For hosts with multiple smaller properties, this safe harbor can absorb tens of thousands of dollars of improvements as current-year deductions instead of multi-decade capitalization. Use it.

## Marcus's deck under each safe harbor

Marcus's $14,200 deck replacement is a useful test of all three safe harbors.

| Safe harbor | Applies? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **De minimis (§1.263(a)-1(f))** | No | $14,200 is a single invoice line; the cap is $2,500 |
| **Routine maintenance (§1.263(a)-3(i))** | No | Replacing the entire deck is not maintenance the host expected to perform multiple times in 27.5 years; it's a major-component replacement |
| **Small taxpayer (§1.263(a)-3(h))** | Maybe | If Marcus's cabin's unadjusted basis is ≤ $500,000 and the $14,200 fits inside 2% of basis ($10,000 max cap), the lesser of the two limits ($10,000) could absorb $10,000 — but $4,200 still capitalizes |

If Marcus's basis is $400,000, the small-taxpayer safe harbor allows him to expense $8,000 (2% of $400,000), with the remaining $6,200 capitalized as a 15-year land improvement. That changes the year-one deduction from $930 to roughly $8,000 + (6,200 ÷ 15 ≈ $413) = **$8,413** — almost ten times the no-election outcome.

Most hosts whose cabins fall under $500K basis miss this. The election is one paragraph on the return. It's worth filing the paragraph.

## The partial asset disposition election

When a major component is replaced — a roof, an HVAC system, a deck — the regulations allow the host to **dispose of the old component's remaining basis** in the year of replacement. This is the *partial asset disposition* election under §1.168(i)-8(d).

What it does: Marcus's old deck, twelve years into a 15-year land improvement schedule, had remaining undepreciated basis of roughly $135 (the original deck cost $2,000 on the 15-year MACRS table, which uses 150% declining balance with switch to straight-line — by year 12 the asset is mostly written down). When Marcus replaces it, the partial asset disposition election lets him deduct that remaining basis as a loss in the year of disposal — on top of starting fresh depreciation on the new deck.

The election is overlooked by virtually every general-practice CPA. The amounts on land improvements are often small once you run the actual MACRS schedule. They get materially larger on building components — a roof or HVAC replacement on a 27.5- or 39-year asset that was placed in service eight or ten years ago can leave thousands of dollars of remaining basis ready to be written off in the year of disposal. They're also cumulative across a portfolio. A host replacing four roofs, two HVAC systems, and a deck across a portfolio over five years is leaving $5,000–$15,000 of partial asset disposition deductions on the table if no one elects.

The election must be made in the year of replacement. It's not retroactively recoverable on amendment.

## The casualty exception

If the property suffers damage from a casualty event — fire, storm, flood, vandalism — and the host claimed a casualty loss adjustment to basis, then the subsequent restoration is automatically capitalized under §1.263(a)-3(k)(1)(i). This is restoration test (a) and it overrides every safe harbor.

Insurance proceeds and the casualty loss math are their own subject and not in this book. The takeaway: post-casualty repair is always capital. Don't try to expense it.

## Common mistakes

**Calling everything "repairs" out of habit.** This is the Marcus mistake at scale. Many bookkeepers default to "repairs and maintenance" because the line is broad. Read every invoice over $1,000 against the BAR test before posting.

**Failing to elect the safe harbors.** All three elections are explicit and must be filed annually. Default treatment without an election is full capitalization — the worst outcome for hosts who could have elected and didn't.

**Capitalizing minor purchases reflexively.** Hosts who buy a $400 microwave and depreciate it over five years are leaving §1.263(a)-1(f) money on the table. Anything under $2,500 per invoice line, with the de minimis election in place, is a current-year expense. Use it.

**Forgetting partial asset disposition.** When a roof, HVAC, or major fixture is replaced, the old component's remaining basis can be written off. Most CPAs don't ask. Most hosts don't know to.

**Mixing repair-and-replace invoices.** A contractor invoice that includes "repair sagging joists ($800), replace deck boards ($11,000), repaint trim ($300)" is *three* line items. The repair and the repaint are current expense; the deck boards capitalize. Itemize at capture, not at year-end.
