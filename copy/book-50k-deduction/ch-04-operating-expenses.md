# Chapter 4 — Operating Expenses

*The recurring ten.*

---

Reagan ran one property — a four-bedroom on the Oregon coast, full-time job otherwise. For three years she had assumed her STR was "barely profitable." She knew her revenue (Airbnb sent the 1099-K every January). She knew her mortgage. She knew her insurance. She had a rough sense of cleaning fees and supplies. Beyond that, the books were vibes.

When she finally sat down to reconcile, the recurring operating expenses on the property — the line items she had been mostly guessing at — totaled **$19,400** for the year. Net result: the property had been running a roughly $7,000 annual *loss* for tax purposes that she had been reporting as a roughly $12,000 *gain.* Three years of unclaimed losses. Three years of overpaid tax.

The $19,400 wasn't an exotic figure. It was ten line items, each of them obvious in isolation, that added up while she wasn't looking.

This chapter is about those ten line items — the recurring operating expenses that anchor every STR's Schedule E. They are not glamorous. They are not the cost-seg play. They are the deductions most hosts already know about and most hosts still under-claim by a third or more, because they don't track them and they don't believe the total can be that large.

The total can be that large. Most of the time, it is.

---

## The framework — §162 plus a test

Every recurring operating expense in this chapter is deductible under the framework you read in Chapter 2: ordinary and necessary, in the segment your property competes in. There is no exotic regulation to learn. The categories are uncontroversial. The work is in the capture.

The simple sanity test: **if you would expect this expense in a long-term rental of similar size, the deduction is iron-clad ordinary.** If the expense is unique to STR — guest-driven consumption, hotel-style amenities — the segment-context test from Chapter 2 applies, and the standard for an STR at any reasonable price point is generous.

Both buckets clear §162. Track them all.

## Deductions #4–13: the recurring ten

These are the line items that should appear, in some form, on every STR's Schedule E. The order below is the order you should expect to see them in dollar magnitude, roughly, on a typical operator's books — but every market is different.

### #4 — Cleaning between guest stays

The single largest line item on most STRs. Pay your cleaner, deduct the payment. Schedule E, line 7 (Cleaning and maintenance) — or line 11 (Management fees) if your cleaner is paid through a property manager.

**Watch:** the cleaning fee you *charge guests* is income; the cleaning fee you *pay your cleaner* is the deduction. Most platforms remit the guest-paid fee to you in your gross 1099-K. You report the gross as income, then deduct the cleaner payment as expense. Net is zero on a pass-through fee, but the gross-up matters for your average-rental-period calculation under Chapter 1 (don't accidentally inflate your day count).

**1099 trigger:** if you pay a single cleaner $600 or more in 2025-and-prior years — or **$2,000 or more for payments made after December 31, 2025** under OBBBA §70433 — and they're an unincorporated contractor, you owe them a 1099-NEC (Chapter 11). Most hosts miss this. The penalty is not the deduction — the deduction stands. Penalties are indexed annually under §6722; for information returns due in 2026 the top tier is approximately $340 per missed 1099, with intentional-disregard penalties starting around $680 with no annual cap.

### #5 — Utilities

Electricity, gas, water, sewer. All four. All fully deductible if billed to the host. Schedule E, line 17.

**Tracking shortcut:** set up an automatic feed from your utility provider's online portal. Most providers issue a year-end summary CSV. One download in January, twelve months of utility expense captured cleanly.

**The seasonal trap:** STRs in vacation markets often see utility bills swing 4× between peak and off-peak months. Capture every month, not just the peak ones — the $130 February water bill is just as deductible as the $620 July one.

### #6 — Internet & streaming services

Wi-Fi for guests. Streaming subscriptions stocked for guest use (Netflix, Hulu, Disney+, ESPN+ if your listing markets the watching experience). Schedule E, line 17 (Utilities) for internet; line 19 (Other) labeled clearly for streaming.

**Mixed-use:** if the same internet line serves your home and the property, and the property has its own service, deduct only the property's. If the property is on your home internet (rare, but happens for backyard ADUs and house-hacks), see Chapter 12 for allocation.

### #7 — Supplies & restocking

The constant trickle. Paper towels, toilet paper, dish soap, hand soap, shampoo, conditioner, body wash, coffee, sugar, salt, dishwasher pods, laundry detergent, trash bags, sponges, light bulbs. Schedule E, line 15 (Supplies).

This is the line item hosts most under-track because it leaks across many small purchases. Two tracking methods both work:

1. **Dedicated card.** Open a credit card used only for the rental. Every purchase is auto-categorized.
2. **Receipt photo discipline.** Photograph every receipt within 48 hours, drop into the workbook's supply tab. Per Chapter 3, contemporaneous capture is what survives audit.

For a typical STR, the supplies-and-restocking line runs **$1,200 to $4,000 per year** on a single property. Hosts who don't track it routinely under-claim by half.

### #8 — Trash & sanitation

If billed separately from your utility bundle (common in many municipalities), capture it on Schedule E line 17. If bundled with your municipal water bill, the water-bill total already includes it — don't double-count.

**Special case — vacation-market premium pickup:** properties in seasonal markets sometimes pay 2× or 3× standard rates for guaranteed pickup during peak weeks. Fully deductible. The premium is a feature of operating an STR in that segment.

### #9 — Lawn and landscape maintenance

Mowing, edging, blowing, mulching. Recurring service contracts: deductible as a current expense (Schedule E, line 7). New planting and major hardscape: capitalize as 15-year land improvement (Chapter 6). The line is recurring vs. one-time; the dollar threshold for capitalization is the same as Chapter 5's safe harbors.

A $90/month lawn service is $1,080/year of pure deduction. Don't miss it.

### #10 — Pest control

Quarterly preventive service: deductible as current expense, Schedule E line 7. One-time treatments (termites, bed-bug remediation): also deductible if they restore the property to operating condition — the test in Chapter 5.

Most STRs in temperate or subtropical markets have a quarterly pest contract; many hosts forget to capture the line item because the cost is low and the service is invisible.

### #11 — Snow removal / weather services

Where applicable. Includes plowing contracts, ice-melt purchases, and emergency snow-removal call-outs. Schedule E line 7.

For mountain and Northeast markets, this can be $1,500–$5,000 a year per property. For Sunbelt markets, it's $0.

### #12 — Pool / hot tub maintenance and chemicals

Chemicals (chlorine, shock, pH balancers, salt for saltwater systems): supplies, line 15. Service contract for cleaning and inspection: line 7 (Cleaning and maintenance). Equipment repairs: depending on scope, either current expense (Chapter 5 safe harbors) or capital improvement.

A hot tub at a $300/night cabin runs $80–$150/month in chemicals plus $100–$200/month in service. This is a $2,500–$4,000/year line on a single property and almost universally under-tracked.

### #13 — Security and monitoring services

Security camera subscription (Ring, Nest, Eufy cloud storage). Smart-lock service plans. Noise-detection devices (NoiseAware, Minut). Alarm monitoring contracts. Schedule E line 19 (Other) labeled clearly.

These services rarely exceed $50/month per property, so the annual total is small. They are universally deductible and disproportionately under-claimed because the recurring charges hide on auto-pay.

## The pass-through trap

The single most consequential bookkeeping error in this category is the **cleaning-fee gross-up.**

When a guest pays an Airbnb cleaning fee, that money flows through Airbnb to the host. Airbnb reports it on the host's 1099-K as part of gross transactions. The host then pays the cleaner. The economic reality is pass-through — the host pockets nothing on the cleaning fee — but the bookkeeping must reflect both sides:

- **Income:** the gross cleaning fee paid by guests, reported on Schedule E line 3 (Rents received) along with nightly revenue
- **Expense:** the amount paid to the cleaner, reported on Schedule E line 7 (Cleaning and maintenance)

Net income effect: zero (or close to it). But the *line items* are full. Hosts who net the cleaning fee — recording only the small spread between guest fee and cleaner payment — under-report income, over-report margin per stay, and create a 1099-K mismatch the IRS computer matches against automatically. Don't net. Gross.

The same trap applies to platform service fees, third-party damage protection, and any other amount the platform collects from the guest and disburses to vendors on your behalf. Gross income; gross expense; zero net effect on tax; clean reconciliation against the 1099-K.

## Common mistakes

**Mixing operating expenses with capital improvements.** Cleaning supplies are operating. A new washer-dryer is capital (Chapter 5–6). A repainted bedroom is borderline — see Chapter 5's safe harbors. The recurring-ten line items in this chapter are all operating; if a question feels capital, it probably is.

**Treating bulk purchases as a single deduction.** A $1,200 Sam's Club run with paper goods, cleaning supplies, and a new microwave is *three* line items: supplies (paper goods, cleaning), capitalize-or-expense (microwave under the §1.263(a)-1(f) de minimis safe harbor — Chapter 5). Itemize at capture, not at year-end.

**Under-claiming during off-season.** Hosts whose properties are dark for 4 months think those months don't have expenses. They do — utilities still flow, lawn still grows in shoulder seasons, security still monitors. Track every month, every year.

**Forgetting auto-pay subscriptions at year-end.** The streaming services, the security monitoring, the pest contract — anything billed to a card on auto-pay — sums to real money and is the easiest to overlook precisely because it never crosses the host's attention. Pull a year-end credit-card statement export and grep for recurring charges. Almost every host finds at least two they hadn't been tracking.

## Why this chapter is bigger than it looks

The recurring ten on a typical single-property STR sum to **$8,000–$25,000 a year**, depending on market, season, and amenity profile. On a three- to five-property portfolio, the aggregate is **$30,000–$120,000 a year** of fully-deductible Schedule E line items.

That total is larger, often by a factor of two or three, than every exotic deduction in this book combined. Cost segregation, the Augusta Rule, §179 furniture expensing, the home office allocation — all of them produce dollars that the recurring ten produce *every year, automatically, just by being captured.*

The exotic deductions are where this book's title comes from. The recurring ten are where the host's tax bill actually moves.
