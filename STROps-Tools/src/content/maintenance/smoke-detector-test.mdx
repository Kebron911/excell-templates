## Smoke + CO detectors: the only maintenance task with criminal liability attached

In most U.S. jurisdictions, a missing or non-functional smoke detector in a rental unit is a building-code violation. In some — Massachusetts, California, parts of Colorado — it's also a criminal offense if a fatality occurs. Airbnb's own host requirements cite "working smoke and CO detectors" as a baseline; failing to comply puts you outside their liability backstop. The 90-day cadence for *testing* is the bare minimum, and it's also the easiest task on this entire list to systematize.

The mistake most hosts make: they test once at install, write "smoke detector — installed" on the maintenance log, and forget about it. Five years later the alarm chirps at 3am because the battery is dead, the guest pulls it off the ceiling so they can sleep, the next guest checks in to a property with no working detector, and the whole insurance situation is now a problem.

## What "testing" actually means (90-day version)

1. **Press and hold the test button** on every detector, one at a time. Each should beep loudly within 5 seconds. If it doesn't, replace the battery and test again. If it still doesn't, replace the unit (every smoke detector is end-of-life at 10 years; CO detectors at 5–7 years — there is a date stamped on the back).
2. **Photograph the unit** with the test button visible, dated stamp visible. Save to a property folder. This is your code-compliance log.
3. **Listen for chirps** during the next visit. Chirps mean low battery; replace immediately, do not wait for the annual swap.

## Annual battery swap (separate task, listed separately)

Every fall: replace every battery in every detector, even ones that haven't chirped. A guest pulling a chirping detector off the ceiling at 3am is the worst-case-scenario for both safety and review scores. Pre-empt it with a fall battery rotation. Use lithium 9V batteries — they last 18 months easily, and the chirp threshold gives you margin.

## Detector placement (before you ever test)

- One smoke detector inside every bedroom (not the hallway — the hallway one is supplementary).
- One CO detector on every floor, especially within 10 feet of any sleeping room.
- Combination smoke + CO units count as both, but follow placement guidance for whichever sensor is more restrictive.
- Hardwired with battery backup is preferred for STRs — battery-only is acceptable but doubles your replacement workload.

## What a "10-year sealed" detector buys you

A modern 10-year sealed detector ($25–$40) has a non-replaceable lithium cell that runs for 10 years, then chirps end-of-life and you replace the whole unit. For an STR, this is the right choice almost everywhere — it eliminates the annual battery swap and reduces your test-fail rate. Worth the upgrade at the next refit.

## What this prevents

A guest pulling a detector off the ceiling at 3am isn't theoretical. It happens often enough that some hosts now post a printed sign next to each detector saying "Do not disturb — beeping means low battery, please text host." That's a band-aid; the actual fix is the 90-day test plus the annual battery swap, both already on this list.
