## Dryer vent cleaning is fire prevention dressed up as a maintenance task

The U.S. Fire Administration logs roughly 2,900 home dryer fires every year, and clogged lint vents cause about a third of them. In a single-family home that's bad. In an STR — where you don't know how the last guest used the dryer, where insurance underwriters specifically ask about commercial-use cycling, and where a single fire claim can void your short-term-rental rider entirely — it's the maintenance task most likely to translate directly into a five-figure insurance fight.

Annual cadence is the conservative number. A heavy-occupancy STR (think 200+ bookings/year, families with sandy beach towels, hosts who provide linens) should be on a 6-month cycle. The signal that you've waited too long: drying takes more than one cycle, the laundry-room ceiling feels warm during a load, or the lint trap is empty after a long dry (because the lint is downstream in the duct, not in the trap).

## How to do it (call a pro, here's why)

The DIY rod-and-brush kits work for the first 6 feet of duct. Most STR vent runs are 15–40 feet, often with elbows, often through an attic or wall cavity, and the brush stops working long before it reaches the exterior cap. Worse, an aggressive DIY can dislodge a lint plug into a low spot in the duct where it sits like a wick for the next dry cycle.

A pro shows up with a 60-foot rotary brush + commercial vacuum and runs the duct from both ends — exterior cap inward, then dryer outward. They check the cap flapper (this is where rodents nest, and the rodent nest is what catches the lint that catches the spark). Cost runs $80–$200 in most U.S. metros and takes 30 minutes.

## What to ask the pro

- "Did you run the brush from both ends?" (yes — anything else is half a job)
- "Did you check the exterior flapper?" (yes — should open under airflow, close at rest)
- "Show me the photo of the duct interior before and after." (most reputable services do this; if they can't, find another)

## When you can't reach the cap

Some STRs have dryer vents that exit through a roof — high-pitch shingle work that requires a ladder, harness, and someone willing to climb. If your pro charges extra for roof access, pay it; the alternative is a cap clogged with bird's-nest debris that your annual vent service can't actually finish.

## What this prevents in plain numbers

A property offline for two weeks during an insurance-claim cleanup, on a $250/night summer rate, is roughly $3,500 in lost revenue plus the deductible plus the underwriter's eyebrow at renewal. The vent cleaning costs $150 and prevents the inquiry from happening at all.
