## Why HVAC filter change is the highest-leverage 60-day task in an STR

A clogged filter is the single fastest way to turn a $4,000 HVAC system into a $9,000 replacement plus a week of refunded bookings. Restricted airflow forces the blower motor to overdraw, the evaporator coil to ice over in cooling mode, and the heat exchanger to overcycle in heating mode. None of those failures show up on day one — they show up six summers later, in the middle of August, with a guest in the unit and a 24-hour HVAC tech callout fee that runs $400 just for someone to walk in the door.

The 60-day cadence assumes a typical 3- to 4-bedroom STR running roughly half-occupied. Higher occupancy, pets, or a desert climate with construction dust nearby pushes that to every 30–45 days. The cheapest MERV-8 pleated filter is $5 at a hardware store, but the right choice for an STR is a MERV-11 pleated filter ($12–$20) — the higher rating catches the dander and dust that drives "the room smelled musty" reviews without choking airflow on a residential blower the way MERV-13 will.

## How to do it (DIY in 90 seconds)

1. Locate the return-air vent (usually a large grate in a ceiling, hallway wall, or directly on the air handler).
2. Slide out the old filter. Note the size printed on the side — *not* the size from the previous filter, because previous owners are not always right.
3. Slide in a new filter, with the airflow arrow pointing toward the air handler.
4. Write the install date on the new filter's edge with a Sharpie. This is non-optional — the filter is the only maintenance log your future cleaner has.

## Pro vs DIY

DIY, full stop. Filters are a swap, not a service call. The only time you call a pro is when you discover the previous "filter" was actually a cardboard rectangle with screening glued to it, which means you also need a coil cleaning ($150–$300). That's a separate task — see *Mini-split filter + coil clean* and the *AC tune-up*.

## Storage trick

Buy six filters at once. Store them in the utility closet next to the air handler. The cleaner-dispatch sheet should list "check filter date" as a turnover item — if the date on the side is older than 60 days, swap it. This pushes the 90-second task into the cleaning team's existing checklist instead of giving you a recurring calendar event you'll forget.

## When to bump cadence to 30 days

- The property allows pets (any pets, not just shedding breeds).
- The HVAC return is in a high-traffic area (kitchen, mudroom, mainstream of guest movement).
- Local construction is happening within ½ mile (drywall dust travels).
- Any guest review mentions "stuffy" or "dusty" — that's the system telling you the filter is loaded.
