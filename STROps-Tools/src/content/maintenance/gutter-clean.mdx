## Why gutters fail STR properties harder than primary residences

A primary residence is occupied year-round; you notice a clogged gutter the first time water sheets off the roof onto your back porch. An STR is empty between bookings, the cleaner doesn't look up, and a clogged gutter spills behind the fascia for six rainstorms before anyone catches it. By that point you have soft fascia, soffit rot, and probably water in the wall cavity above the kitchen.

The 6-month cadence on this list (spring + fall) is correct for most U.S. climates. Heavy tree canopy properties — a pine-needle problem in particular — push to quarterly. The signal that you're behind cadence: any visible plant growth in the gutter (yes, real plants, this happens fast), staining on the siding directly below a downspout, or water sheeting off a corner during a rainstorm.

## Why this is a pro task even when you can DIY

Technically you can clean gutters yourself for free. Realistically:

- **Ladder safety on STR rooflines** is non-trivial. Two-story properties with steep grades require articulating ladders ($300+ to buy, $50/day to rent).
- **Single-handed gutter work** is dangerous. The standard pro setup is two people — one on the ladder, one steadying.
- **Time cost.** A full property gutter clean takes a homeowner 3–5 hours of careful, sustained ladder work. A pro crew of two does it in 45 minutes with a leaf blower and a tarp.

Pro cost runs $100–$250 in most U.S. metros for a typical 2,000-3,000 sq ft single-family. Worth every dollar for an asset you're trying to keep cosmetically pristine for guest photos.

## What to ask the pro to do

Beyond the obvious "clear the channel," a competent pro should:

1. **Flush each downspout** with a garden hose to confirm flow (a clogged downspout under a clean gutter still spills).
2. **Tighten or replace any loose gutter hangers** spotted while working. Loose hangers sag, sag pools water, pooled water freezes in winter and rips the gutter off the fascia.
3. **Check the roof edge for shingle damage,** debris on roof valleys, or any visible flashing problems. This isn't part of a gutter clean, but a 10-second look while they're up there catches the next problem before it becomes a leak.
4. **Photograph the after.** This is your maintenance log entry; insurance asks for it more than you'd expect.

## Ice dam prevention (cold climates only)

In any climate that gets repeated freeze-thaw, fall gutter cleaning is the single best ice-dam prevention you have. Clogged gutters back up under shingles when the next thaw refreezes; that re-freeze migrates under the roof deck and into the wall. Clean gutters drain, drained gutters don't form ice dams, and you don't end up with a $4,000 emergency roofer in February.

## Gutter guards: not a free pass

Mesh and reverse-curve gutter guards reduce frequency but don't eliminate the task. They turn quarterly cleanings into annual ones, but the annual is still real — pollen, fine debris, and shingle grit accumulate even on the best system. If you have guards, drop the cadence on this task to annual but keep it on the calendar; don't let "we have guards" become "we don't think about it."
