The two minutes between a cleaner texting "guest damaged the mattress" and you filing an AirCover claim is the most expensive two minutes of an STR operator's week. If you do not already know the replacement cost — to the dollar — you will under-claim, which means Airbnb pays out short, which means the deductible eats whatever was left of the booking's profit.

Operators who claim well share one habit: they have the number before they need it. They do not Google "average mattress cost." They know that a king mattress in their property runs $720-$1,100, that the bed frame is $340, and that the labor to swap them at a property 30 minutes away is $80-$120. They walk into the claim with a defensible total.

Here are the 2026 replacement-cost ranges for the items that actually get damaged in short-term rentals, with notes on how to document each one.

## Why "replacement cost" is the wrong framing 80% of the time

Most damage claims are not full-replacement events. They are "this got broken and the closest equivalent costs $X to source and install." The right number is rarely the original sticker price. Three reasons:

1. **Depreciation.** A 4-year-old sofa that gets cut is not worth a new sofa's price. AirCover and most insurers apply depreciation tables.
2. **Like-for-like sourcing.** A specific dining chair from CB2 in 2022 may not exist in 2026. The right replacement is the closest current equivalent, not a hand-search for the original SKU.
3. **Installation labor.** A king mattress costs $720; the labor to deliver, swap, and dispose of the old one runs $60-$140. Both go in the claim.

Build your damage cost log to capture three numbers per item: original cost (for depreciation math), current equivalent cost (for the claim), and labor + disposal (for the total).

## What AirCover actually pays

Airbnb's Host Damage Protection (the new name for what was called AirCover for Hosts) covers up to $3 million in damage reimbursement, but the coverage has carve-outs that hosts often miss:

- Wear-and-tear is not covered. A 5-year-old mattress with a stain that "could be from any guest" gets denied.
- Reasonable depreciation applies. A 7-year-old sofa is paid out at depreciated value, not full replacement.
- Documentation is required: pre-stay photos (of the undamaged item) and post-stay photos (of the damage), plus a receipt or quote for replacement.
- Claims must be filed within 14 days of the next guest's check-in OR before the next guest's check-in, whichever is sooner.

The 14-day window is the most-missed rule. Cleaner reports damage Tuesday, you tell yourself you'll deal with it after the weekend, next guest checks in Thursday — your window has closed before you even started typing the claim.

## How to document for a winning claim

Three layers of evidence:

**Pre-stay photos.** A standing photo library of every durable item in the property, with timestamps that establish the item's pre-incident condition. Most hosts do this once at property setup and forget. Re-shoot annually. Store in a dedicated folder per property.

**Damage photos.** Wide shot showing the item in its room context, mid shot showing the damage area, close-up showing the specific damage. Three photos, every angle.

**Cost documentation.** A current quote from the supplier (not a 3-year-old receipt). For a mattress: a screenshot from the manufacturer's site showing today's price. For labor: a written estimate from the contractor or delivery service.

## The cleaner's role in the claim window

The single highest-leverage process change most operators can make: train the cleaner to text damage photos to the operator within 30 minutes of arriving for the turn. Not "before the next guest" — within 30 minutes.

That turns the claim window from theoretical (14 days) to operational (the same day). It also catches damage that pre-checkout walk-throughs miss because the cleaner has the time and lighting to actually look.

## Worked example: a $1,400 claim

Real-world claim, anonymized:

- Property: 4-bedroom beach house, 8-year-old furnishings.
- Damage: red wine spilled on a 3-seat sectional sofa, original cost $2,200 in 2018.
- Claim approach: full replacement, 2018 receipt attached.

Outcome: AirCover offered $580 (depreciated value at 8 years).

Better approach:
- Confirm professional cleaning cannot remove the stain — get a written quote from a fabric specialist ($120, denied as restorable). Document the denial.
- Source current equivalent sofa: $2,400 in 2026.
- Apply Airbnb's depreciation table (typically 10-year life for upholstered furniture): 2 years remaining of life × $240/year = $480 residual.
- Counter-claim: 2026 equivalent ($2,400) – residual ($480) = $1,920 for replacement, plus $120 documented cleaning attempt, plus $200 disposal/delivery labor = $2,240.

Settled at $1,400. The host who walked in with the math got nearly 2.5x the claim that the host with "the original receipt" would have gotten.

## Items that almost never get damaged (but always get claimed)

A small but persistent category: things hosts try to claim that AirCover routinely denies.

- **Linens stained but washable.** Even a stubborn coffee stain on white sheets is treated as wear-and-tear. Claim only if the linen is actually unusable (e.g., red wine on a duvet that bleeds through to the comforter).
- **"Slight scratches" on hardwood floors.** AirCover treats normal traffic wear as host responsibility. Claim only deep gouges or substantial damaged areas.
- **Drinkware breakage.** Standard cup breakage is wear-and-tear. Stock cheap, replaceable glassware on purpose; do not claim individual breaks.
- **Toilet paper, soap, "consumables used aggressively."** Not covered. This is what your supply restock budget covers — see [the supply restock post](/blog/airbnb-supply-restock-calculator).

Filing claims that get routinely denied makes future legitimate claims harder. AirCover support staff weigh the operator's claim history, and a string of denied claims hurts the next valid one. Be selective.

## Cross-post: how damage cost meets your tax filing

Damage write-offs hit your taxes in two distinct ways: as repair expenses (deductible immediately, like patching drywall) or as capital improvements (depreciated over 5-27.5 years, like a new HVAC unit). The line between them is the "betterment" test from IRS Pub 527 — a repair restores the original condition, an improvement makes the property better than original.

Replacing a same-grade mattress with a same-grade mattress: a repair. Replacing a basic mattress with a luxury hybrid: partly a repair, partly an improvement (the differential is capitalized). ## The shortest possible damage protocol

When the cleaner texts that something is broken:

1. Photo set within 30 minutes of damage discovery (cleaner's responsibility per the [cleaner dispatch SMS template](/blog/airbnb-cleaner-sms-template)).
2. Pre-stay photo from your library to compare (your responsibility, retrieved in 90 seconds).
3. Replacement quote from current supplier (not old receipt).
4. Labor + disposal estimate written down.
5. Submit claim within 24 hours of the turn, not 24 hours before the deadline.

That is it. The five-step protocol takes about 20 minutes total per claim. The hosts who run it successfully recover roughly 80-90% of the documented damage cost over the year. The hosts who skip steps recover 30-50%.
