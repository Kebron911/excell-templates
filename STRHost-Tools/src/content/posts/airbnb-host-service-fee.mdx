The first time a host opens a payout statement and notices Airbnb kept 14% of the booking instead of 3%, the reaction is uniform: *I thought it was 3%.* Sometimes it is. Sometimes it isn't. The fee structure changes silently based on where your listing is registered, what kind of property it is, and a checkbox in your account settings most hosts have never opened.

This post breaks down the two fee models Airbnb uses in 2026, the math on each, and how to tell which one your listings are actually on. The companion calculator — [airbnb-fee-calculator](/airbnb-fee-calculator) — runs the math live for any booking value.

## The two fee structures

Airbnb operates two parallel host-fee models in 2026. Same platform, completely different economics.

### Model 1 — Split fee (host 3% + guest ~14%)

The classic structure most US hosts started on. Airbnb takes:

- **3% from the host** — deducted from the payout
- **~14% from the guest** — added on top of the nightly rate at checkout (varies 12–16% by stay length and region)

The guest sees a "service fee" line on the booking total. The host sees a 3% deduction on the payout statement. Both parties pay; neither pays much individually.

Default for: most US single-family STR listings, professional hosts with smaller portfolios, hosts in markets where Airbnb hasn't required the alternative.

### Model 2 — Host-only fee (~14–16% from host, no guest fee)

Here Airbnb takes:

- **14% to 16% from the host** — deducted from the payout, no guest-side service fee
- The guest sees a "clean" total that's just the nightly rate × nights + cleaning + taxes

Default (now mandatory) for: most listings outside the US/Canada, listings managed by software-connected channel managers (PMS-connected accounts), and a growing share of US hosts as Airbnb migrates the platform.

The 14–16% range moves with stay length, location, host status, and a small "Plus" or "Luxe" overlay. Most hosts on this model land at 14–15%.

## Why Airbnb runs two models

The honest framing: the host-only fee shows the guest a smaller "total before taxes" number, which improves Airbnb's competitive positioning against Vrbo and Booking.com. The same booking that costs the guest $1,142 on the split-fee model costs $1,000 on the host-only model — because the guest's $142 of fee got pushed onto the host's payout instead.

The marketing line is "transparent pricing for guests." The economic effect is the host now eats Airbnb's full take.

This isn't unique — Vrbo offers similar dual options, Booking.com is host-side only — but it's the line item that drives the most "wait, what just happened to my margin?" reactions in the host forums.

## How to tell which model you're on

Three places to check, in order of speed:

1. **Open a recent payout statement.** If the deduction line is around 3% of the subtotal, you're on split. If it's around 14–16%, you're on host-only.
2. **Account → Settings → Payments & Payouts → Service fee.** Some accounts can toggle between models; many can't (it's set by region or PMS connection).
3. **Compare a guest's "View your bill" to your payout.** If the guest paid $1,142 and you received $1,108 (≈3% deduction off the $1,142 minus their fee, in this case ≈$1,000 net base × 3%), that's split. If the guest paid $1,000 and you received $860 (≈14% deduction), that's host-only.

Most hosts who've never looked are on whatever default Airbnb assigned the first time the listing was created. That default has been migrating toward host-only since 2020.

External reference: [Airbnb Help Center — Service fees](https://www.airbnb.com/help/article/1857). The official explanation, written carefully, with the underlying ranges.

## A worked comparison — same property, both fee models

Cabin in Asheville. Nightly rate $250, 4-night stay, $150 cleaning fee, no taxes for this example.

### Split-fee math

Subtotal (the "host's revenue base"): 4 × $250 + $150 cleaning = **$1,150**

Host service fee (3% of subtotal): $1,150 × 0.03 = **$34.50**

Host payout: $1,150 − $34.50 = **$1,115.50**

Guest service fee (14% of subtotal, typical): $1,150 × 0.14 = **$161**

Guest pays: $1,150 + $161 = **$1,311**

Airbnb's total take: $34.50 + $161 = **$195.50** (14.9% of guest total)

### Host-only fee math

To present the guest with a similar checkout experience, the host raises the nightly rate to absorb where the guest fee used to be. If the host wants the same gross of ~$1,150 base + cleaning, they need to charge enough for the *post-fee* payout to land at $1,115.50:

Host gross at 15% fee: $1,115.50 / 0.85 = **$1,312.35**

If the cleaning fee stays $150, the nightly rate becomes ($1,312.35 − $150) / 4 = **$290.59/night**

Guest pays: $1,312.35 (no separate service fee shown)

Airbnb's host-only fee: $1,312.35 × 0.15 = **$196.85**

Host payout: $1,115.50 (matched intentionally)

Guest's total bill: nearly identical to split-fee scenario. Airbnb's take: nearly identical. **The shift is purely in *who appears to pay*.**

### What's actually different

If the host *doesn't* raise the nightly rate to compensate, host-only is materially worse for the host. At a $250 nightly rate held flat:

- Host-only payout at 15%: ($1,150) × 0.85 = **$977.50**
- Versus split-fee payout: **$1,115.50**

Difference: **$138 of host margin lost per booking** by failing to re-price.

The hosts who get hurt by host-only aren't being charged "more"; they're being charged the same fee that used to be split with the guest, while *not* raising the nightly rate to compensate. The host who reprices captures back almost all of the difference.

## What the fee actually buys

It's tempting to read 14% as pure platform tax. The honest accounting is more mixed:

- **Acquisition.** Airbnb's marketing engine produces booked nights. A host's organic reach (referrals, repeat guests, direct bookings) is real but smaller than most hosts admit in Year 1–2.
- **Payment processing.** Bundled into the fee. A direct-booking site running Stripe pays roughly 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction — about 3% on a $1,000 stay.
- **AirCover.** Damage protection up to $3M, liability up to $1M. Real coverage with real exclusions; many pro hosts replace it with a dedicated STR policy and umbrella, but for a host with one property it has cash value.
- **Dispute handling and customer service.** Mixed reviews. Useful when it works, frustrating when it doesn't.
- **Listing visibility infrastructure.** Search, filters, instant-book mechanics, smart-pricing inputs.

Add it up and the actual *cost-equivalent* of what Airbnb provides is often 5–8% on a booking — roughly Stripe + a CRM + a marketplace listing. The 7–10% delta is the platform's margin. That's not a moral judgment; it's a reasonable margin for the marketplace effect Airbnb produces. But it explains why direct-booking strategies (custom domain, Stripe checkout, repeat-guest CRM) become more attractive once a host is past their first $50K in bookings.

## The fee changes most hosts don't see

Airbnb has been quietly making the host-only fee the default for new listings, channel-manager-connected listings, and most non-US markets since 2020. The trend is one direction: more host-side, less guest-side. The guest-facing copy gets simpler; the host-side margin gets thinner unless the host reprices.

Three practical responses:

1. **Check which model each of your listings is on, today.** Pull a recent payout. If it's host-only, audit your nightly rates against the assumption that 14% comes off the top.
2. **Reprice when the model switches.** A listing that goes from split to host-only without a rate change is leaving 10%+ of revenue on the table.
3. **Build direct-booking optionality.** A custom domain with Stripe checkout, a guest CRM, and 20% repeat-booking rate eliminates Airbnb's fee on those nights. Not a complete replacement; a hedge.

## FAQ

**Is the host service fee deductible on my taxes?** Yes. Whether you're on split (3%) or host-only (14–16%), the fee deducted from your payout is a deductible business expense. Report your gross revenue (before the fee) and the fee separately on Schedule E Line 5 (Advertising) or Line 19 (Other).

**Why does my fee percentage move slightly between bookings?** Most fee variation is from stay length (long stays sometimes get a different rate), Plus/Luxe overlays, or local-market modifiers. The base 3% or 14–16% is consistent; the wiggle is usually 0.5–1.5 percentage points around the base.

**Can I switch from host-only back to split?** Sometimes. If your account has the toggle, yes. Many hosts find the option is no longer offered after they've been moved to host-only — particularly if they're connected through a PMS or operating in a region where Airbnb has gone host-only by default.

**Does Airbnb's fee include payment processing?** Yes — Airbnb absorbs Stripe-equivalent processing in the fee. You don't pay separately for card processing.

**What about Vrbo's fee?** Vrbo runs a similar dual model (subscription option + commission option). For a 2-property pro host on commission, Vrbo's all-in take typically lands at 8–10% of booking — somewhat lower than Airbnb's 14–16% host-only, but with smaller booking volume. Most hosts list on both.

**Do I owe lodging tax on the host service fee?** No. The fee is paid to Airbnb, not collected from the guest in the lodging-tax sense. Lodging tax applies to the rental rate + cleaning fee, not to platform service fees.
