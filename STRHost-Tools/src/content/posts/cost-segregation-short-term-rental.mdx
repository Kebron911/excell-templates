The cost-segregation pitch in STR investor circles is loud: "$50,000 of first-year deductions, completely legal, the IRS encourages it." The pitch is roughly true. The omitted parts — the cost of the study, the recapture on sale, the passive-loss limits, the threshold below which the math just doesn't work — are why most hosts who hear the pitch don't actually do the study.

This piece walks through what cost segregation is, when it actually pays for itself, the **STR-specific** wrinkles (material participation, average stay rules), and the worked numbers on a real-feeling property.

## What cost segregation actually is

When you buy a short-term rental, you're buying one asset on paper but several assets in reality. The default tax treatment lumps almost everything into "27.5-year residential rental property" and depreciates it straight-line over 27.5 years. Cost segregation breaks the property into its component asset classes and applies the IRS's actual recovery periods to each:

| Asset class | Recovery period | What's in it |
|---|---|---|
| 5-year MACRS | 5 years | Furniture, appliances, electronics, decor, carpet, smart-home devices |
| 7-year MACRS | 7 years | Office equipment, certain fixtures (rare for STR) |
| 15-year MACRS | 15 years | Land improvements: driveway, fencing, landscaping, exterior lighting, pool shell |
| 27.5-year MACRS | 27.5 years | The structure itself: walls, roof, plumbing, electrical |
| Non-depreciable | — | Land |

The point: 5- and 15-year property is **eligible for bonus depreciation**. 27.5-year residential property is not. Reclassifying $80K of components from 27.5-year to 5-year doesn't just speed up the deduction by some percentage — it unlocks the bonus deduction stack on top of it.

## Bonus depreciation in 2026 — the moving target

Bonus depreciation is on a phase-down schedule that started in 2023:

- 2023: 80%
- 2024: 60%
- 2025: 40%
- 2026: 20%
- 2027: 0% (absent legislation extending it)

In 2026, you can deduct **20% of qualifying 5- and 15-year property in the year placed in service**, then continue MACRS depreciation on the remaining 80%. The 2017 TCJA created the 100% rate; the phase-down is in current law and Congress has not (as of this writing) passed an extension.

A property placed in service in 2026 with $80K of 5-year property gets **$16K of bonus** (20%) plus regular MACRS on the remaining $64K. Compare to placing the same property in service in 2025 — $32K bonus — or 2027 — $0 bonus. Timing matters.

This phase-down is also why the urgency around cost segregation has cooled since 2023. The math still works for many STRs; it just doesn't produce the headline numbers it did three years ago.

## The STR-specific reason this matters more than for long-term rentals

Here's where short-term rentals get their own loophole.

For a long-term rental, depreciation losses are **passive losses** under IRC §469. You can only use them to offset *passive income*, not your W-2 paycheck. A massive cost-seg deduction on a long-term rental sits unused, suspended until you sell or generate passive income elsewhere.

Short-term rentals, under Treasury Regulation §1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii), can escape passive treatment if both are true:

1. **Average guest stay is 7 days or less**, OR average stay is 30 days or less and the host provides "substantial services."
2. The host **materially participates** under one of the seven §1.469-5T tests (most common: 500+ hours/year, OR 100+ hours and more than anyone else).

When both conditions are met, the STR is **non-passive** and depreciation losses can offset W-2 income directly. This is the "STR loophole" and it's the entire reason cost segregation gets pitched as aggressively as it does in real-estate-investor circles.

The catch: the IRS audits this. The 100-hour or 500-hour participation test must be supported by a contemporaneous log — not reconstructed at tax time. *"I thought about my listing for an hour every day"* doesn't survive an audit.

## When the study pays for itself

Three thresholds determine whether cost segregation is worth doing at all.

### Threshold 1 — purchase price

Below roughly $400K, an engineer-led study ($3,000–$8,000 fee) doesn't pay for itself in tax savings during the first 1–2 years. The breakeven is roughly:

```
Engineer study cost ≤ (component reclass × bonus rate × marginal tax rate)
```

For a 22% federal bracket and 20% bonus, every $1 of study cost needs to unlock about $23 of reclassified component basis. That's typically a $400K+ property.

**Below $400K**, a DIY allocation (or "self-segregated study") using purchase documents, county appraisal ratios, and benchmark cost data is the right answer. The IRS accepts reasonable methodology; you don't need a PE-stamped engineer report for a $300K cabin.

**$400K–$1M**, lite engineer studies ($1,500–$3,000) often make sense.

**$1M+ or unique structures (luxury, multi-unit, mixed-use)** — full engineer-led study every time.

### Threshold 2 — material participation

If you can't legitimately log 100+ hours of participation (more than any other person involved) or 500+ hours (anyone), you can't use the cost-seg deduction against W-2 income. The deduction still helps — it shelters STR income — but the math gets less compelling. Heavily property-managed STRs where the manager runs everything fail this test.

### Threshold 3 — exit horizon

Depreciation reverses at sale. If you cost-seg today and sell in 3 years, you'll face **§1245 recapture** on the 5- and 15-year property at *ordinary income rates* (not the 25% §1250 cap on building recapture). Hosts planning to hold 7+ years usually come out ahead. Hosts flipping in 2–3 years often don't.

## A worked example — Smokies cabin, $480K purchase

Amanda buys a 3BR cabin in the Smokies in March 2026. Purchase price $480,000. County assessor's land/improvement ratio is 17/83, so land = $80,000, depreciable basis = $400,000.

### Default treatment (no cost seg)

Full $400,000 depreciated as 27.5-year residential, mid-month convention. March placement = 9.5 months of Year 1.

Year 1 depreciation: $400,000 / 27.5 × (9.5/12) = **$11,515**

### Cost-segregated treatment

A self-segregated study allocates the $400K basis as follows (based on county data, seller's furniture inventory, and standard STR component benchmarks):

- 5-year property (existing furniture, appliances, electronics, decorative fixtures, carpet): $80,000
- 15-year property (driveway, deck, landscaping, exterior lighting): $40,000
- 27.5-year property (the structure): $280,000

Year 1 depreciation in 2026 (20% bonus rate):

- 5-year bonus: $80,000 × 20% = $16,000
- 5-year MACRS on remaining $64,000 (200% DB, half-year convention, Year 1 = 20%): $12,800
- 15-year bonus: $40,000 × 20% = $8,000
- 15-year MACRS on remaining $32,000 (150% DB, half-year, Year 1 = 5%): $1,600
- 27.5-year on $280,000, mid-month, 9.5 months: $8,061

**Year 1 total: $46,461**

Difference: **$34,946 of additional Year 1 deduction** vs. default treatment.

At Amanda's 24% federal bracket (married filing jointly, joint income $220K), that's **$8,387 of cash tax savings in Year 1**. Add state tax (varies; Tennessee has none, so for this property zero) and the bottom line is $8,387 of real cash kept.

If Amanda passes material participation (4-night average stay, 500+ hours), the $34,946 also offsets her husband's W-2 income — meaning the savings is real cash, not a suspended loss waiting to be released.

### What it costs to capture this

- Self-segregated DIY: ~6 hours of her time, plus a $47 depreciation tracker workbook to keep the schedule clean. Net: $8,340.
- Engineer-lite study ($2,500): net ~$5,887 Year 1, plus more accurate component allocation that compounds across years 2–5.

Year 1 isn't the whole story. Years 2–5 also benefit from accelerated 5-year depreciation on the $64K remaining after bonus. Total NPV of the cost-seg election at a 7% discount rate: roughly **$11,000–$13,000** for this property, before considering recapture at sale.

## Catching up missed depreciation — Form 3115

If you've owned the STR for 2+ years and depreciated it the default way, you can file **Form 3115 (Application for Change in Accounting Method)** with your current-year return and apply cost segregation retroactively. The catch-up adjustment — called a §481(a) adjustment — can compress 5+ years of "should have been faster" depreciation into a single year's deduction.

Form 3115 is CPA-complex. For a host with a $400K+ STR who's been depreciating at 27.5-year for 3+ years, it commonly produces a $30K–$60K single-year deduction in the year of filing. The fee a CPA charges to prepare it ($800–$2,500) is usually a fraction of the savings.

This is a **legitimate, IRS-blessed procedure** under Rev. Proc. 2015-13. It's not amended-return territory; it's an automatic accounting method change. Done correctly it doesn't trigger heightened audit risk.

## The recapture question

When you sell, depreciation comes back. Two flavors:

- **§1250 recapture (the 27.5-year building)**: capped at 25% federal rate.
- **§1245 recapture (the 5- and 15-year components)**: ordinary-income rates, up to 37%.

This is why holding period matters. A 3-year flip with $80K of accelerated 5-year deductions hits §1245 recapture at ordinary rates, partially eroding the Year 1 benefit. A 10-year hold lets the deduction's compounding tax-deferred return outpace the eventual recapture cost.

A **1031 exchange** at sale defers all recapture into the replacement property. For hosts building a multi-property portfolio, this is the standard playbook.

## FAQ

**Can I do cost segregation on a property I bought 5 years ago?** Yes — Form 3115 captures the missed depreciation in the current year. No need to amend old returns.

**Does Airbnb's "average stay" determine the 7-day rule?** Yes. Pull your annual booking history; total nights divided by total stays = your average. If it's 7.0 or below for the year, you meet that prong.

**What if I have a property manager handling everything?** You probably fail material participation. The 100-hour test specifically requires you to participate **more than any other person** — including the manager. Self-managed or co-host arrangements are easier to qualify.

**Is the 20% bonus rate the same in 2027?** No. Under current law, bonus drops to 0% in 2027. Congress has discussed extensions, but nothing has passed as of mid-2026. Place property in service in 2026 to capture the 20%.

**Do I need a PE-stamped engineer's study?** For properties under ~$500K, no. The IRS accepts a reasonable allocation methodology. Above $1M, an engineer's study is standard practice and worth the fee.
