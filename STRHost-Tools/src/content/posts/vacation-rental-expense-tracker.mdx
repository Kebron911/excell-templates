A vacation rental expense tracker is not a budgeting tool — it's a tax-time defense. The categories you capture during the year decide which deductions survive a return review and which get left on the table. Most off-the-shelf STR spreadsheets stop at mortgage, utilities, cleaning, and supplies. That covers maybe 70% of what a single-property host actually spends; the other 30% is where the money sits.

This piece walks through the 12 categories most hosts under-track, with real numbers from a single-property scenario, and how to set up a tracker that maps cleanly to Schedule E without restructuring at year end. If you'd rather skip the build and start from a worksheet, the **Cost-Per-Stay Calculator** at [The STR Ledger](https://thestrledger.com) is a free Excel tool that breaks each booking into the cost categories below.

*Not tax advice. Always verify with a CPA who handles short-term rental clients.*

## The minimum viable expense tracker

Five fields per row, every row, every transaction:

```
Date | Vendor | Amount | Category | Property
```

That's it. Add a sixth field — payment method — if you use more than one card or account, because reconciliation against bank statements is where most host bookkeeping breaks down. Don't add a "notes" field; it never gets filled in, and category granularity beats free-text every time.

The category list is where the work happens. The 12 below are the ones routinely missing from generic templates.

## The 12 categories most trackers miss

**1. Platform host service fee.** Airbnb's standard 3% host fee, or the ~14–16% host-only fee (mandatory in some regions), is a deduction — not a netting against gross. Your 1099-K reports gross guest payment; the platform fee comes off as Schedule E Line 5 (Advertising) or Line 19 (Other expenses). Track the dollar amount, not just the percentage. On a property grossing $84,000 in 2026 at standard split, that's $2,520 in deductible platform fees most hosts roll into "net deposits."

**2. Payment processor fees you can see.** Stripe, PayPal, Venmo Business — any time a guest payment routes outside the Airbnb / Vrbo flow (direct bookings, OwnerRez, Hospitable Direct), you eat 2.9% + $0.30. On a $4,200 direct-booking month, that's $130. Categorize as a banking/processor expense, not as part of cleaning or supplies.

**3. Lodging tax remittance shortfall.** Tax you collected from guests and remitted is pass-through and not deductible. Tax you ate — because the guest paid less than the statutory rate, or because you discovered a city occupancy tax mid-year and back-paid — *is* deductible. The [50-state lodging tax index](/lodging-tax) flags which states have full platform collection and which have gaps; the gap, when you eat it, is a real expense category.

**4. Smart-home service fees.** Smart locks, smart thermostats, leak detectors, noise sensors. The hardware is depreciable 5-year property; the *monthly subscription* (Schlage, NoiseAware, August Connect, MinutKey) is a current-year deduction. A typical host runs $25–$60/mo on smart-home subscriptions — $300–$720/yr — that often disappears into "miscellaneous."

**5. Listing software and revenue management.** PriceLabs, Wheelhouse, Beyond Pricing, Hospitable, Hostfully, OwnerRez, Turno. A single-property host typically runs $50–$120/mo across these tools. $600–$1,440/yr deductible. Track separately from "marketing" so the audit trail is clean.

**6. Photography re-shoots.** The original listing photo session is sometimes capitalized into the property's basis; subsequent re-shoots (after a furniture refresh, after a renovation) are typically current-year deductions. Most hosts who refresh photos every 18–24 months don't track this as a separate line — it ends up in "supplies" and the deduction is harder to defend.

**7. Guest-screening services.** Autohost, Safely, Truvi, Superhog. Per-booking fees range $1–$5; subscription tiers $30–$100/mo. Frequently rolled into "Airbnb fees" by hosts who don't realize these are independent vendors.

**8. Aircover supplementary insurance.** Aircover (Airbnb), Host Damage Protection (Vrbo), and dedicated STR carriers like Proper Insurance and Obie. The dedicated policy is Schedule E Line 9 (Insurance); the supplementary platform programs, if separately charged, also belong here. A typical Proper policy on a $400,000 property runs $1,400–$2,200/yr and is fully deductible.

**9. HOA, condo, and mandatory amenity fees.** Schedule E Line 19. Mountain-resort HOAs commonly charge $350–$650/mo. Beach condos with mandatory amenity fees stack on top. A property with $5,400/yr of HOA fees that gets coded to "supplies" by accident is a deduction sitting in the wrong column on audit.

**10. STR permit, license, and registration fees.** Distinct from the lodging tax itself. The annual permit fee — $200 in some cities, $1,500+ in jurisdictions with stricter STR programs — is a current-year deduction. Track separately from lodging tax remittance.

**11. Mileage to and from the property.** The IRS standard business mileage rate for 2026 is **$0.70 per mile** (verify the current rate at the [IRS standard mileage rate notice](https://www.irs.gov/tax-professionals/standard-mileage-rates) — the figure is updated annually). A host who drives 6 miles round-trip to a turnover, twice a month, plus 4 quarterly maintenance visits at 12 miles each, logs roughly 200 miles/year — about $140 of deductions. A host who self-cleans and lives 18 miles away easily clears 1,500+ miles, or $1,050 in deductions. Without a contemporaneous log, this category is unreclaimable. The [airbnb mileage deduction](/blog/airbnb-mileage-deduction) post breaks down the log requirements.

**12. Capex reserves.** Strictly speaking, reserves aren't a deduction — they're a cash-management practice. But pairing your tracker with a capex reserve column means the actual capital spend (HVAC replacement, water heater, new mattress at year 5) is captured at the moment of expenditure as a depreciable asset, not as a vague "repair." Misclassifying a $9,400 HVAC replacement as a Line 14 repair instead of a Form 4562 depreciable asset is one of the more common audit-triggers in STR returns.

Bolded rows are the categories most off-the-shelf trackers miss. Total of just those bolded rows: **$9,619** of deductions a host with a generic spreadsheet would routinely undercount or misclassify. At a 24% federal marginal rate, that's $2,309 of tax — roughly two months of mortgage payments — riding on category discipline.

## How to set the tracker up so it flows to Schedule E

Three columns matter for tax-season speed: **Schedule E line**, **property**, and **category**. Build the workbook so each line of Schedule E has a fixed code, every transaction tags to a code, and a pivot-table or SUMIF rolls up automatically.

A workable category map for a Schedule E filer:

```
SE-05  Advertising / Platform fees / Photography
SE-06  Auto and travel (mileage, rental car)
SE-07  Cleaning and maintenance (contractor labor)
SE-09  Insurance
SE-10  Legal and professional
SE-11  Management fees / Co-host
SE-12  Mortgage interest
SE-13  Other interest (credit card, LOC)
SE-14  Repairs (non-capital)
SE-15  Supplies and consumables
SE-16  Property taxes / Permit fees
SE-17  Utilities
SE-18  Depreciation (Form 4562 driven)
SE-19  Other — itemized: HOA, software, smart-home, screening, aircover
```

Every transaction you enter gets one of those codes. At year end, the SUMIF for each code is the dollar amount on each line of Schedule E. No reconstruction, no late-night reclassification, no "what was this $480 charge in March."

The mid-year discipline matters more than the year-end discipline. A tracker is only useful if it's current within seven days of every transaction; a tracker reconstructed in February from twelve months of bank statements is roughly half as accurate as one maintained weekly, and the half that's missing is disproportionately the bolded rows in the table above.

## When to switch from spreadsheet to software

A clean spreadsheet handles 1–3 properties. Past four properties, the reconciliation cost of manual entry starts to exceed the cost of a $25/mo bookkeeping tool. Tools that map well to STR-on-Schedule-E workflows include Stessa (free for owners; aggressive marketing but solid bank-feed bookkeeping), Hostfully + a connector, or the underrated approach of QuickBooks Self-Employed with an STR-specific chart of accounts. Whichever path: the chart of accounts has to mirror Schedule E lines, not generic small-business categories. The [str bookkeeping](/blog/str-bookkeeping) post walks through a chart of accounts that maps cleanly without retrofitting.

For most single-property hosts in 2026, the right answer remains a spreadsheet — provided the category list above is in it.

## FAQ

**Do I need a separate tracker per property?** No — one workbook with a property column is fine for up to 5 properties. Past that, per-property tabs reduce error rates. The Schedule E line-code system above scales either way.

**Can I just use Stessa or a free bank-feed tool?** Yes, with one caveat: every STR-specific category (host fee, smart-home subs, screening services) needs a custom category, not the default "rent / supplies / utilities" Stessa ships with. The bolded rows above don't have generic-tool defaults that map to Schedule E lines correctly.

**What about credit-card statements as a tracker?** Workable for the highest-volume month-to-month expenses, but useless for cash, check, and Zelle payments — which is where most cleaner and contractor money lives. A bank-statement-only tracker undercounts by a meaningful amount for most hosts.

**How long do I need to keep the records?** Federal: three years from the filing date for routine returns, six years if there's substantial under-reporting, indefinitely if no return was filed or fraud is alleged. The IRS [recordkeeping guidance](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/how-long-should-i-keep-records) is the source. For STR — where depreciation runs 27.5 years on the building — keep purchase, capital improvement, and depreciation records for the life of the property plus three years.

**Does the Cost-Per-Stay calculator replace this tracker?** No — it's a per-booking modeling tool, not a transaction log. The two pair: the calculator estimates margin per stay, the tracker captures actual spend. The reconciliation between them is where most hosts find the gap.
