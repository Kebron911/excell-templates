*General educational information, not tax or legal advice. Entity choice has long-term tax and liability consequences; an attorney and CPA should sign off before you file articles of organization.*

The Airbnb host forums treat this question as a religious war. One camp says "you must form an LLC, you'll lose everything otherwise." The other says "LLCs are a waste of money for hosts under $X revenue." Both are partly right, and both are mostly missing the point.

The actual question is narrower: at what point does the **annual cost of an LLC** (formation, registered agent, annual report fees, separate banking, separate bookkeeping) become smaller than the **expected value of the liability protection plus tax flexibility** it provides? That number depends on three things: how much equity you have at stake, how many properties you operate, and how active your business looks to a court.

This post walks through the liability math, the tax math, and where the breakpoints sit. Sticky in the middle: **Entity Decision Flowchart** PDF — a one-page yes/no flow you can run any new property through. Linked at the end.

## What "sole proprietor" actually means for hosts

If you started hosting and never formed an entity, you're a sole proprietor by default. Income flows directly onto your personal tax return — Schedule E if it's a passive rental, Schedule C if it's a substantial-services trade or business. There's no separate legal entity. The property and the person are the same legal pocket.

The implication: a guest who slips on your stairs, sues, and wins a judgment beyond your insurance limits can collect against your personal assets — your home, your savings, your other rentals, your wages. There's no corporate veil because there's no corporation.

For most hosts, the practical liability shield against this scenario is **insurance**, not entity structure. An STR-specific policy (Proper, Obie) plus a $1M–$2M umbrella on top covers virtually every scenario a guest can produce. The cases where insurance fails — exclusions, intentional acts, damages above policy limits, plaintiff-friendly jurisdictions — are exactly the cases an LLC was built for.

## What an LLC actually protects against

A single-member LLC is a "disregarded entity" for federal tax purposes (you still file Schedule E or C on your personal return) but a separate legal entity for liability purposes. Done correctly:

- A judgment against the LLC stops at the LLC's assets — typically the property, the bank balance, and any furniture/fixtures owned by the LLC.
- Personal assets (home, retirement, other LLCs' properties) are **not** reachable by the plaintiff.

Three operational requirements for the protection to actually hold up in court:

1. **The LLC must be properly capitalized.** A property held by an LLC with $80 in its checking account and a $1,200/month mortgage looks like a shell. Courts pierce shells.
2. **Books must be separate.** Personal money in, business money out → veil pierced. Use a dedicated bank account, dedicated card, dedicated bookkeeping.
3. **Title must be in the LLC's name.** A property deeded to you personally doesn't get LLC protection regardless of what your operating agreement says.

Hosts who skip the operational steps and assume "I formed an LLC, I'm protected" are usually wrong. *Veil-piercing* is the most common way LLC owners lose at trial.

## The cost of running an LLC

Annual carrying cost varies sharply by state:

| State | Formation fee | Annual report / franchise tax | Registered agent (if needed) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wyoming | $100 | $60 | $50–$200/yr |
| Texas | $300 | $0 (no franchise under $1.23M revenue) | $50–$200/yr |
| Florida | $125 | $138.75/yr | $50–$200/yr |
| Tennessee | $300 | $300/yr minimum | $50–$200/yr |
| California | $70 | $800/yr **minimum franchise tax** | $50–$200/yr |
| New York | $200 | $25/yr biennial + publication ($500–$2,000) | $50–$200/yr |

California is the standout punisher: $800/yr regardless of profit. Wyoming and Florida are common choices for out-of-state hosts who want a clean structure at a reasonable cost.

On top of state fees, expect:

- Separate business bank account: $0–$15/mo
- Separate bookkeeping (your time or ~$100–$300/mo to a bookkeeper)
- CPA tax-prep premium for the entity return (if multi-member): $300–$800/yr
- Optional: STR-specific business insurance through the LLC (often replaces or supplements personal STR coverage)

Total annual cost for a single-member LLC in a host-friendly state: **$300–$1,500/yr**. In California: $1,100–$2,800/yr.

## The tax math — almost identical, with two exceptions

For a single-member LLC, federal tax treatment is **identical to sole proprietor**. Same Schedule E or C, same self-employment tax (or absence of it), same depreciation rules, same passive-activity treatment. The IRS doesn't see the LLC at all.

Two scenarios where the entity choice changes federal tax:

### Exception 1 — S-corp election

A sole proprietor or single-member LLC can elect S-corp treatment (Form 2553). This only matters if your STR is properly classified as a **trade or business filing Schedule C** (substantial services, average stay typically under 7 days, material participation). Pure Schedule E rentals can't S-corp, full stop.

For a Schedule C operation netting $80K+, S-corp lets you split income between:

- **W-2 salary to yourself** — subject to 15.3% payroll tax
- **K-1 distribution** — *not* subject to payroll tax

Done right, S-corp can save 5–10% of net income in self-employment tax. Done wrong (unreasonably low salary), it's an audit magnet. Don't elect S-corp without a CPA who handles them.

### Exception 2 — Multi-member LLC

A two-or-more-member LLC files Form 1065 (partnership return) and issues K-1s to each member. This is how spousal-owned, partner-owned, and family-owned STRs typically structure. Tax-equivalent to sole prop in most ways, but the K-1 lets you allocate income/loss disproportionately to the members based on the operating agreement — useful when one partner contributed cash and the other contributed labor.

Co-host arrangements where the co-host actually owns equity (not just paid a percentage) live here. For pure fee-based co-hosts, the [co-host split calculator](/cohost-split-calculator) handles the operational math without entity restructuring.

## A worked example — when the LLC math turns

Two hosts, identical $400,000 STR cabin generating $35K/yr net.

**Host A** — One property, $200K in mortgage, $200K equity. Other personal assets: $30K in retirement, $50K in cash. Total equity at stake: ~$280K.

**Host B** — Three properties, $1.4M total, $700K equity. Other personal assets: $800K (paid-off home + retirement). Total equity at stake: ~$1.5M.

Both carry $1M umbrella plus STR-specific policies.

For Host A: a catastrophic guest injury scenario producing a $1.5M judgment exhausts the umbrella ($1M) and reaches into the personal $80K of non-property assets. Sole prop loses ~$80K. LLC loses zero personal assets but loses the property anyway (it's the LLC's asset). **Marginal LLC benefit: ~$80K.** Annual LLC cost: $400/yr in Florida. Breakeven probability of catastrophic event: 0.5%/yr — comparable to long-tail catastrophic insurance pricing.

For Host B: same scenario produces a $1.5M judgment. Sole prop loses umbrella + reaches into the $800K personal assets and the equity in the *other* properties. Sole prop catastrophic loss: $1.4M+. LLC structure (one LLC per property, ideally) limits the loss to the single property's equity: ~$233K. **Marginal LLC benefit: $1.2M.** Annual LLC cost: $1,500/yr (three LLCs in Florida). Breakeven probability of catastrophic event: ~0.13%/yr.

The break point isn't about absolute revenue. It's about **equity at stake**. Once you cross roughly $300K of net worth — with multiple properties or significant non-property assets — the LLC math gets aggressive enough that not forming one is the bet.

## The series-LLC and parent-LLC patterns

Hosts running 3+ properties usually don't form three single-member LLCs. Two cleaner structures:

### Series LLC

Available in ~15 states (Delaware, Texas, Tennessee, Illinois, Nevada, Wyoming, others). One parent LLC with internal "series" — each series owns one property and has its own liability shield. One filing, one annual fee, one bank-account hierarchy. Dramatically reduces administrative cost vs. multiple LLCs.

The catch: not every state recognizes another state's series LLC. A Delaware series-LLC owner with a property in Florida may find the Florida courts uncertain about the inter-series shield. Talk to a real-estate attorney before forming a series across state lines.

### Parent LLC + child LLCs

A holding LLC owns several subsidiary LLCs, each holding one property. More overhead than a series, more recognized cross-state. Standard structure for hosts with $5M+ in property.

For most hosts at the 1–3 property level, the simpler answer is one LLC per property, all formed in either the property's home state or a host-friendly state with foreign-qualification in the property's state.

## When sole proprietor is the right answer

Two patterns where staying sole prop is defensible:

1. **One property, owner-occupied with a rental unit** (basement, ADU, garage apartment). The "house hacker" scenario. Insurance is the primary shield; LLC complicates the financing (residential lenders won't lend to LLCs at residential rates) and the mortgage interest deduction.
2. **One property, low equity, modest income, no other assets at risk**. A first-time host with $20K equity in a $300K cabin and modest savings can rationally rely on insurance through Year 1–2 while building the cushion that makes the LLC worth forming.

For everyone else with multiple properties or material non-property assets, the LLC is the default. The cost is small relative to the protection, and the operational discipline (separate books, separate accounts) is good practice anyway.

## Refinancing into an LLC

The most common host trap: you bought the property in your personal name on a residential mortgage at 6.5%, then realize you want it in an LLC.

Three options:

1. **Quitclaim deed to the LLC, leave the loan personal.** Fast, free, but the lender's "due-on-sale" clause technically allows them to call the loan. In practice, lenders rarely do this on transfers to a wholly-owned LLC if the borrower stays current — but it's a real risk during a rising-rate cycle.
2. **Refinance to a DSCR/commercial loan in the LLC's name.** Cleaner but rates are typically 1–2% higher than residential rates. On a $300K loan, that's $3K–$6K/yr in interest cost.
3. **Hold off on the LLC for properties already mortgaged residentially. Form the LLC for next-purchase properties only.** Common for hosts mid-portfolio.

If you already have a residential mortgage and equity is small, option 3 is usually right. As you scale, transition to LLC + DSCR financing on new acquisitions.

## FAQ

**Can I deduct LLC formation costs?** Up to $5,000 in startup costs is deductible in the year the LLC starts business; excess is amortized over 15 years (IRC §195). The annual filing fees are deductible as ordinary business expenses.

**Does an LLC change how Airbnb pays me?** Yes — once you change the host account's payout to the LLC's bank account and update the W-9 to the LLC's EIN, the 1099-K issues to the LLC. Update the Airbnb account's "tax info" before you switch banks; mismatches cause payout holds.

**Should I form the LLC in Wyoming or Delaware if I live elsewhere?** Almost never worth it for a single-property host. You'll pay foreign-qualification fees in your operating state plus the formation state's annual fees. The "Wyoming LLC for asset protection" pitch mostly serves the formation services that sell it. For the Series-LLC strategy across multiple properties, the analysis is different — talk to a real-estate attorney.

**Does an LLC affect my mortgage interest deduction?** Single-member LLC: no, it flows through the same as sole prop. Multi-member LLC filing 1065: same flow-through but on a K-1 instead of Schedule E directly. S-corp: more complex.

**My state requires LLC publication (NY). Is it worth the cost?** New York's publication requirement runs $500–$2,000 depending on county. For an STR in NYC, factor it in. For an STR upstate in a low-cost county, the cost is closer to $500. Either way, the protection still typically pays for itself if equity is meaningful.
