The IRS standard business mileage rate for 2026 is **$0.70 per mile** — a number worth knowing precisely because it gets multiplied across every legitimate trip you make for the rental. A host who self-cleans a property 22 miles round-trip, twice a month, plus four quarterly maintenance runs, logs around 670 miles a year. That's $469 in deductions. A host with three properties spread across a metro area easily clears 2,500–4,000 miles, which is $1,750–$2,800.

The deduction is real, the math is simple, and the part that disqualifies most hosts at audit isn't the formula — it's the absence of a contemporaneous log. This piece is about which trips count, which don't, the standard-versus-actual decision, and the recordkeeping that holds up when an examiner asks. The master `/47` Airbnb deductions checklist at [The STR Ledger](https://thestrledger.com) treats mileage as a single line item; this post is the long-form expansion of that line.

*Not tax advice. Always verify with a CPA who handles short-term rental clients.*

## The IRS rate, and where it comes from

The standard mileage rate for business use is set annually by the IRS in a notice issued each December. For tax year 2026 the rate is **$0.70 per mile** for business miles driven (verify against the [IRS standard mileage rates page](https://www.irs.gov/tax-professionals/standard-mileage-rates) before filing — the figure has moved $0.04–$0.07 each year recently). The rate is meant to bundle gas, maintenance, depreciation, insurance, and routine wear-and-tear into a single per-mile number so the host doesn't have to track each underlying cost.

The authority for which trips qualify, and the substantiation rules, is **[IRS Publication 463](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/p463.pdf)** (Travel, Gift, and Car Expenses). Every claim in this post traces back to that publication.

## What counts as a deductible business mile

A mile is deductible if the trip's primary purpose is the rental business. The clean cases:

- Driving to the property to handle a turnover (clean, restock, inspect)
- Driving to the property to meet a contractor, handyman, or inspector
- Driving from the property to a hardware store, supply run, or laundromat for the rental
- Driving to a meeting with your CPA, attorney, or insurance agent for STR-related business
- Driving between two of your STR properties on the same day
- Driving to deliver replacement linens, restock consumables, or troubleshoot a guest issue
- Driving to a STR-specific networking event, mastermind, or local-host meeting (substantiation harder, but allowed)

The trip starts at your home (or other origin) and ends at the rental, hardware store, or destination. Round-trip miles count.

## What does NOT count

The places hosts trip the deduction:

**Commuting from your home to your "regular workplace."** This is the base IRS rule — commuting miles are personal, not business. For a host whose STR isn't their workplace (most W-2-employed hosts), this isn't a constraint. For a full-time STR operator whose home office is the workplace, the rule generally doesn't apply because the home office *is* the workplace; trips from home office to property are business miles.

**Personal-stay miles.** Driving to your own STR for a personal weekend, even if you do "a little maintenance while you're there," is not deductible. Miles attributable to the maintenance portion can be argued, but the IRS position on personal-purpose-bundled trips is unfavorable.

**Mileage on a vehicle you also use for personal driving, without an allocation.** A vehicle used for both personal and business purposes requires an allocation — typically expressed as a business-use percentage, computed from the log. You don't have to use a separate vehicle, but you do have to track which miles were business.

**Reconstructed mileage with no log.** This is where most hosts lose the deduction. The IRS requires *contemporaneous* records — meaning the log was kept at or near the time of the trip, not built from memory at year end. A spreadsheet filled out at tax time, with reconstructed dates and odometer guesses, is widely held to be insufficient under [Pub 463 substantiation rules](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/p463.pdf) and is one of the most-disallowed STR deductions on audit.

## Standard rate vs actual expenses

Two methods, one election per vehicle, locked in for the life of the vehicle in most cases:

**Standard mileage rate.** $0.70/mile in 2026. Multiply business miles by the rate. Add parking and tolls (always separately deductible regardless of method). Done. Simple, audit-friendlier, and almost always the right answer for a host using a personal vehicle for occasional rental trips.

**Actual expenses.** Track every gas receipt, oil change, tire, insurance premium, and registration fee. Compute total annual vehicle cost. Multiply by business-use percentage (business miles / total miles). The result is the deduction. Adds depreciation on the vehicle separately.

When does actual win? When the vehicle is used primarily for the rental business (think: a dedicated truck for a multi-property operator) and the actual cost per mile is well above $0.70. A host running a 2018 F-150 with $7,200/yr in fuel + maintenance + insurance + depreciation, driven 8,000 business miles, has actual cost of $0.90/mile — actual method wins.

For most hosts with a personal car driven occasionally for the rental, **standard rate wins on both math and audit defensibility**. One election per vehicle: if you start with standard, you can switch to actual later (with limitations); if you start with actual using accelerated depreciation, you cannot switch back to standard for that vehicle. Talk to a CPA before electing actual on a new vehicle.

## A worked example

Sarah owns one STR — a townhouse 14 miles from her primary residence. She self-cleans most turnovers, dispatches a contractor for the rest. Her 2026 driving log:

- 38 turnover trips at 28 miles round-trip = 1,064 mi
- 6 contractor-meeting / maintenance trips at 28 miles = 168 mi
- 4 supply runs from STR to Lowe's and back = 24 mi
- 1 trip to her CPA's office for a tax-planning meeting = 22 mi
- 2 trips to a regional STR meetup = 90 mi
- **Total business miles: 1,368**

Multiplied by $0.70/mile: **$957.60 deduction** on Schedule E Line 6 (Auto and travel). Plus $34 of parking from one of the meetups, separately deducted. Total auto deduction: **$991.60**.

If Sarah had instead reconstructed the mileage in March from a vague memory of "I cleaned roughly twice a month," she would likely have written down 800–900 miles at most, deducted $560–$630, and lost roughly $360 to the absence of a real log. She would also have a deduction more vulnerable to disallowance if the return is examined.

## What the log has to contain

[Publication 463](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/p463.pdf) lists the required elements. A defensible mileage log captures, per trip:

- **Date** of the trip
- **Starting odometer** reading (or starting location if using GPS-derived miles)
- **Ending odometer** (or ending location)
- **Total miles** for the trip
- **Business purpose** in one short phrase ("turnover at 1234 Oak St", "Home Depot run for stocking", "meeting w/ Greg the handyman")
- **Destination** (address or recognizable location)

A spreadsheet with those columns, updated within a week of the trip, satisfies the rule. A mileage-tracking app (MileIQ, Stride, Everlance, TripLog) does the same automatically — most cost $5–$8/month and the subscription is itself deductible. The log medium doesn't matter; the contemporaneity does.

Two patterns that fail audit:

1. **Year-end reconstruction.** "I cleaned about twice a month, the property is 14 miles away, so 14 × 4 × 12 = 672 miles." Multiplied by 0.70, deducted $470. Plausible. Not contemporaneous. On audit: disallowed.
2. **Round-number consistency.** Every trip in the log is exactly 28 miles. Every supply run is exactly 12. The IRS examiner sees no GPS variance, no "I had to take the detour around the bridge work in May" reality, and concludes the log was built from a formula. Disallowed.

The fix for both: a real log, kept in real time. The friction is roughly 30 seconds per trip, total maybe 20 minutes a year for a typical host, in exchange for hundreds to thousands of dollars of audit-defensible deduction.

## Mileage and the Schedule C / Schedule E question

The mileage deduction works the same way on either form — Schedule E Line 6 for the rental real estate filer, Schedule C Line 9 for the host filing as an active trade or business. The auto-and-travel deduction is independent of the passive-activity-versus-active-business question. Hosts who toggle between the two forms across years (rare, but happens after a structural change) keep the same vehicle log; only the form line changes.

For the Schedule E vs Schedule C decision itself, see the [airbnb schedule e](https://thestrledger.com) primer on The STR Ledger.

## Two cases hosts often miss

**1. Bookkeeping miles.** If you drive to a co-working space, library, or coffee shop *for* the purpose of doing rental bookkeeping or owner work — and the trip's primary purpose is rental business — the miles are deductible. This is a small line item but it adds up for hosts who don't have a home office.

**2. Mileage during a one-day trip with multiple stops.** If your trip is home → STR → Home Depot → STR → home, all 60 miles are business. The trip doesn't "reset" to personal between stops as long as the dominant purpose remains rental business. Mixed-purpose trips (home → STR → Trader Joe's for personal groceries → home) require allocation: the STR-to-grocery leg becomes personal.

## When mileage isn't worth claiming

Two conditions where the deduction's audit cost exceeds its tax value:

- **Fewer than 100 business miles per year.** $70 of deduction at the 24% bracket is $17 of tax. The hour of recordkeeping isn't worth it.
- **No contemporaneous log and no plan to start one.** Claiming a reconstructed deduction is worse than not claiming. The IRS doesn't punish hosts who didn't claim a legitimate deduction; it does penalize hosts who claimed an undocumented one.

If either applies, skip the line for now and start a real log on January 1 of next year.

## FAQ

**Do I have to use the same vehicle every trip?** No. The log just has to identify which vehicle was used per trip and the miles per vehicle. If you have two cars, track both. The standard-vs-actual election is per vehicle.

**Can I deduct mileage if I drive to my STR to use it personally?** Not for the personal-use portion. If a trip mixes personal use with business purposes, allocate. The cleanest approach: the day you drove there for personal stay = personal miles; a separate day-trip later in the week to handle a real maintenance task = business miles.

**Does the standard rate cover parking and tolls?** No — those are separately deductible regardless of method. Track them as a distinct expense.

**What about driving to a property to take listing photos before it goes live?** Pre-rental-placement-in-service trips are typically capitalized into the property's basis (start-up costs), not deducted as current-year mileage. Once the property is placed in service and listed, subsequent trips are deductible.

**Is the rate different for moving or medical mileage?** Yes. The 2026 medical/moving rate is significantly lower than the business rate, and moving mileage is deductible only for active-duty military members. Don't conflate categories — the business rate ($0.70/mi for 2026) is the only one that applies to STR trips.

**My CPA never asks about mileage. Do I bring it up?** Yes. CPAs whose other clients aren't STR hosts often skip the prompt because mileage is a small deduction for traditional rental real estate (which is mostly hands-off). For STR — where you may drive to the property weekly — it's material. If the CPA dismisses it without seeing your log, that's the wrong CPA.
