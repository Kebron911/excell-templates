*General educational information, not tax or legal advice. STR tax situations vary; verify with a CPA before filing or paying estimates.*

The first April most hosts realize quarterly taxes exist is the April they get a penalty notice for not paying them. The IRS expects tax to be paid as income is earned, not in a single April lump. For W-2 employees, payroll withholding handles that. For Airbnb hosts, no one is withholding anything — and the moment your STR throws off real net income, the obligation lands on you.

This piece covers who actually owes quarterly estimates, how to size the payment without overpaying, and the safe-harbor rule that lets you ignore your actual income for the year and still avoid penalties.

## Who actually owes quarterly taxes

The IRS rule (Form 1040-ES) is short: if you expect to owe **$1,000 or more** of federal tax after withholding and refundable credits, you owe quarterly estimates. Most hosts cross that line the first profitable year their STR runs, sometimes earlier.

Three patterns cover almost every Airbnb host:

**1. STR is your only meaningful income.** Full-time host, full-time co-host, or property manager whose 1099s land on Schedule C. You owe quarterlies as soon as your projected net is in the low five figures, because there is no other withholding to absorb the bill.

**2. STR is a side business and your W-2 already over-withholds.** A teacher with one cabin in Pigeon Forge, a software engineer with a downtown loft. If your day-job withholding already covers your total tax bill, the STR profit may not push you over the $1,000 threshold. Many side-hustle hosts in this bucket genuinely don't owe quarterlies — but the math has to be checked, not assumed.

**3. Spouse W-2 covers most withholding, STR is the second income.** Most common in real life. The W-2 spouse may need to bump up their withholding (Form W-4, line 4c) to absorb the STR's tax, or the STR-running spouse pays quarterlies. Either approach is fine; the IRS doesn't care which spouse pays as long as the total lands.

The trip-wire: a profitable STR throws off self-employment-style income that nobody is withholding on. Even a property netting $18,000/yr at a 22% bracket is roughly $4,000 of federal tax — well past the $1,000 threshold.

## The 2026 due dates

Federal estimates use four "quarters" that don't actually match calendar quarters:

| Period covered | Due date |
|---|---|
| Jan 1 – Mar 31 | April 15, 2026 |
| Apr 1 – May 31 | June 15, 2026 |
| Jun 1 – Aug 31 | September 15, 2026 |
| Sep 1 – Dec 31 | January 15, 2027 |

Q1 covers three months, Q2 two months, Q3 three, Q4 four. Hosts who try to "true-up at year-end" get penalized for under-payment in earlier quarters even if they pay the full year on time. The IRS treats each quarter as its own deadline.

States vary. Most pile on top of the federal calendar with the same dates; a handful (California, NY) have their own quirks. Always check your state DOR.

## How to size the payment — three methods

Three valid ways to compute the quarterly number. Pick whichever produces the lower legal payment for your situation.

### Method 1 — Project current-year income (the realistic method)

Estimate your full-year STR net income, add it to your other taxable income, run it through 2026 brackets, divide by four. This is the most accurate but the most volatile — a strong July ruins your projection from April.

The [profit calculator](/profit-calculator) gets you to a defensible annual net in five minutes. Take that net, multiply by your marginal federal rate (plus 15.3% self-employment if you file Schedule C), divide by four, and that's your quarterly payment.

### Method 2 — Annualized installment method

For hosts whose STR income is concentrated in summer or winter peaks, Form 2210 (Schedule AI) lets you pay estimates that match when the income actually arrives. Q1 and Q2 are tiny; Q3 and Q4 carry the load. This is paperwork-heavy but eliminates the "I overpaid Q1 and underpaid Q3" trap.

### Method 3 — The safe harbor (the easy method)

This is the one most hosts should use the first profitable year:

- Pay **100% of last year's total tax** (110% if your prior-year AGI was over $150,000), spread across four equal quarters.
- The IRS will not assess an underpayment penalty regardless of what you actually owe at year-end.

Translation: take whatever line 24 on your 2025 Form 1040 said, divide by four, and pay that on each due date. If your STR has a banner year, you'll owe more in April — but no penalty. If your STR collapses, you may have overpaid and get a refund.

Safe harbor is the floor. It does not protect you from owing the bill in April; it protects you from the **penalty for under-paying along the way**. Underpaid balances at filing still carry interest at the federal short-term rate plus 3%.

## A worked example

Sarah owns one Smokies cabin. 2025 facts: $94,000 gross, $58,000 expenses (cleaning, mortgage interest, utilities, repairs, depreciation, lodging tax remittance), **$36,000 net**. Her W-2 spouse earns $115,000 with adequate withholding. Joint AGI: $151,000.

Her 2025 federal tax liability ended up at **$22,400** (after credits). 2026 expectations: similar STR performance, similar W-2.

Three paths for 2026 quarterlies:

- **Safe harbor (110% rule applies — joint AGI over $150K):** 110% × $22,400 = $24,640. Spouse's W-4 withholding will cover ~$18,000 of that. Sarah owes $24,640 − $18,000 = **$6,640 across four quarters = $1,660/quarter**.
- **Current-year projection:** Sarah expects $36,000 STR net at a 22% marginal rate = ~$7,920 federal tax on the STR alone. With $18,000 already withheld on the W-2, she owes **$1,980/quarter**.
- **Annualized installment:** the cabin runs 70% of revenue in Jun–Oct. Her Q1 and Q2 estimates would be ~$400 each; Q3 and Q4 would be ~$3,000 each.

The safe harbor is the cheapest *and* the most defensive. She pays $1,660 quarterly, and even if 2026 ends up being her best year ever, the IRS cannot assess an underpayment penalty.

## How to actually pay

Three options:

1. **EFTPS (eftps.gov)** — IRS's free direct-debit system. Schedule all four 2026 payments in January, walk away. This is the cleanest path. Setup takes one week (PIN mailed to your address).
2. **IRS Direct Pay** — one-off ACH from a bank account, no signup. Fine for single payments, but you have to log in four times a year.
3. **Credit card** — payable through IRS-approved processors, ~1.85% fee. Worth it only if you're chasing a sign-up bonus that nets out positive.

Whichever you pick, the moment the payment confirms, log it in your STR P&L. Estimated tax payments are *not* a deductible business expense — they're a personal tax draw — but they need to be tracked separately so you don't double-count them in April.

## The state quarterly trap

Federal isn't the whole story. Most states with income tax also require quarterly estimates and assess their own penalties.

Common gotchas:

- **California (FTB Form 540-ES)** — front-loaded schedule: 30% in Q1, 40% in Q2, 0% in Q3, 30% in Q4. Hosts following the federal even-quarter cadence under-pay California Q1 and Q2.
- **New York (IT-2105)** — separate state safe harbor; doesn't accept federal safe-harbor compliance as state compliance.
- **No-income-tax states** — Texas, Florida, Tennessee, Washington, Nevada, Wyoming, South Dakota, New Hampshire, Alaska. No state quarterly. Lodging tax is still owed separately — see our [lodging tax compliance primer](/blog/airbnb-lodging-tax-compliance) for the gap analysis.

If you operate in two states (live in one, STR in another), you typically owe quarterlies in both. The state where the property sits has first claim on rental income.

## What happens if you skip

The federal underpayment penalty is calculated quarter-by-quarter at the federal short-term rate + 3%, currently around 8% annualized. On a $4,000 underpayment for half the year, that's roughly $160 of penalty — small enough that some hosts deliberately under-pay early quarters to keep cash. The math sometimes works (especially if you can earn more than 8% on the cash). It usually doesn't, because:

1. State penalties stack (often 5–10% additional).
2. The penalty is non-deductible.
3. Cash management gets harder, not easier, when April brings a $20K bill instead of four $5K bills.

The 8% you "save" by holding cash is gross. After tax, after state penalty, after the cash-flow stress, the safe harbor wins.

## FAQ

**I had a W-2 all year and started Airbnb in November. Do I owe Q4 estimates?** Probably yes if the STR netted enough to push your total tax owed above $1,000 over withholding. Pay by January 15, 2027 to avoid Q4 underpayment.

**Can I pay all four quarters in April to keep cash?** No. The IRS treats each quarter as its own deadline. Paying $20K in April covers Q1 only; Q2/Q3/Q4 still get assessed underpayment penalties. EFTPS schedules four payments in advance for free — use it.

**Does Airbnb withhold federal tax?** No. Airbnb issues a 1099-K reporting your gross payouts; no federal tax is withheld. Some states require platform withholding for non-resident hosts in specific scenarios — check your state DOR.

**My STR ran at a loss this year. Do I still owe quarterlies?** If your *total* tax liability projects under $1,000, no. If your day job is profitable enough that you owe more than $1,000 even with the STR loss, yes — pay on the W-2 income side.

**Is the safe-harbor amount adjusted for STR depreciation deductions?** The safe-harbor amount is your prior-year *total tax owed*, which already reflects whatever depreciation was taken. You don't re-adjust it for current-year depreciation plans.
