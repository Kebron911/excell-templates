## How Washington lodging tax works

Washington stacks three components on short-term rental stays:

1. **6.5% state sales tax** — applies to the room charge.
2. **Local sales tax** — counties and cities add 1%–4%, varies by jurisdiction.
3. **Special hotel/motel tax** — cities with active tourism authorities add 2%–4% specifically for lodging.

Effective rates in the top markets:
- **Seattle** (King County): 6.5% state + 3.85% local + 2% special = **roughly 12.35%**
- **Spokane**: 6.5% state + 2.4% local + 2% special = **roughly 10.9%**
- **Leavenworth**: 6.5% state + 1.6% local + 2% special = **roughly 10.1%**
- **Tacoma**: 6.5% state + 3.7% local + 1.5% special = **roughly 11.7%**
- **Olympic Peninsula** (Clallam County): 6.5% state + 2.4% local + 2% special = **roughly 10.9%**

## Platform collection

Airbnb and Vrbo collect and remit **all three layers** (state sales tax, local sales tax, special hotel/motel tax) as marketplace facilitators across most of Washington. The state's marketplace-facilitator law is one of the broadest in the country.

The [Washington Department of Revenue](https://dor.wa.gov/) publishes the current facilitator agreements. Coverage is near-universal for major STR markets.

## What this means in practice

- For **all three tax layers on platform bookings** — Airbnb and Vrbo handle it. You don't file.
- For **direct bookings** — you must register for a Washington UBI (unified business identifier), collect all three layers yourself, and file with the DOR (typically quarterly).
- For **30+ night stays** — exempt from the special hotel/motel tax. State sales tax may still apply depending on the rental structure (long-term residential lease is exempt).
- **Seattle** has additional STR licensing requirements separate from tax registration. License compliance is a precondition for operating, even if tax is handled by the platform.
- **King County** (Seattle metro) tracks STR operators aggressively. Get the license; renewing late triggers fines.
