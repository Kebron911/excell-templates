## The 47

### Operating expenses — the obvious ones most hosts still miscount

**1. Cleaning + maintenance fees paid to contractors** — Sched E Line 8.
Every cleaner, handyman, landscaper, pool guy, hot-tub tech, pest control. The miss: many hosts net cleaning fees against rental income instead of recording them as a separate expense, which inflates revenue and triggers higher self-employment tax exposure on the wrong base. Typical: $3,000–$15,000/yr.

**2. Repairs (vs. improvements — they're treated differently)** — Sched E Line 15.
Patch a hole = repair (deduct now). Replace the roof = improvement (depreciate). Most hosts conflate the two and either expense improvements (audit risk) or capitalize repairs (overpaid taxes). Typical: $1,000–$8,000/yr.

**3. Supplies + consumables** — Sched E Line 16.
Toilet paper, paper towels, soap, shampoo, laundry detergent, coffee, kitchen basics, batteries, light bulbs, lock batteries. Add up over a year — easily $1,500+ per property, often un-tracked. ⚠ verify the line categorization for high-volume consumables vs. capital items like linens > $200.

**4. Utilities** — Sched E Line 18.
Electric, gas, water, sewer, trash, propane. The miss: hosts forget the utility deposit at startup (capitalizable or expensed depending on terms) and the back-bill catch-ups on seasonal properties. Typical: $1,800–$6,000/yr.

**5. Internet + cable + streaming** — Sched E Line 18.
WiFi for the rental, smart TV streaming subscriptions (Netflix/Hulu accounts dedicated to the rental), security camera cloud storage. Typical: $600–$1,800/yr per property.

**6. Property insurance + STR-specific liability riders** — Sched E Line 10.
Standard hazard policy plus the STR endorsement, plus standalone short-term rental liability (Proper, Slice, etc.). Most hosts catch base insurance; many miss the rider. Typical: $1,200–$3,500/yr.

**7. AirCover/Vrbo Liability — supplemental commercial liability** — Sched E Line 10.
If you carry a commercial liability policy in addition to the platform's coverage (most serious hosts should — platform coverage has gaps), the premium is fully deductible. Typical: $400–$1,200/yr.

**8. Umbrella policy (proportional)** — Sched E Line 10.
Personal umbrella policies often have a rental-property allocation. Deduct the rental-allocated portion. Typical: $200–$600/yr.

**9. HOA/COA dues + special assessments** — Sched E Line 17 (Taxes) or Line 19 (Other) ⚠ verify line.
Monthly dues are deductible; one-time assessments may be capitalizable depending on what they fund (capital improvement to common area = capitalize). Typical: $0–$8,000/yr.

**10. Property tax** — Sched E Line 17.
Including any STR-specific local levies, lodging tax registration fees (not the lodging tax itself, which is collected and remitted separately), occupancy tax license fees. Typical: $1,500–$12,000/yr.

**11. Mortgage interest (Form 1098)** — Sched E Line 13.
Catch the entire 1098 amount, not just principal+interest payments. Mortgage insurance premiums are also deductible if the rules apply. ⚠ verify PMI deductibility status for current tax year — this provision has expired and been reinstated multiple times.

**12. Mortgage points (amortized)** — Sched E Line 13.
Points paid at closing are deducted over the life of the loan (not all at once for rentals — different from primary residence). Typical missed amount: $50–$500/yr depending on loan size.

**13. Other interest (HELOC/credit cards used for rental)** — Sched E Line 14.
HELOCs explicitly used to renovate or carry the rental, and credit cards used solely for rental expenses. Trace-the-funds rule applies. ⚠ verify tracing rules under current §163 regs.

### Marketing, platform, and processing

**14. OTA fees / platform commissions** — Sched E Line 19 ("Other").
The 3% Airbnb host fee, Vrbo fees, Booking.com commissions. Most hosts net these from revenue rather than expensing them — same trap as item #1 — wrong revenue base, wrong tax. Typical: $1,500–$12,000/yr.

**15. Payment-processing fees (Stripe, Square, PayPal)** — Sched E Line 19.
For direct-booking hosts. Each transaction's processing cut. Typical: $200–$2,000/yr.

**16. Listing photography** — Sched E Line 6 (Advertising).
Professional photo shoot. Same with video walkthroughs and 3D tours (Matterport). One-time but often $300–$1,500. ⚠ verify whether expensive shoots cross into "capitalize" territory for your situation.

**17. Listing copy + SEO services** — Sched E Line 6.
Hiring a copywriter, SEO consultant, or service like AirHostNomad to optimize your listing. Typical: $100–$500/yr.

**18. Ads** — Sched E Line 6.
Google Ads to your direct-booking site, Facebook ads, Pinterest promoted pins. Typical: $0–$3,000/yr depending on direct-booking strategy.

### Professional services

**19. Property management fees** — Sched E Line 12.
The 15–25% PM cut, or the % co-host fee. Often deducted automatically by the PM before payout — make sure your books reflect the gross-then-net, not just the net deposits. Typical: 15–25% of gross.

**20. Tax prep / CPA fees (allocated)** — Sched E Line 11.
The portion of your CPA bill that relates to Schedule E preparation. A good CPA will break this out. Typical: $300–$1,500/yr.

**21. Bookkeeping fees** — Sched E Line 11.
Monthly bookkeeping services (Bench, Pilot, freelance). Typical: $1,200–$6,000/yr.

**22. Legal fees** — Sched E Line 11.
Contract review, lease drafting, eviction, LLC formation costs (usually capitalize organizational costs under §248 ⚠ verify), entity restructuring. Typical: $0–$2,500/yr.

**23. Consulting fees** — Sched E Line 11.
STR coaching, business strategy, dynamic-pricing consultants (one-time setup), photographer site visits. Typical: $0–$2,500/yr.

### Travel + vehicle

**24. Mileage (standard rate)** — Sched E Line 7.
The 2026 IRS standard mileage rate is **70¢/mile** ⚠ verify current-year rate before publish. Every trip to the property, supply runs, vendor meetings, banking. 3,000 miles/year × $0.70 = $2,100. (See TAX-001 for the tracking template.)

**25. Vehicle actual costs (alternative to mileage)** — Sched E Line 7.
If the property is far enough that actual costs exceed standard rate, track gas, maintenance, depreciation, insurance allocation. Have to choose one method per vehicle per year — switch rules apply. Pub 463.

**26. Parking + tolls during business travel** — Sched E Line 7.
On top of the standard mileage rate (these are not included in the per-mile amount). Typical: $50–$300/yr.

**27. Travel to the property (out-of-town)** — Sched E Line 7.
Flights, hotels, ground transportation, car rentals when you travel to inspect / set up / handle issues at a remote property. Substantiation rules apply. Typical: varies wildly.

**28. Business meals while at the property** — Sched E Line 7. ⚠ verify deductibility for rental owners — meal deduction for landlords vs. active trade/business under §274.
50% of qualifying meal costs while on a business-purpose property visit. Substantiation: receipts + business purpose noted.

### Depreciation — the biggest deduction most hosts skip

**29. Building depreciation (27.5-year SL)** — Sched E Line 20. Pub 946.
The single largest annual deduction for most rental properties. $400K property with $75K land = $11,800/yr at 27.5-year SL. Most hosts miss it because the math feels intimidating. (Built into TAX-002 P&L Tracker.)

**30. Improvements depreciation (15-year SL or 5/7)** — Sched E Line 20.
New flooring, roof, HVAC, kitchen renovation, deck. Each improvement starts its own depreciation schedule. Typical missed: $500–$5,000/yr.

**31. Furniture + appliances depreciation (5-year SL or §179)** — Sched E Line 20.
Beds, sofas, dining sets, refrigerators, washer/dryer. Often eligible for §179 immediate expensing up to current limits ⚠ verify §179 cap. Typical: $1,500–$8,000 in year-1 deduction.

**32. Bonus depreciation (§168(k))** — Sched E Line 20. ⚠ verify current bonus depreciation percentage (was 100% through 2022, phased down 80%/60%/40%/20% on a schedule — Daniel must check 2026 rate before publish).
Lets you deduct a percentage of qualifying property in year-1 instead of straight-line. Massive impact on first-year tax bill, especially after a cost-seg study.

**33. Cost segregation study** — Pub 946.
Reclassifies portions of the property (cabinets, lighting, landscaping) from 27.5-year to 5/7/15-year recovery — accelerates depreciation. Typical study cost: $3,000–$10,000. Typical year-1 boost: $20,000–$80,000 in additional deductions for a $500K+ property. ⚠ verify when cost-seg pays off — small properties (<$300K) often don't justify the study cost.

**34. Land improvements depreciation (15-year)** — Sched E Line 20.
Driveway paving, fencing, landscaping installation, exterior lighting, parking pad. Often missed because hosts lump it into general "improvements." Typical: $300–$2,500/yr.

### The advanced moves most hosts have never heard of

**35. The STR Loophole (active vs. passive — IRC §469)** — major.
If your **average guest stay is ≤ 7 days** AND you materially participate (100+ hours, more than anyone else), your rental can be treated as active rather than passive — meaning rental losses can offset W-2 / business income (huge for high-income W-2 hosts in year 1 of ownership with a cost-seg study). ⚠ verify material participation thresholds and the 100-hour rule precisely. Built into TAX-002 audit-defense block.

**36. Pre-startup costs (IRC §195)** — Sched E Line 19 ⚠ verify line.
Up to **$5,000 in startup costs** can be deducted in year 1 (the rest amortized over 15 years). Costs incurred before the first guest checks in: scouting trips, market research, consultant fees, training, even some pre-listing supplies. Most hosts capitalize these or skip them entirely. Typical missed: $1,500–$5,000 year 1.

**37. Section 195 organizational costs (LLC formation)** — ⚠ verify line / handling.
Same $5,000 first-year deduction for LLC formation costs (filing fees, legal, registered agent first year). Typical: $300–$1,500.

**38. Augusta Rule (§280A(g)) — rent your own home** — *advanced, situational*.
You can rent your **personal residence** (not the rental) for up to **14 days/year** to your own STR business and the rental income is **tax-free to you personally** while remaining a deduction for the business. Documentation has to be airtight. ⚠ verify — significant audit risk if structured wrong, but legitimate when done correctly. Typical benefit: $5,000–$15,000/yr if you have a defensible "fair market rent" for your own home as meeting space.

**39. Home office (Pub 587)** — Sched C if applicable, otherwise allocated to rental.
A dedicated room used regularly + exclusively for managing the STR. Two methods: simplified ($5/sq ft up to 300 sq ft = $1,500 cap) or actual (% of home expenses). ⚠ verify deductibility for Schedule E filers — rules differ from Schedule C.

**40. Cell phone (business %)** — Sched E Line 19.
Allocate the business-use percentage of your cell phone bill. 50% is a common defensible split for active hosts. Typical: $300–$800/yr.

**41. Home internet (business %)** — Sched E Line 19.
Same logic — % allocation of your home internet bill that's used for managing the STR. Typical: $200–$500/yr.

**42. Software + SaaS subscriptions** — Sched E Line 19.
PriceLabs, Hostfully, AirDNA, Touch Stay, Properly, QuickBooks, password manager, accounting software. Typical: $400–$2,000/yr.

**43. Education + courses** — Sched E Line 19.
STR conferences, online courses, books on STR investing, professional development directly related to running the business. Typical: $200–$2,000/yr. ⚠ verify that purely "before you have a rental" education isn't deductible — has to be improving an existing trade or business.

**44. Bank + credit card fees (business accounts)** — Sched E Line 19.
Monthly maintenance fees, wire fees, overdraft fees on dedicated business accounts. Typical: $100–$500/yr.

**45. Refunds + chargebacks given to guests** — net against revenue, not Line 19. ⚠ verify treatment — refunded amount typically reduces gross rents (Line 3), not booked as expense.
Make sure your books capture the GROSS revenue + the refund, not just the net deposit.

**46. Bad debt / unpaid bookings** — Sched E Line 19. ⚠ verify — bad debt deductibility for cash-basis taxpayers (most landlords) is limited.
If a guest stiffs you on a direct booking and you've already recognized the revenue, the bad debt may be deductible on accrual basis. Typically not on cash basis. Typical: rare.

**47. QBI deduction (§199A) — 20% of net rental income** — *if eligible*. ⚠ verify safe harbor.
Not technically a "deduction" — it's a 20% reduction of qualified business income, taken below the line. Rev Proc 2019-38 lays out a **safe harbor** requiring 250+ hours of rental services per year (you, your employees, or contractors hired by you, separate property records, etc.). ⚠ verify — this is a giant deduction (a $30K net rental income year saves ~$1,440 at 24% bracket from this alone) and the rules are nuanced. The TAX-002 audit-defense block flags whether you've met the threshold.
