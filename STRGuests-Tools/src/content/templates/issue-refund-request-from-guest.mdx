## What hosts hear in a refund request

Almost every host reads the first refund request as an attack. Most refund requests
aren't — they're guests trying to recover from a real issue without being asked to
write a bad review to get heard. The template above lands well because it:

1. Asks for a conversation, not a transaction.
2. Names a specific issue rather than gesturing at "disappointment."
3. Proposes a number — which gives the host something concrete to react to.

A vague "this stay was disappointing, can you do something" message gets a 30%
response rate. The structured template above hits closer to 80%.

## What to put in the description

Two to four sentences max. Lead with the facts (no AC for two days; cleaner missed
the bedrooms). Skip emotional language. Don't list every minor issue — pick the
biggest two.

## What's a fair number

A reasonable rule of thumb hosts respect:

- **One full bad night** (no AC, no hot water): one night's rate
- **Half-impacted stay** (one bedroom unusable, intermittent issue): 25–40% of total
- **Cleanliness issue resolved within 24 hours**: 10–15%

Asking for the full stay back unless the property was truly uninhabitable usually
ends in a counter-offer or a Resolution Center fight. Aim fair, not maximal.

## When to escalate to Airbnb

If the host doesn't respond within 48 hours, or counters with $0, you can open a
Resolution Center case. Keep your tone the same; the algorithm reads back-and-forth
messages and a level-headed history wins more cases.
