A guest stands on a porch at 9 p.m. with a phone flashlight and three suitcases. They scroll the Airbnb thread looking for the door code. They find it — but it's buried under three paragraphs about the parking spot and a friendly note about the sunset over the canyon. The kid is whining. The smart-lock keypad is mounted lower than expected and the porch light hasn't come on. They text.

That message wasn't caused by missing information. It was caused by the *format* the information arrived in. The same five facts, presented as three labelled photos with one sentence each, would have ended that arrival in 90 seconds without a host involved.

## The three-photo format

Self check-in instructions are not a how-to guide. They are a sequence of three photos, in arrival order, with one sentence per photo. The brain processes a labelled photo in under a second; it processes a paragraph in seven. At 9 p.m. with kids in the car, the difference is the message.

The three photos, in order:

**Photo 1 — the keypad close-up.** The smart lock or lockbox, framed tight enough that the buttons are clearly visible, shot in the lighting condition the guest will arrive in. Caption: "Code is 4-7-2-9-#. Press the # at the end."

**Photo 2 — the door, viewed from the porch, opened to show the interior.** Crops in on the door handle and lock. The guest sees what success looks like before they unlock. Caption: "Push the door — it sticks slightly in summer."

**Photo 3 — the entryway, shot from inside the door looking in.** Shows the wall switch and any immediate orientation cue. Caption: "Light switch is on the right at shoulder height. Welcome."

Three photos. Three captions. Eighteen words total. That document, sent 24 hours before arrival, eliminates the most common arrival-day messages: *which lock?*, *did I get the code right?*, *is this the right door?*, *where's the light?*

## The worked example

A real check-in document from a 4.92-star Catskills cabin:

> **Photo 1 (keypad close-up, taken at dusk):** "Code is 5-2-8-3-#. Press the lock symbol after entering. Light turns green when it works."

> **Photo 2 (front door, half open, showing interior beyond):** "Push slightly — the door swells in humidity. You'll see the kitchen straight ahead."

> **Photo 3 (entryway, shot from inside):** "Light switch is the second one on the wall to your right. Bags can go on the bench. Welcome to the cabin."

Total document: a single PDF page. Three photos stacked vertically with captions below each. Sent in the pre-arrival message at the 24-hour mark. The host went from 6–8 arrival-day messages per booking to fewer than 1.

What makes this document work is what it *doesn't* contain. There's no parking instructions, no wi-fi, no welcome paragraph, no host signature. Each of those lives somewhere else. The check-in document does one job — get the guest from car to inside the door, with the lights on — and refuses to do anything more.

## Photo composition: the rules nobody told you

The three-photo format only works if the photos are usable. The most common failure is taking the photos in midday sun when guests will arrive at dusk, or framing the keypad too far away to read the buttons. Specifics that matter:

- **Shoot in the lighting condition guests actually arrive in.** Most check-ins happen between 3 p.m. and 8 p.m. — shoot in afternoon shadow or early evening, not noon. If guests arrive after dark, shoot at the same time of evening, and ensure your porch light is on in the photo.
- **Keypad photos: 18–24 inches away.** Close enough to read the numbers, far enough to show context (whether the keypad is at waist or shoulder height, what the door looks like around it).
- **Crop tight, but not cropped to the keypad alone.** Guests need a 6-inch border of door visible to know they're at the right spot.
- **Avoid the "real estate listing" angle.** The wide-from-the-driveway shot looks pretty in your listing but is useless for navigation. Save those for the listing photos. Check-in photos are operational documents, not marketing.
- **Take the photos yourself, on your phone, not the cleaner's, not a stock shot.** They need to match the actual current state of the property — the same bench, the same coir mat, the same wall color the guest will see.

Reshoot when anything visible changes. New welcome mat? Reshoot. Lock got replaced? Reshoot. Front door painted? Reshoot. The whole document should be reproducible in an afternoon.

## Text alongside the photos: word count discipline

The caption under each photo is one sentence. Two if the second sentence solves a known failure mode (door sticks, light is dim, second-floor unit, etc.). The temptation is to over-explain — *resist*.

The pattern that fails:

> The keypad is to the left of the door. You'll see a black box with numbers on it, mounted at about chest height. Enter the code 4-7-2-9 followed by the # symbol. You may need to press the buttons firmly. If the light flashes red, the code didn't take — wait 5 seconds and try again. The lock will beep once when it accepts the code. You can also check the YouTube video we sent if you need a visual.

Same information, the format that works:

> Code is 4-7-2-9-#. Light flashes green when it works.

Six words versus 65. Both contain the load-bearing facts. The first wraps the facts in 60 words of warm-host noise that the guest's brain has to filter. At 9 p.m. with luggage, the filter doesn't work.

## Timing: when to send the document

Three send moments work. One — the 24-hour pre-arrival message — does most of the work. The other two are insurance.

1. **24 hours before check-in.** Primary send. Guest is in pre-arrival mindset, packing or driving, has time to read. Door code lives here. The [4-message guest cadence](/blog/guest-message-cadence-pre-arrival-to-checkout) covers the surrounding messaging architecture.
2. **2 hours before check-in.** Optional re-send for late-evening or after-dark arrivals. Brief: *"Arrival in a couple hours — here's the check-in document again, attached. Text us if anything's confusing."*
3. **At check-in time.** Auto-message that fires at the booking's exact check-in hour. *"Hope check-in went smoothly. The wi-fi password is in the welcome book on the kitchen counter."* This pivots from arrival to in-stay and signals the host is paying attention.

Don't send the document at booking confirmation. It's noise then — the guest hasn't packed yet and the doc gets buried in the thread. They'll search for it 24 hours later and find your booking-day message instead. Send late.

## Password vs unlock-code: the phrasing that doesn't get questioned

Words matter when guests are stressed. The same physical action gets called five different things across listings:

- *Door code* (clear, common, the right default)
- *Lock code* (clear, slightly less common)
- *Pin* (ambiguous — sounds like a hotel safe)
- *Password* (wrong word; reserve for wi-fi)
- *Combination* (sounds like a school locker, not a smart lock)

Use **door code** in the message text and on the photo caption. Reserve **password** strictly for wi-fi. Reserve **lockbox combination** for actual lockboxes (the four-dial physical kind), which are a different UX and need different photos.

If your property has both a door code *and* a separate gate code, label them clearly: "Gate code: 1-9-8-2 · Door code: 4-7-2-9-#". Two codes on one line, gate first since the guest hits it first. Don't bury the gate code in a paragraph.

## Fallback flow: when the smart lock fails

Smart locks fail. Battery dies, wi-fi drops, the lock firmware gets confused. The fallback flow is the difference between a guest who texts you calmly and a guest who leaves a 1-star review about being locked out.

Build the fallback into the document, not as a separate troubleshooting page. One additional line under photo 1:

> If the keypad doesn't respond, the backup key is in a lockbox to the right of the door — combination 4-2-8-1.

Or:

> If anything's stuck, call/text us at 555-241-9982. We answer.

A backup key in a physical lockbox is the most common fallback. A live phone line is the most reassuring. Both is best — backup key for self-service, phone for "the lockbox is also broken." ## Accessibility: small notes that matter

Two accessibility considerations turn a check-in document from "good for most" to "good for everyone":

- **Caption font size in the document.** 14pt minimum, 16pt better. Older travelers and anyone reading on a phone in low light need it.
- **Step-free arrival noted explicitly.** If your property is step-free, say so in the document caption ("step-free entry, no threshold lip"). If it isn't, say *that* clearly in the listing copy and pre-arrival message — surprise stairs at 9 p.m. with a wheelchair is a review nuke.

Audio alternatives (a 30-second voice-memo version of the instructions) are nice but rarely worth the build effort below the 4-property scale. The three-photo PDF works for 95% of guests; for the 5% who need more, a quick host phone call covers the gap.

## What changes when you actually use this format

Within 30 days of switching from a paragraph-based check-in message to the three-photo PDF format:

- **Arrival-day messages drop 70–80%.** "Where's the door?", "did the code work?", "which entrance?" — gone.
- **Late-checkout requests sometimes drop too.** Guests who had a smooth arrival are slightly more likely to leave on time, because they don't need a recovery hour.
- **Review content shifts.** "Easy check-in" appears as a phrase in 30%+ of reviews vs ~10% before. The listing-search algorithm treats this as positive signal.

The cost is one afternoon of taking three photos and writing six sentences. The return is one fewer hour of arrival-day messaging per booking and a small but real bump in 5-star rate.

## FAQ

**Should the photos be inside the Airbnb message or as a PDF attachment?** Both. Drop the photos directly inline in the message thread (Airbnb supports inline images), and attach a single-page PDF for guests who want to save it offline. Per [Airbnb's self check-in guidance](https://www.airbnb.com/help/article/2299), guests overwhelmingly prefer inline visuals over linked PDFs.

**What if I have a building lobby code AND a unit door code?** Three photos still works — photo 1 becomes the lobby keypad, photo 2 the unit door, photo 3 the entryway inside the unit. Same format, more important than ever for multi-unit buildings.

**Do I need a video tour?** No. A walkthrough video is nice but does a different job (orienting once inside the unit). It belongs in the welcome book, not the arrival document. Mixing them means guests skip both.

**What if my smart lock has an app that the guest needs?** Add a fourth photo only for app-required locks: a screenshot of the unlock button in the app, captioned with the exact tap sequence. App-required locks have higher arrival friction across the board — consider whether the keypad fallback is worth wiring up.

**Can I use the same document for every property?** Each property needs its own, because every door is different. The *format* is reusable; the photos are not. If you operate 5 properties, expect 5 documents — each is a single page, each takes 20 minutes to assemble.
