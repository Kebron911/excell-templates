Roughly 80% of U.S. cities with meaningful tourism now regulate short-term rentals in some form. The other 20% are working on it. A buyer who underwrites a deal without reading the current ordinance — not the version from two years ago — is buying a coin flip on the most consequential variable in the deal.

You don't need a lawyer to do the first pass. You need to know which eight clauses to find and what the red-flag language looks like.

## Where to find the ordinance

Start at the city's official website. Search for any of:

- "short-term rental" + city name
- "vacation rental"  
- "transient lodging" / "transient occupancy"
- "STR program"
- "tourist accommodation"

Most cities now have a dedicated STR program page with the full ordinance, the application form, the public license registry, and contact info for the program administrator. If you can't find it on the city site, search the **municipal code** directly — Municode and AmericanLegal host most U.S. city codes.

For county-jurisdiction properties (outside city limits), check the county code separately. The city ordinance does not apply.

## The eight clauses to find

**1. Definition of "short-term rental"**

Look for the night-count threshold. Most ordinances define STR as a stay shorter than 28, 30, or 31 nights. Some define it as fewer than 14 nights. The definition matters because:

- Mid-term rentals (30+ nights) often escape the STR ordinance entirely.
- A 28-night threshold blocks the most common mid-term loophole.
- Some ordinances exclude properties only rented fewer than N times per year ("incidental" exemption).

**2. License / permit requirement**

- Is a license required? (Yes in most regulated cities.)
- What's the application fee + annual renewal fee?
- What's the typical processing time? (Some cities run 60–120 days.)
- Is the license **transferable** to a new owner? **This is the big one.** Non-transferable licenses mean you're buying the property without buying the right to operate it as an STR. The seller's TTM revenue is irrelevant if you can't get a new license issued.

**3. Caps and quotas**

- Is there a citywide cap on STR licenses? (Charleston SC, Santa Monica CA, others.)
- Is there a per-zone cap or density limit?
- Is there a per-block cap (no more than X STRs per block)?
- Is the cap currently full? (Public registry will tell you.)

If the city is at-cap, your only path to operating is buying a property that already holds a license — and only if that license is transferable. If it's neither, the deal is dead even if the seller is operating an STR today.

**4. Owner-occupancy distinction**

Many ordinances allow owner-occupied STRs (Type 1) freely while restricting non-owner-occupied (Type 2) heavily. Look for:

- Are non-owner-occupied STRs permitted in the property's zone?
- Is there a "primary residence" requirement (e.g., owner must live there 270+ days/year)?
- What proves owner-occupancy? (Driver's license, voter registration, utility bills, tax returns.)

**5. Zoning restrictions**

- Which zoning categories permit STR use? Residential single-family (R-1)? Multifamily (R-2/R-3)? Commercial?
- Are there overlay districts (historic, coastal, university, downtown) that override base zoning?
- Are HOA / CC&R rules deferred to (most cities yes)?

Pull the zoning map for the property's address. Verify the zoning permits the use you intend.

**6. Operational requirements**

- Local contact requirement (must be reachable within X minutes/hours).
- Insurance minimums.
- Smoke/CO detector + emergency information posting.
- Trash / parking / noise rules.
- Maximum occupancy formula (often "2 per bedroom + 2" or "by sleeping room").
- Minimum stay requirements (some cities require 2-night minimums).

**7. Tax obligations**

- Transient occupancy tax (TOT) / lodging tax / hotel tax — usually 6–15% on top of state sales tax.
- Who collects? (Airbnb/Vrbo collect in many jurisdictions but not all.)
- Filing frequency — monthly, quarterly, annually.

**8. Enforcement**

- Penalties for operating without a license.
- Penalties for repeat violations.
- License revocation triggers.
- Appeals process.

## Red-flag language

When you find any of the following, treat the deal as high-risk and price the regulation tail accordingly:

- **"License is non-transferable upon sale of the property."** Single biggest deal-killer.
- **"No new STR licenses are being issued at this time."** Cap is full or moratorium in place.
- **"All STR licenses sunset on [date]"** — the program is being wound down.
- **"Primary residence requirement"** for the zone the property sits in, if you're not planning to live there.
- **"STRs are prohibited in [zone]"** — if your property's zone is named, walk.
- **Recent ordinance amendment date** within the last 6–12 months — the city is actively iterating, expect more changes.
- **Pending litigation** referenced anywhere — pull the case docket. The Texas / Austin / Houston cases reshaped Texas STR law repeatedly between 2019 and 2024.

## Cross-checks the ordinance won't tell you

The ordinance is necessary but not sufficient. Also verify:

- **The HOA or condo association's CC&Rs** — many newer subdivisions ban STR regardless of city rules.
- **The deed restrictions** — older properties sometimes have deed restrictions that supersede zoning.
- **Active enforcement posture** — search the local newspaper / Reddit / NextDoor for STR controversy. A permissive ordinance loosely enforced by a hostile city can be worse than a strict ordinance evenly enforced.
- **Pending council agendas** — agendas are public. If "short-term rental ordinance amendment" is on the next council meeting, you're underwriting yesterday's rules.

## When to bring in a lawyer

DIY ordinance review gets you to a yes/no/maybe. For a maybe, spend $400–$800 on an STR-savvy local real estate attorney to review:

- The current ordinance and any pending amendments.
- The specific property's zoning, deed restrictions, and HOA documents.
- The license registry record for the address (if applicable).
- Recent enforcement history in the immediate neighborhood.

That's the cheapest insurance dollar you'll spend on a six-figure investment.

Run the [market score](/market-score) for the city — it'll surface the regulation status (permissive / moderate / restrictive / banned) before you get this deep.

## Pitfalls

- **Reading the ordinance once.** STR ordinances are amended frequently — re-pull the version on the day you go under contract.
- **Trusting the listing agent's verbal summary.** "It's been an STR for years" is not a legal opinion.
- **Skipping the license registry check.** A "currently operating" property may have a lapsed or disputed license.
- **Confusing city rules with county rules.** A property "in Austin" may be in unincorporated Travis County, with a totally different rule set.
- **Underwriting as if the ordinance is permanent.** It isn't. Build a Plan B (mid-term rental, traditional 12-month lease) into the underwriting for any deal in a regulated market.

The ordinance is the most important piece of due diligence on an STR purchase. Read it before the inspection, not after.
