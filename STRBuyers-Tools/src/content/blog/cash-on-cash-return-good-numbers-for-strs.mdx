The cash-on-cash return numbers you see in YouTube STR breakdowns are almost always inflated, because the people quoting them either:

1. Excluded a major cash line from the denominator (FF&E, reserves, setup),
2. Included a major revenue line in the numerator that doesn't actually flow to the owner (gross revenue, not net), or
3. Used peak-season ADR annualized as base case.

Here's how to calculate cash-on-cash honestly, what good looks like by market tier, and why I increasingly underwrite STRs on a different metric.

## The honest CoC formula

```
Cash-on-cash return = Annual pre-tax cash flow / Total cash invested
```

Both sides of the ratio need scrutiny.

**Numerator — annual pre-tax cash flow:**

```
Gross revenue
  − Cleaning fees passed through to cleaners
  − Operator/co-host fee (if applicable)
  − Variable supplies and consumables
  − Utilities, internet, streaming
  − Property management software (PMS, dynamic pricing, locks)
  − Maintenance & repairs (budget 1–2% of property value annually)
  − Property tax (annual)
  − Insurance (annual)
  − HOA dues
  − Mortgage interest + principal (PI)
  = Pre-tax cash flow
```

A common mistake: subtracting only mortgage interest, treating principal as "still mine, building equity." That's correct as a wealth-accounting matter but wrong for cash flow — principal payments are cash that left your bank account this year. CoC is a cash metric.

**Denominator — total cash invested:**

```
Down payment
+ Closing costs
+ FF&E & setup
+ Smart-home infrastructure
+ Photography & listing setup
+ First 60 days operating cash
+ LLC formation, insurance setup, misc closing-adjacent
= Total cash invested
```

This is where the optimistic math happens. People plug in down payment ($90K) and report CoC against $90K, ignoring the $80–100K of FF&E + reserves + closing costs that also went out the door.

Run the [cash-on-cash calculator](/cash-on-cash-calculator) with the full denominator. The numbers look very different.

## What good looks like by market tier

There's no single "good" CoC number — it depends on the market profile, the regulatory risk, and your alternative use of capital. Realistic ranges I see:

| Market tier | Typical CoC range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Tier-1 destination, mature (Park City, Gatlinburg, 30A) | 4–8% | High ADR, high competition, high property prices. CoC is compressed. |
| Tier-2 destination, growing (smaller mountain town, secondary lake) | 8–14% | Best CoC tier in 2026. Less crowded, decent ADR, moderate prices. |
| Mid-size urban with strong event calendar | 6–12% | Wide variance. Regulation risk usually present. |
| Suburban / mid-term hybrid market | 5–9% | Lower variance, more boring, easier to manage. |
| Edge / experimental markets | 2–6% or negative | Low confidence in TTM data. Higher risk premium needed. |

A 12%+ CoC on a tier-1 destination is almost always one of three things: bought before 2020, optimistic underwriting, or a property with a competitive moat (rare view, unusual amenity, exceptional design).

A negative or single-digit CoC isn't automatically a bad deal — appreciation, principal paydown, and tax advantages can stack to a strong total return. But if you're buying for cash flow specifically, single-digit CoC usually doesn't justify the operational complexity of an STR over a long-term rental.

## Why I'm increasingly underwriting on something else

CoC has two structural problems for STRs:

1. **It doesn't account for vacancy or seasonal cash gaps.** A 12% annualized CoC may include 4 months that net negative.
2. **It treats one-time setup costs identically across underwriting periods.** A $40K FF&E spend hits Year 1 cash hard but should amortize across 5–7 years of useful life.

Two metrics that are often more useful for STRs:

**RevPAR / cost basis** — annual gross revenue per available night divided by cost basis. Lets you compare deals across markets without the FF&E noise. A property earning $80 RevPAR on a $400K cost basis (7.3% gross yield) gets benchmarked apples-to-apples against a $120 RevPAR on a $700K cost basis (6.3% gross yield).

**Stabilized year-2 CoC** — calculate Year 2 cash flow (after stabilization, no setup costs in the denominator) divided by Year 1 invested cash. This is the number that tells you what the property does once it's running.

## A defensible underwriting approach

For each deal:

1. Pull comp set and compute median, P25, P75 RevPAR (see [analyzing comps](/blog/analyzing-airbnb-comps-before-you-buy)).
2. Compute total cash invested with the full denominator.
3. Project Year 1 cash flow (under-rev, full setup costs, partial occupancy ramp).
4. Project Year 2+ cash flow (stabilized).
5. Compute Year 1 CoC (informational only — usually negative or low).
6. Compute Year 2 stabilized CoC at P25, median, P75 RevPAR.
7. **The deal must clear your CoC threshold at P25.** Not at median, not at P75. At P25.

If a deal only pencils at median or above, you're betting on average performance in a market where 25% of operators do worse than that. The math is asymmetric — when you miss your number, you miss it badly.

## Pitfalls

- **Quoting CoC against down payment, not total cash invested.** The denominator is the trick.
- **Annualizing peak-season ADR.** Don't use SXSW/F1/Christmas-week numbers as your base case.
- **Ignoring principal in the cash flow calculation.** Principal payments are real cash leaving your account.
- **Comparing STR CoC to long-term rental CoC apples-to-apples.** STRs have 5–10x the operational variance. A 10% LTR CoC is almost always less risky than a 10% STR CoC.

The right underwriting question isn't "will this hit 12% CoC?" It's "does this hit my hurdle rate at the P25 RevPAR scenario, with full denominator math, in Year 2?" Answer that honestly and most of the dumb deals filter themselves out.
