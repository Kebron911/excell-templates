Most short-term rental buyers come into the search with one financing assumption — "I'll get a conventional loan, put 20% down, done." That assumption holds for a primary residence with a guest room you list on Airbnb three weekends a month. It falls apart fast for a non-owner-occupied investment property in a destination market. The wrong loan product can cost you 1–2% in rate, $30K+ in extra down payment, or — in the worst case — disqualify you entirely.

This is the loan-product cheat sheet I wish someone had handed me before my first STR underwrite.

## The three buckets

There are roughly three loan archetypes a buyer will encounter when shopping a short-term rental:

1. **Conventional investment loan** (Fannie Mae / Freddie Mac conforming, non-owner-occupied)
2. **DSCR loan** (Debt Service Coverage Ratio — a non-QM commercial-style product)
3. **FHA / VA / "house hack"** (owner-occupied, you live in the property)

USDA, jumbo conventional, portfolio loans, and hard money exist in the margins, but for a typical first STR purchase under $1M, the decision tree is one of those three.

## Conventional investment loan

The good news: rates are usually the lowest of the three options because the loan conforms to GSE underwriting standards. The bad news is everything else.

- **Down payment:** 20–25% minimum for a single-family non-owner-occupied. 25–30% for a 2–4 unit.
- **Reserves:** Six months of PITI for the subject property, plus reserves for any other financed properties you own.
- **Income qualification:** Underwritten on your personal DTI. Your W-2 income, tax returns, and existing debt all count. STR rental income is generally **not** counted toward qualifying income unless the property has a 12-month operating history with documented receipts.
- **Property limit:** Fannie Mae caps you at 10 financed properties before you have to switch products. In practice many lenders cap at four to six.

The killer for STR buyers: conventional underwriting wants you to qualify on your existing DTI, not on the property's projected income. If you already own a home with a mortgage and have student loans or a car payment, you may not qualify even with strong reserves.

## DSCR loan — the workhorse for STR investors

DSCR loans flip the underwriting model: the property qualifies, not you. The lender computes Debt Service Coverage Ratio (gross monthly rent ÷ monthly PITIA) and lends if it clears their threshold — typically 1.0 to 1.25 depending on lender and tier.

- **Down payment:** Usually 20–25%, sometimes 15% on a top-tier deal.
- **Income qualification:** No tax returns, no W-2 verification, no DTI. The deal lives or dies on the rent number. Most DSCR lenders will use **either** 12-month STR receipts (if the property has them) or a market rent appraisal (Form 1007 / 1025) for projected long-term rent. A few specialist lenders will underwrite to projected STR income via AirDNA or Mashvisor data — that's the niche where you want to be shopping if your numbers depend on STR ADR.
- **Rate:** Typically 1.0–2.5 percentage points higher than conventional. The premium is the cost of the no-doc convenience.
- **Property type flexibility:** Most DSCR lenders will fund 1–4 unit, condotels, mid-term rentals, and in some cases pure STR vacation homes that conventional won't touch.

For someone who's self-employed, has a dozen rentals already, or whose W-2 income doesn't pencil for the deal even though the property does, DSCR is the path. The rate premium is real, but so is the ability to actually close.

## FHA / VA / "house hack" — owner-occupied path

If you're willing to live in the property for at least 12 months, FHA opens up a 3.5% down payment on a 1–4 unit. VA goes to 0% down for eligible veterans. You list one or more units on Airbnb while living in another, and the lender doesn't care because — on paper — it's your primary residence.

This is the cheapest capital you'll ever access for a rental property. The catch: most cities with strong STR demand have aggressive owner-occupancy verification (utility bills, voter registration, mail forwarding) and the city's STR ordinance may distinguish owner-occupied STRs from non-owner-occupied STRs in ways that matter. Run the [market score tool](/market-score) on your target city to see the regulation status before you commit to the house-hack play.

## The side-by-side

| Loan type | Down payment | Rate (relative) | Income docs | Property limit |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Conventional investment | 20–25% | Lowest | Full doc | 10 financed |
| DSCR | 15–25% | +1.0–2.5 pts | None (DSCR-only) | Effectively unlimited |
| FHA owner-occupied | 3.5% | Lowest | Full doc | 1 at a time |

## Which one fits you?

A few quick filters:

- **W-2 income doesn't qualify, but the deal does?** DSCR.
- **You're a first-time investor, low cash reserves, willing to live in the property?** FHA house hack.
- **You're a strong-DTI buyer with one or two rentals max planned?** Conventional, every time. Cheapest money wins.
- **You already own four or more financed properties?** DSCR — you're approaching the conventional cap.

The most common mistake I see is buyers defaulting to conventional, getting DTI-rejected at 60 days into the contract, and scrambling for DSCR at a worse rate because they didn't pre-qualify both paths. If you're not certain, get pre-quals from a conventional lender **and** a DSCR lender on day one. The DSCR pre-qual is essentially free and gives you a backup if the conventional path falls through.

## Pitfalls

- **Underwriting STR income on a conventional loan.** Most won't credit it. Don't model your DTI assuming projected Airbnb revenue counts — it almost never does on a Fannie/Freddie underwrite.
- **DSCR rate sticker shock.** That 1.5-point premium adds $200–400 to your monthly PITI on a typical $400K loan. Make sure your deal still cash-flows after you bake it in.
- **FHA and STR rules in the same city.** Some jurisdictions explicitly prohibit STR in owner-occupied multi-units (or cap nights/year). Read the ordinance before you close.
- **Lender shopping by rate alone.** A DSCR lender that funds in 21 days at 8.25% is worth more than one quoting 7.75% with a 60-day timeline if you're under contract.

The right loan for your STR isn't a trivia question — it's the difference between a deal that closes and one that doesn't.
