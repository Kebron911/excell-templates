## How market scoring works

Investors evaluate STR markets along four dimensions: how much you can charge per
night (ADR), how often the unit books (occupancy), whether you're allowed to
operate (regulation), and how much competition you'll face (saturation).

This tool compresses all four into a single 0–100 score, weighted to reflect
real-world deal-killers:

- **ADR** — 30 points, scaled $80 (0 pts) → $400+ (30 pts)
- **Occupancy** — 30 points, scaled 30% (0 pts) → 75%+ (30 pts)
- **Regulation** — 20 points (allowed = 20, restrictive = 10, banned = 0 + score zeroed)
- **Saturation** — 20 points (low = 20, medium = 12, high = 4)

If regulation is banned, the score is forced to 0 — no amount of ADR rescues a market
where you can't legally operate.

## How to use this calculator

1. Pull the **median ADR** for the market from AirDNA / PriceLabs (use median, not
   mean — STR markets have long right tails).
2. Pull the **annual occupancy** from the same source. Most data tools express it
   as a decimal (0.62 = 62%).
3. Categorize **regulation** based on the city/county ordinance.
4. Estimate **saturation** based on year-over-year listing growth + cap on growth.

## Reading the label

- **Strong (70+)** — high-ADR, high-occupancy, allowed, low-medium saturation. Worth
  underwriting deals here.
- **Mixed (50–69)** — depends entirely on the property. Some neighborhoods score high
  while the market overall is mid-tier.
- **Marginal (30–49)** — at least two factors working against you. Possible but every
  property has to outperform the median.
- **Avoid (under 30, or banned)** — the math doesn't work for new entrants.

## FAQ

### What if my market is hot but saturation is high?

That's a "mixed" market by design — high occupancy lifts the score, but high
saturation pulls it down. Investigate whether the saturation is at unit count or
permit cap; permit-capped markets often hold ADR better than uncapped ones.

### Should I use this instead of AirDNA's MarketScore?

This is a complement, not a substitute. AirDNA's score weights more heavily on
revenue per available rental (RevPAR). Use both. They tend to agree on extremes
(strong markets, banned markets) and diverge on mid-tier — that divergence is where
deeper diligence is most valuable.
